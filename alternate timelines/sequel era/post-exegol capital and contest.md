# Part 1

The victory at Exegol did not end the war in a single night. It shattered the spine of the First Order, but the body still thrashed.

Across the galaxy, millions watched the destruction of the Sith fleet through intercepted transmissions and eyewitness reports carried by desperate pilots fleeing the battle. At first, many worlds refused to believe it. For years the First Order had seemed unstoppable—an iron shadow descending system by system while governments collapsed, navies surrendered, and populations learned to survive through silence. Even after Exegol, fear lingered. Citizens had spent too long being punished for hope.

Then the uprisings began.

On dozens of occupied worlds, local resistance cells emerged from hiding within days. Former senators, smugglers, planetary defense officers, labor unions, criminal syndicates, religious groups, and ordinary civilians all moved at once, often without coordination. Garrisons found supply depots sabotaged. Stormtrooper patrols vanished in alleys and industrial tunnels. Star destroyers above occupied capitals lost access to fuel and communications as dockworkers revolted.

The First Order attempted to restore control through terror, but terror no longer carried the same weight after Exegol. Rumors spread faster than official statements. Citizens whispered that the Supreme Leader was dead. That the Sith Eternal fleet had burned. That the Resistance had survived. Entire sectors began refusing First Order directives outright.

The surviving Resistance leadership understood immediately that the conflict had entered a dangerous phase. A wounded empire could still kill millions.

General Poe Dameron, now burdened with responsibilities he had never wanted, organized the Resistance into mobile liberation fleets. Rather than trying to conquer territory directly, they focused on connecting rebellious worlds together and destroying First Order logistical routes. Every liberated hyperlane became another artery cut.

Finn became one of the most important figures of the post-Exegol war. Former stormtroopers emerged from hiding throughout the galaxy after hearing stories of his defection and leadership during the battle. Many had deserted years earlier and survived under false identities. Others mutinied only after Exegol convinced them the regime could truly fall. Finn traveled constantly between liberated systems, speaking not as a general but as someone who understood exactly what the First Order had made them into.

Entire stormtrooper units surrendered after hearing his broadcasts.

Some did not surrender at all—they turned on their commanders.

The First Order fragmented unevenly. In the Core Worlds, many officers attempted to preserve authority through emergency coalitions and martial law. In the Mid Rim and Outer Rim, isolation bred ambition. Admirals, governors, and moffs began transforming themselves into localized rulers, carving territory from the remains of the regime. Some claimed loyalty to the old order. Others abandoned ideology entirely and ruled through simple coercion.

The galaxy descended into a year of scattered wars.

On Kuat, shipyard workers disabled orbital production lines while rival First Order admirals fought each other for control of the planet’s fleet reserves. On Mon Cala, surviving occupation forces were trapped underwater and starved into surrender after native resistance fighters reclaimed the oceans. On Corellia, armed militias battled retreating stormtrooper legions through industrial districts while merchant guilds financed private defense fleets to keep trade routes open.

Coruscant became one of the bloodiest struggles of all.

The First Order had invested enormous resources into controlling the old galactic capital. Massive security sectors divided the ecumenopolis into heavily monitored districts, and countless officials feared what would happen if Coruscant fell into chaos. The uprising there began deep in the lower levels, spreading upward floor by floor like fire through dry circuitry. Former Republic bureaucrats resurfaced from hiding. Criminal networks armed civilians in exchange for future political protection. Maintenance crews sabotaged surveillance grids and transit lines.

For months, fighting raged across the endless city-world.

The Resistance could not afford a full-scale orbital assault on Coruscant; the casualties would have been catastrophic. Instead, they supported coordinated urban rebellions while defecting First Order officers quietly negotiated surrenders sector by sector. By the time the last major garrison capitulated, vast sections of the planet were scarred by bombardment, famine, and infrastructural collapse.

Yet when the smoke cleared, the old capital still stood.

The final organized First Order fleet action occurred nearly a year after Exegol in the Colonies region, where several warlords attempted to unite under a surviving high command officer. The alliance lasted less than three months before internal rivalries destroyed it from within. Resistance fleets and sector defense forces crushed the remnants soon afterward.

By then, the war had already ended in spirit.

The galaxy faced a different question now: what came next?

The destruction of Hosnian Prime still haunted every political discussion. The old New Republic had collapsed with horrifying speed because too much authority had become paralyzed by caution, decentralization, and political fragmentation. Entire sectors had been left defenseless while senators debated procedure. Even worlds that had distrusted centralized power now admitted that the galaxy could not survive another era of leaderless drift.

The surviving political class slowly emerged from exile, hiding, and military service.

Former senators gathered first in temporary assemblies hosted aboard cruisers and secure planetary compounds. Representatives from liberated systems arrived alongside Resistance commanders and local governors who had effectively ruled their regions during the war. Arguments erupted immediately over military authority, reconstruction funding, sector autonomy, and emergency powers.

But beneath every disagreement lay one unavoidable issue.

The New Republic needed a capital.

The rotating capital system of the previous era had once been designed as a symbol of equality between worlds. In practice, many now blamed it for weakening the government’s cohesion and responsiveness. Bureaucracies had become nomadic and slow. Ministries fragmented across hyperspace. Defense coordination suffered. During crises, senators often prioritized their home sectors over collective action.

This time, stability mattered more than symbolism.

The debate over where to establish the restored galactic government became the defining political struggle of the reconstruction era. Hundreds of worlds quietly explored the possibility, but only three candidates emerged as serious contenders.

Coruscant.

Chandrila.

Corellia.

Each represented a different vision for the future of the New Republic.

Coruscant carried the immeasurable weight of history. For thousands of years it had served as the heart of galactic civilization. Its infrastructure, archives, universities, financial institutions, and diplomatic networks remained unmatched despite the devastation of recent years. Supporters argued that governing the galaxy from anywhere else was impractical romanticism. Trade routes, communications, and administration naturally converged there. To them, reclaiming Coruscant meant reclaiming continuity and legitimacy after decades of war.

Its critics saw something else: the graveyard of every failed galactic regime.

Chandrila represented restraint and republican idealism. The homeworld of many prominent New Republic founders had avoided the excesses associated with Coruscant and still possessed strong institutional credibility despite the Republic’s collapse. Advocates described it as stable, cultured, and politically trustworthy—a capital that would encourage measured governance rather than imperial grandeur.

Its opponents argued that Chandrila lacked the scale necessary to command the galaxy in an age of instability.

Corellia emerged as the surprise contender.

The war had transformed the ancient industrial world. Its shipyards and trade fleets had become indispensable during the anti-First Order campaigns, and many military leaders admired Corellia’s strategic position and manufacturing strength. Supporters envisioned a dynamic capital built around commerce, defense, and reconstruction—a government connected directly to the industries rebuilding the galaxy.

Critics feared Corellian ambition almost as much as they feared Coruscanti centralization.

The provisional senate convened at last on a neutral station orbiting Hosnian Prime’s ruined system, chosen deliberately as a memorial to the billions lost there. Delegations arrived beneath heavy security. Resistance veterans walked corridors alongside diplomats who had once spent years denouncing each other in senate chambers. Entire wings of the station buzzed with journalists, lobbyists, military aides, economists, and intelligence personnel.

Outside the station’s viewing galleries, the stars of the destroyed Hosnian system glimmered faintly against the void.

Inside, the future of the galaxy prepared to argue over where its heart would beat next.

The delegations from Coruscant, Chandrila, and Corellia arrived within hours of one another.

And the debates were about to begin.

# Part 2

The Hall of Concordance had been constructed only months earlier within the rotating frame of the Hosnian Memorial Station. It was circular by design. No throne stood at its center. No elevated dais overlooked the chamber. Every delegation occupied identical platforms arranged around the hall, each marked only by the crest of its world.

The symbolism was deliberate.

No world ruled the others anymore.

Yet everyone understood that by the end of the debates, one world would become the political center of the galaxy.

The proceedings opened in silence.

Representatives from hundreds of liberated systems stood together beneath a vast holographic projection of the Hosnian system as it had existed before its destruction. Five bright worlds rotated slowly in blue light above the chamber floor. No one spoke while the names of the dead systems were read aloud.

Only after the memorial concluded did Chancellor Provisional Tynnra Pamlo formally begin the debates.

“The New Republic rises again,” she declared. “Not from triumph, but from survival. Let this chamber choose wisely.”

The rules were brutally simple.

Each candidate world would present its case before the assembly. Delegates could challenge, question, and rebut one another freely. When the debates concluded, every recognized member world of the restored New Republic would cast exactly one vote.

One world. One vote.

Plurality would determine the capital.

No second rounds. No weighted influence for wealth, population, or military power.

The first delegation to speak was Chandrila.

Its representative, Senator Kel Varaine, was an elderly woman whose calm demeanor contrasted sharply with the tension in the chamber. Chandrila’s presentation emphasized stability, restraint, and democratic continuity. Varaine spoke at length about the failures that had doomed previous galactic governments—not merely corruption or militarism, but arrogance.

“Every age believes itself immune to collapse,” she said. “Every age is wrong.”

She argued that Chandrila’s smaller scale was its greatest strength. Coruscant, she warned, swallowed governments whole beneath layers of bureaucracy and political inertia. Corellia, meanwhile, risked allowing industrial and military interests to dominate reconstruction policy.

“Power should reside where it can still remember humility.”

Chandrila promised a carefully limited central administration, strong protections for sector autonomy, and constitutional reforms intended to prevent executive overreach. Varaine repeatedly invoked the memory of senators who had resisted authoritarianism during both Imperial and First Order rule.

Many delegations responded warmly, especially smaller Mid Rim and Outer Rim worlds that feared domination by the Core Worlds once again.

But criticism came swiftly.

Representatives from devastated frontier systems accused Chandrila’s philosophy of sounding dangerously similar to the old Republic’s complacency. Some openly questioned whether idealism had become a luxury the galaxy could no longer afford.

A delegate from Bracca summarized the concern bluntly.

“The last Republic debated procedure while planets burned.”

The chamber remained tense when Corellia’s delegation entered the floor.

Its representative, Trade Minister Jorad Senn, projected confidence bordering on defiance. Unlike Chandrila’s measured tones, Corellia’s argument was aggressive and practical. Senn spoke constantly of fleets, infrastructure, commerce, reconstruction, and deterrence.

“The galaxy did not survive because committees saved it,” he said. “It survived because worlds fought.”

Corellia promised rapid rebuilding through industrial coordination and military readiness. Shipyards would expand under federal contracts. Hyperlanes would be secured permanently. Regional defense forces would integrate into a stronger centralized navy capable of responding immediately to future threats.

Senn openly criticized what he called “romantic distrust of strength.”

“We do not honor the dead of Hosnian by building another fragile government waiting to be shattered.”

The speech electrified many war-torn systems desperate for security and economic recovery. Corellian representatives distributed detailed reconstruction proposals promising jobs, fleet modernization, and infrastructure investment across dozens of sectors.

But Corellia’s opponents found the presentation alarming.

Delegates questioned whether placing the capital within one of the galaxy’s strongest industrial and military centers risked creating a government too closely tied to corporate interests and naval power. Some remembered old Corellian separatist movements and accused the planet of seeking influence disproportionate to republican values.

One senator from Kashyyyk asked publicly whether Corellia envisioned “a republic protected by fleets, or a republic ruled by them.”

Then came Coruscant.

The chamber quieted before the delegation even spoke.

No world carried more symbolic weight.

Coruscant’s representative was unusually young compared to the others: Administrator Lira Venn, a reconstruction coordinator who had helped organize civilian governance during the uprising against the First Order occupation. She did not arrive with the polished grandeur many expected. The scars of recent war remained visible on her face and hands.

She began without theatrics.

“You all know what Coruscant was,” she said. “So I will speak instead about what it became.”

Her address differed sharply from both Chandrila and Corellia. She did not defend the old systems unquestioningly, nor did she promise sweeping transformation. Instead, she spoke about scale.

About reality.

“The galaxy is wounded,” she said. “It requires coordination on a scale no other world can presently sustain.”

She described the surviving administrative infrastructure buried beneath Coruscant’s devastation: communication nexuses, judicial archives, diplomatic districts, hyperspace coordination arrays, universities, hospitals, financial exchanges, and transportation systems capable of handling millions of officials and travelers simultaneously.

“Government is not symbolism alone. It is function.”

When critics raised the specter of past tyrannies born on Coruscant, Venn did not deny the danger.

“Yes,” she answered plainly. “Empire rose on Coruscant. So did the Republic that defeated it. So did the senate destroyed by the First Order. Coruscant reflects the galaxy itself. Its failures are our failures.”

Then she delivered the line repeated endlessly across the HoloNet afterward.

“If we abandon Coruscant forever because tyrants once ruled there, then tyrants have already claimed victory over our future.”

The debate lasted eleven days.

Delegates argued deep into artificial nights beneath the station’s rotating lights. Political alliances formed and collapsed by the hour. Economic projections circulated through every corridor. Journalists dissected every speech. Lobbyists flooded diplomatic quarters. Smaller systems became the center of intense persuasion campaigns as all three candidates searched desperately for additional votes.

Outside the station, crowds gathered across countless worlds to follow the proceedings.

For the first time in years, the galaxy argued about politics instead of war.

At last, the voting began.

Each member world entered the central chamber individually and cast its declaration before the assembly. The process was intentionally public. Every system would stand openly beside its choice.

Chandrila dominated many smaller and academically influential worlds. Corellia performed strongly among industrial systems and regions hardest hit by piracy and postwar instability.

Coruscant accumulated support steadily across nearly every sector.

Not always enthusiastically.

Not always proudly.

But steadily.

As the tally grew, a pattern became impossible to ignore. Worlds deeply distrustful of one another still kept arriving at the same conclusion: governing the galaxy from anywhere else would make rebuilding slower, weaker, and more fragmented.

The final votes were cast shortly after dawn station-time.

Silence consumed the chamber as the totals appeared above the assembly floor in blue-white light.

Coruscant: 184 votes.

Corellia: 121 votes.

Chandrila: 96 votes.

No world had achieved a majority.

But Coruscant had won the plurality.

For several seconds, the chamber simply stared upward at the numbers.

Then the noise erupted.

Some delegations applauded immediately. Others remained seated in grim resignation. Several Corellian representatives argued furiously among themselves while Chandrilan senators exchanged exhausted looks of disappointment.

Administrator Lira Venn did not celebrate.

She stood motionless beneath the holographic results while the chamber thundered around her.

Everyone understood what the vote truly meant.

The galaxy had not chosen Coruscant because it trusted power.

It had chosen Coruscant because after years of collapse, occupation, civil war, and terror, stability outweighed fear.

The motion was formalized before the session adjourned.

Coruscant would once again serve as the capital of the New Republic.

Outside the station windows, the shattered remnants of Hosnian Prime drifted silently through space while the government that had failed to save it began rebuilding itself anew.

# Part 3

By the fifth year after Exegol, the galaxy had changed in ways few would have dared predict during the war.

Trade flowed freely across the major hyperlanes again. Reconstruction fleets no longer traveled under constant military escort. Piracy still existed, as it always had, but organized war on a galactic scale had vanished into memory. Children on worlds liberated from the First Order were now old enough to barely remember occupation. Senate sessions on Coruscant increasingly focused not on emergency decrees or reconstruction budgets, but on education, commerce, exploration, and culture.

Peace had not merely returned.

It had settled in.

The New Republic nevertheless refused to repeat the mistakes that had nearly destroyed it before. One principle guided defense policy above all others: deterrence through readiness. The Senate funded regional defense fleets, anti-piracy patrols, and modernization programs designed to ensure that no future authoritarian movement could exploit complacency again.

But as years passed, military preparedness slowly became something else as well.

A source of pride.

The galaxy had always admired starships. Pilots became legends. Engineers became celebrities. Entire planetary identities revolved around shipbuilding traditions stretching back thousands of years. During the war, shipyards had symbolized survival. In peace, they symbolized possibility.

It began as a proposal from a coalition of senators representing industrial and shipbuilding worlds. Officially, the event would demonstrate the New Republic’s commitment to maintaining technological superiority and defensive capability. Unofficially, everyone understood the truth immediately.

The galaxy wanted a spectacle.

The Senate approved the initiative overwhelmingly.

The official title sounded appropriately grandiose:

**The Ultimate Ship Contest.**

Almost instantly, the HoloNet shortened it to USC.

The rules were simple enough for every citizen in the galaxy to understand. Participating shipyards would each design and construct a single vessel intended to embody the pinnacle of modern starship engineering. The ships would compete across five evaluation categories:

* Speed
* Agility & Maneuverability
* Resilience
* Weapon Strength
* Fuel & Power Efficiency

For each category, first place would receive three points, second place two points, and third place one point. Total points across all five categories would determine the overall champion.

Originally, dozens of manufacturers expressed interest. Smaller firms quickly realized they lacked the resources to compete at the required scale. Corporate mergers and sponsorship negotiations collapsed under scrutiny. Eventually, three entrants emerged as the uncontested giants of the contest.

Kuat Drive Yards.

Corellian Shipyards.

Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards.

The announcement ignited the galaxy.

Each company represented not merely a manufacturer, but an entire philosophy of starship design.

Kuat Drive Yards carried the legacy of monumental engineering and military supremacy. Their vessels historically emphasized overwhelming firepower, heavy armor, and system integration on scales few competitors could rival. Critics called them arrogant. Admirers called them unmatched.

Corellian Shipyards specialized in versatility, speed, and practicality. Their ships were famous for reliability, modularity, and aggressive maneuver performance. Corellian engineers often mocked Kuat designs as “flying fortresses built by bureaucrats.”

Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards approached engineering almost artistically. Their ships blended elegance with sophisticated systems architecture and unusually efficient internal harmonization. Mon Cala vessels frequently appeared almost organic compared to the harsher aesthetics of Core and Rim competitors alike.

For nearly two years, the galaxy became obsessed.

Construction secrecy reached absurd levels. Industrial espionage investigations multiplied across the Core Worlds. Rumors spread constantly through the HoloNet. Anonymous “leaks” appeared weekly claiming impossible innovations: experimental shield lattices, exotic reactor systems, next-generation inertial compensators.

Every major news outlet maintained dedicated USC coverage.

Betting syndicates flourished despite repeated Senate disapproval.

At last, the unveiling ceremony arrived.

The contest venue had been constructed in orbit above Coruscant itself: an enormous artificial evaluation platform positioned safely beyond major civilian traffic lanes. Millions gathered aboard observation stations and civilian cruisers while trillions watched remotely across the galaxy.

The atmosphere resembled a festival more than a military demonstration.

Then the contestants arrived.

Kuat Drive Yards unveiled the **Aegis Dominus**.

The vessel was immense for a competition craft, nearly approaching light destroyer scale while remaining technically classified as a heavy cruiser prototype. Angular and imposing, its dark metallic hull carried layered armor plating integrated directly into shield projection architecture. Massive ventral cannons dominated its forward profile.

The ship looked less like a starfighter evolved upward and more like a fortress taught how to fly.

Corellian Shipyards answered with the **CSR Tempest**.

Sleek, predatory, and aggressively compact, the Tempest emphasized velocity above all else. Its hull geometry minimized drag through atmospheric and vacuum transitions alike. Multiple overclocked sublight engine arrays stretched along swept stabilization wings, giving the ship a profile many commentators compared to a hunting animal moments before striking.

Corellian pilots immediately nicknamed it “the knife.”

Finally came the Mon Calamari vessel.

The **Starweaver** emerged from hyperspace to stunned silence.

Unlike the brutal lines of the Aegis Dominus or the sharpened aggression of the Tempest, the Starweaver curved gracefully through space with luminous hull contours and flowing structural transitions that appeared almost impossible for a warship. Yet beneath its elegance hid astonishing engineering density: shield harmonics, reactor efficiency systems, and integrated maneuver controls unlike anything publicly demonstrated before.

Many viewers described it as the first warship ever built to appear peaceful.

The contests began three days later.

The Speed Trials took place first.

Pilots raced through a massive orbital course incorporating acceleration gates, hyperspace calibration checkpoints, and realspace transition segments. The Tempest dominated immediately. Corellian engineers had built the vessel almost entirely around thrust optimization and inertial compensation. It accelerated so violently during the final sprint that several sensor operators initially believed telemetry equipment had malfunctioned.

First Place: Corellian Shipyards.
Second Place: Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards.
Third Place: Kuat Drive Yards.

The Agility & Maneuverability Trials followed.

Obstacle fields composed of drones, debris simulations, and rapid-fire navigation gates tested precision handling under combat conditions. Again, the Tempest excelled, darting through impossible trajectories with terrifying responsiveness. Yet the true surprise came from the Starweaver, whose fluid control systems allowed movements so smooth they seemed almost predictive.

The Aegis Dominus performed admirably for its size but could not match its competitors’ responsiveness.

First Place: Corellian Shipyards.
Second Place: Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards.
Third Place: Kuat Drive Yards.

By this point, Corellian celebrations had already begun across entire sectors.

Then came the Resilience Trials.

The ships endured shield stress testing, simulated combat bombardment, ion disruption fields, and systems degradation scenarios designed by Republic Defense Command engineers.

Here, Kuat struck back brutally.

The Aegis Dominus absorbed punishment on a level bordering on absurdity. Layer after layer of armor and shield reinforcement held under bombardments that visibly destabilized the other competitors. At one point, commentators believed the Mon Cala vessel might withdraw entirely after sustaining cascading shield fluctuations.

It recovered.

Barely.

First Place: Kuat Drive Yards.
Second Place: Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards.
Third Place: Corellian Shipyards.

The Weapon Strength Demonstration became the contest’s most watched event.

Targets ranged from asteroid fortifications to shielded drone flotillas and moving combat simulations. The Aegis Dominus unleashed devastation unlike anything previously displayed publicly outside wartime operations. Entire target formations disappeared beneath synchronized heavy cannon volleys.

The Tempest relied on precision strike capability and performed impressively against mobile targets. The Starweaver demonstrated astonishing targeting synchronization but lacked the raw destructive output of Kuat’s weapons architecture.

First Place: Kuat Drive Yards.
Second Place: Corellian Shipyards.
Third Place: Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards.

By the final category, the standings were dangerously close.

Corellian Shipyards led narrowly.

Everything depended on Fuel & Power Efficiency.

For this evaluation, engineers monitored reactor sustainability, energy loss ratios, shield efficiency, long-duration operational capacity, and fuel consumption under sustained stress conditions.

The result shocked much of the galaxy.

The Starweaver was not merely efficient.

It was revolutionary.

Mon Calamari engineers had developed an integrated reactor-distribution architecture so advanced that the ship maintained operational performance levels while consuming dramatically less fuel and generating far lower thermal waste than either competitor. During extended simulations, the Starweaver sustained combat effectiveness nearly twice as long as the Tempest.

Even Kuat engineers reportedly applauded privately after reviewing the data.

First Place: Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards.
Second Place: Corellian Shipyards.
Third Place: Kuat Drive Yards.

The final point totals were calculated before a live audience on Coruscant.

Kuat Drive Yards:
1 + 1 + 3 + 3 + 1 = **9 points**

Corellian Shipyards:
3 + 3 + 1 + 2 + 2 = **11 points**

Mon Calamari Orbital Shipyards:
2 + 2 + 2 + 1 + 3 = **10 points**

The margin between first and second place was only a single point.

When Chancellor Pamlo announced the results before the assembled crowds, the orbital platforms erupted into noise loud enough to overwhelm several transmission channels temporarily.

Corellian Shipyards had won the Ultimate Ship Contest.

The Tempest became an instant legend.

Corellian celebrations spread across shipyards, trade ports, and fleet academies throughout the galaxy. Pilots painted Tempest insignias onto starfighters. Recruitment into Republic flight programs surged. HoloNet analysts debated the results for months afterward, with endless arguments over whether versatility had truly triumphed over raw power or engineering elegance.

Yet perhaps the most important result was subtler.

For the first time in generations, the galaxy had gathered around military technology not because it feared annihilation—

—but because it believed peace was strong enough to enjoy competition again.

# Part 4

By the time the next chancellor election approached, the New Republic had entered an unfamiliar political era.

Normalcy.

The emergency coalitions that had guided the galaxy through reconstruction were beginning to fracture into actual political movements. Senators argued over taxation rates instead of military survival. Trade disputes dominated committee hearings. Reconstruction bonds matured. Frontier sectors demanded expanded infrastructure investment while Core Worlds pushed for budget discipline. The Republic was no longer fighting to exist.

It was learning how to live again.

That made the election unusually open.

No singular crisis dominated public opinion. No war hero stood as the obvious consensus candidate. Several experienced senators announced campaigns built around familiar promises: economic modernization, defense reform, hyperspace development, anti-corruption oversight.

Most citizens barely paid attention.

Then Lando Calrissian announced his candidacy.

At first, the galaxy assumed it was a joke.

The announcement occurred aboard a luxury diplomatic cruiser orbiting Bespin, where reporters expected a charity gala appearance related to veteran relief programs. Instead, Lando emerged before the press wearing an immaculate cape and casually informed the assembled journalists that he intended to run for Chancellor of the New Republic.

The room reportedly remained silent for nearly ten full seconds.

Then came the inevitable question.

“Administrator Calrissian,” one reporter asked carefully, “what qualifies you to govern the galaxy?”

Lando smiled immediately.

“Look, I’ve run a city before. I’ve run an enterprise before. I’ve run a massive fleet before. Running a republic ain’t gonna be much different.”

The quote spread across the HoloNet within minutes.

Political commentators initially dismissed the campaign as celebrity theatrics. Career senators privately laughed at the idea that a former smuggler, gambler, businessman, military general, and floating-city administrator could seriously compete for the highest office in the galaxy.

Then the polling numbers began arriving.

And the laughter stopped.

Lando occupied a uniquely powerful place in galactic memory. To older generations, he was one of the heroes who helped defeat the Empire. To younger citizens, he was the charismatic commander who had rallied civilian fleets at Exegol when the galaxy needed courage most. Unlike many political figures, he carried relatively little association with the failures of either the old Republic or the reconstruction bureaucracy.

More importantly, people liked him.

Not merely respected him.

Liked him.

He spoke plainly. He joked during interviews. He openly admitted when he did not know something. He projected confidence without sounding ideological. In a political environment exhausted by years of procedural language and committee rhetoric, Lando felt startlingly alive.

His critics attacked constantly.

Opponents questioned his administrative qualifications, his business history, his years in smuggling circles, and his tendency toward improvisation. Several senators warned publicly that charisma was not governance. Others argued that galactic leadership required discipline and institutional rigor, not charm and storytelling.

Lando’s campaign turned those criticisms into weapons.

“You know what the problem with galactic government is?” he asked during one debate on Coruscant. “Too many people in this city think sounding important is the same thing as getting things done.”

The line became another famous campaign quote.

He traveled relentlessly during the election season, often ignoring standard political schedules entirely. Instead of formal senate receptions, he appeared in shipyards, marketplaces, mining colonies, and civilian docking ports. He held campaign events in casinos one night and veterans’ hospitals the next. On Corellia, he spent nearly three hours talking with mechanics inside an active repair hangar while holocameras rolled.

Citizens found him strangely believable.

Not because he seemed traditionally presidential.

Because he didn’t.

By election day, the establishment candidates had split much of the traditional political vote among themselves while Lando built broad coalitions across demographics few analysts had predicted: veterans, traders, pilots, Outer Rim settlers, younger voters, and even many centrists tired of rigid factional politics.

When the final counts arrived, the result stunned Coruscant.

Lando Calrissian had been elected Chancellor of the New Republic.

The celebrations on Bespin lasted nearly six straight days.

The Senate, meanwhile, braced nervously for uncertainty.

Those fears intensified almost immediately after he took office.

Lando approached governance with an attitude many aides initially mistook for unseriousness. Within weeks, however, officials realized he possessed a surprisingly coherent philosophy.

He genuinely intended to run the New Republic like an enormously scaled-up Cloud City.

The comparisons began subtly.

First came the economic reforms.

Lando viewed commerce not as a secondary concern beneath politics, but as the bloodstream of stability itself. He aggressively streamlined trade regulations, reduced bureaucratic redundancies, and expanded incentives for intersector commercial partnerships. Customs processing times across major hyperlanes dropped dramatically within a year after his administration reorganized several overlapping Republic agencies into a unified transit authority.

“People don’t start wars when they’re making money together,” he remarked during one cabinet meeting.

Then came infrastructure modernization.

Cloud City had survived because systems worked efficiently and visibly. Lando despised hidden dysfunction buried beneath administrative layers. He launched sweeping public maintenance initiatives across Republic space, investing heavily in transit systems, orbital docking facilities, communications relays, and planetary utility grids.

His critics mocked the projects as “government beautification.”

Citizens loved them.

Spaceports became safer and cleaner. Trade moved faster. Bureaucratic wait times fell noticeably. Republic service offices actually responded to citizens within reasonable timeframes for the first time in decades.

Then Lando made his most controversial move.

Hospitality policy.

He believed governments projected strength not merely through fleets and laws, but through atmosphere. Coruscant under previous administrations often felt austere, paranoid, and politically isolated from ordinary citizens. Lando hated that immediately.

He ordered extensive renovations to senate districts, diplomatic quarters, and public plazas. Entertainment venues reopened near government sectors. Cultural festivals received major federal sponsorships. Tourism campaigns encouraged citizens to visit Coruscant not as pilgrims approaching power, but as participants in galactic civilization.

One senator complained privately that the capital was beginning to resemble “a luxury resort with legislative committees attached.”

Lando reportedly considered that a compliment.

His administration also transformed diplomatic culture.

Foreign delegates arriving on Coruscant suddenly encountered personalized receptions, curated accommodations, trade showcases, artistic exhibitions, and elaborate social events designed to encourage relationship-building outside formal negotiation chambers.

Many old bureaucrats found it frivolous.

Diplomatic success rates improved sharply.

Lando understood something many career politicians did not: people negotiated differently after sharing meals, music, gambling tables, and stories together.

Not everything worked.

His casual management style frequently infuriated senior administrators accustomed to rigid hierarchy. Lando delegated aggressively and trusted personal relationships perhaps more than institutional procedures. Several scandals erupted involving ambitious business interests attempting to exploit the administration’s commercial openness.

And there was one constant criticism that never disappeared:

Some feared the Republic was becoming too comfortable.

Too luxurious.

Too convinced peace would last forever.

Lando understood the concern better than most realized.

He never weakened the Republic Defense Forces. Military readiness remained strong throughout his administration. Patrol fleets expanded modestly. Anti-piracy operations intensified. Veterans’ funding increased substantially. Behind the glamour and commerce, deterrence remained intact.

Cloud City, after all, had always required security beneath its elegance.

Still, the cultural shift was undeniable.

For the first time in generations, Coruscant stopped feeling primarily like the seat of survival.

Under Chancellor Lando Calrissian, it began feeling like the capital of a civilization that actually expected to have a future.
