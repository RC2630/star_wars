# Part 1

The night was still.

On Ossus, the wind moved softly through the stone corridors of the Jedi temple, carrying with it the quiet breath of sleeping students. Lanterns burned low. The Force, as Luke Skywalker had come to understand it, rested in a fragile kind of balance—alive, but calm.

Then it tore open.

Luke staggered where he stood, one hand braced against a cold pillar as the vision seized him. It did not come gently. It struck like a blade—sharp, immediate, undeniable.

Fire. Screams. The temple in ruin.

Students cut down where they stood. Some by soldiers in black, faceless and merciless. Others—Luke’s breath caught—by one of their own.

Ben.

Not the boy Luke had trained since childhood, not the uncertain young man who struggled and doubted and tried. This was something else. Eyes hardened, voice cold, power unleashed without restraint. The Force bent violently around him, as if recoiling and obeying at once.

Luke saw himself too—too late. Reaching, failing, watching everything he had built burn to ash.

And beneath it all, like a current running through deep water, something older. Watching. Waiting. Feeding the darkness.

The vision snapped.

Luke gasped, pulling in air as though he had been drowning. His heart hammered against his ribs. The stone floor beneath his boots felt suddenly unsteady.

“No…” he whispered, though there was no one to hear.

He had seen darkness before. In himself. In his father. In the galaxy at large. But this—this was intimate. Immediate. Preventable.

Ben.

Luke closed his eyes, reaching out through the Force. He found him easily. A familiar presence, restless even in sleep. Turbulent. Knotted with conflict, but not yet lost.

Not yet.

That was the word that lingered.

Not yet.

Luke stood there for a long time, unmoving, the weight of what he had seen pressing down on him. The future was not fixed. He knew that. He had always known that. Visions were warnings, not certainties.

But some warnings came with a terrible clarity.

This one felt… anchored. Like a storm already gathering strength beyond the horizon.

His hand trembled as it drifted toward his lightsaber at his belt.

“No,” he said again, firmer this time. A denial. A refusal.

He turned away.

And then he stopped.

The images surged back—not as a vision, but as memory. The fire. The bodies. The unmistakable certainty that if nothing changed, it would come to pass.

Luke’s jaw tightened. He turned back toward the corridor that led to the student quarters.

Each step felt heavier than the last.

—

Ben Solo slept.

The room was simple—stone walls, a narrow bed, a small table scattered with datapads and half-finished notes. A training remote lay deactivated in the corner. The quiet signs of a life still in progress.

He looked younger in sleep. Whatever turmoil churned within him softened at the edges. There was something almost peaceful about it.

Luke stood in the doorway for several seconds before entering.

The Force whispered uneasily around him.

He approached slowly, each movement deliberate, as though any sudden shift might shatter something fragile and unseen. When he reached the bedside, he looked down at Ben’s face.

His sister’s son.

Han’s son.

His student.

Luke’s chest tightened. For a moment, the vision felt distant. Uncertain. Something that could still be avoided without… this.

He reached out through the Force again, gently this time, touching Ben’s presence not as a watcher, but as a teacher.

There it was—the darkness he had sensed before. Coiled. Fed by fear, by anger, by something Luke had not fully understood. It wasn’t all-consuming. Not yet. But it was real.

And beneath it, something else. That same distant presence from the vision. Faint. Influencing. Waiting.

Luke’s expression hardened.

If he waited… if he hesitated…

He saw the fire again.

The screaming.

The dead.

His thumb brushed the activation switch of his lightsaber.

For a single, suspended instant, the galaxy seemed to hold its breath.

Then a blade of green light filled the room.

Ben’s eyes snapped open.

There was confusion there first. Recognition a heartbeat later.

“Uncle—?”

The word never finished.

Luke moved.

It was clean. Instinctive. A motion honed over years of battle and discipline. There was no hesitation left in it, no uncertainty.

The light vanished as quickly as it had appeared.

Silence rushed back in.

Luke stood frozen, the lightsaber deactivated in his hand, staring down at what he had done.

The Force recoiled.

Not violently, not explosively—but unmistakably. Like a wound opening in something vast and unseen.

“No…” Luke breathed, but this time the word carried no denial. Only horror.

Ben Solo lay still.

The darkness Luke had sensed was gone.

So was everything else.

—

The first to arrive were the other students.

A tremor had passed through the temple—not physical, but something deeper. Those attuned to the Force felt it as a sudden absence, a note cut short in the middle of a song.

Doors opened. Footsteps echoed.

A few of the older students reached Ben’s quarters and stopped cold at the threshold.

“Master Luke?”

There was no answer.

Luke had not moved.

The room felt wrong. Heavy. The air thick with something none of them could name, but all of them feared.

One of them stepped forward, then stopped again as the truth settled into place.

“What… what happened?”

Luke’s grip on the lightsaber tightened, then loosened. Slowly, deliberately, he lowered it to his side.

“I saw what he would become,” he said.

His voice sounded distant, as though it belonged to someone else.

“I saw what he would do.”

The students exchanged uncertain glances. Fear crept into their expressions—not just at the words, but at the calm with which they were spoken.

“He hadn’t done anything,” one of them said, quieter than the rest.

Luke didn’t respond.

Because there was no answer he could give that would make that untrue.

—

By morning, Ossus was no longer quiet.

Word spread quickly, though no one spoke it loudly. Whispers carried through corridors. Lessons were canceled. Training grounds sat empty.

Some students packed their belongings before anyone could stop them.

Others demanded answers Luke could not provide.

A few stayed, but even they kept their distance, as though proximity alone might draw them into something they did not understand.

Luke convened them in the central hall.

He stood before them, not as the legend they had followed, but as something diminished. The weight of what he had done hung visibly on him now.

“I failed,” he said simply.

No one interrupted.

“I believed I could guide him. That I could help him overcome what he struggled with.” His gaze drifted, unfocused. “I was wrong.”

“Master—” one student began, but Luke raised a hand.

“I acted out of fear,” he continued. “I told myself it was to prevent suffering. To protect all of you. But the truth is… I was afraid of what I saw. And I chose certainty over compassion.”

The words landed heavily.

“Leave,” Luke said.

That drew a reaction.

“What?”

“Return to your families. To the Republic. To wherever you feel you must go.” His voice did not waver, but it had lost all warmth. “This temple will not continue as it is.”

“And you?” someone asked.

Luke hesitated.

“I don’t know,” he admitted.

—

The message reached Leia first.

She was in her office on Chandrila when the transmission came through—priority, encoded, unmistakably from Luke.

She answered it without hesitation.

“Luke, what is it—”

She stopped.

She saw his face.

Something inside her seemed to brace before he even spoke.

“Leia,” Luke said, and there was a strain in his voice she had never heard before. “There’s no easy way to say this.”

Her grip tightened on the edge of her desk.

“Where is Ben?”

Luke closed his eyes for a fraction of a second.

“I’m sorry.”

The silence that followed was absolute.

“No,” Leia said, too quickly. “No, you don’t get to start with that. Where is my son?”

Luke forced himself to meet her gaze.

“He’s gone.”

The words seemed to hang in the air, incomprehensible.

Leia stared at him, searching for something—clarification, denial, anything that would reshape what she had just heard into something else.

“How?” she asked, her voice dangerously quiet.

Luke didn’t answer immediately.

That was all the answer she needed.

The realization hit her like a physical blow.

“You,” she said.

It wasn’t a question.

Luke swallowed. “I saw—”

“I don’t care what you saw!” The force of her voice cut through the transmission, sharp and raw. “You don’t get to tell me about visions or possibilities. You don’t get to decide who he might become and act like it’s already happened!”

Her composure shattered, grief breaking through the anger.

“He was my son.”

Luke had no defense. No justification that didn’t sound hollow the moment it left his mouth.

“I know.”

“Do you?” Leia shot back. “Do you know what you’ve done?”

The question lingered, unanswered, because there was no answer large enough to contain it.

—

Han didn’t take the news over a transmission.

Leia went to him.

He saw it on her face before she spoke. Whatever she said after that barely registered. The only word that mattered was Ben.

And then the next one.

Gone.

Han stood there for a long time, unmoving, as if refusing to let the meaning settle.

“How?” he asked at last.

Leia hesitated.

That was enough.

Han’s expression hardened in a way Leia hadn’t seen in years.

“No,” he said under his breath. “No. Not him.”

He turned away, pacing once, then again, the energy building with nowhere to go.

“I trusted him,” Han said. “We both did.”

Leia didn’t respond. She couldn’t.

Han stopped.

“I’m going to Ossus.”

“Han—”

“I’m going.”

There was nothing in his voice that left room for argument.

—

By the time the New Republic received confirmation, the story had already begun to fracture.

A Jedi Master killing his own student.

The son of Leia Organa and Han Solo.

A vision. A preemptive strike. A moment of fear.

The details shifted depending on who told them, but the core remained the same—and it unsettled everything.

The Jedi had been returning, slowly, carefully, under Luke’s guidance. Trust had been tentative, but it had been growing.

Now it cracked.

In the Senate, voices rose—not in unity, but in suspicion and alarm.

“If this is what the Jedi have become—”

“One man’s actions do not define an order—”

“He killed a child—”

“He was not a child, he was a trained Force user—”

“On whose authority?”

The arguments spiraled, pulling in fear that had never fully disappeared.

On Ossus, the temple emptied.

Not all at once, but steadily. Quiet departures. Avoided conversations. Unanswered questions.

Luke remained.

He walked the corridors alone now. The same halls that had once carried laughter, training, purpose.

Now they held only echoes.

In Ben’s quarters, nothing had been moved.

Luke stood in the doorway again, as he had that night, but this time he did not enter.

The Force felt different here.

Not darker. Not lighter.

Just… diminished.

He had acted to prevent a future of fire and loss.

And in doing so, he had brought a different kind of ruin into the present—one that no vision had shown him how to escape.

# Part 2

The corridors of the Ossus temple no longer echoed.

By the time the last of the students departed, there was no ceremony, no final gathering—only absence. Luke did not try to stop them. He did not ask them to stay.

He sealed nothing behind him when he left.

The temple remained as it was: doors open, rooms untouched, a place suspended between purpose and abandonment.

His destination was not exile.

It was judgment.

—

Hosnian Prime gleamed with order and intent, the heart of the New Republic alive with motion—senators in constant debate, officials moving between towers of glass and light, the machinery of governance turning without pause.

Luke Skywalker arrived without escort.

He did not wear robes of ceremony or the symbols of a Jedi Master. Only simple garments, travel-worn, unremarkable. At his side, his lightsaber remained clipped to his belt, but he made no move to hide it.

Security forces met him at the central judiciary complex.

“I’m here to surrender,” Luke said before they could speak.

They did not resist him.

There was nothing to resist.

—

The trial drew attention from across the Republic.

Not just because of what had happened, but because of who stood at its center.

Luke Skywalker—the name carried history with it. The fall of the Empire, the restoration of hope, the return of the Jedi. For many, he was not just a man, but a symbol.

Now that symbol stood accused.

The central chamber of the High Court was filled beyond capacity. Senators observed from elevated galleries. Members of the public crowded the outer tiers. Holocams recorded every moment.

Leia Organa sat near the front.

She held herself with the same composure she had brought to countless political battles, but it was thinner now, stretched tight over something far more fragile. Grief had not left her. It had simply settled deeper.

Han Solo sat beside her, restless even in stillness. His gaze remained fixed on Luke, unblinking, as if looking away might make the moment less real.

Behind them, several of Luke’s former students sat together, their presence uneasy. Some watched with conflicted loyalty. Others with open doubt.

Luke stood alone at the center of the chamber.

No restraints. No guards at his side.

The charges had been stated clearly: the killing of Ben Solo, unprovoked, without immediate threat, carried out while the victim slept.

When asked how he pleaded, Luke did not hesitate.

“I did it,” he said.

The proceedings moved quickly after that. There were no witnesses to the act itself beyond Luke. No conflicting accounts. Only the facts, and the question of what they meant.

When it was his turn to testify, the chamber fell into a silence so complete it seemed to press inward.

Luke did not look at the galleries. He did not look at Han.

He looked at Leia.

Then he began.

“I experienced a vision through the Force,” he said. “Not a dream. Not an impression. A vision.”

He spoke carefully, precisely, as though each word had to be placed exactly where it belonged.

“I saw the temple on Ossus destroyed. I saw my students dead. Some killed by soldiers. Some…” He paused, but only briefly. “By Ben Solo.”

A murmur moved through the chamber, quickly silenced.

“I saw him as something else. Not the person he was, but what he would become. Powerful. Controlled by anger, by fear. Acting in service of something beyond himself.”

He lifted his gaze slightly, not to the crowd, but somewhere distant.

“There was another presence in the vision. I could not see it clearly. But I felt it. Older than Ben. Stronger. Guiding him.”

The presiding judge leaned forward slightly. “And you believed this vision to be certain?”

“No,” Luke said.

The answer rippled outward.

“I believed it to be possible,” he continued. “But I also believed it to be likely. The clarity of it… the detail… I had experienced visions before. This was different.”

“Different how?”

“It didn’t feel like a warning,” Luke said. “It felt like a consequence already set in motion.”

“And so you acted.”

Luke’s hands remained at his sides, still.

“Yes.”

“Explain your reasoning.”

The question hung in the air, heavy with implication.

Luke did not rush to answer it.

“I told myself I was preventing something catastrophic,” he said at last. “That I was protecting my students. The Republic. The future.” His voice tightened, but did not break. “But the truth is simpler than that.”

He met the judge’s gaze directly.

“I was afraid.”

No one spoke.

“I saw what he might become, and for a moment… I believed the only way to stop it was to end it before it began.” His jaw tightened. “I didn’t seek another solution. I didn’t wake him. I didn’t try to reach him.”

His voice dropped, quieter now.

“I made a decision alone. And I carried it out.”

The judge studied him. “Do you believe you were justified?”

Luke held that question longer than any before it.

“No,” he said.

The word landed with more weight than any defense could have.

—

Deliberations did not take long, but they were not simple.

Within the closed chambers of the court, the arguments clashed sharply.

“He admits the act. He admits there was no immediate threat.”

“He also prevented a potential mass casualty event of unknown scale.”

“On the basis of a vision.”

“A vision he has every reason to understand better than anyone in this room.”

“That does not place him above the law.”

“No—but it does place his judgment in a context we cannot ignore.”

“And what precedent does that set?”

“That we weigh intent alongside outcome.”

“And the outcome here is the death of a young man who had committed no crime.”

“And the possible prevention of something far worse.”

The debate circled, returning again and again to the same impasse.

In the end, it was not certainty that guided the decision—but balance.

—

The chamber filled again for the verdict.

Leia sat unmoving. Han’s arms were crossed tightly, his expression unreadable.

Luke stood where he had stood before.

The presiding judge spoke clearly, each word measured.

“Luke Skywalker, you have confessed to the killing of Ben Solo. The court finds that this act was carried out without immediate provocation and in violation of the laws of the New Republic.”

A pause.

“However, the court also recognizes your role in the fall of the Empire, your service to the Republic, and the unique circumstances under which this act was committed—including the Force vision you have described.”

Another pause, longer this time.

“In light of these factors, the court declines to impose the maximum sentence.”

A faint stir moved through the chamber.

“Instead, you are hereby remanded into the direct service of the New Republic. Your actions will no longer be your own. You will operate under Republic command and oversight.”

Luke did not react.

“You will be tasked with investigating the presence you described in your vision—the source of the influence you believe acted upon Ben Solo. You will identify it, locate it, and report your findings.”

The judge’s gaze sharpened.

“This is not a request. It is your sentence.”

Silence followed.

Then, from the Senate gallery, a single voice spoke.

Leia stood.

The court allowed it.

“If there is something out there,” she said, her voice steady despite the strain beneath it, “something that could turn my son… that could do the same to others… then it needs to be found.”

Her eyes moved to Luke.

“And stopped.”

There was no forgiveness in her expression.

But there was purpose.

—

Afterward, there were no crowds waiting for Luke. No spectacle.

Only a controlled exit, quiet and contained.

Leia did not approach him immediately.

Han did.

He closed the distance in a few long strides, stopping just short of striking range—not that it mattered.

For a moment, neither of them spoke.

Then Han said, “You don’t get to disappear.”

Luke met his gaze.

“I won’t.”

Han’s jaw tightened. “Good. Because running off into nowhere—that would be easy.”

Luke didn’t argue.

Han leaned in slightly, his voice lower now.

“You’re going to find whatever you think you saw,” he said. “And when you do…” He stopped, the rest of the sentence hanging unspoken.

Luke nodded once.

“Yes.”

Han held his gaze for another second, then stepped back.

It wasn’t forgiveness.

But it wasn’t nothing.

—

Within days, the orders were formalized.

Resources were allocated—limited, but precise. Access to Republic intelligence archives. Clearance to investigate regions long neglected or intentionally avoided. A small, rotating support detail, though Luke requested minimal accompaniment.

He was given a ship, not new, but reliable.

Functional.

Unremarkable.

Before departure, Leia met him once more.

This time, in private.

For a while, neither of them spoke.

“I meant what I said,” she told him at last. “If something was influencing him… I need to know.”

Luke nodded. “I felt it. I don’t know what it is yet, but it was there.”

Leia studied him.

“You’re not doing this to redeem yourself,” she said.

It wasn’t a question.

Luke shook his head.

“No.”

“Good,” Leia said quietly. “Because that’s not what this is.”

A pause.

“This is about making sure it doesn’t happen again.”

Luke met her gaze.

“It won’t,” he said.

It wasn’t confidence.

It was resolve.

—

When Luke finally left Hosnian Prime, there was no ceremony marking his departure.

The ship lifted into the sky, joined the flow of traffic, and disappeared into the vastness beyond.

Behind him, the Republic watched.

Ahead of him, something waited—something he had already felt once, in a moment that had changed everything.

This time, he would not turn away from it.

And he would not face it blindly.

# Part 3

Dagobah had not changed.

From orbit, it was still a world of endless green and shadow, its surface wrapped in mist and slow-moving cloud. No cities marked its land. No signals reached out from its depths. It remained what it had always been—forgotten, avoided, alive in a way most worlds were not.

Luke guided the ship down through the atmosphere, the hull shuddering lightly as it cut through thick layers of moisture. When the landing struts settled into the soft, uneven ground, the only sound that followed was the low hum of cooling engines—and the distant chorus of unseen creatures moving through the swamp.

He stepped out into heavy air.

It clung to him immediately, warm and damp, carrying the scent of water, soil, and decay. The ground shifted slightly under his boots. Vines hung low between twisted trees. Nothing here was still, even when it seemed quiet.

Luke stood there for a moment, taking it in.

He had come here once before, years ago, searching for answers. Back then, he had been uncertain, impatient, driven by questions he barely understood.

Now he carried answers he wished he didn’t have.

He moved deeper into the swamp without hesitation. The path, if it could be called that, came back to him—not as memory alone, but as instinct. The Force here was dense, layered, almost tangible. It pressed close from all sides, not guiding so much as surrounding.

When he reached the place where Yoda’s hut still stood, he stopped.

Time had worn it down, but not erased it. The structure leaned slightly now, its surfaces softened by moss and age. The doorway remained open.

Luke didn’t go inside.

Instead, he sat on a low, exposed root nearby, closed his eyes, and let the swamp settle around him.

For a while, there was nothing but breath.

In.

Out.

The weight he carried did not vanish. It did not even lessen. But it shifted, settling into something he could hold without being overwhelmed by it.

He reached out through the Force.

Not searching for a person. Not searching for a place.

Listening.

Dagobah answered in its own way—life, death, growth, decay, all woven tightly together. It was not light or dark, not in any simple sense. It was everything at once, unfiltered.

Luke let himself sink into it.

Time passed. Minutes, hours—he didn’t mark them.

At some point, he became aware that he was no longer alone.

“You return.”

The voice was small, familiar, carrying a cadence that time had not diminished.

Luke opened his eyes.

Yoda stood a short distance away, as he had once stood countless times before—small, robed, leaning lightly on his cane. But there was no weight to him now, no disturbance in the swamp where he stood. The air seemed to pass through him, even as his presence in the Force was unmistakable.

“Master Yoda,” Luke said quietly.

Yoda studied him, his expression unreadable, though not unkind.

“Changed, you are.”

Luke let out a breath that might have been a faint, humorless laugh. “I suppose I am.”

“Hmm.”

Yoda stepped closer, or perhaps simply appeared closer—the movement was difficult to follow.

“Troubled, your spirit is. Heavy.”

Luke didn’t deny it.

“I made a decision,” he said. “And I can’t take it back.”

Yoda’s ears lowered slightly. “The future… always in motion it is. Act, we must. But certain, we never are.”

“I know that,” Luke said, a touch sharper than he intended. Then, quieter, “I knew that.”

“Yet act, you did.”

Luke met his gaze. “I saw what he would become.”

Yoda’s eyes narrowed slightly. “Saw… or feared?”

The question landed cleanly.

Luke didn’t answer right away.

“Both,” he said at last.

Yoda nodded once, as if that confirmed something rather than clarified it.

“Dangerous, that is. When fear and sight become one.”

Luke looked down briefly, then back up. “There was something else in the vision. Not just Ben. Something… behind him. Guiding him.”

Yoda’s expression shifted, subtly.

“Felt this, I do,” he said. “A shadow… long it has grown.”

Before Luke could respond, another presence touched the edge of his awareness—familiar, steady, like a calm current beneath turbulent water.

“Luke.”

He turned.

Obi-Wan Kenobi stood beside Yoda now, his form as insubstantial and as real as the other’s. His expression was grave, but there was warmth in it too—the same quiet reassurance Luke remembered.

“You’ve come a long way,” Obi-Wan said.

“Not far enough,” Luke replied.

Obi-Wan gave a faint, knowing look. “That depends on where you’re trying to go.”

Luke didn’t rise. He remained seated, grounded, as if standing might break whatever fragile connection allowed this moment to exist.

“I failed him,” Luke said. “I saw darkness and I chose to destroy it instead of understand it.”

Obi-Wan’s gaze didn’t waver. “And now?”

“Now I need to know what I was really seeing.”

A third presence entered the space—not gently, not cautiously, but with a weight that was impossible to ignore.

Luke felt it before he saw it.

He turned slowly.

Anakin Skywalker stood there.

Not as Vader. Not as the broken figure encased in armor that haunted so many memories. But as he had been before—strong, resolute, and carrying a depth of experience that went far beyond what Luke had ever known of him in life.

For a moment, neither of them spoke.

Then Anakin said, “You think you’re the first one to act out of fear of the future?”

There was no accusation in his tone. Only recognition.

Luke held his gaze. “No.”

Anakin stepped closer.

“I saw things too,” he continued. “Visions. Loss. Pain. People I cared about dying.” His jaw tightened slightly. “I told myself I could stop it. That if I acted early enough, decisively enough, I could control what was coming.”

Luke didn’t look away.

“And did it work?” he asked.

Anakin’s expression answered before his words did.

“No,” he said.

The swamp seemed to grow quieter around them.

“What you felt in that vision,” Anakin went on, “that wasn’t just Ben. It wasn’t just his choices.”

Luke leaned forward slightly. “Then what was it?”

Before Anakin could answer, the air shifted again—lighter this time, but no less profound.

A fourth figure appeared.

Qui-Gon Jinn.

He stood slightly apart from the others, his presence calm, almost meditative, as though he had been there longer than the rest.

“You’re asking the right question,” Qui-Gon said.

Luke rose to his feet now, drawn by the weight of their combined presence.

“Then answer it,” he said.

Qui-Gon regarded him steadily.

“What you encountered was not a moment,” he said. “It was a current.”

Luke frowned slightly. “A current?”

“In the Force,” Qui-Gon continued. “There are disturbances that pass quickly. And there are those that endure. Build. Deepen over time.”

Yoda nodded. “Growing, this darkness has been.”

“Hidden,” Obi-Wan added.

“Patient,” Anakin said.

Luke absorbed that, his thoughts aligning with something he had felt but not fully understood.

“It was strong,” he said. “Stronger than anything I’ve felt since…” He stopped, but the meaning was clear.

“Yes,” Qui-Gon said. “Because it is not new.”

A silence followed that.

Then Luke asked the question that had been forming since the vision itself.

“Can it be found?”

The four of them regarded him.

Yoda spoke first. “Find it, you can. But seek with more than sight.”

“Trust what you feel,” Obi-Wan said.

“Not what you fear,” Anakin added.

Qui-Gon’s gaze sharpened slightly. “The Force will not give you a map. It will give you direction. The rest… you must choose.”

Luke closed his eyes again, just for a moment.

He reached out—not broadly this time, not searching everywhere at once, but narrowing his focus. Remembering the sensation from the vision. That presence beneath everything else.

Dark.

Not chaotic, not wild—but concentrated. Intentional.

Waiting.

When he followed that feeling, it did not spread outward across the familiar reaches of the Republic.

It pulled.

Distant.

Beyond known space.

Luke’s eyes opened.

“I feel it,” he said.

The others watched him, saying nothing.

“It’s far,” Luke continued. “Past the regions we charted. Past the territories we know.” His voice grew more certain with each word. “Out there… where the maps end.”

Yoda’s expression remained steady. “Into the unknown, then.”

Luke nodded.

“The unknown regions.”

The swamp stirred softly around them, as if acknowledging the decision.

When Luke looked again, the figures before him were already beginning to fade—not abruptly, but like reflections dissolving into water.

“Luke,” Obi-Wan said, his voice the last to linger, “whatever you find… remember who you are.”

Anakin’s gaze held his for a fraction longer. There was something like understanding there. And something like warning.

Then they were gone.

Luke stood alone again on Dagobah.

But not uncertain.

He returned to his ship without hesitation.

This time, when he left the swamp behind and the stars opened before him, he did not look back.

His course was already set.

Out beyond the known galaxy.

Toward the source of the darkness he had felt—and the truth behind what had taken Ben Solo long before the blade ever fell.

# Part 4

Stars stretched into long lines as Luke’s ship jumped to hyperspace, the familiar tunnel of blue-white light swallowing the galaxy behind him.

Inside the cockpit, everything was quiet.

No voices. No demands. No judgment.

Only the steady hum of the engines and the distant pull he felt through the Force—faint, but insistent. Like a current beneath still water, drawing him forward.

He sat with it.

He didn’t try to force clarity from it. The lessons on Dagobah still lingered—listen, don’t impose. Follow, don’t control.

Still, the questions pressed in.

What had reached Ben? What had been strong enough to twist a future so violently that Luke had seen it as inevitable?

And why had he felt it only then—so late, so suddenly?

The console chimed.

A secure channel request.

Luke’s attention shifted immediately. There were only a handful of people who had clearance to reach him out here.

He accepted it.

Leia’s image resolved in front of him, flickering slightly with the distortion of distance. Even through the projection, the strain in her expression was unmistakable—but there was something else there too. Focus.

“Leia,” Luke said.

“Luke,” she replied, without preamble. “I wasn’t sure I’d catch you before you went too far out.”

“I’m already on my way,” he said. “But you did.”

“I’ve been thinking,” she said. “About Ben. About everything you told the court. About what you felt in that vision.”

“There’s something I never told anyone,” Leia continued. “Not because I was hiding it. I just… didn’t understand it. And then it stopped.”

“When I was pregnant with Ben,” she said slowly, “there were times… not often, but enough that I remember them clearly… when I would feel something through the Force.”

Her voice lowered.

“A presence.”

Luke’s expression sharpened, but he didn’t interrupt.

“It wasn’t like anything I’d felt before,” she went on. “Not anger. Not fear, exactly. Just… cold. Distant. Like something very far away, but aware.”

A faint tension crept into her posture as she spoke, as if recalling it brought back more than just the memory.

“And every time it happened,” she said, “Ben would react.”

Luke’s breath slowed.

“How?” he asked.

“He would stir,” Leia said. “Suddenly. Strongly. As if something had reached him.” Her brow tightened slightly. “It wasn’t random. It only happened when that presence… touched the edges of my awareness.”

Luke felt something align, faint but unmistakable.

“And you’re certain it came from far away?” he asked.

“Yes,” Leia said without hesitation. “Not just distance in space. It felt… removed. Isolated. Like it wasn’t part of everything else I could sense.”

She held his gaze.

“And then it stopped.”

“When?” Luke asked.

“The day he was born,” she said. “After that… nothing. No trace of it.”

Silence settled between them for a moment, filled only by the low hum of Luke’s ship.

He considered her words carefully, turning them over against what he had felt—on Ossus, in the vision, on Dagobah.

A presence.

Cold. Distant. Watching.

Reaching.

Not recently.

For a long time.

“You think it’s the same thing,” Luke said at last.

Leia didn’t answer immediately.

“I don’t know,” she said. “But when you described what you felt… it sounded familiar in a way I couldn’t ignore.”

Luke nodded slowly.

“That means it didn’t start with Ben,” he said. “Whatever this is—it was already there. Already reaching out.”

“And it chose him,” Leia said quietly.

Luke didn’t challenge that.

He couldn’t.

“I don’t know what to do with that yet,” he admitted.

Leia’s expression softened slightly—not with comfort, but with understanding.

“I didn’t expect you to,” she said. “I just needed you to know.”

Luke inclined his head. “I’m glad you told me.”

Another pause.

“Luke,” Leia said, her voice tightening just a fraction, “if this thing has been out there that long…”

“I know,” he said.

“If it reached him before he was even born—”

“I know,” he repeated, more firmly this time.

Their eyes met.

There was no argument left in either of them. Only the shared weight of what that possibility meant.

“Be careful,” Leia said at last.

Luke gave a faint, almost imperceptible nod.

“I will.”

The transmission flickered.

Then it was gone.

—

The cockpit returned to silence.

But it wasn’t the same silence as before.

Luke leaned back slightly, staring out at the endless rush of hyperspace.

A presence, reaching across that kind of distance… not once, but repeatedly. Not random, but deliberate.

And then stopping.

Why?

Because it no longer needed to reach?

Or because something had changed?

His thoughts circled the question, but no clear answer formed.

Not yet.

He let it go.

For now.

Instead, he returned to the one thing that had guided him this far.

The feeling.

That same distant pull, threading through the Force, subtle but unwavering.

Now, with Leia’s words in his mind, it felt… sharper.

More defined.

Not just darkness.

Something old.

Something patient enough to reach across years—across space—to touch a life before it had even begun.

Luke’s gaze hardened slightly.

“Then I’m already on the right path,” he murmured to himself.

He adjusted the navigation, refining his trajectory—not by coordinates alone, but by instinct, by the quiet alignment of something deeper than calculation.

The stars stretched endlessly ahead of him.

Beyond the borders of the Republic.

Beyond the maps.

Into regions where even the Force felt less familiar.

Luke kept Leia’s words with him, not as answers, but as a piece of something larger—something that was finally beginning to take shape.

And he followed that shape into the dark.

# Part 5

The stars began to thin.

It was subtle at first—routes less traveled, then not traveled at all. Familiar patterns gave way to long stretches of uncharted space, where navigation relied more on instinct than data. Luke adjusted his course again and again, not by coordinates alone, but by that persistent pull in the Force.

It was stronger now.

No longer distant in the abstract, but near enough to feel like pressure—like standing at the edge of something vast and unseen.

Then it snapped.

A violent jolt tore through the ship. Warning lights flared red across the console as the hull shuddered under impact.

Luke’s hands moved instantly, stabilizing the controls as another blast streaked past the viewport.

He hadn’t sensed it.

Not until it struck.

A second craft dropped out of the void ahead—sleek, unfamiliar, its design angular and severe, moving with deliberate precision. It didn’t hail. It didn’t hesitate.

It fired again.

Luke veered hard to port, the blast grazing the ship’s side. Systems flickered. Power dipped.

“This isn’t random,” he muttered.

The presence he’d been following surged suddenly, unmistakable now—no longer a distant current, but a focused point.

Close.

Very close.

Another volley forced his hand. The ship couldn’t take much more.

He scanned quickly—one nearby planetary mass, barely charted, its atmosphere dense but survivable.

It would have to do.

Luke angled the ship downward, pushing through the turbulence as the attacker pursued. The descent was rough, controlled only by experience and instinct as systems began to fail one after another.

The ground came fast.

Too fast.

The ship slammed into the surface in a violent skid, tearing through rock and soil before grinding to a halt in a plume of smoke and debris.

Silence followed.

Broken only by the ticking of damaged systems.

Luke exhaled slowly, then reached for his lightsaber as he unstrapped himself.

“I know you’re out there,” he said under his breath.

The hatch hissed open.

—

The air outside was dry, thin, carrying fine dust across a barren landscape of fractured stone and jagged ridges. The sky above was a dull, muted gray, the light of a distant star filtered through a hazy atmosphere.

Luke stepped away from the wreckage, every sense alert.

The presence was no longer subtle.

It was overwhelming.

“Good,” a voice echoed—not through the air, but directly into his mind. Smooth. Cold. Amused. “You followed it.”

Luke turned.

A figure stood atop a nearby rise of stone, tall and draped in dark robes that seemed to absorb the light around them. His face was pale, elongated, marked by deep lines that gave him an unnatural, almost carved appearance.

His eyes burned with quiet intensity.

Snoke.

Luke didn’t need the name spoken. The Force carried it with recognition.

“You’ve been reaching,” Luke said, his voice steady despite the pressure pressing in from all sides. “For years.”

Snoke smiled faintly.

“And you’ve been blind to it,” he replied, descending the ridge without effort, his movement unnervingly smooth. “Until now.”

Luke ignited his lightsaber. The green blade cut through the muted light, steady and bright.

Snoke didn’t draw a weapon.

He didn’t need to.

“I wondered how long it would take you,” Snoke continued. “You felt it, didn’t you? Even before this moment. The unease. The doubt.” His head tilted slightly. “The fear.”

Luke held his stance. “You reached into my family.”

“I guided it,” Snoke said. “A whisper here. A touch there. The boy was… receptive.”

A sharp edge entered Luke’s voice. “He was a child.”

“He was potential,” Snoke corrected. “And you saw it. Oh, yes—you saw it clearly.” His smile widened, just slightly. “What he would become. What you could not control.”

Luke’s grip tightened.

“You’re the reason I saw that future.”

“I am the reason it existed,” Snoke said calmly. “You simply lacked the courage to embrace it.”

Without warning, the ground beneath Luke buckled.

An invisible force slammed into him, sending him skidding backward across the rock. He caught himself, rolling to one knee just as another crushing wave followed.

He raised his hand, pushing back, the Force colliding between them in a violent, unseen clash.

Snoke didn’t move.

“You feel it now,” Snoke said, his voice cutting through the strain. “That edge. That anger. You killed him, Luke Skywalker. Your own blood.” His tone sharpened. “Do not pretend you stand apart from me.”

Luke surged forward, closing the distance in a blur, his blade arcing toward Snoke.

It never reached him.

The air itself seemed to harden, stopping the strike inches from its target. Luke strained against it, but the resistance held firm.

Snoke’s eyes gleamed.

“So much power,” he said softly. “And still you chain it.”

With a flick of his hand, Luke was thrown aside again, slamming hard against the ground. Pain flared through his side as he struggled back up, breath tightening.

Snoke advanced now, slow, deliberate.

“Join me,” he said. “You’ve already taken the first step. You’ve done what your Order would never allow. You chose action over hesitation. Power over weakness.”

“I chose wrong,” Luke said through clenched teeth, rising again despite the pain.

Snoke stopped a few paces away.

“Then choose differently now.”

The pressure intensified—an invisible weight pressing down, probing, testing, searching for fractures.

Luke closed his eyes for a fraction of a second.

Not to retreat.

To center.

Dagobah.

Yoda’s voice. Obi-Wan’s calm. Anakin’s warning. Qui-Gon’s clarity.

Not fear.

Direction.

Luke moved.

This time, he didn’t strike directly. He shifted, letting the Force flow through him instead of forcing it outward. The pressure bent—not broken, but redirected.

Snoke’s expression flickered, just briefly.

Luke pressed the opening, advancing with controlled precision, each movement measured despite the pain building in his body.

Snoke raised a hand—and this time, Luke met it fully.

The collision of their power rippled outward, cracking the ground between them. For a moment, they stood locked in place, neither yielding.

Then Snoke stepped back.

Just once.

It was small.

But it was enough.

Luke saw it.

And so did Snoke.

The smile returned—but thinner now.

“Interesting,” Snoke said.

Luke didn’t lower his guard.

“You’re not as untouchable as you think,” he said.

Snoke’s gaze sharpened.

“No,” he agreed. “But neither are you.”

The next strike came faster than the others.

Luke blocked it—but not fully.

The impact sent a surge of pain through him, dropping him to one knee as the world tilted sharply. His vision blurred at the edges.

Snoke watched him, assessing.

Then, slowly, he lowered his hand.

“This isn’t the end,” he said.

Luke forced himself to look up.

“I know,” he replied.

Snoke studied him for a moment longer, then stepped back.

And was gone.

No ship. No sound. Just absence—like a shadow withdrawing into nothing.

The pressure in the Force receded with him, leaving behind a hollow quiet.

Luke remained where he was for a long moment, breathing hard, the pain in his side sharp and insistent.

When he finally rose, it was slow.

His ship behind him was barely recognizable now—hull torn, systems dead, no chance of flight.

Stranded.

He reached for the comm, hands steady despite everything.

“Leia,” he said.

—

The wait was not long.

The Millennium Falcon dropped out of hyperspace above the barren world, its familiar silhouette cutting through the gray sky as it descended toward Luke’s position.

When the ramp lowered, Han was already there.

“Kid,” he said, stopping short as he took in Luke’s condition. “You look terrible.”

Luke managed a faint, tired expression. “Good to see you too.”

Han didn’t argue. He just stepped aside, letting Luke board.

Leia was waiting inside.

She moved toward him immediately, her focus sharp, assessing—not just his injuries, but something deeper.

“You found it,” she said.

Luke nodded.

“Yes.”

—

They left the planet behind quickly.

Inside the Falcon, as it made the jump back toward Republic space, Luke sat at the central table, a med kit open beside him. Leia remained across from him. Han stood nearby, arms crossed, listening.

Luke told them everything.

The ambush.

The crash.

The man who had been waiting.

“Snoke,” Leia repeated quietly.

“He’s the one,” Luke said. “The presence I felt. The one in the vision. The one you sensed before Ben was born.”

Han’s expression darkened. “So this… thing’s been poking around our lives for that long?”

“Yes,” Luke said. “Not constantly. But deliberately. He was shaping something. Guiding it.”

“Guiding Ben,” Leia said.

Luke met her gaze. “Yes.”

Silence settled over them.

It wasn’t the same silence as before.

This one had direction.

“So,” Han said at last, “we’re dealing with someone who can reach across the galaxy, mess with people’s heads before they’re even born, and throw you around without lifting a finger.”

Luke gave a slight nod. “That’s accurate.”

Han exhaled sharply. “Great.”

Leia didn’t react to the tone. Her focus remained fixed.

“He wanted you to join him,” she said.

“Yes.”

“And?”

Luke’s answer came without hesitation.

“I refused.”

She studied him for a moment longer, then nodded once.

“Good.”

Another pause.

Then Han spoke again, more serious now.

“If he’s behind all of this… if he’s the reason Ben—” He stopped, jaw tightening. “Then this isn’t just some shadow in the dark.”

“It’s the source,” Leia said.

Luke nodded.

“The vision. What you felt during your pregnancy. All of it connects back to him.”

Leia’s expression hardened—not with anger alone, but with clarity.

“Then we stop him,” she said.

Han glanced at her. “That simple, huh?”

“No,” she said. “Not simple. But necessary.”

She looked back at Luke.

“You said he retreated.”

“Yes.”

“Which means he wasn’t ready to finish that fight,” Han said.

“Or he didn’t need to,” Luke added.

That hung there for a moment.

“Either way,” Han said, “we can’t just sit around waiting for him to try again.”

Leia nodded. “We won’t.”

She leaned forward slightly, her voice steady.

“We need to understand him. Where he is. What he’s building. And how far his reach actually goes.”

Luke exhaled slowly.

“He’s in the unknown regions,” he said. “Deep. Far beyond what the Republic has mapped.”

Han gave a short, humorless chuckle. “Of course he is.”

Leia didn’t look away from Luke.

“Then that’s where this leads.”

Luke met her gaze.

Whatever stood between them—grief, anger, what had been lost—was still there.

But it was no longer the only thing.

Now there was something else.

A shared enemy.

A purpose that cut through everything else.

“We’ll find him,” Luke said.

Leia nodded once.

Han pushed off the wall slightly, heading back toward the cockpit.

“Then let’s make sure next time,” he muttered, “we’re the ones doing the surprising.”

The Falcon surged forward through hyperspace, carrying them back—not to an ending, but to the beginning of something far larger than any of them had faced before.

# Part 6

Time moved differently in the quiet.

Han and Leia’s residence—set far from the constant motion of the Republic’s core—became a place of stillness, at least on the surface. Luke remained there for months, recovering from wounds that never fully faded.

Some healed cleanly.

Others did not.

The injury Snoke had dealt him lingered in ways bacta couldn’t fully mend. It wasn’t just physical. There was a strain in the Force itself, like a fracture that had sealed but never quite set right.

Luke adapted.

He trained again, slowly at first. Movement before speed. Control before strength. Leia occasionally joined him—not as a student, not quite, but as someone reconnecting with something she had once set aside. What she lacked in refinement, she made up for in focus.

They didn’t speak much about Ossus.

Or about Ben.

But neither of them forgot.

Han kept his distance from the training sessions. Not out of disinterest—out of something more complicated. He watched, sometimes, from afar. Said little.

And then something approached that demanded to be marked.

Since Endor.

Since the fall of the Empire.

Since a hastily arranged ceremony that had bound Han Solo and Leia Organa together before the galaxy had even caught its breath.

No urgency.

Just memory—and the people who had survived long enough to share it.

They chose Endor.

Not with spectacle—but with connection.

Leia stood beside Han, her expression steady, though her eyes carried the weight of everything that had led them here. Han, for once, looked almost at ease—uncomfortable with formality, but grounded by the people around him.

Luke stood nearby.

Not at the center.

But present.

Watching.

For a while, it was enough.

Words were spoken—not grand declarations, but reflections. Memories of battles fought and survived. Of losses endured. Of something built, imperfect but real, in the years that followed.

The forest listened.

And then it changed.

Luke felt it first.

A tightening.

A distortion.

The air seemed to pull inward, the Force itself recoiling as something cold and immense pushed into the space.

His head turned sharply.

Too late.

“Well, well, well.”

The voice came from everywhere at once, smooth and unmistakable.

“You all act like the Empire is old history,” Snoke continued, his presence settling over the clearing like a shadow. “Yet I can single-handedly destroy all of you right now.”

The celebration shattered into alarm.

Hands moved to weapons. Eyes searched the treeline.

Luke stepped forward, already reaching—

The strike came instantly.

Lightning tore through the air, violent and blinding, slamming into him with a force that drove him to the ground. The impact ripped a cry from him before he could suppress it, his body convulsing under the surge.

“Luke!” Leia’s voice cut through the chaos.

The lightning ceased.

For a fraction of a second.

Long enough.

Luke forced himself up, every nerve screaming, his hand finding his lightsaber. The green blade ignited as Snoke emerged from the edge of the clearing, descending with the same unnatural calm as before.

There was no hesitation.

Luke moved.

The duel was immediate and brutal.

Snoke did not hold back. The Force bent violently around him, each movement precise, overwhelming. Luke countered, drawing on everything he had—discipline, instinct, the fragile equilibrium he had rebuilt since their last encounter.

But he was not whole.

The earlier wounds slowed him. Subtly—but enough.

Snoke pressed the advantage without mercy.

“You came all this way,” Snoke said, his voice cutting through the clash, “and still you learned nothing.”

Luke struck again—faster, sharper.

Snoke deflected with a gesture, sending Luke staggering back.

“Look at them,” Snoke continued, almost conversationally. “Clinging to victories that mean nothing now.”

Another surge of power slammed into Luke, driving him to one knee.

He forced himself up again.

“I won’t—” he began.

The next strike ended the sentence.

It came not as a blast, but as a focused, crushing force that hit with devastating precision.

Luke’s lightsaber fell from his grasp.

He hit the ground hard.

And did not rise.

The clearing fell into a stunned, fractured silence.

Snoke stood over him, unmoving for a moment, then lifted his gaze—taking in the gathering, the fear, the shock.

A faint, satisfied expression touched his face.

And then—

A flicker.

Too small for most to notice.

Leia did.

She was already moving.

Luke’s lightsaber lay just beyond his outstretched hand.

She reached it first.

For a brief instant, it felt unfamiliar in her grip—weight, balance, memory all colliding at once.

Then it settled.

Snoke turned.

Too late.

Leia closed the distance with a speed born not of training, but of certainty. The blade ignited in her hands as she struck—not hesitating, not questioning.

The green light cut cleanly.

Snoke’s expression shifted—not to anger, not even to surprise.

Something else.

He staggered, the strength in his posture collapsing as the wound took hold.

The pressure in the Force shattered.

Leia stood there, breathing hard, the lightsaber still in her hand.

Snoke fell to his knees.

For a moment, no one moved.

Then Leia stepped closer.

“Who are you, really?” she demanded, her voice steady despite everything.

Snoke looked up at her.

For the first time, the certainty in his expression was gone.

“I don’t know,” he said.

The answer hung there, raw and unguarded.

Leia’s grip tightened slightly.

“Who are your parents?”

A faint, almost distant look crossed his face.

“I was artificially created,” he said. “Not born.”

A murmur rippled through those close enough to hear.

“Where were you created?” Leia pressed.

Snoke’s gaze unfocused slightly, as if reaching for something that refused to come.

“I have no idea.”

“Who created you?”

“I have no idea.”

The answers came without resistance.

Without deflection.

Leia’s voice hardened.

“What is your homeworld?”

A pause.

Longer this time.

“I don’t remember having one,” Snoke said.

The forest seemed to hold its breath.

“Where is your earliest memory?” Leia asked.

Snoke’s eyes shifted, not to her, but somewhere far beyond the clearing.

“Somewhere… far in the unknown regions…” he said.

The words faded with him.

The last of his strength left his body, and he collapsed fully to the ground.

The presence that had loomed over them—cold, vast, suffocating—vanished.

Gone.

As if it had never been there at all.

But the silence it left behind was heavier than anything before it.

Leia stood motionless, the lightsaber still in her hand.

Then, slowly, she turned.

Luke lay where he had fallen.

And this time—

He did not move.

# Part 7

No one spoke for a long time.

The forest had gone still, as if it understood something sacred had been broken.

Leia knelt beside Luke’s body first.

She didn’t touch him immediately. Just looked—taking in the stillness, the absence where there had always been presence. When her hand finally found his, it was steady.

Han stood a few steps behind her, unmoving. Chewbacca let out a low, mournful sound that seemed to echo through the trees, deep and aching. Around them, the others—old soldiers, friends, survivors—stood in quiet disbelief, the celebration they had gathered for now unrecognizable.

R2-D2 rolled forward slowly and stopped near Luke’s side, emitting a soft, uneven series of tones. C-3PO stood behind him, unusually silent.

There was no discussion about what to do next.

Not at first.

Then Leia rose.

Her voice, when she spoke, was calm—but it carried.

“We don’t leave him like this,” she said.

No one argued.

—

They didn’t return to the Republic for a formal ceremony.

They didn’t send word across the galaxy.

There was no need.

Almost everyone who would have come… was already there.

Instead, they searched.

Luke had once spoken, quietly and without ceremony, about where he had laid Anakin Skywalker to rest after bringing him back from darkness. It hadn’t been marked on any map, not formally recorded—but Leia remembered enough.

And the Force guided the rest.

It took time—hours moving through dense forest, guided by memory, instinct, and something deeper than either. Eventually, they found it.

A quiet place, set apart.

Simple.

Unadorned.

But not forgotten.

The ground there felt… settled. Not empty, not heavy—just at peace.

“This is it,” Leia said.

No one questioned it.

They worked in silence.

The soil was soft, the forest floor giving way without resistance. No machinery. No shortcuts. Just hands, tools, and intention.

When it was done, they laid Luke to rest beside his father.

Not as a legend.

Not as a symbol.

As family.

Leia was the last to step back.

For a moment, she stood between the two graves, her expression unreadable.

Then she returned to the others.

—

They formed a circle.

No structure had been planned, but something about the moment shaped itself naturally. Old comrades stood shoulder to shoulder with new generations. Wookiees beside humans. Droids quietly present at the edge.

The Ewoks gathered just beyond them, watching in silence.

The forest itself seemed to listen.

No one rushed to speak.

Then Han did.

He wasn’t the kind of man for formal words. Never had been. But he stepped forward anyway, rubbing a hand across his face before letting it fall.

“He was… a pain sometimes,” Han said, voice rough. “Too serious. Always thinking about the bigger picture.”

A faint ripple of recognition passed through a few of the older faces.

“But he showed up,” Han continued. “Every time it mattered. Didn’t matter how bad things got—he was there.”

He glanced toward the fresh earth.

“And he didn’t run from it. Even when he should’ve.”

That was all.

Han stepped back.

Chewbacca stepped forward next, letting out a long, low series of sounds—mournful, resonant. The meaning wasn’t understood by everyone, but the emotion was unmistakable.

Others spoke too.

Briefly. Simply.

Memories. Gratitude. Loss.

Leia waited until the end.

When she stepped forward, the clearing seemed to tighten around her.

“He believed in people,” she said.

Her voice didn’t waver.

“Even when they didn’t believe in themselves. Even when they didn’t deserve it.”

She paused.

“And when he stopped believing… even for a moment… it cost him everything.”

The words settled heavily.

“But that’s not what defines him,” she continued. “What defines him is that he didn’t stop trying to make it right.”

Her gaze drifted to the grave.

“And he didn’t stop protecting us.”

A quiet followed.

Not empty.

Full.

Then something shifted.

It was subtle at first—a soft stirring in the Force, like a breeze moving through still air. The kind of sensation Luke had described before. The kind Leia had felt only rarely.

Now it was unmistakable.

The air seemed to brighten—not visibly, but perceptibly.

And then—

He was there.

Luke stood at the edge of the circle.

Not as he had been in death.

But whole.

Luminous, yet solid enough to be seen clearly. The weight he had carried in life was gone, replaced by a calm that felt deeper than anything they had ever known from him.

No one moved.

Leia’s breath caught.

“Luke…” she said, barely above a whisper.

He looked at her.

And smiled.

“I’m here,” he said gently.

The sound of his voice—familiar, steady—broke something open in the silence.

“I don’t have long,” Luke continued, his expression shifting, growing more serious. “So listen carefully.”

The air tightened again, not with fear—but urgency.

“The darkness you felt,” he said, his gaze moving briefly across the group before returning to Leia, “it’s not gone.”

Leia’s grip tightened slightly at her sides.

“Snoke was part of it,” Luke said. “A piece. Not the source.”

A murmur passed through the group.

“If you stop now,” he went on, “it will return. Maybe not the same way. Maybe not the same face. But it will come back.”

Leia stepped forward slightly. “Then what do we do?”

Luke met her gaze directly.

“You find where he came from,” he said. “Not just a place—but the truth of it. Who—or what—created him.”

“The unknown regions,” Han said, more statement than question.

Luke nodded.

“Yes.”

Leia’s voice was steady, but there was something deeper beneath it now.

“And when we find it?”

Luke held her gaze.

“That’s where this ends,” he said.

A quiet settled again.

Then Leia asked, softer this time, “And Ben?”

Luke’s expression changed.

Not darker.

But more distant. More reflective.

“I can feel him,” he said. “Not as he was… but as part of the Force.”

Leia’s breath caught.

“I’m going to try to reach him,” Luke continued. “It won’t be easy. It may take time. But if I can… if I can guide him—help him find his way—”

He paused.

“Then he might be able to appear like this,” he said. “To speak to you.”

Leia didn’t speak.

She couldn’t.

But she nodded.
