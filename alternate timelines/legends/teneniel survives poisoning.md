# Part 1

The Yuuzhan Vong War had already pushed every world in the galaxy to its limits when turmoil began to spread through the royal family of the Hapes Consortium.

For years, tensions had simmered beneath the surface of the marriage between Prince Isolder and Queen Mother Teneniel Djo. Isolder had been born into the intricate political world of Hapes, where every alliance, every marriage, and every bloodline carried enormous significance. Teneniel, by contrast, had been raised among the Force-sensitive witches of Dathomir. Their marriage had once symbolized the joining of two very different worlds, but the passage of time and the strain of war had exposed old disagreements that neither had fully resolved.

The greatest source of friction concerned their daughter, Tenel Ka.

Isolder believed that one day Tenel Ka should fully embrace her responsibilities to Hapes and prepare herself to rule. Teneniel respected the Consortium and its people, but she had never wanted her daughter trapped by political expectations. Tenel Ka herself had chosen the path of the Jedi, training alongside the next generation of Jedi Knights and serving the New Jedi Order during the darkest years of the war.

As arguments continued, rumors spread throughout the Fountain Palace that Teneniel might return permanently to Dathomir.

Those rumors delighted one person more than anyone else.

Ta'a Chume.

The former Queen Mother had spent decades manipulating the politics of Hapes from behind the scenes. Though she no longer wore the crown, many still feared her influence. She viewed Teneniel as an obstacle and regarded Tenel Ka's commitment to the Jedi as a threat to the traditions she cherished.

Again and again, Ta'a Chume pressured Isolder.

He owed Hapes a strong ruler, she insisted.

He should divorce Teneniel.

He should remarry.

He should secure the future of the Consortium.

When Isolder resisted, Ta'a Chume became more direct.

One private conversation left him deeply unsettled.

She reminded him that many powerful nobles would never accept the current state of affairs forever. If he refused to act, she warned, others might act for him. Hapes had a long history of political murder. Their family was not immune.

Most disturbing of all was her proposal that he eventually marry Jaina Solo, daughter of Leia Organa Solo.

The suggestion horrified him.

He promised only that he would think about the future.

Nothing more.

But Ta'a Chume had already decided not to wait.

—

The poisoning occurred during a routine evening meal.

At first the symptoms seemed minor. Teneniel complained of dizziness and fatigue. Within hours she collapsed.

The palace erupted into panic.

Medical droids and physicians rushed to her chambers. Blood tests quickly revealed what many feared.

Poison.

A sophisticated poison designed specifically to evade normal detection.

By dawn, Queen Mother Teneniel Djo hovered between life and death.

Messages were sent immediately.

One of them reached Tenel Ka.

She was serving with the Jedi when the transmission arrived.

The moment she heard the words "your mother has been poisoned," she departed for Hapes.

—

When Tenel Ka arrived at the Fountain Palace, she found an atmosphere thick with fear and suspicion.

Guards filled the corridors.

Servants whispered.

Nobles watched one another carefully.

Her father looked exhausted.

Isolder had scarcely slept.

He was overseeing investigations, questioning palace staff, and demanding reports from security personnel while refusing to leave Teneniel's bedside for long.

Tenel Ka barely paused to speak with him before entering her mother's chambers.

The sight shook her.

Teneniel looked pale and weak. Medical equipment surrounded the bed. Physicians were still struggling to identify every component of the poison.

Teneniel smiled faintly when she saw her daughter.

"You came quickly."

"Of course."

The older woman reached for her daughter's hand.

For several minutes they spoke quietly.

Then Teneniel drifted back into an exhausted sleep.

Tenel Ka sat beside the bed.

And thought.

The poison.

The timing.

The recent political tensions.

The pressure on Isolder to abandon his wife.

The answer appeared almost immediately.

Ta'a Chume.

The suspicion struck her with such force that she rose from her chair before she consciously decided to move.

She left the room without another word.

—

Ta'a Chume received her granddaughter in one of the palace's private chambers.

The elderly noblewoman appeared calm.

Almost amused.

"Tenel Ka."

"Grandmother."

There was no warmth in either voice.

Tenel Ka stood motionless.

"You poisoned my mother."

Ta'a Chume raised an eyebrow.

"A serious accusation."

"Did you?"

"No."

The answer came instantly.

Too instantly.

Tenel Ka felt the Force swirling around her emotions.

Fear.

Anger.

Concern.

She pushed them aside.

Years of Jedi training had given her extraordinary discipline.

"You are lying."

Ta'a Chume's expression hardened.

"Be careful."

"No."

The single word echoed through the chamber.

Tenel Ka stepped forward.

The Force surged.

Ta'a Chume felt it immediately.

For the first time, genuine alarm appeared in her eyes.

Tenel Ka reached into her grandmother's mind.

Not with the gentle touch used by Jedi diplomats.

Not with subtle persuasion.

She drove directly through every barrier she could find.

The effort required immense concentration.

Memories erupted before her.

Private conversations.

Secret meetings.

Encrypted messages.

Payments.

Orders.

Assassins.

Poison.

Every piece fit together.

Tenel Ka saw Ta'a Chume arranging the entire operation.

Saw her impatience.

Saw her certainty that Teneniel's death would solve everything.

Saw her plans for controlling events afterward.

The vision ended.

Tenel Ka released the connection.

The room was silent.

Ta'a Chume stared at her.

Gone was the dignified mask.

Gone was the denial.

Only fear remained.

"You have no right—"

A turquoise blade ignited.

The lightsaber's hum filled the chamber.

For a brief instant neither moved.

Then Ta'a Chume understood.

This was not a political confrontation.

This was judgment.

"You would kill your own grandmother?"

"You tried to murder my mother."

Ta'a Chume's mouth opened.

Perhaps to threaten.

Perhaps to bargain.

Perhaps to deny everything once more.

No one ever learned.

The lightsaber flashed.

The former Queen Mother collapsed.

Silence returned.

Tenel Ka deactivated the blade.

Without another glance, she turned and walked away.

—

The palace exploded into chaos.

The death of Ta'a Chume sent shockwaves through every level of Hapan society.

Security forces sealed entire sections of the palace.

Nobles demanded answers.

Rumors spread at impossible speed.

Tenel Ka ignored all of it.

She returned directly to her mother's chambers.

Teneniel still lived.

That was all that mattered.

She sat beside the bed and focused on the Force.

Jedi healers possessed techniques capable of aiding natural recovery. Dathomiri traditions contained healing practices of their own. Tenel Ka had learned from both worlds.

Hour after hour she worked.

Guiding her mother's strength.

Supporting damaged organs.

Helping her body purge lingering traces of poison.

The process exhausted her.

She continued anyway.

Each day Teneniel improved.

Slowly at first.

Then steadily.

By the end of the week physicians began speaking openly about a complete recovery.

—

Isolder confronted his daughter shortly afterward.

The meeting took place in a private chamber overlooking one of the palace gardens.

For a long moment he simply looked at her.

"You killed my mother."

Tenel Ka met his gaze.

"Yes."

There was no attempt to evade responsibility.

No apology.

No excuse.

The answer seemed to age him several years.

Then she told him everything.

She described her suspicions.

The confrontation.

The Force probe.

The memories she had witnessed.

The proof she had seen with her own eyes.

When she finished, silence settled between them.

Isolder walked to the window.

Outside, fountains shimmered beneath the afternoon sun.

At length he spoke.

"Are you certain?"

"Completely."

He closed his eyes.

The question was not whether Ta'a Chume was capable of such a crime.

Everyone who truly knew her understood that she was.

The question was whether he trusted his daughter.

The answer came easily.

He did.

More than that, he realized he had spent years ignoring warning signs he should never have ignored.

When he finally turned back toward Tenel Ka, much of his anger had faded.

"I believe you."

Neither spoke for several moments.

Then Isolder crossed the room and embraced his daughter.

The gesture surprised them both.

—

Teneniel's recovery changed everything.

Faced with the possibility of losing her forever, Isolder found many of his old priorities suddenly unimportant.

The arguments that had consumed them for years no longer seemed worth continuing.

When Teneniel was finally strong enough to leave her chambers, husband and wife spent long hours talking.

Not as rulers.

Not as political figures.

Simply as two people who had nearly lost one another.

They discussed Hapes.

Their family.

Their mistakes.

And eventually they discussed Tenel Ka.

Isolder admitted that he had pushed too hard.

He had spent years trying to shape his daughter into the ruler he imagined Hapes needed.

In doing so, he had often failed to see the woman she had actually become.

Tenel Ka was a Jedi.

A capable one.

She had chosen her path.

And she had earned the right to follow it.

When he finally told her this himself, she listened quietly.

Then she nodded.

That was enough.

—

The removal of Ta'a Chume also transformed the political atmosphere within the Consortium.

For decades, powerful families had maneuvered around her influence.

Many had feared her.

Others had depended on her.

Without her presence, old networks began to unravel.

Teneniel resumed her duties as Queen Mother.

This time, however, she governed without constantly looking over her shoulder.

The threat that had haunted her reign was gone.

Isolder supported her openly.

Their relationship strengthened.

The palace grew calmer.

The government grew more stable.

Even nobles who disliked recent events recognized that the royal family now presented a united front.

Months passed.

The war beyond Hapes still raged, and the Jedi remained desperately needed.

As Teneniel's health returned completely and political affairs settled into a new equilibrium, an unavoidable question emerged.

Would Tenel Ka remain?

Or would she return to the Jedi Order?

The answer had never truly been in doubt.

One evening, as the royal family shared a meal together, Teneniel smiled across the table at her daughter.

"You are thinking about leaving."

"Yes."

"You miss the Jedi."

"I do."

Isolder exchanged a glance with his wife.

Then he nodded.

"Hapes will survive."

Tenel Ka almost smiled.

Almost.

"You are certain?"

"We are."

For the first time in years, they truly were.

—

The day of her departure was bright and clear.

Members of the royal household gathered at the spaceport.

Teneniel stood beside Isolder as Tenel Ka prepared to board her transport.

No crisis remained unresolved.

No illness lingered.

No political uncertainty demanded her immediate presence.

The Queen Mother was healthy.

The royal family was united.

The Consortium was stable.

At last, Tenel Ka was free to return to the life she had chosen.

Teneniel embraced her daughter first.

Isolder followed.

"I am proud of you," he said quietly.

Tenel Ka inclined her head.

The words meant more than either of them cared to admit.

Then she turned and walked up the boarding ramp.

Moments later, the transport lifted into the sky above Hapes.

The world grew smaller beneath her.

Ahead lay the Jedi Order, the war, and the uncertain future of the galaxy.

Behind her, for the first time in many years, she left a family at peace.

# Part 2

When Tenel Ka returned to the Jedi Order after the crisis on Hapes, she sought out Luke Skywalker almost immediately.

The Grand Master listened in silence as she recounted everything.

She described her mother's poisoning.

Her suspicions regarding Ta'a Chume.

The confrontation.

The invasive Force probe.

The memories she had uncovered.

The execution that had followed.

Luke asked questions only occasionally, allowing her to tell the story in her own way.

When she finally finished, neither spoke for several moments.

At length, Luke folded his hands and looked thoughtfully at his former student.

"I understand why you did it."

Tenel Ka said nothing.

Luke continued.

"That does not mean I agree with it."

"I expected as much."

"You acted as investigator, judge, and executioner."

"Yes."

"And if every Jedi did the same?"

"They should not."

Luke nodded slightly.

The answer did not surprise him.

Tenel Ka had never been reckless. If anything, she tended toward an almost severe sense of discipline.

"What happened with your mother affected you deeply."

"Of course."

"Because you love her."

Tenel Ka met his eyes.

"Yes."

Luke smiled faintly.

"That isn't a revelation."

She seemed mildly puzzled.

"The Jedi are often accused of forming no attachments at all. The reality is more complicated. Parents love their children. Children love their parents. Friends love one another. None of that surprises me."

"You believe my actions resulted from attachment."

"I believe they resulted from a particularly strong attachment."

Luke leaned back.

"But I also think most people would react strongly after discovering someone had attempted to murder their mother."

For perhaps the first time since leaving Hapes, some of the tension left Tenel Ka's posture.

Luke was not excusing what she had done.

Neither was he pretending not to understand it.

That distinction mattered.

—

The story spread through the Jedi Praxeum with remarkable speed.

Within days nearly every Jedi knew some version of the events.

Opinions varied dramatically.

Some openly approved.

To them, Ta'a Chume had been a murderer who had finally faced consequences.

Others were horrified.

They argued that Tenel Ka had violated core Jedi principles by carrying out an execution.

Many occupied positions somewhere in the middle.

They understood her motives while questioning her methods.

Discussions continued for months.

Even years later, the incident remained a frequent topic of debate among Jedi scholars and students.

Throughout all of it, two people stood firmly beside her.

Jacen Solo.

And Jaina Solo.

Both had known Tenel Ka since childhood.

Both knew her character.

Both understood how much her family meant to her.

Neither agreed with every aspect of what had happened.

Neither condemned her.

When others criticized her actions, they often found themselves arguing with one or both Solo twins soon afterward.

The experience strengthened a friendship that had already survived war, loss, and years of shared training.

—

As the years passed, that friendship deepened further.

The end of the Yuuzhan Vong War left many Jedi searching for direction.

The galaxy was rebuilding.

The Order itself was changing.

Through all of it, Jacen, Jaina, and Tenel Ka remained close.

By 32 ABY, one relationship had clearly evolved beyond friendship.

Jacen and Tenel Ka had become inseparable.

Neither spoke much about it publicly.

They simply spent more and more time together until eventually everyone noticed.

Including Luke.

The Grand Master chose not to interfere.

Instead, he quietly began assigning missions that frequently placed them together.

Diplomatic missions.

Investigations.

Exploration assignments.

Peacekeeping operations.

Whenever possible, Luke paired them.

Neither objected.

In fact, neither seemed to notice what he was doing.

Or perhaps they noticed and simply appreciated the convenience.

Either way, the arrangement continued.

By then their relationship had become serious.

Among those who knew them well, there was little doubt about that.

—

Three years later the galaxy faced a new crisis.

The Dark Nest conflict spread across multiple systems as the Killik hive minds expanded their influence.

The situation grew increasingly complicated.

Jedi became entangled in disputes involving the Killiks, the Chiss, and numerous other factions.

Throughout much of the conflict, Jacen and Tenel Ka worked together.

During that time Tenel Ka became pregnant.

The news brought both joy and uncertainty.

Neither had expected parenthood to arrive amid a galactic crisis.

Yet neither questioned whether they would face it together.

As her pregnancy progressed, they continued traveling from world to world.

The constant proximity revealed aspects of Jacen that few others witnessed.

At first the changes seemed subtle.

A sharper edge to his opinions.

Less patience for compromise.

More certainty that he alone understood the correct solution.

Tenel Ka noticed.

She paid attention.

And she grew concerned.

—

The conflict surrounding the Killiks gradually became an obsession for Jacen.

He viewed them as a growing threat.

A danger that would only worsen if left unchecked.

Initially his arguments focused on military necessity.

Then they became increasingly extreme.

One evening, after a particularly tense briefing, he spoke openly about eliminating the Killiks entirely.

Not defeating them.

Not containing them.

Destroying them.

Every nest.

Every colony.

Every hive.

Tenel Ka challenged him immediately.

The discussion lasted hours.

At first she managed to calm him.

She reminded him that entire species could not simply be erased because they posed difficulties.

She appealed to his better instincts.

For a time, it seemed to work.

But the obsession remained.

And over the following months it intensified.

—

The confrontation came without warning.

Tenel Ka found Jacen preparing his ship for departure.

His movements were hurried.

Focused.

Determined.

Something felt wrong immediately.

"Where are you going?"

He did not look up.

"To finish this."

She knew exactly what he meant.

"The Killiks."

"Yes."

"Alone?"

"If necessary."

He sealed a compartment and turned toward her.

His eyes were hard.

"The Jedi won't act."

"Because exterminating an entire species is not justice."

"It is survival."

"No."

His jaw tightened.

"People are going to die if nobody stops them."

"We can stop them without committing genocide."

"We've tried."

"Not enough."

"We've wasted enough time."

The argument escalated rapidly.

Every attempt at compromise failed.

Eventually Jacen moved toward the boarding ramp.

Tenel Ka stepped directly into his path.

He stopped.

"Move."

"No."

His expression darkened.

"You cannot stop me."

"I can try."

"I have made my decision."

"And I have made mine."

For several seconds they stared at one another.

Then Jacen spoke quietly.

"Nobody will stop me now."

Tenel Ka drew her lightsaber.

The turquoise blade ignited between them.

"If necessary," she said, "I will."

—

Jacen activated his own weapon.

The duel began immediately.

Neither wanted it.

Neither believed there was another option.

Jacen attacked first.

His emotions drove every movement.

Anger.

Fear.

Frustration.

His strikes were powerful but increasingly erratic.

Tenel Ka remained composed.

Years earlier she had faced her grandmother and acted in anger.

She remembered that lesson.

This time she refused to let emotion dictate her actions.

She focused on discipline.

Control.

Precision.

The difference became obvious within moments.

Jacen fought like a storm.

Tenel Ka fought like a wall.

Again and again his attacks failed.

Again and again she redirected them.

Eventually she created an opening.

Her blade struck.

Then again.

Not fatal wounds.

But enough.

The fight ended.

Jacen collapsed to one knee.

His lightsaber slipped from his hand.

The realization slowly reached him.

He had lost.

—

His injuries required extensive treatment.

The quest ended before it began.

By the time he recovered, events elsewhere had already changed the situation dramatically.

The Swarm War eventually reached its conclusion.

The conflict that had consumed so many lives finally ended.

One of the most important developments came when Jaina Solo was freed from the influence of the Killik hive mind.

For Jacen, that mattered enormously.

Much of his fear and anger had originated from watching his sister become entangled in the crisis.

With Jaina restored and the war over, the emotional pressure that had fueled his obsession finally eased.

The campaign of extermination he had envisioned was abandoned.

The matter was closed.

At least on the surface.

—

Around that same period, Tenel Ka gave birth to a daughter.

They named her Allana.

The arrival of their child transformed both parents.

For Tenel Ka, the experience brought profound happiness.

For Jacen, it introduced responsibilities unlike any he had previously known.

The months that followed were among the most peaceful either had experienced in years.

Yet Tenel Ka could not forget what had happened.

The duel remained firmly in her memory.

Not because Jacen had lost.

Because he had been willing to try.

She had witnessed firsthand how dangerous his thinking could become when left unchecked.

His intelligence, conviction, and strength in the Force made his flaws far more dangerous than those of ordinary people.

Ignoring that reality would be irresponsible.

—

Rather than abandon him, she chose another path.

She confronted the issue directly.

Over countless conversations she challenged his assumptions.

His certainties.

His tendency to force complicated problems into absolute solutions.

Sometimes they argued.

Sometimes they agreed.

Sometimes neither convinced the other.

But the discussions continued.

She also introduced him more deeply to traditions he had only partially encountered before.

The teachings of the Dathomiri witches.

The philosophies she had learned from her mother and from Dathomir itself.

One lesson in particular fascinated Jacen.

The witches did not always approach the Force exactly as the Jedi did.

Some traditions acknowledged that darker emotions and impulses existed within every individual.

The goal was not necessarily to deny their existence.

The goal was to master them.

To recognize them.

To prevent them from controlling one's actions.

Again and again Tenel Ka emphasized the same principle.

Power itself was not the issue.

Intent mattered.

Discipline mattered.

Character mattered.

A person could face darkness without surrendering to it.

A person could acknowledge anger without becoming cruel.

A person could possess great strength without seeking domination.

Jacen listened.

Over time he absorbed more of these teachings.

The process was gradual.

Measured in years rather than months.

There were setbacks.

Moments of frustration.

Old habits resurfacing.

Yet genuine progress occurred as well.

Eventually he made a promise.

Not to become perfect.

Not to become someone else.

Simply to become better.

To think more carefully.

To act more responsibly.

To remember the consequences of his choices.

Tenel Ka accepted the promise.

Then she held him to it.

—

The years that followed were defined less by galactic crises and more by family.

Jacen and Tenel Ka remained active Jedi.

They continued serving the Order.

They continued traveling when duty required it.

But increasingly their lives revolved around Allana.

Together they taught her.

Protected her.

Guided her first steps.

Answered endless questions.

Celebrated small victories that would have seemed insignificant to anyone else.

The responsibilities of parenthood proved challenging.

Neither complained.

For all the uncertainty that still existed in the galaxy, one thing remained clear.

Whatever difficulties lay ahead, they intended to face them together.

# Part 3

By 39 ABY, much had changed.

The years following the Swarm War had been kind to Jacen Solo and Tenel Ka.

The concerns that had once shadowed their relationship had not vanished overnight, but they had been confronted directly and repeatedly. Jacen no longer viewed every problem through the lens of absolute solutions. He had learned to recognize dangerous patterns in his own thinking before they spiraled out of control. The lessons Tenel Ka had spent years teaching him—discipline, self-awareness, and respect for the consequences of one's choices—had taken root.

Most importantly, he had become far more sensitive to the temptations of the dark side.

He no longer dismissed concerns from friends and family as obstacles to be overcome. He listened. He reflected. Sometimes he still disagreed, but he no longer assumed that disagreement meant others simply lacked understanding.

The improvement had strengthened every aspect of his life.

His friendships improved.

His relationship with Luke improved.

His relationship with Jaina improved.

And nowhere was the change more visible than in his relationship with Tenel Ka.

After years together, after surviving war, political crises, philosophical disagreements, and parenthood, they finally reached a simple conclusion.

It was time to marry.

—

The location was never really in doubt.

Isolder and Teneniel had insisted from the beginning.

The wedding would take place on Hapes.

The Fountain Palace prepared for weeks.

Guests arrived from across the galaxy.

Jedi.

Military officers.

Diplomats.

Old friends.

Extended family.

Even those accustomed to galactic-scale events found themselves impressed by the spectacle.

For once, however, politics and ceremony felt secondary.

The day belonged to Jacen and Tenel Ka.

And to three-year-old Allana, who had somehow managed to become the center of attention despite not being the bride.

—

The ceremony itself was beautiful.

When it concluded, celebrations spread throughout the palace.

Music echoed through grand halls.

Guests exchanged stories.

Old friends reunited.

Children raced through corridors while increasingly exhausted adults attempted to keep track of them.

At one point during the reception, Isolder and Teneniel found themselves standing beside Han Solo and Leia Organa Solo.

The four grandparents watched guests moving throughout the hall.

For a while the conversation remained pleasant.

Then Isolder smiled faintly.

"You know," he said to Han, "there is something almost ironic about all of this."

Han immediately narrowed his eyes.

"Whenever someone starts a sentence with that phrase, I know I'm about to hear something dangerous."

"Thirty-one years ago we were fighting over the same woman."

Leia groaned.

Han laughed.

Teneniel rolled her eyes.

Isolder continued anyway.

"And now we're standing here as family."

Han looked around the room.

Jacen and Tenel Ka were speaking with guests.

Allana was somewhere nearby, hopefully under supervision.

Hopefully.

"I suppose we are."

"We're co-grandfathers."

Han shook his head.

"When you put it that way, it sounds even stranger."

"Life is strange."

"History too."

Han took a drink.

"History seems to have a habit of turning former enemies into family."

Isolder looked amused.

"Examples?"

Han pointed across the room.

"Luke and Mara."

A reasonable example.

Then he pointed elsewhere.

"Jaina and Jag."

Isolder laughed.

"Fair enough."

The mention of Jagged Fel naturally brought up old memories of the Swarm War and everything that had followed.

The mood remained friendly.

Until the conversation drifted toward Allana.

Then things became complicated.

—

Isolder folded his arms.

"Teneniel and I have been discussing the future."

Han immediately looked suspicious.

"The future of what?"

"Allana."

That did not improve matters.

Leia silently considered leaving before things escalated.

Experience suggested that would be wise.

Unfortunately, curiosity kept her rooted in place.

Isolder continued.

"Tenel Ka has never shown any interest in eventually becoming Queen Mother."

Teneniel nodded.

"That has not changed."

"So," Isolder said, "we have been considering alternatives."

Han already disliked where this was going.

"What alternatives?"

"We may eventually skip a generation."

Han blinked.

"What?"

"Teneniel would remain Queen Mother. Then eventually Allana would inherit directly."

Han stared.

"You're serious."

"Entirely."

The Hapan prince gestured toward the crowded reception.

"The Consortium needs a future ruler."

"And?"

"And that means Allana needs to spend considerable time on Hapes."

Han leaned back.

"No."

"No?"

"No."

Isolder frowned.

Han continued.

"She should probably have the same upbringing Tenel Ka had."

Teneniel immediately knew where this was going.

That did not stop it.

"The Jedi Order?" Isolder asked.

"Exactly."

"Immersed among Jedi. Away from politics."

"Yes."

Isolder's expression darkened.

"The Jedi are going to destroy the Hapan line eventually."

Han laughed.

"No, they aren't."

"If every generation follows that path, there will never be an heir."

"There will be heirs."

"There won't."

"There will."

"There won't."

Leia closed her eyes.

The argument had somehow become less mature than the conversation between the three-year-olds elsewhere in the palace.

—

Eventually Han crossed his arms.

"If your hereditary system is so weak that a few young women deciding to become Jedi can end the line, then maybe it deserves to end."

The words hit like a thermal detonator.

Isolder stared at him.

Teneniel immediately regretted remaining nearby.

Leia began wondering whether she could still escape.

"Deserves to end?" Isolder repeated.

"That's what I said."

"You think centuries of Hapan tradition deserve to end?"

"If that's all it takes to break it, yes."

The argument escalated rapidly.

Voices rose.

Gestures became sharper.

Nearby guests quietly began relocating to safer areas.

—

Then Isolder made a mistake.

A spectacular mistake.

One that would haunt him for years.

He pointed at Han.

"Would you kidnap her?"

The question stunned everyone.

"What?"

"Allana."

Han blinked.

"What are you talking about?"

"If you decided I was giving her the wrong upbringing, would you kidnap her?"

Han stared.

Then realization dawned.

"Oh no."

"You have a history."

"Oh no."

"You've personally kidnapped people before."

Leia pinched the bridge of her nose.

Han groaned.

Teneniel looked upward as if seeking patience from the Force itself.

"You kidnapped Leia."

"That is not the same thing."

"You kidnapped her."

"Completely different circumstances."

Leia made the mistake of speaking.

"In hindsight, it was probably a good idea."

Silence.

Everyone looked at her.

Leia immediately regretted opening her mouth.

Unfortunately it was too late.

Isolder pointed dramatically.

"There!"

"Oh no," Leia muttered.

"You would support kidnapping Allana."

"That's not what I said."

"It sounded like what you said."

"It absolutely was not what I said."

Teneniel chose exactly the wrong moment to contribute.

"To be fair, Han taking Leia to Dathomir was a good idea."

Everyone stared at her now.

"If he hadn't," Teneniel continued, "I never would have met Isolder."

Isolder looked horrified.

"You too?"

"What?"

"You support kidnapping Allana?"

"No!"

"That's what it sounds like."

Teneniel folded her arms.

"Only because you're determined to misunderstand everything."

Isolder looked moments away from storming off entirely.

He was rapidly becoming convinced that everyone around him had joined some bizarre conspiracy against him.

—

Then salvation arrived.

In the form of a three-year-old girl.

Allana wandered up to the group.

She looked from one grandparent to another.

Everyone immediately stopped arguing.

"What's going on?"

Silence.

The tension evaporated almost instantly.

Allana tilted her head.

"I heard you talking about kidnapping Allana."

Four adults froze.

"And kidnapping Leia."

Four adults became visibly uncomfortable.

"I wanted to know why."

Nobody spoke.

The four grandparents slowly looked toward Jacen and Tenel Ka.

Surely the parents would rescue them.

The grandparents were on their own.

The battle itself.

Absolute silence.

Nothing came out.

Failed.

Teneniel attempted to formulate a response.

Failed.

Everything.

Every detail.

Every story.

Every question.

When she was older.

Much older.

Perhaps many years older.

All of it.

"Take her."

Jacen wisely chose not to argue.

Entirely herself.

Stubborn.

Curious.

Isolder sighed.

"No."

Teneniel smiled.

"Agreed."

"She's going to decide for herself."

Then they accepted it.

The argument was over.

Not because anyone had won.

Because the subject of the argument had already made the outcome obvious.

Everything.

Every.

Single.

Thing.

Tenel Ka closed her eyes.

Neither parent interrupted.

"I used to think they had moved beyond their rivalry."

Jacen laughed.

"They have."

"No."

"They have."

She looked at him.

"Have they?"

Then he smiled.

"Perhaps not."

Neither man still viewed the other as an enemy.

They respected one another.

They trusted one another.

They had become family.

Yet beneath all of that remained the same competitive spark that had existed since the day they first met.

The form had changed.

The foundation had not.

And judging from the wedding reception, it probably never would.

Not that either of them would ever admit it.

# Part 4

The year 40 ABY began with growing political unrest.

What had started as disagreements between Corellia and the Galactic Alliance steadily escalated into a full-fledged crisis.

Many Corellians had grown increasingly frustrated with Alliance authority and wanted greater independence. Some openly advocated secession. Others demanded sweeping reforms. Political negotiations deteriorated as both sides became more entrenched.

The conflict placed many prominent families in uncomfortable positions.

Han Solo's loyalties were obvious.

Corellia was his homeworld.

It always would be.

Though he did not agree with every decision made by Corellian leaders, he instinctively sympathized with their desire to determine their own future.

Leia found herself largely in agreement.

The couple spent much of the crisis advocating for dialogue and compromise rather than coercion.

Meanwhile Luke Skywalker attempted to keep the New Jedi Order neutral.

The Jedi would serve the galaxy.

They would protect lives.

They would help preserve peace.

But they would not become a military arm of either side.

Maintaining that position proved increasingly difficult as tensions rose.

—

Amid all of this, life continued.

Jedi still had missions to complete.

Threats still needed investigation.

Diplomatic disputes still required mediators.

One such mission brought together Jacen Solo, Tenel Ka, Jaina Solo, and three-year-old Allana.

The presence of a child on a Jedi mission would have seemed unusual to outsiders.

In practice, however, Jacen and Tenel Ka had little choice.

Parenthood and Jedi service often required creative solutions.

This happened to be one of them.

The assignment itself appeared routine.

Nothing suggested it would become one of the most significant confrontations of the era.

—

The encounter occurred on an otherwise unremarkable world.

The Jedi had completed most of their duties when an unexpected visitor appeared.

Lumiya.

At first glance she looked harmless.

Not a Sith Lord.

Not a warrior.

Not even particularly threatening.

She approached openly.

Calmly.

As though she merely wished to have a conversation.

Her attention focused entirely on Jacen.

"I would like to speak with you."

Jacen immediately became suspicious.

He knew enough about Lumiya's reputation to distrust any invitation she offered.

Still, refusing outright would accomplish little.

"I'll listen."

Lumiya smiled.

The two began walking.

Several paces behind them, Tenel Ka, Jaina, and Allana followed.

Far enough away to provide privacy.

Close enough to intervene if necessary.

Neither Jacen nor Tenel Ka trusted Lumiya for a moment.

—

Initially Lumiya relied on persuasion.

She spoke of power.

Of knowledge.

Of hidden truths.

She promised greater ability to protect those he loved.

She promised deeper understanding of the Force.

She promised control.

Security.

Influence.

Everything a Sith recruiter had promised for generations.

When those failed, she adjusted her approach.

She attempted a more subtle argument.

The dark side, she claimed, did not require cruelty.

One could embrace Sith teachings while remaining benevolent.

One could wield darkness for noble purposes.

One could have power without corruption.

It was an old argument.

A familiar argument.

One Jacen had already examined from every possible angle during years of self-reflection and discussion with Tenel Ka.

He simply shook his head.

"No."

Lumiya frowned.

"No?"

"I know where that path leads."

"You think you do."

"I do."

"And yet you still fear it."

"I respect it."

The distinction mattered.

Jacen no longer viewed the dark side as something fascinating or misunderstood.

He viewed it as something dangerous.

Something that demanded caution.

Something capable of destroying even the most intelligent and well-intentioned people.

The conversation continued.

Nothing Lumiya said gained traction.

Nothing tempted him.

Nothing weakened his resolve.

Eventually she realized persuasion had failed.

—

Then she noticed something.

Tenel Ka.

Jaina.

Allana.

Watching from behind.

A new plan formed instantly.

Lumiya stopped walking.

Turned.

And struck.

The Force exploded outward.

A powerful telekinetic shove shot directly toward Allana.

The attack happened so quickly that most observers would have missed it entirely.

Tenel Ka did not.

Years of training and instinct took over.

The instant she sensed danger she thrust out a hand.

A wave of Force energy erupted from her position.

The two invisible forces collided.

The impact shattered loose debris and sent dust spiraling through the air.

The attack dissipated harmlessly.

Allana remained unharmed.

For a brief moment silence followed.

Then Lumiya smiled.

She had never truly expected the attack to succeed.

The attack had been bait.

A provocation.

An attempt to force Jacen into exactly the emotional state she wanted.

Fear.

Rage.

Hatred.

Vengeance.

The dark side thrived on such emotions.

If Jacen would not willingly embrace darkness, perhaps he could be driven into it.

—

For an instant the emotions arrived exactly as Lumiya intended.

Fear for his daughter.

Anger at the attack.

Protective fury.

Every parent present felt it.

Especially Jacen.

Lumiya watched eagerly.

Waiting.

Expecting the inevitable explosion.

Instead Jacen closed his eyes.

Only briefly.

Then he turned away from her.

And ran toward his family.

Lumiya blinked.

The reaction caught her completely off guard.

Jacen ignored her entirely.

His attention focused on Allana.

Upon reaching her, he immediately knelt beside her.

"Are you hurt?"

She shook her head.

"No."

Only then did he visibly relax.

Lumiya's trap had failed.

The negative emotions had appeared.

But they had not taken control.

They had been acknowledged.

Managed.

Contained.

Exactly as Tenel Ka had spent years teaching him.

—

Jacen rose.

His expression had changed.

Not uncontrolled fury.

Not hatred.

Determination.

He looked at Jaina.

"Take Allana."

Jaina immediately understood.

"Jacen—"

"Take her home."

For a moment Jaina looked unhappy.

Then she nodded.

She trusted his judgment.

Without another word she scooped up Allana.

The little girl looked confused but not frightened.

Within moments the two were retreating rapidly from the area.

Soon they disappeared entirely.

—

Now only three remained.

Lumiya.

Jacen.

Tenel Ka.

The Sith Lady finally abandoned all pretense.

Her lightwhip ignited.

The yellow weapon crackled violently.

In response, two lightsabers came alive.

Tenel Ka's.

Jacen's.

The duel began.

—

Later accounts would describe the battle in almost mythic terms.

Witnesses were scarce.

The terrain itself bore the scars afterward.

The combatants moved with astonishing speed.

Lumiya's lightwhip created unpredictable attack patterns that forced constant adaptation.

Jacen and Tenel Ka fought as a coordinated pair.

Years of partnership showed in every movement.

Each understood the other's intentions almost instinctively.

One attacked while the other defended.

One advanced while the other repositioned.

Again and again Lumiya attempted to separate them.

Again and again she failed.

The battle stretched on.

Minutes felt like hours.

Powerful Force techniques shook the surrounding landscape.

Trees splintered.

Stone shattered.

The ground itself cracked beneath repeated impacts.

Yet despite Lumiya's skill, a simple reality gradually became impossible to ignore.

She was facing two of the Order's most capable Jedi.

Working together.

Protecting one another.

Trusting one another completely.

The balance slowly shifted.

Then shifted again.

Until eventually it became clear.

Lumiya was losing.

The final moments happened quickly.

A coordinated sequence.

One opening.

One mistake.

One decisive strike.

When it ended, Lumiya lay defeated.

The Sith threat died with her.

News of Lumiya's death spread rapidly.

Within the Jedi Order, reactions ranged from relief to outright celebration.

Among governments and intelligence agencies, similar sentiments prevailed.

A dangerous figure had finally been removed from the galactic stage.

The timing proved significant.

The Corellian crisis continued for a short while longer, but momentum gradually shifted toward negotiation.

Without additional provocations and escalating fears, cooler heads began gaining influence.

The Galactic Alliance became more willing to compromise.

Corellia became more willing to negotiate.

Concessions were exchanged.

Agreements were reached.

Eventually Corellia formally rejoined the Galactic Alliance.

The feared war slowly fizzled out.

Not because every disagreement vanished.

But because enough people chose compromise over escalation.

Several months later, Luke Skywalker addressed the Jedi Council.

The discussion was brief.

The decision unanimous.

Jacen Solo and Tenel Ka would be elevated to the rank of Jedi Master.

Their accomplishments already justified the promotion.

The defeat of Lumiya merely removed any remaining doubt.

The ceremony was attended by family, friends, and fellow Jedi.

Both accepted the honor with characteristic humility.

Though privately, many suspected Allana was more excited than either of her parents.

Afterward a small gathering formed.

Friends congratulated the newly promoted Masters.

Stories circulated.

Laughter echoed through the room.

"I would like it officially recorded that I am still annoyed."

Jacen smiled.

"About what?"

Tenel Ka immediately knew where this was going.

Jaina pointed accusingly at both of them.

"If I had stayed and fought Lumiya too, I would probably be a Jedi Master now."

Jacen laughed.

"You were protecting Allana."

"I know."

"You were doing exactly what needed to be done."

"I know."

"Because I could have been promoted."

Tenel Ka shook her head.

"I believe that is not the Jedi perspective."

"It absolutely is the Jedi perspective."

"It is not."

"It is today."

The conversation drew amused attention from nearby Jedi.

Among them was Luke Skywalker.

The Grand Master had overheard enough to understand the subject immediately.

He approached.

Jaina straightened slightly.

Luke smiled.

"Do not worry."

Jaina narrowed her eyes suspiciously.

"About what?"

"Your opportunity will come."

The room became quiet.

Jaina blinked.

"What does that mean?"

Luke's smile widened.

"It means exactly what I said."

Then he walked away.

Leaving behind an increasingly suspicious Jaina and several highly amused spectators.

For years afterward, Jacen would insist that Luke had timed the remark perfectly.

Jaina would insist it had been intentionally cryptic.

Tenel Ka privately agreed with both assessments.

Whatever Luke had meant, however, one thing was clear.

The next chapter of galactic history had already begun.

# Flashback: Han & Isolder Rivalry

Historians who studied the Solo and Hapan royal families often found themselves confronted by an unusual problem.

Many major galactic events were documented extensively.

Wars.

Political treaties.

Military campaigns.

Jedi missions.

Yet buried among those records was something far more difficult to explain.

A rivalry.

Not a political rivalry.

Not a military rivalry.

Not even a particularly serious rivalry.

At least not by the end.

Instead, it was a decades-long contest between Han Solo and Prince Isolder that somehow survived marriages, children, wars, grandchildren, and the passage of more than thirty years.

Nobody could quite explain why.

Least of all the participants themselves.

—

The beginning was well known.

In 8 ABY, after the events on Dathomir, Isolder formally ended his pursuit of Leia Organa Solo.

The matter was settled.

Leia had chosen Han.

Isolder had chosen Teneniel Djo.

Everyone involved moved on.

Or at least, almost everyone.

The trouble began shortly afterward.

The four of them had gathered privately before departing Dathomir.

The atmosphere was relaxed.

The crisis had ended.

Everyone was alive.

For once, things seemed simple.

Then Han made a mistake.

A very Han Solo mistake.

He slapped Isolder on the shoulder.

"Good game."

Isolder blinked.

"What?"

"You know."

Han grinned.

"The whole Leia thing."

Leia immediately buried her face in her hands.

Teneniel sighed.

Han continued.

"I guess that makes the score Han one, Isolder zero."

Isolder stared at him.

"Score?"

"You know."

"No."

Han leaned closer.

"The competition."

"There was no competition."

"There definitely was."

"There absolutely was not."

Han grinned wider.

"Sounds like something somebody who lost would say."

Teneniel later claimed she saw the exact moment Isolder decided vengeance would be necessary.

The prince slowly smiled.

Not a pleasant smile.

A very deliberate smile.

"Enjoy your victory, Solo."

"Oh, I will."

"Because someday I will get even."

Han laughed.

"I'm terrified."

"You should be."

Han laughed even harder.

This turned out to be a mistake.

—

Several years later, Isolder struck back.

The details became legendary.

Not because the scheme itself was especially dangerous.

But because of how absurdly elaborate it was.

Han spent nearly two weeks dealing with increasingly bizarre complications.

Cargo manifests disappeared.

A customs inspection somehow followed him across three star systems.

His favorite sabacc deck vanished and reappeared repeatedly.

One of the Falcon's maintenance records was altered to indicate that the ship technically belonged to a Hapan agricultural cooperative.

Nobody could explain any of it.

Han certainly couldn't.

Eventually he became convinced someone was targeting him.

The mystery ended when he received a personal transmission.

Isolder appeared on the screen.

Perfectly composed.

"Hello, Han."

Han immediately groaned.

"No."

"Yes."

"No."

"Yes."

"You did all this?"

"Every bit of it."

Han stared.

"Why?"

Isolder folded his arms.

"Because now we're even."

There was a long pause.

Then Han realized.

The score.

The ridiculous score.

Years later Isolder would insist the expression on Han's face made the entire operation worthwhile.

Han eventually admitted it probably had.

The score stood at one to one.

Neither intended to stop there.

—

The following decades became increasingly ridiculous.

Initially the rivalry retained traces of genuine competitiveness.

Both men occasionally tried to outmaneuver one another.

Neither trusted the other entirely.

But as years passed, the hostility faded.

The competition remained.

The animosity did not.

Instead, the rivalry evolved into something resembling a permanent practical joke.

Whenever either man discovered an opportunity to score a point, he took it.

No matter how trivial.

No matter how ridiculous.

No matter how little sense it made.

—

One famous incident involved a hunting expedition.

Han successfully tracked and captured a dangerous predator on a remote world.

Before he could celebrate, Isolder produced documentation showing he had successfully tracked the same animal three days earlier.

The argument over who deserved the point lasted six months.

Luke eventually settled the matter by declaring the point invalid.

Both men accused him of bias.

—

Another dispute emerged after a diplomatic summit.

Han accidentally offended three senators.

Isolder offended four.

Han claimed victory because fewer people were angry with him.

Isolder argued that offending more senators demonstrated greater political influence.

Neither position made any sense.

The argument lasted two weeks.

Leia eventually awarded neither side a point.

Both men complained.

—

Soon Leia and Teneniel became involved.

Not willingly.

Simply by proximity.

Han occasionally attempted to recruit Leia into his schemes.

Isolder attempted the same with Teneniel.

Neither woman appreciated this.

Both occasionally retaliated.

One memorable occasion saw Leia and Teneniel secretly cooperate to embarrass both men simultaneously.

Han and Isolder spent nearly a year arguing over whether that counted as a point for either side.

Luke refused to adjudicate.

—

Then the children became involved.

Jacen.

Jaina.

Anakin.

Tenel Ka.

At first they simply observed.

Then they discovered how entertaining the rivalry could be.

The adults quickly learned that children possessed a natural talent for exploiting absurd situations.

One year Jaina deliberately manipulated events so both men believed they had won the same point.

The resulting argument became so convoluted that nobody remembered what the original point had been about.

Jaina considered this one of her finest achievements.

—

By the early 30s ABY, matters became even stranger.

Jacen and Tenel Ka's relationship had deepened significantly.

This created opportunities.

Han noticed first.

Then Isolder.

Soon both fathers had reached the same conclusion.

If they could not directly defeat one another, perhaps they could compete through their children.

Jacen and Tenel Ka quickly discovered what was happening.

Neither objected.

In fact, they found it hilarious.

Whenever possible, they played along.

Sometimes they even deliberately manipulated events to confuse both fathers.

One occasion ended with Han and Isolder simultaneously claiming victory.

Only to discover Jacen and Tenel Ka had engineered the entire situation for their own amusement.

Both older men reluctantly awarded the younger pair a point.

Neither enjoyed doing so.

The rivalry evolved further after Jacen and Tenel Ka became parents.

Soon Han began treating Jacen's accomplishments as his own.

If Jacen succeeded, Han claimed indirect credit.

Isolder immediately adopted the same policy regarding Tenel Ka.

This created endless opportunities for disagreement.

The most famous example occurred during the Swarm War.

Word spread that Tenel Ka had defeated Jacen in a lightsaber duel.

The circumstances were serious.

The debate that followed was not.

Isolder immediately claimed a point.

Han objected.

Violently.

"You didn't do anything."

"I raised her."

"That doesn't count."

"It absolutely counts."

"No it doesn't."

"It does."

"You weren't even there."

"I contributed to her existence."

Han stared.

Then looked toward Luke.

"Tell him that's not how points work."

Luke rubbed his temples.

"I don't know how points work."

"Exactly," Han said triumphantly.

"That supports my position."

"It really doesn't," Luke replied.

The ruling eventually favored Han.

Isolder appealed.

The appeal failed.

He complained for months.

Eventually the rivalry reached perhaps its most dangerous stage.

The arrival of Allana.

Not dangerous in the military sense.

Dangerous in the sense that both grandfathers now possessed a highly mobile, highly curious, highly impressionable new proxy.

The opportunities were endless.

The consequences were immediate.

Around 38 ABY, Allana became a toddler.

At which point Han and Isolder made a discovery.

Toddlers enjoyed repeating things adults told them.

Especially embarrassing things.

The possibilities proved irresistible.

Han would teach Allana harmless but embarrassing stories about Isolder.

Then send her to Hapes.

A few days later Isolder would retaliate by teaching her embarrassing stories about Han.

Then send her back.

Awkward courtship attempts.

Embarrassing wardrobe accidents.

Questionable hairstyles.

Poor decisions involving speeders.

Even worse decisions involving animals.

Somehow every visit resulted in new information.

Jacen and Tenel Ka eventually realized what was happening.

They were not entirely comfortable with it.

But they also recognized that no actual harm was occurring.

Allana simply enjoyed hearing stories.

The grandfathers enjoyed scoring points.

And everyone else enjoyed watching.

The rivalry required bookkeeping.

Extensive bookkeeping.

Both men maintained records.

Detailed records.

Painfully detailed records.

The score mattered.

Not because either could explain why.

Only because it mattered.

By 39 ABY the official tally stood at thirty-one points for Han and twenty-eight for Isolder.

Unofficially, both claimed significantly higher numbers.

This created problems.

Soon neutral arbitration became necessary.

The preferred mediator was Jaina Solo.

Not because she wanted the job.

Because everyone trusted her.

Relatively speaking.

Witness statements.

Mission reports.

Occasionally security recordings.

She would then issue a ruling.

Neither side was usually happy.

Both accepted her authority.

Mostly.

When Jaina was unavailable, the responsibility fell to Luke.

Luke found the entire process absurd.

Yet he continued participating.

Partly because it amused him.

Partly because watching Han and Isolder argue over technical scoring regulations was unexpectedly entertaining.

And partly because he feared what might happen if nobody mediated.

Probably.

Still, nobody wanted to find out.

Everyone understood it.

Nobody truly controlled it.

The competition no longer resembled the conflict that had begun over Leia decades earlier.

The original cause had long since disappeared.

Han loved Leia.

Isolder loved Teneniel.

Both families had become deeply intertwined.

The rivalry survived anyway.

No longer hostile.

No longer serious.

Yet somehow permanent.

Even the participants occasionally admitted as much.

Only occasionally.

And never while points were being counted.
