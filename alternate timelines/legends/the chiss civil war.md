# Part 1

In 46 ABY, the matter of Raynar Thul returned to the Jedi Order with a force that none of them had wanted to face again.

Only a year earlier, Jedi had brought Thul away from the Thuruht Killik nest. The decision had been difficult, but it had been made for reasons that reached far beyond one man’s wishes. Raynar had once become UnuThul, a leader within the Killik collective, and the memory of the Swarm War had not faded from either the Jedi or the Chiss Ascendancy. The Chiss had watched Killik nests become a coordinated military danger. The Jedi had watched friends become Joiners, their identities and loyalties entangled in a hive mind.

Then Raynar escaped.

The report arrived at Shedu Maad shortly before dawn.

Jaina Solo Fel stood in the communications chamber beside Luke Skywalker as the holoimage of Master Tekli flickered above the projector. The Chadra-Fan Jedi’s ears were held low.

“He did not resist us,” Tekli said. “That is what troubles me most.”

“Tell me exactly what happened,” Luke said.

“Raynar asked to walk in the gardens after evening meditation. Two Knights accompanied him. At some point, he used the Force to distract them—nothing violent, nothing lasting. By the time they recovered, he had taken a shuttle.”

“Destination?” Jaina asked.

“Unknown at first. We traced the ship as far as the outer route. Then it vanished from our sensors.”

Luke’s expression did not change, but Jaina felt the weight settle over him.

“And now?” he asked.

Tekli swallowed. “A Killik relay intercepted a message. Raynar has returned to Thuruht.”

Jagged Fel stood near the chamber’s rear wall, arms folded. Since leaving the Imperial throne before his marriage to Jaina, he had worked with the Jedi as a combat instructor. He wore no uniform now, only dark practical clothing suited to the training grounds of Shedu Maad. But the old Chiss discipline remained in the way he listened: motionless, alert, calculating every consequence.

“Has he asked for extraction?” Jag said.

“No,” Tekli replied. “He has refused it.”

Luke looked at Jaina. “Did he send a message to the Order?”

Tekli nodded. “Only this: ‘I have chosen where I belong. Do not come for me again.’”

For several seconds, no one spoke.

Jaina remembered Raynar as he had been in the years after the Swarm War: exhausted, fractured, fighting to reclaim the parts of himself that had been absorbed into the Killik collective. She remembered how hard it had been to speak of him as a person rather than as UnuThul. She also remembered Chiss ships burning in the darkness and Jag’s face when the war had put them on opposite sides.

Jag broke the silence.

“The Chiss will learn of this,” he said.

“They may already know,” Luke replied.

“And they will not hear ‘Raynar Thul has returned to a nest.’” Jag’s gaze shifted to Jaina. “They will hear ‘UnuThul has returned to the Killiks.’”

Jaina felt the truth of it. “Raynar says he wants a life with them. That doesn’t mean he intends to build another Colony.”

“No,” Jag said quietly. “But the Ascendancy will remember what happened when they waited for intentions to become actions.”

Luke turned back toward Tekli’s holoimage. “Maintain observation. Do not approach the nest without authorization. If Raynar asks for help, we will answer. If he does not, we will not turn this into a siege.”

Tekli hesitated. “Master Skywalker… the Chiss may see restraint as weakness.”

“Then we will have to show them that restraint is not indifference.”

The Chiss response came before the next day had ended.

A formal diplomatic message arrived through channels so carefully structured that Jag recognized the style before the translation droid finished parsing it. The Ascendancy requested an immediate meeting with representatives of the Jedi Order. Their concern, the message said, was “the renewed proximity of the former Prime Unu known as Raynar Thul to Killik collective activity.”

Jaina read the words twice.

“‘Renewed proximity,’” she said. “As if he were a chemical spill.”

Jag gave a humorless breath. “For them, he may be worse.”

Luke stood at the window overlooking the pale stone terraces of Shedu Maad.

“They are asking us to retrieve him again,” Jaina said.

“They are asking us to prevent him from ever returning,” Luke replied.

“And can we?”

Luke turned toward her. “Not without making him a prisoner.”

Jag’s expression tightened. “The Chiss delegation will say that Raynar has already demonstrated he cannot be trusted to remain away voluntarily.”

“He is still a person,” Jaina said.

“Yes,” Jag said. “And the Chiss will say that one person has already become a weapon once.”

Luke’s voice was calm, but firm. “We will not deny Raynar’s personhood to make diplomacy easier.”

“Then the Chiss will need to hear that directly,” Jaina said.

Luke studied her for a moment. “You and Jag will meet them.”

Jaina raised an eyebrow. “Both of us?”

“You are a Jedi Master, a member of the High Council, and someone who understands the danger of Joining from the inside.” Luke looked to Jag. “And Jag understands the Chiss better than almost anyone who can speak for the Jedi.”

Jag’s mouth tightened at that description, but he inclined his head. “I can make their concerns clear.”

“And I can make ours clear,” Jaina said.

Luke nodded. “Do that. Make it clear that the Order will not assist a renewed Killik threat. Make it equally clear that we will not abduct Raynar simply because the Chiss are afraid of what he might become.”

The meeting was arranged at a neutral station on the edge of a trade route, far from both Chiss territory and the known Killik regions. The station had once been a refueling depot; its diplomatic chamber was circular, windowed, and deliberately spare. A single long table divided the room. Its polished surface reflected the stars.

Jaina arrived with Jag and two Jedi Knights assigned as security. The Chiss delegation was already waiting.

There were three principal negotiators. The senior representative, a blue-skinned woman with black hair drawn tightly back from her face, introduced herself as Syndic Ral’vyr’onn. Her two aides stood at either side of her, their expressions guarded.

Behind them waited four Chiss bodyguards.

Jag noticed them immediately.

Their uniforms were correct, their posture disciplined, their weapons holstered in accordance with the station’s security agreement. But their eyes moved too much. Not the measured scanning of professionals watching a room. Something sharper. Something restless.

Jaina sensed Jag’s attention shift.

“What is it?” she murmured.

“Nothing I can prove,” he said under his breath. “Stay aware.”

Syndic Ral’vyr’onn inclined her head toward them.

“Master Solo Fel. Jagged Fel.”

“Jaina is fine,” Jaina said.

The Syndic’s gaze rested on her for a moment. “The Ascendancy remembers you.”

“I know.”

“And you, Fel,” Ral’vyr’onn said, turning to Jag. “Have not been forgotten.”

Jag’s face remained composed. “I would not expect to be.”

They took their places at the table.

Ral’vyr’onn began without ceremony. “Raynar Thul has returned to the Thuruht nest. Is this accurate?”

“It is,” Jaina said.

“Does the Jedi Order intend to remove him?”

“The Order intends to speak with him,” Jaina replied. “We intend to ensure he is not being coerced or controlled. But we will not treat him as a prisoner.”

One of the Chiss aides leaned forward. “He was once UnuThul.”

“He was,” Jaina said.

“He became a central figure in a collective that expanded toward Chiss territory.”

“He did.”

“He was connected to Jedi who fought Chiss forces.”

Jaina’s voice hardened. “And many of those Jedi were Joiners. They were not acting as agents of the Jedi Order.”

The aide’s red eyes narrowed. “That distinction did not protect Chiss lives.”

Jag spoke before the exchange could sharpen further.

“The Jedi Order understands the gravity of the history,” he said. “That is why Master Skywalker ordered Raynar retrieved after his previous return to Thuruht. That is why he has ordered observation now.”

Ral’vyr’onn looked at him closely. “Observation.”

“Yes.”

“Not removal.”

“Not forced removal,” Jag said.

The Syndic folded her hands. “The Ascendancy requests a stronger guarantee. Raynar Thul must be removed from the nest. He must be prevented from returning. Permanently.”

Jaina shook her head. “We cannot promise that.”

“Cannot,” Ral’vyr’onn repeated, “or will not?”

“Both.”

The room grew still.

Jaina kept her hands open on the table. “Syndic, I understand why your government is afraid. I have seen what the Killiks can do. I have been joined to them. I have fought in that war. But Raynar has not attacked anyone. The Thuruht have not attacked anyone. We cannot take a man by force and call it peace.”

“You ask the Ascendancy to wait,” said the aide.

“I ask the Ascendancy not to begin a war over fear alone.”

Jag watched the bodyguards again.

The one nearest the door had shifted his weight. Barely. A small adjustment of stance. The guard beside him glanced toward Jag, then toward the far wall.

Jag’s eyes narrowed.

“Jaina,” he said softly.

She did not look away from the delegation. “I see them.”

Ral’vyr’onn’s expression changed. “What is the matter?”

Before Jag could answer, one of the bodyguards stepped forward.

The movement was fast enough to turn warning into action.

Two Chiss guards crossed the distance in a blur of dark uniforms. One seized Jag’s arm and twisted it behind his back. The other locked an arm across his chest, hauling him backward from his chair.

Jag drove an elbow toward the attacker’s ribs, but the hold tightened. The table jolted as his chair struck it.

“Jag!” Jaina shouted.

The official Chiss delegation rose in shock.

“Guards!” Ral’vyr’onn snapped. “Stand down!”

But the bodyguards did not obey.

One of them drew a compact stun baton. Another moved toward the exit, clearing a path.

“This is not authorized!” the Syndic shouted. “Release him!”

Jag planted his feet and tried to wrench free. “Jaina—don’t let them move me!”

The Jedi Knights reached for their weapons, but the abductors had Jag between themselves and the Jedi.

For one instant, Jaina saw only fragments: Jag’s shoulder forced back, the stun baton rising, the Chiss negotiators staring in disbelief, and the sudden certainty in the Force that this was not a misunderstanding.

Her hand moved.

A purple blade snapped to life with a sharp, violent hiss.

Jaina Solo Fel stepped around the table, lightsaber raised and aimed directly at the Chiss bodyguards holding her husband.

“Let him go,” she said.

# Part 2

Jaina’s purple blade hummed between Jag and the Chiss bodyguards.

“Release him,” she said. Her voice was low, but every word carried through the chamber. “Release him now, or I will not hold back.”

The Chiss holding Jag did not release him.

One tightened his arm across Jag’s chest. Another dragged the stun baton across the weapon’s activation stud, its crackling charge brightening. The guard nearest the exit backed away, making room for them to force Jag through the doorway.

“Master Solo Fel,” Syndic Ral’vyr’onn said, horrified, “they are not acting under my authority.”

“I know,” Jaina replied.

The nearest abductor pulled Jag toward the exit.

Jaina moved.

Her blade cut through the stun baton before its wielder could bring it down again. The weapon fell in two smoking pieces. The bodyguard lunged toward her, trying to seize her lightsaber arm.

Jaina turned aside, caught his wrist, and drove him down against the floor. He fought with a desperation that was more frightening than skill. His free hand clawed toward the holster at his belt.

“Stop,” Jaina ordered.

He did not.

She kicked the holster away and stepped back, giving him room to surrender.

The Chiss bodyguard rose again.

“Let him go!” Jaina shouted.

He charged her.

Jaina parried his reaching hands with the flat of her blade, then swept his legs out from beneath him. He struck the floor hard, rolled, and came up with both hands raised—not in surrender, but to grab for Jag again as the other guards hauled him toward the doorway.

Jaina felt the Force tighten around her. Jag’s face had gone pale. The stun baton had struck him more than once; his legs were no longer holding him upright. His head lolled as the abductors dragged him away.

The unarmed bodyguard sprang at Jaina from the side.

She spun.

The purple blade flashed once.

The Chiss bodyguard fell and did not rise.

For a fraction of a second, the room went silent.

Then the remaining abductors dragged Jag through the doorway.

“Jaina!” one of the Jedi Knights called.

She ran.

The station corridor became a blur of metal walls and flashing emergency lights. The Chiss bodyguards had reached the docking passage. Jag’s boots scraped against the deck as they hauled him toward a compact Chiss shuttle at the end of the corridor.

“Stop!” Jaina shouted.

One of them looked back and raised a blaster.

Jaina’s lightsaber caught the bolt. It struck the ceiling in a burst of sparks.

The guards did not slow. Jag hung between them, unconscious and limp, his head falling against one captor’s shoulder. Another stun baton struck him again, as though the bodyguard feared even an unconscious prisoner might resist.

Something broke loose inside Jaina.

She sprinted forward.

The first guard turned just in time to see her blade. He fell before he could fire again. Jaina carried her momentum into the second, cutting through the weapon in his hand and then through him as he tried to pull Jag toward the shuttle hatch.

The last bodyguard shoved Jag aside and reached for the controls.

Jaina was already there.

When it was over, the docking passage was still except for the hiss of damaged machinery and the low hum of her lightsaber.

Jaina stood over Jag, breathing hard.

Then she deactivated the blade.

“Jag,” she said, dropping to her knees beside him. “Jag, can you hear me?”

He did not answer.

The two Jedi Knights arrived seconds later.

“Get him back inside,” Jaina said. “Now.”

Together, they carried Jag away from the shuttle and back toward the station’s medical bay.

The negotiation chamber was no longer a place for diplomacy.

Syndic Ral’vyr’onn stood beside the table, staring at the doorway through which the violence had spilled. Her aides were pale beneath their blue skin. The remaining Chiss security personnel had secured the room, but none of them looked at Jaina with accusation. They looked at her with the same stunned disbelief that had settled over everyone.

Jaina approached the Syndic slowly.

“I need to know whether you knew,” she said.

Ral’vyr’onn’s jaw tightened. “You think I ordered this?”

“I need to know.”

Jaina reached out with the Force.

The Syndic’s mind met hers with shock, anger, and fear. There was no concealed satisfaction. No preparation. No knowledge of the abduction plot. Her aides carried the same stunned confusion.

Jaina withdrew.

“You didn’t know,” she said.

“No,” Ral’vyr’onn replied. “Those guards were selected through channels I believed secure.”

“They were not your guards,” Jaina said. “Not really.”

“No.” The Syndic looked toward the corridor. “They were something else.”

Neither side attempted to resume the talks.

“This is not the time,” Ral’vyr’onn said. “My government must be informed.”

“So must mine,” Jaina replied.

The Chiss delegation departed under station security. The Jedi left shortly afterward with Jag.

Back on Shedu Maad, Jag was placed in a bacta tank.

Jaina stood outside the transparent cylinder, watching the pale blue fluid move around him. The bruises along his face and neck were already fading beneath the treatment. His eyes remained closed.

Luke Skywalker arrived quietly beside her.

“Master Skywalker,” Jaina said.

“Tell me what happened.”

She did.

She described the negotiations, the warning signs Jag had noticed, the sudden seizure, the stun baton, the first bodyguard’s refusal to stop, the desperate pursuit through the docking passage.

When she finished, Luke did not answer immediately.

“I need to see it,” he said.

One of the Jedi Knights stepped forward. “Master Skywalker, I recorded the meeting.”

Luke looked at him. “You recorded all of it?”

“Yes. The chamber, the corridor, the docking passage. My recorder stayed active.”

“Show us.”

They watched the footage in a small briefing room beside the medical wing.

Jaina stood with her arms folded as the recording played: the calm opening of the negotiation, the Chiss demand that Raynar be permanently separated from the Killiks, Jag’s careful answers, the sudden movement of the bodyguards.

Then came Jaina’s voice.

“Release him now, or I will not hold back.”

Luke watched without speaking.

The recording showed her disarming the first bodyguard. It showed her stepping back and ordering him to stop. It showed him rise again and continue trying to reach Jag. It showed the abduction continue even after the official Chiss delegation ordered the guards to stand down.

When the recording ended, the room remained quiet.

Jaina looked at Luke.

“I killed them,” she said.

“You gave them chances,” Luke replied.

“I could have done something else.”

“Perhaps.” Luke’s voice was gentle. “But you were defending Jag from an armed kidnapping. They continued after you disarmed them. They continued after their own delegation disavowed them. They continued after you warned them.”

Jaina looked down at her hands.

Luke continued, “You acted to stop an immediate threat. I do not blame you for what happened.”

The official message from Csilla arrived not long afterward.

The Chiss Ascendancy demanded an explanation for the deaths of its personnel.

Luke sent the full recording.

For days, the Jedi received no answer.

Then the answer came—not first from the Ascendancy government, but from a Chiss organization that had previously existed only as scattered voices, private networks, and angry statements after the Swarm War.

They called themselves the Never Killiks Again Association.

The NKAA declared that the dead bodyguards had acted under its authority. It proclaimed them heroes and martyrs. Its spokespeople insisted that Jagged Fel’s abduction had been necessary to force the Jedi Order to remove Raynar Thul from the Killiks permanently.

“We will protect the Chiss from the Killiks at all costs,” declared one NKAA broadcast. “The Jedi have failed to understand the danger. They will understand when the consequences are placed in their hands.”

The group’s most prominent supporters were familiar to those who remembered the Swarm War. They were the same kinds of voices that had once argued for developing and using parasite bombs against the Killik species—weapons meant not merely to defeat a military threat, but to exterminate an entire people.

The Ascendancy government publicly condemned the NKAA.

“The attempted abduction of Jagged Fel was ruthless, dangerous, and provocative,” the government statement declared. “It was not authorized by the Ascendancy. It did not serve Chiss security. It endangered Chiss lives and threatened to ignite conflict with the Jedi Order.”

The NKAA answered with defiance.

Its supporters multiplied.

At first, the Ascendancy believed it could contain the movement through arrests and public denunciations. Then violence began. Government offices were attacked. Military depots were raided. NKAA cells seized communications stations and demanded that the Ascendancy prepare for total war against the Killiks and any Jedi who protected them.

The conflict spread across Csilla.

Then it spread beyond Csilla, into other Chiss worlds.

Government forces still outnumbered the NKAA, but only barely. New recruits joined the radicals every day, drawn by fear of the Killiks, anger over the past, and the belief that their leaders had become too cautious to defend the Ascendancy.

At last, the Ascendancy government sent a request to Shedu Maad.

It asked for Jedi assistance.

Luke Skywalker read the request in silence, then looked toward Jaina and Jag. Jag had recovered from the stun baton injuries. He stood beside Jaina, his posture straight again, though a faint stiffness remained in one shoulder.

“There is one condition,” Luke said.

The Chiss representatives waited.

“Jagged Fel’s exile must end,” Luke said. “He cannot be sent into Chiss space as an ally of your government while remaining formally condemned by it.”

The answer came quickly.

The Ascendancy agreed.

Jag’s exile was ended.

The NKAA refused to recognize the decision. Its broadcasts declared that Jag remained an exile and would be punished if he entered Chiss territory. But neither the Ascendancy government nor the Jedi Order recognized the NKAA as legitimate authority, and neither accepted its claim.

Luke assembled a strike team.

Jaina would lead it. Jag would accompany her. Ten Jedi Knights were placed under Jaina’s command.

When their ship emerged above Csilla, Jag stood beside Jaina at the viewport.

The world below was vast and cold, its atmosphere swirling over ice and stone. Chiss vessels moved through the darkness in patrol formations. Farther away, warning beacons marked areas where the civil war had already reached orbit.

Jag was quiet for a long time.

Then he said, “It feels oddly refreshing to be back.”

Jaina looked at him.

“Even now?” she asked.

“Even now.” He watched Csilla turn beneath them. “I spent ten years knowing I could never return. It is strange to see it again.”

“You came back to a civil war.”

Jag gave a small, tired smile. “I never said it was a pleasant feeling.”

Their mission led them through contested territory toward an NKAA-held installation. The Jedi strike team moved with precision, entering through a damaged service corridor while government forces kept the main defenders occupied elsewhere.

Jaina led from the front, purple lightsaber ignited.

The ten Jedi Knights followed, their blades casting light across the dark corridor. Jag moved beside her in beskar armor, Mandalorian crushgaunts covering his hands, a blaster held ready in his grip.

They had barely reached the central approach when NKAA fighters emerged from behind barricades.

“Jagged Fel!” one of them shouted. “Exile! You are under arrest!”

Jag raised his blaster. “Your government disagrees.”

“The government has betrayed the Ascendancy!”

The NKAA fighters surged forward.

Their goal was clear from the first seconds: not simply to drive back the Jedi, but to seize Jag.

Jaina stepped in front of him.

“No one takes him,” she said.

The corridor erupted into blasterfire and light.

Jaina’s purple blade turned bolts aside. The Jedi Knights formed a defensive line around Jag, deflecting fire and forcing the attackers back. Jag fired in controlled bursts, using the beskar armor to absorb glancing hits while his crushgaunts smashed aside a weapon when an NKAA fighter got too close.

More fighters pressed forward.

Two tried to rush Jag from the side.

Jaina intercepted them, her blade cutting through their weapons and driving them back. A Jedi Knight pulled Jag away from a collapsing barricade as a grenade detonated against the far wall.

“Still want to be arrested?” Jaina called over the noise.

Jag fired at an advancing fighter’s rifle, sending it spinning from his hands. “Not particularly.”

The NKAA attack faltered.

Their fighters had expected a target. Instead, they had found Jaina Solo Fel, ten Jedi Knights, and Jagged Fel refusing to be taken again.

One by one, the radicals began to fall back.

“Retreat!” someone shouted.

The remaining NKAA forces withdrew through the far passages, leaving damaged barricades, smoking weapons, and the echo of their retreat behind them.

Jaina kept her lightsaber raised until the last of them disappeared.

Then she turned to Jag.

“Are you hurt?”

He checked the armor at his side, where a blaster bolt had scorched the beskar.

“Nothing serious.”

Jaina let out a breath.

Around them, the Jedi Knights regrouped.

Beyond the walls of the installation, Csilla’s civil war continued.

# Part 3

The NKAA did not regard the battle on Csilla as a defeat alone.

It regarded it as proof.

Across its broadcasts, the association repeated the same accusation: Jedi had entered Chiss space, fought Chiss citizens, and protected Jagged Fel from arrest.

“The Jedi Order has chosen a side in our internal conflict,” declared an NKAA speaker. “It has brought weapons into Chiss territory and raised lightsabers against those who defend the Ascendancy. It must learn that Chiss security cannot be dictated from outside.”

The leadership of the NKAA met in secret. At its center stood its chairman, **Vur’kesh’raun**, known to his followers as Vurkesh. He was an older Chiss with a narrow face and an unwavering conviction that caution had failed his people.

“Fel has returned under Jedi protection,” Vurkesh told his commanders. “The Jedi believe their academy is beyond consequence.”

A fleet was assembled.

The NKAA possessed no great shipyards of its own. Its capital ships were almost entirely vessels that had surrendered, defected, or been seized from Ascendancy government forces during the civil war. But it had enough Star Destroyers, escorts, and starfighters for a raid.

Its target was Shedu Maad.

The attack came without warning.

Sensors above the Jedi academy detected the first hyperspace disturbances only moments before NKAA Star Destroyers emerged from the darkness. Their wedge-shaped hulls turned toward the world below. Fighter bays opened.

“Hostile fleet!” the academy’s defense officer shouted. “All pilots to launch stations!”

At Shedu Maad’s landing fields, alarms sounded across the academy.

Jedi students and Masters ran for shelters or ships. Knights with pilot training raced toward waiting starfighters. Maintenance crews scrambled to arm vessels that had been intended for exercises, patrols, or simple transport.

The Millennium Falcon lifted from its berth in a roar of engines.

Han Solo sat in the pilot’s seat, hands moving across familiar controls. Leia Organa Solo took the co-pilot’s chair beside him.

“Five squadrons coming in fast,” Leia said.

Han leaned forward. “Then we’d better make them regret it.”

The Falcon tore into the sky.

Its first target was an NKAA fighter diving toward the academy’s outer hangars. Han rolled the freighter beneath it, then climbed sharply. Quad laser fire flashed across the fighter’s path.

The enemy ship exploded.

“That’s one,” Han said.

Leia checked the tactical display. “Three more on the northern approach.”

“Make that four.”

The Falcon turned again.

By the time Han and Leia had cut through the first attacking formation, five NKAA starfighters had been destroyed. But more continued descending from the Star Destroyers above.

Then Luke Skywalker launched.

At sixty-five, he still flew his X-wing with the controlled precision that had made him legendary. His ship cut through the defending Jedi fighters and into the attacking swarm.

“Master Skywalker is engaging,” a young pilot said over the comm.

Luke’s voice answered, calm and clear. “Stay with your wingmates. Don’t chase them into open space. Protect the academy.”

NKAA fighters tried to surround him.

Luke rolled between them, fired twice, and sent one spinning away with a damaged engine. He slipped through a burst of blasterfire that should have trapped him, then drove another fighter away from the atmosphere.

To the enemy pilots, he became a constant problem: an X-wing that refused to be cornered, appearing where their attack lines were weakest and vanishing before they could concentrate on him.

Jedi of every age joined the defense. Some had flown in wars. Others had learned only enough to patrol the skies around Shedu Maad. All of them launched.

But the Jedi could not easily answer the Star Destroyers.

The fighters could disrupt attack runs and defend the academy’s airspace. The Falcon could strike vulnerable fighters and evade nearly anything sent after it. Yet the capital ships remained above, their shields holding, their turbolasers continuing to threaten the world below.

Then a new fleet arrived.

The first Hapan Battle Dragon emerged from hyperspace with the vast, curved shape of a predator. Then came another. Then another, followed by a screen of Hapan starfighters.

At their center was the **Dragon Queen II**.

Tenel Ka Djo stood aboard her flagship’s command deck.

“Shedu Maad is within the Hapes Cluster,” she said. “This attack is an assault upon the Hapes Consortium.”

The Hapan fleet opened fire.

The NKAA Star Destroyers turned to meet it, but the balance of the battle had changed too quickly. Tenel Ka had brought a substantial portion of the Hapan fleet. The NKAA had brought every capital ship it could spare, but it had no factories producing replacements and no reserve force equal to the Battle Dragons now closing around it.

“Target their lead destroyer,” Tenel Ka ordered.

The Hapan ships moved with ruthless coordination.

Turbolaser fire crossed the void. Hapan starfighters swept through the NKAA formations, forcing enemy pilots away from the Jedi defense line. One Star Destroyer lost its shields beneath concentrated Battle Dragon fire. Another broke apart after a series of explosions ran along its hull.

Below, Jaina’s squadron returned from Chiss space.

Her StealthX dropped from hyperspace alongside Jag’s. Their strike team followed in their own fighters.

“Shedu Maad, this is Jaina Solo Fel,” Jaina said over the comm. “We are entering the battle.”

Jag’s voice came immediately after hers. “We have enemy fighters between the Hapan line and the academy. Let’s clear them.”

Jaina and Jag were among the Order’s finest StealthX pilots. They entered the fighting without hesitation.

Jaina’s purple blade of light was not in her hand now, but the Force guided her through the chaos as surely as any lightsaber duel. Her StealthX slipped past an incoming fighter, spun, and fired into its engines. The enemy craft burst apart.

Jag flew beside her, beskar armor no longer visible beneath his flight gear but his reflexes just as steady. He drew three fighters away from a damaged Jedi vessel, cut between them, and destroyed the lead ship before the others could react.

“Still refreshing to be back in Chiss affairs?” Jaina asked.

Jag’s answer came with the sound of weapons fire. “Ask me after this.”

Together, they destroyed fighter after fighter.

The NKAA fleet began to collapse.

The last Star Destroyer tried to withdraw, but Hapan Battle Dragons closed from two sides. Its shields failed. A final barrage struck its command tower, then its engines. The vessel broke apart in a widening field of fire.

What remained of the invading NKAA force fled.

Damaged fighters jumped for Chiss space. The few surviving escorts followed. Above Shedu Maad, the Hapan fleet held position while Jedi pilots returned to the academy.

The attack had failed.

Tenel Ka arrived at Shedu Maad shortly afterward.

She met Jaina on the landing platform, the wind pulling at both women’s robes.

“You came quickly,” Jaina said.

“Fact,” Tenel Ka replied. “The enemy attacked a Jedi academy in Hapan space. A response was required.”

Jag stepped forward. “You brought a great deal more than a response.”

Tenel Ka looked toward the departing Battle Dragons. “The NKAA required a decisive answer.”

The answer did not end with Shedu Maad.

Tenel Ka pledged Hapan support to the Jedi Order and the lawful government of the Chiss Ascendancy. Battle Dragons and Hapan starfighters joined Ascendancy fleets. The civil war shifted.

The NKAA still had soldiers, ships, and strongholds. But it no longer faced only the strained resources of the Ascendancy government. It faced Chiss forces reinforced by one of the galaxy’s most formidable fleets.

The stalemate began to break.

When Chiss intelligence finally uncovered the location of the NKAA headquarters, the government acted immediately.

The headquarters stood within a heavily fortified complex, protected by hardened walls, layered defenses, and troops who believed surrender would mean betrayal of everything they had fought for.

Jaina led the strike team.

Tenel Ka joined it personally.

Her turquoise lightsaber, mounted in a hilt of rancor tooth, ignited beside Jaina’s purple blade. The ten Jedi Knights formed around them. Jag moved among them in beskar armor and Mandalorian crushgaunts, blaster ready.

Elite Chiss soldiers secured the exterior. Ascendancy and Hapan ships provided air support, striking defensive emplacements and keeping NKAA reinforcements from reaching the complex.

Then the Jedi entered.

They fought corridor by corridor.

NKAA fighters fired from behind barricades. Jaina’s purple blade deflected bolts into walls and weapons. Tenel Ka advanced with the direct, uncompromising focus that had carried her through battles since youth. The Jedi Knights moved in pairs, clearing each passage while Jag covered angles their lightsabers could not reach.

“Left corridor!” Jag called.

A squad of NKAA troops rushed from a side passage.

Tenel Ka stepped forward. Her turquoise blade cut through the first rifle, then the second. Jaina drove the remaining fighters back with a wave of the Force.

“Move,” Jaina ordered.

They reached the central command level after hours of fighting.

The doors to the main office chamber stood sealed.

Jag examined the control panel. “Reinforced lock.”

“Can you open it?” Jaina asked.

Jag flexed one crushgaunt. “Probably.”

Tenel Ka raised an eyebrow. “Direct method.”

“Effective method.”

The beskar gauntlet struck the panel. Sparks flew. Jag hit it again. The lock failed.

The doors opened.

Vur’kesh’raun stood behind a broad command desk. Displays around him showed collapsing NKAA positions across Chiss space. He wore no armor. His hands rested openly on the desk.

Jaina entered first, purple lightsaber raised.

Tenel Ka stood beside her, turquoise blade pointed at Vurkesh.

“It is over,” Jaina said. “Surrender.”

Vurkesh looked from one blade to the other.

“The Jedi have come a long way to settle a Chiss matter,” he said.

“The Ascendancy asked us to help,” Jaina replied.

“The Ascendancy is weak.”

“The Ascendancy is still standing,” Tenel Ka said.

Vurkesh’s eyes moved toward Jag.

“And Fel returns beneath Jedi protection.”

“Fel returns because his exile was ended,” Jaina said. “By the lawful government of the Ascendancy.”

For a moment, Vurkesh said nothing.

Then he slowly raised his hands.

“I surrender.”

Jaina nodded to the Jedi Knights.

“Restrain him.”

Two Knights stepped forward.

The ambush began at once.

Hidden panels in the walls burst open. NKAA commandos poured into the chamber from every side. Blasterfire filled the room.

“Down!” Jag shouted.

Jaina threw herself toward the nearest Jedi Knight, knocking him behind the command desk as bolts tore through the air. Tenel Ka turned, her turquoise blade catching fire from two directions.

Vurkesh moved in the confusion.

A commando’s blaster bolt struck him before he could reach cover.

He fell beside his desk.

The battle became close and desperate.

Jaina’s purple blade cut through a commando’s rifle. Jag drove another back with blasterfire, then smashed the weapon from his hands with a crushgaunt. Tenel Ka held the center of the room, her lightsaber moving in swift arcs through the smoke.

Three Jedi Knights fell before the ambush was broken.

Two more were seriously injured.

But Jaina, Jag, and Tenel Ka remained standing.

The last NKAA commando fell. The chamber was silent except for the crackle of damaged consoles and the harsh breathing of the survivors.

Jaina looked toward Vurkesh.

He was dead.

So were every NKAA-affiliated person in the chamber.

“Evacuate,” Jaina said. “Now.”

The surviving strike team carried the wounded out of the headquarters. Chiss soldiers secured the complex behind them. Hapan and Ascendancy air cover kept the route clear as their transports lifted away.

They returned to Shedu Maad.

The injured Jedi Knights received immediate medical treatment. The three who had died were mourned by the Order, their names spoken in the academy’s halls.

Soon afterward, the remaining NKAA forces surrendered.

The surrender was unconditional.

The Never Killiks Again Association was formally disbanded under Ascendancy government oversight. Its ships were seized, its weapons secured, and its surviving leadership placed under Chiss authority.

With the civil war concluded, representatives of the Chiss Ascendancy, the Jedi Order, and the Hapes Consortium met to decide what came next.

Luke Skywalker sat with Jaina, Jag, Tenel Ka, and the Chiss representatives.

“The question remains Raynar Thul,” said one Chiss official.

“It does,” Luke replied.

“He remains with the Killiks.”

“He does.”

“And if he becomes UnuThul again?”

Luke’s expression was grave. “Then we will respond to what he does, not merely to what we fear he may do.”

Tenel Ka spoke next. “The Hapes Consortium will monitor the situation.”

“The Ascendancy will do the same,” said the Chiss official.

Jaina looked at Jag, then back to the table.

“The Jedi will watch Raynar and the Killiks,” she said. “If they become aggressive or hostile, action will be taken. But if they do not, Raynar will be left to live the life he has chosen.”

The agreement held.

Years passed.

Raynar Thul did not lead the Killiks against the Chiss. The Killiks did not attack Chiss worlds. The feared return of UnuThul did not become a new war.

Slowly, the anger that had fueled the NKAA began to fade.

Many Chiss who had once demanded that the Jedi remove Raynar by force came to accept that the status quo was stable. The Thuruht nest remained watched, but it was not treated as an enemy merely for existing.

The Jedi remained vigilant.

The Chiss remained cautious.

The Hapes Consortium remained prepared.

But the galaxy moved, at last, toward peace again.
