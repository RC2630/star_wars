# Episode III (After the Point of Divergence from Canon)

The fires of Mustafar never stopped moving.

Rivers of lava crawled beneath black volcanic rock, casting the mining complex in an endless glow of red and gold. Smoke drifted across the horizon in slow, suffocating waves.

Anakin Skywalker stood alone on the landing platform outside the Separatist command center.

His cloak shifted in the hot wind.

Behind him, the facility was silent now.

Everyone inside was dead.

For a long moment he simply stared out over the burning landscape, jaw tight, eyes hollow with exhaustion and something darker beneath it.

Then—

A distant engine hum.

Anakin looked upward.

A sleek silver vessel descended through the haze.

A Naboo skiff.

Padmé.

For the first time in what felt like days, something softened in his expression.

The ship landed gently on the platform near his Jedi starfighter.

Before the ramp had fully lowered, Anakin was already moving toward it.

Padmé hurried down to meet him.

“Padmé—”

She threw her arms around him immediately.

“Oh, Anakin!”

He held her tightly.

For a brief moment, Mustafar disappeared.

“It’s all right,” he said softly. “You’re safe now.”

He pulled back slightly, searching her face.

“What are you doing out here?”

Padmé looked at him carefully, relief and fear mixing together in her eyes.

“I was so worried about you. Obi-Wan told me terrible things.”

Something hardened in Anakin immediately.

“What things?”

“He said you’ve turned to the dark side.” Her voice trembled slightly. “That you killed younglings.”

Anakin looked away.

“Obi-Wan is trying to turn you against me.”

“He cares about us.”

Anakin’s eyes snapped back to her.

“Us?”

“He knows,” she said quietly. “He wants to help you.”

Anakin’s expression darkened further.

“Is Obi-Wan going to protect you? He can’t. He can’t help you. He’s not strong enough.”

Padmé stepped closer.

“Anakin, all I want is your love.”

He clenched his jaw.

“Love won’t save you, Padmé. Only my new powers can do that.”

“At what cost?” she asked. “You are a good person. Don’t do this.”

Emotion surged through him instantly.

“I won’t lose you the way I lost my mother!” he burst out. “I’ve become more powerful than any Jedi has ever dreamed of—and I’ve done it for you. To protect you.”

Padmé’s eyes glistened.

“Come away with me,” she pleaded softly. “Help me raise our child. Leave everything else behind while we still can.”

Anakin turned away sharply.

“Don’t you see? We don’t have to run away anymore.” He gestured outward toward the burning world beneath them. “I have brought peace to the Republic. I am more powerful than the Chancellor. I can overthrow him, and together you and I can rule the galaxy. Make things the way we want them to be.”

Padmé stared at him in disbelief.

“Please,” she whispered. “Listen to me.”

Anakin closed his eyes briefly.

“I can’t.” His voice cracked slightly. “Padmé… I really can’t.”

At that moment, a familiar series of electronic beeps interrupted the silence.

R2-D2 rolled down the skiff ramp toward them.

Anakin frowned faintly.

“Artoo?”

The little astromech stopped between them and gave a determined chirp.

Then a hologram flickered to life.

Blue light shimmered into the air.

Anakin froze.

Padmé looked up sharply.

The projection showed another time.

Another place.

A fire burning warmly within the lakeside retreat on Naboo.

Younger versions of themselves sat together in front of it.

Anakin watched himself enter the frame.

“May I tell you something?”

Padmé’s recorded image smiled faintly.

“I don’t know.”

“Then how can I tell you?”

“Maybe you should use your Jedi intuition.”

“It doesn’t work around you.” The younger Anakin laughed softly. “My mind is always a muddle… I can only think of you.”

The present-day Anakin’s face tightened.

The hologram continued.

“From the moment I met you, all those years ago, a day hasn’t gone by when I haven’t thought of you.”

The lava winds howled softly around them.

“And now that I’m close to you again, I’m in agony.”

Padmé slowly turned to look at the real Anakin standing beside her.

“The thought of not being with you makes my stomach turn over—my mouth goes dry. I feel dizzy. I can’t breathe…”

Anakin’s breathing became uneven.

“I’m haunted by the kiss you should never have given me.”

The holographic fire crackled.

“You are in my very soul, tormenting me.”

Silence.

Then the final line:

“What can I do? I will do anything you ask…”

The hologram faded.

Only the glow of Mustafar remained.

Anakin stared downward, unable to speak.

Padmé stepped closer again.

“Anakin,” she said gently, “you literally said… ‘I will do anything you ask.’”

He swallowed hard.

“But—”

“If you truly meant it back then,” she whispered, “now is the time to keep your word.”

The wind swept across the platform.

For several seconds, Anakin said nothing.

His face twisted with conflict.

Anger.
Fear.
Grief.
Exhaustion.

And beneath all of it—

love.

“You don’t understand,” he said weakly. “I did this to save you.”

“I never asked for this.”

His eyes shut tightly.

“You were dying.”

“And now you’re dying instead.”

The words hit him harder than any lightsaber strike ever could.

Padmé reached for his hand.

“Come back with me,” she said quietly. “Not to the Republic. Not to the war. Just… come back.”

Anakin looked at her.

Really looked at her.

Not as something to save.
Not as something to possess.
Not as the center of some grand destiny.

Just Padmé.

The woman he loved.

The woman who had crossed a galaxy to reach him even now.

Slowly, painfully, he nodded once.

“All right,” he said quietly.

Padmé exhaled shakily.

Relief washed across her face.

At that moment, a voice emerged from behind the skiff.

“Anakin.”

Anakin turned instantly.

Obi-Wan Kenobi stepped slowly down the ramp.

Anakin tensed reflexively—but stopped himself.

Obi-Wan looked exhausted. Saddened more than angry.

His gaze moved between both of them carefully.

“I’m not here to kill you,” Obi-Wan said immediately.

The tension eased slightly.

Anakin stared at him uncertainly.

For a long moment, nobody moved.

Then Obi-Wan spoke softly.

“Come with us.”

Anakin looked away.

“I don’t know if I can.”

“You can,” Padmé said.

Obi-Wan nodded slowly. “The Chancellor has manipulated you.”

At the mention of Palpatine, uncertainty flickered across Anakin’s face.

“He promised he could save her.”

“He lied,” Obi-Wan said gently.

Anakin’s jaw tightened again.

Before anyone could continue—

A new sound tore through the air.

A shuttle descending fast.

All three turned upward.

The black craft landed violently on the far side of the platform.

Its ramp lowered immediately.

Dark robes emerged through the steam.

Darth Sidious.

His yellow eyes fixed instantly on Anakin.

The atmosphere changed at once.

Cold.
Oppressive.
Wrong.

Sidious descended the ramp slowly.

“My apprentice,” he said, voice calm but dangerous. “You disappoint me.”

Anakin stepped subtly in front of Padmé.

Sidious noticed.

His expression darkened.

“You would abandon everything,” Sidious said softly, “for weakness.”

Padmé tightened her grip on Anakin’s arm.

Obi-Wan shifted into a guarded stance.

Anakin looked between them all.

And suddenly—

he saw it clearly.

The years of manipulation.
The lies.
The fear.
The isolation.

Every path Sidious offered led only here:
alone,
consumed,
forever trapped beneath his shadow.

Sidious extended a hand.

“Come,” he commanded. “We have much left to accomplish.”

Anakin didn’t move.

Sidious’s eyes narrowed.

“She turns you against your destiny.”

“No,” Anakin said quietly.

Sidious paused.

Anakin stepped backward toward Padmé instead.

“She’s the only thing that ever made me remember who I was.”

The air seemed to darken.

Sidious’s expression twisted into fury.

“So be it.”

With a vicious SNAP-HISS, his crimson blade ignited.

Padmé gasped softly.

Obi-Wan instantly drew his own lightsaber.

But before Sidious could move—

another blade ignited beside him.

Blue.

Anakin stood facing the Emperor directly.

Lightsaber trembling slightly in his hand.

But held firm.

Sidious stared at him in disbelief.

For the first time in many years—

Anakin Skywalker was pointing his weapon at the Sith Lord.

“You were right about one thing,” Anakin said quietly.

Sidious’s eyes narrowed.

“What is that?”

Anakin stepped fully beside Obi-Wan.

“You are the enemy.”

Behind them, Padmé slowly drew the blaster hidden beneath her cloak.

Sidious’s face contorted with rage.

Then he attacked.

And the duel began.

—

Lightning exploded from Sidious’s hands, tearing across the platform. Anakin and Obi-Wan barely deflected it, staggering under the sheer force. They pressed forward together—precise, practiced, unified.

For a time, it worked.

Anakin’s power, Obi-Wan’s discipline—they forced Sidious back step by step. Sparks flew. Lava spat into the air. The Force itself seemed to twist under the strain.

From behind them, Padmé fired—controlled, deliberate shots. Not reckless, not wild—each one calculated to disrupt, to create openings.

Sidious adapted.

He always did.

With a sudden surge, he shattered their rhythm. A blast of Force energy hurled Obi-Wan into a column of black rock. Anakin charged alone—rage and resolve colliding—but Sidious met him head-on.

“Still so easy,” Sidious hissed, locking blades with him. “Still so predictable.”

A violent pulse of the Force erupted.

Anakin was thrown hard against the ground. His lightsaber skidded away.

Obi-Wan tried to rise—too slow.

Sidious lifted a hand.

The pressure was invisible, but crushing. Both men froze, pinned by a power neither could break.

“Such potential,” Sidious said, almost regretful. “Wasted.”

He raised his other hand, lightning gathering—

A shot rang out.

Sidious jerked, the energy sputtering. A smoking mark scorched across his shoulder.

Padmé stood behind them, blaster steady.

For the first time, Sidious looked… annoyed.

Another shot. And another.

He deflected the next, but it bought seconds—just enough.

The pressure lifted.

“Enough,” Sidious snarled.

The air twisted violently. Heat surged. The ground cracked.

And then—

He was gone.

—

Padmé dropped to her knees beside Anakin.

“Anakin—Anakin, stay with me—”

Obi-Wan groaned nearby, struggling upright.

They were alive.

But something was wrong.

Anakin reached for the Force—and felt… silence. Not absence, but distance. Like trying to grasp something just out of reach.

Obi-Wan felt it too.

“What has he done to us…” he murmured.

Padmé didn’t care.

She helped them both to their feet.

“We’re leaving,” she said.

—

The Empire rose anyway.

From Coruscant, Palpatine—Darth Sidious—declared the Jedi traitors and the Republic reorganized into the First Galactic Empire. Order 66 had already done its work. Across the galaxy, the Jedi were gone.

Almost.

On a distant world, far from the Senate and its lies, three survivors began again.

Padmé gave birth to twins: Luke and Leia.

She lived.

Anakin held them both, his expression torn between awe and something quieter—something heavier. Guilt that would never fully leave him.

“I thought I lost everything,” he said softly.

“You didn’t,” Padmé replied. “You came back.”

Obi-Wan stood nearby, watching. He had forgiven Anakin—but he had not forgotten. Neither had Anakin.

And neither would let the galaxy forget what had happened.

—

Years passed.

The rebellion did not begin as a war. It began as whispers—then networks, then alliances. Padmé moved through it like a current, uniting senators, systems, and survivors. Obi-Wan advised. Anakin trained what few Force-sensitive allies they could find.

But neither of them fought.

Sidious had taken that from them.

When they reached for the Force in battle, it slipped, fractured, failed them. The cost of Mustafar lingered—permanent, unforgiving.

So they adapted.

They became teachers.

Guides.

Symbols.

—

And Sidious was not idle.

At his side stood a new apprentice: **Darth Virex**.

Where Vader might have been rage and fire, Virex was precision and cold ambition. Powerful—terrifyingly so. Not Anakin’s equal in raw potential, perhaps, but close enough to matter.

Together, Sidious and Virex hunted what remained.

The Empire tightened its grip.

The Death Star began its silent construction.

And the war stretched on.

—

Luke and Leia grew up in the shadow of it all.

Leia, sharp and unyielding, took after her mother—politics, strategy, leadership. But the Force never answered her. Not once. It passed her by like a closed door.

Luke was different.

He felt it early—faint at first, then undeniable. Instincts he couldn’t explain. Reflexes that saved him before danger struck.

Anakin noticed.

He said nothing at first.

When Luke was old enough, Obi-Wan approached him.

They stood at the edge of a quiet field, far from the war.

“There’s something inside you,” Obi-Wan said. “Something your father once had.”

Luke glanced back toward the distant homestead. “Had?”

Anakin stood there, watching. Silent.

Obi-Wan followed Luke’s gaze.

“He still does,” he said. “Just… not in the way he once did.”

Luke turned back. “Can you teach me?”

Obi-Wan smiled faintly.

“Yes,” he said. “I can.”

Behind them, Anakin lowered his eyes—not in shame this time, but in acceptance.

The next generation would carry what he no longer could.

And somewhere, in the depths of the Empire, Darth Sidious watched the galaxy shift—and prepared for what was coming.

# Episode IV

The war had learned patience.

Years after Mustafar, the Empire no longer struck wildly—it chose its targets with intent. And when Darth Sidious chose Naboo, it was not for strategy alone.

It was personal.

—

### **A Spark in the Dark**

A small Rebel corvette tore through space, engines straining.

Inside, alarms blared.

“They’ve locked on!” a pilot shouted.

Through the viewport, a wedge-shaped shadow swallowed the stars—an Imperial Star Destroyer. And beyond it, looming like a cold moon, something worse.

The Death Star.

On the bridge stood **Darth Virex**, still as stone, his voice low.

“Disable them. I want what they’re carrying intact.”

Tractor beams engaged. The corvette shuddered—and was pulled in.

Moments later, stormtroopers flooded the corridors.

Blaster fire. Smoke. Chaos.

A small astromech droid—**R2-D2**—rolled quickly through the confusion, accompanied by a nervous protocol droid.

“Are you sure this is a good idea?” **C-3PO** protested.

R2-D2 beeped insistently.

Hidden within R2’s memory: **partial schematics of the Death Star**, stolen at great cost—and something else.

A transmission.

A call for help.

—

### **Naboo**

Naboo had not forgotten how to be peaceful.

Green fields stretched under quiet skies. Lakes shimmered. And far from the capital, tucked among rolling hills, stood a modest homestead.

Luke Skywalker adjusted a vaporator, wiping sweat from his brow.

“You’re doing it wrong,” Leia called from the porch.

Luke glanced up. “Oh, and you’re suddenly an expert?”

“I’m an expert at watching you mess it up.”

He smirked. “That still counts?”

Before she could reply, a familiar hum echoed.

Obi-Wan Kenobi approached, robes brushing the grass.

“Luke,” he said. “Walk with me.”

Anakin stood in the distance, watching.

—

### **The Message**

That night, R2-D2 projected a flickering hologram.

A Rebel officer—wounded, urgent.

“To any surviving Jedi… the Empire has completed a planet-destroying weapon. We have partial schematics, but not enough. Naboo is the target. Repeat—Naboo is the target.”

Silence filled the room.

Padmé’s hand tightened.

“They’re coming here,” Leia said quietly.

Anakin closed his eyes.

“I know.”

—

### **Han Solo**

Help came from an unexpected direction.

A battered freighter dropped out of hyperspace just beyond Naboo’s orbit—the **Millennium Falcon**.

Inside, **Han Solo** leaned back in his chair, grinning.

“Not my usual kind of job,” he said. “Smuggling information into a war zone.”

Chewbacca growled.

“Yeah, yeah. Big money, big risk. Same story.”

But when he landed and met the people behind the job—the former Queen of Naboo, the Jedi who ended the Clone Wars, the man whispered about in Imperial fear—his smirk faded just a little.

“You’re serious,” Han said. “They’re actually building something that can destroy a planet.”

Leia met his gaze. “They’re not building it. They’re here.”

Han hesitated.

Then: “Alright. I’m in.”

—

### **The First Attempt**

The Death Star moved into position.

On its surface, energy gathered—slow, terrible.

On Naboo, alarms rang.

“Evacuations are underway,” a Rebel commander reported.

Padmé shook her head. “We can’t evacuate a planet.”

Obi-Wan stepped forward. “Then we delay them.”

The beam fired.

A streak of green fury—

—and then it faltered.

Mid-flight, it destabilized, scattering into the atmosphere in a harmless cascade of light.

Cheers erupted.

But Anakin didn’t smile.

“They’ll fix it,” he said.

—

### **The Second Attempt**

They did.

This time, Rebel starfighters launched preemptively. X-wings, Y-wings—everything they had.

“Target the focusing array!” a pilot shouted.

Blaster fire rained against the Death Star’s surface. One critical dish shattered just as the weapon fired.

The beam veered—missing Naboo entirely.

But the cost was high.

Many pilots didn’t return.

—

### **The Third Attempt**

“Why isn’t it working?” an Imperial officer demanded.

Darth Virex answered calmly. “Because we are allowing interference.”

Sidious stood behind him, silent.

“Then remove it,” Sidious said.

The next attack was brutal.

TIE fighters swarmed. Rebel defenses crumbled.

The beam charged again.

On Naboo, the sky itself seemed to hold its breath.

—

### **A Plan Born of Desperation**

In a dim command room, Luke stared at the schematics.

“There,” he said, pointing. “The beam isn’t just one blast—it’s a convergence. Multiple energy streams focusing through a central axis.”

Obi-Wan nodded. “Go on.”

“If we disrupt it precisely—at the right moment—we could redirect it.”

Han blinked. “Redirect a planet-killer?”

“Not redirect,” Luke said. “Reflect.”

Silence.

“That’s impossible,” a pilot muttered.

Anakin stepped forward.

“No,” he said quietly. “It isn’t.”

All eyes turned to him.

“The beam has to maintain perfect alignment,” Anakin continued. “Even a slight deviation—if timed exactly—could cause it to collapse back along its own path.”

Han crossed his arms. “And you’re just going to… eyeball that?”

Luke met his father’s gaze.

“I won’t be guessing.”

—

### **The Final Attempt**

The Death Star loomed larger than ever.

Its weapon charged—steady, stable, unstoppable.

Rebel ships launched in full force.

“Red Squadron, lock in!” Luke’s voice rang over comms.

Han’s Falcon roared beside them.

“Kid, if this works, I’m never letting you forget it.”

“Just keep them off me.”

TIE fighters swarmed.

Laser fire lit the void.

Luke flew straight toward the firing axis.

“Stay on target…” Obi-Wan’s voice echoed in his mind.

The beam fired.

A column of green annihilation—

At that exact instant, Luke fired a precise shot into a critical node along the focusing ring. Simultaneously, coordinated Rebel strikes hit secondary points—each one calculated, each one timed.

For a heartbeat, nothing happened.

Then—

The beam bent.

Not outward.

Back.

Energy collapsed inward, ricocheting through the weapon’s own conduits. Systems overloaded in a chain reaction.

On the Death Star’s surface, Darth Virex turned sharply.

“Master—”

Sidious’s eyes narrowed.

“Impossible…”

The station began to tear itself apart.

“Evacuate,” Virex said.

Sidious did not move.

For a moment, he simply watched.

Then, with a flicker of dark power, he vanished.

—

### **The End of the Death Star**

The Death Star exploded in a silent, blinding bloom.

Fragments scattered across space.

On Naboo, the sky cleared.

The planet still stood.

—

### **Aftermath**

Celebration spread across the Rebel ranks—but it was different from the stories of old victories.

Harder-earned.

More fragile.

Han leaned against the Falcon, shaking his head. “I take it back. That was insane.”

Leia smiled faintly. “And it worked.”

Across the field, Luke approached Anakin.

For a moment, neither spoke.

Then Luke said, “I felt it. The timing. The alignment. It was like… everything clicked.”

Anakin nodded.

“The Force,” he said. “Trust it.”

Luke hesitated. “Did I… do it right?”

Anakin placed a hand on his shoulder.

“You did better than I ever could have.”

Obi-Wan watched from a distance, a quiet smile on his face.

—

Far away, in the shadows of the Empire, Darth Sidious stood beside Darth Virex.

“The boy,” Sidious murmured. “Skywalker’s son.”

Virex inclined his head. “A threat.”

Sidious’s smile returned—thin, cold.

“A possibility.”

The war was far from over.

# Episode V

The victory over the Death Star did not end the war.

It sharpened it.

—

### **A Galaxy Under Pressure**

The Empire struck back with precision and fury.

Without the Death Star, it turned to overwhelming force—Star Destroyers blockading systems, legions deployed across contested worlds. And always, always, Naboo remained in its sights.

Green fields burned under orbital bombardment. Cities endured siege after siege.

“Evacuate,” advisors urged.

Padmé Amidala stood firm in the Theed palace, unshaken. “Naboo is my home. It will remain my home.”

Anakin stood beside her.

“Then I stay too.”

He could not fight as he once had. The Force still answered him—but not in battle, not in the way it mattered. Mustafar had taken that from him.

So he became something else.

A leader.

—

### **The Rebel Command**

In a hidden command center beneath Naboo’s surface, Anakin addressed a gathered group—pilots, operatives, smugglers.

Including **Han Solo**, leaning casually against a console, and **Leia**, arms crossed beside him.

“We can’t outgun them,” Anakin said. “So we outmaneuver them. Hit supply lines. Disrupt communications. Stay unpredictable.”

Han smirked. “Unpredictable I can do.”

Leia shot him a look. “Try ‘reliable’ for once.”

“Hey, I’m both.”

Chewbacca growled in agreement.

Anakin allowed a faint smile. “You’ll take the Falcon. Recon and extraction. Leia—you’re with him.”

Leia nodded without hesitation.

Their partnership had become constant—missions, arguments, near-death escapes. Somewhere in between, something else had started to form.

Neither of them said it yet.

—

### **Luke’s Training**

Far from the front lines, Luke trained.

Under Obi-Wan’s watchful eye, his connection to the Force deepened. He moved with more certainty now, less doubt.

Still, there were gaps.

“You’re reaching,” Obi-Wan said as Luke attempted a difficult maneuver. “But you’re still holding on.”

“To what?”

Obi-Wan studied him. “Everything.”

Luke exhaled. “That’s not helpful.”

“It’s the truth.”

—

### **The Hunter Arrives**

They came without warning.

A ripple in the Force.

Obi-Wan stiffened. “Luke—run.”

But it was too late.

A transport descended from the clouds. From it emerged **Darth Virex**, flanked by stormtroopers. And behind him—

A presence colder than space.

Darth Sidious.

Luke ignited his lightsaber.

Virex stepped forward, calm, deliberate. “You’ve grown.”

Luke didn’t answer. He attacked.

The clash was immediate—and uneven.

Virex was relentless. Each strike precise, efficient. Luke held his ground—but barely.

Behind them, Sidious watched, amused.

“Promising,” he murmured.

Luke faltered.

Virex moved in for the final blow—

Obi-Wan stepped between them.

The blade struck.

For a moment, everything froze.

Luke’s eyes widened. “No—”

Obi-Wan met his gaze, calm even now.

“Go.”

Virex withdrew his blade.

Obi-Wan collapsed—but not entirely.

His form shimmered, fading.

“Trust the Force, Luke,” he said.

And then he was gone.

—

### **Escape and Loss**

Luke ran.

Not out of cowardice—but because Obi-Wan had given him no other choice.

Behind him, Sidious’s voice echoed faintly.

“Let him go.”

Virex turned slightly. “My master?”

“He will lead us to more.”

—

### **A Ghost’s Warning**

Back on Naboo, Luke found Anakin.

“He’s gone,” Luke said, voice hollow. “Obi-Wan… he sacrificed himself.”

Anakin closed his eyes.

For a long moment, he said nothing.

“I want you to train me,” Luke continued. “Finish what he started.”

Before Anakin could answer, the air shifted.

A familiar presence.

Obi-Wan’s voice—gentle, distant.

“No.”

Luke turned. “Why?”

“Because your bond is too strong,” Obi-Wan said. “Attachment clouds judgment. It already has… once.”

Anakin stiffened.

“There is another,” Obi-Wan continued. “On Dagobah. Yoda.”

Luke hesitated.

Anakin stepped forward. “He’s right.”

Luke looked at him. “You’re sending me away?”

“I’m sending you where you need to go.”

—

### **Dagobah**

Dagobah was… not what Luke expected.

Swamps. Fog. Decay.

And a small, strange creature.

“Train you, I will,” Yoda said, peering up at him. “But difficult it will be.”

Luke nodded. “I’m ready.”

Yoda chuckled. “We shall see.”

—

### **The Falcon**

Meanwhile, the war dragged on.

Han and Leia flew mission after mission aboard the Millennium Falcon.

Close calls became routine.

Arguments became… something else.

“You’re impossible,” Leia snapped after a narrow escape.

“And you’re still here,” Han shot back.

She stepped closer. “Don’t get any ideas.”

“Too late.”

A pause.

Then—

A kiss.

Brief. Unexpected.

But real.

Neither of them mentioned it afterward.

—

### **The Empire Advances**

The Empire adapted.

A new project began—larger, more secure.

**A second Death Star.**

And Naboo remained under siege.

Orbital strikes. Ground assaults. Relentless pressure.

Still, Padmé refused to leave.

“This is where we stand,” she said.

Anakin stood with her.

—

### **Trials on Dagobah**

Luke’s training intensified.

“You must unlearn what you have learned,” Yoda told him.

Luke struggled—with patience, with fear, with the pull of everything he’d left behind.

Visions came.

Pain.

Loss.

And always, the sense that the war was slipping further from their grasp.

—

### **A Dark Confrontation**

It came suddenly.

A disturbance.

Luke stepped into a clearing—and there they were.

Sidious.

Virex.

“You’ve improved,” Virex said.

Luke ignited his saber.

This time, he lasted longer.

But not long enough.

He was disarmed, forced to his knees.

Sidious approached slowly.

“Your father failed me,” he said. “Perhaps you will not.”

Lightning gathered—

Then stopped.

Sidious tilted his head.

“No,” he said softly. “Not yet.”

He stepped back.

“Let him grow.”

Virex lowered his blade.

Luke remained, shaken—but alive.

—

And somewhere in the Force—

Obi-Wan watched.

Waiting.

---

# Episode VI

The war had lasted long enough that hope no longer arrived as a surge—it endured as a choice.

—

### **Dagobah: The Last Lesson**

Mist curled low over the swamps.

Luke knelt beside Yoda, whose breathing had grown shallow, each word now a careful effort.

“Strong you have become,” Yoda said softly. “Finish your training, you have.”

Luke lowered his head. “I won’t fail.”

Yoda’s eyes rested on him, ancient and knowing. “Already, you have faced failure. Learned from it… you did.”

A pause.

“Pass on what you have learned,” Yoda continued. “The Force… runs strong in your family.”

Luke hesitated. “Master… is there anything more I need to know?”

Yoda’s gaze flickered, distant.

“Only this,” he said. “The end… is near.”

He closed his eyes.

The Force shifted—gently, like a breath released.

Yoda was gone.

For a moment, there was only silence.

Then a familiar presence.

Obi-Wan.

“Your training is complete,” his voice said.

Luke stood slowly.

“Then it’s time,” he replied.

—

### **Return to Naboo**

Naboo still burned—but it had not fallen.

Luke returned to find the war sharper, tighter, closing toward something final.

Anakin stood at the command center, older now, quieter.

Padmé beside him—unchanged in resolve.

Leia and Han nearby, their easy arguments now softened by something deeper, steadier.

“The Empire is consolidating,” Leia reported. “Everything points to a single objective.”

“The second Death Star,” Luke said.

Anakin met his eyes. “Then we end it.”

—

### **The Plan**

The Rebel Alliance gathered its remaining strength.

The Death Star was not yet complete—but it was operational.

Worse, intelligence confirmed what many feared:

**Darth Sidious himself was aboard.**

“It’s a trap,” Han said immediately.

“Of course it is,” Leia replied.

“That doesn’t mean we don’t spring it.”

The plan formed quickly:

* A strike team would infiltrate the forest moon of **Endor** and disable the shield generator protecting the Death Star
* The Rebel fleet would engage the Imperial forces in orbit
* A small team would infiltrate the station itself

Luke stepped forward.

“I’m going to the Emperor.”

Silence.

Anakin frowned. “Alone?”

Luke shook his head.

“No.”

He looked at them—both of them.

“We finish this together.”

—

### **Endor**

The forest moon was alive—dense, watchful.

Han, Leia, Chewbacca, and a strike team moved through the undergrowth, searching for the shield generator.

They weren’t alone.

Small figures watched from the trees.

**Ewoks.**

Primitive—but not foolish.

At first, they captured the group.

Then, through a mix of translation, patience, and a well-timed display of diplomacy (and a little luck), an alliance formed.

“We could use them,” Han admitted.

Leia smiled faintly. “They’re already using us.”

—

### **The Assault Begins**

In orbit, the Rebel fleet arrived.

Star Destroyers moved to intercept.

At the center of it all, the unfinished Death Star waited.

“Commence attack,” came the order.

Fighters launched.

The battle began.

—

### **Into the Lion’s Den**

A stolen shuttle slipped through the chaos.

Aboard it: Luke, Anakin, and Padmé.

No one spoke as the Death Star filled the viewport.

“Once we’re inside,” Padmé said, checking her blaster, “there’s no turning back.”

Anakin looked at her. “There never was.”

They landed.

Moved quickly.

Through sterile corridors, past distracted guards, up—always up.

Toward the throne room.

—

### **The Throne Room**

The doors opened.

Darkness.

Then—

The Emperor.

Darth Sidious stood before the viewport, hands clasped behind him.

At his side: **Darth Virex**.

Sidious did not turn.

“I wondered,” he said, “if you would come.”

Luke stepped forward.

“This ends now.”

Sidious chuckled softly.

“Oh, it has already begun.”

He turned.

His gaze fell on Anakin.

“And you,” he said. “The one who almost was.”

Anakin said nothing.

Virex stepped forward, igniting his blade.

“Shall I, my master?”

Sidious inclined his head.

The room exploded into motion.

—

### **The Final Duel**

Virex struck first—fast, precise.

Luke met him.

Their blades clashed, light and dark colliding in a storm of motion.

Anakin moved to assist—but faltered.

The Force resisted him.

Just as it always had since Mustafar.

Virex pressed Luke back.

Sidious raised a hand—

Lightning surged toward them.

Padmé fired, forcing him to shift, buying seconds.

“Anakin!” she shouted.

He closed his eyes.

For years, the Force had been fractured for him—unreachable in battle, slipping away when he needed it most.

Fear had broken it.

Guilt had locked it away.

He had accepted that.

Until now.

He looked at Luke.

At Padmé.

Everything he had once tried to control… now simply was.

No fear.

No grasping.

Just presence.

The Force answered.

Fully.

For the first time since Mustafar.

Anakin ignited his saber.

The sound cut through everything.

Virex turned—

Too late.

Anakin entered the fight.

Not as he had been—but as something steadier.

Balanced.

The tide shifted.

Together, father and son drove Virex back.

Strike by strike.

Until—

Luke disarmed him.

Anakin followed through.

A final, decisive blow.

Darth Virex fell.

—

### **The Emperor’s End**

Sidious did not retreat.

He smiled.

“Good,” he said. “Now… you understand.”

Lightning erupted—far stronger than before.

It slammed into Luke.

He cried out, collapsing under the force of it.

Padmé fired again, but Sidious deflected the bolts effortlessly.

Anakin stepped forward—absorbing part of the storm, pushing through it.

“Still clinging,” Sidious hissed.

“Still blind.”

The lightning intensified.

Luke struggled to rise.

Padmé moved beside him, pulling him back.

Anakin reached Sidious.

Their hands met—

Light and dark colliding directly.

For a moment, neither moved.

Then—

Anakin surged forward.

Not with rage.

With clarity.

He seized Sidious and drove him backward—toward the open shaft behind the throne.

Sidious’s eyes widened—not in fear, but in realization.

“You have learned,” he said.

Anakin didn’t answer.

He pushed.

Sidious fell.

His scream echoed into the depths.

Gone.

—

### **Escape**

Alarms blared instantly.

“Reactor critical,” a voice echoed.

Outside, the shield dropped—the Endor team had succeeded.

The Rebel fleet pressed the attack.

Inside, Luke staggered to his feet.

“We have to go.”

They ran.

Corridors collapsing, systems failing.

Padmé led—blaster clearing their path.

Anakin supported Luke.

They reached the hangar.

A shuttle—barely intact.

“Go!” Padmé shouted.

They boarded.

The hangar exploded behind them as they lifted off.

—

### **The Death Star Falls**

Outside, the battle reached its peak.

“Target the core!” came the call.

Fighters dove.

The Falcon at the lead.

Han’s voice crackled over comms. “I’ve got a bad feeling about this.”

Leia, beside him, smiled. “You always do.”

They flew into the superstructure.

Through fire and debris.

One shot.

Perfect.

The core ignited.

—

### **End**

The shuttle burst free of the station just as the Death Star erupted into light.

The explosion rolled across space—brilliant, final.

The Empire’s greatest weapon—gone.

Its master—gone.

Its enforcer—gone.

—

### **Aftermath**

On Endor, celebration spread.

Rebels. Ewoks. Survivors.

Victory—not easy, not clean—but real.

Luke stood beside Anakin and Padmé.

For once, there was no shadow between them.

“You did it,” Luke said.

Anakin shook his head.

“We did.”

Padmé took both their hands.

“For the galaxy,” she said.

“And for us.”

Above them, the stars felt lighter.

And in the Force—

There was balance.

# Epilogue

Peace did not arrive all at once.

It came slowly—unevenly—like dawn through smoke.

—

### **A Galaxy Without an Empire**

The fall of the Empire left behind silence… and ruin.

Worlds long oppressed now stood free—but broken. Trade routes had collapsed. Governments had vanished overnight. Entire systems, once held together by fear, now struggled to remember how to govern themselves.

The Rebels—no longer merely rebels—stood at a crossroads.

On Coruscant, in a chamber that had once echoed with the rigid authority of the Republic and then the Empire, delegates gathered.

“What replaces this?” one voice demanded. “We can’t leave a vacuum.”

“The Republic failed,” another argued. “That’s what led us here.”

Eyes turned, almost instinctively, toward Padmé Amidala.

She stood calmly, hands folded, her voice measured but firm.

“Centralization was the flaw,” she said. “Too much power, in too few hands, too far removed from the people it governed.”

A murmur rippled through the chamber.

“We don’t rebuild the Republic,” she continued. “We build something new.”

—

### **The Galactic Union**

Padmé’s vision was radical—and, to many, necessary.

Each planet would govern itself. Local systems would retain autonomy over their laws, cultures, and leadership. A **Galactic Council** would exist—not to rule, but to coordinate.

“No single leader,” Padmé insisted. “No throne. No chancellor. Only delegates—one from each world. Decisions made together, or not at all.”

“What about enforcement?” someone asked.

“Shared responsibility,” she replied. “Agreed upon, not imposed.”

It was not perfect.

But perfection had never survived reality.

After long debate, the proposal was accepted.

The **Galactic Union** was born.

—

### **Rebuilding the Ruins**

Aid spread outward from the core worlds.

Food. Materials. Medical support.

But no world bore the scars more deeply than Naboo.

Years of siege had left cities fractured, landscapes scarred, lives uprooted.

And yet—

It endured.

When the people of Naboo gathered, their voices were unified.

They wanted their Queen back.

Padmé did not hesitate.

“I never truly left,” she said.

—

### **The Queen Returns**

Theed Palace rose again—stone by stone, arch by arch—rebuilt not just as a symbol, but as a promise.

When Padmé took her place within it once more, it was not as a figurehead of ceremony, but as a leader ready to rebuild from the ground up.

At her side stood Anakin.

Not as a general.

Not as a Jedi.

But as her consort.

He had felt the Force surge through him one final time during the battle with Sidious—but it had faded again, permanently this time. What remained was quieter, but no less real.

He had found his place.

And he did not intend to leave it.

—

### **Coruscant and the Road Not Taken**

There had been calls—many of them.

“Anakin Skywalker should lead,” some argued. “He ended the Sith.”

Anakin refused.

“My place is here,” he said simply.

Then they turned to Luke.

“You are the future of the Jedi,” they told him. “The galaxy needs a figure like you.”

Luke shook his head.

“The galaxy needs balance,” he said. “Not another center of power.”

It was after that impasse that Padmé’s decentralized vision gained momentum.

And the idea of a singular ruler… quietly faded.

Coruscant remained the seat of the Galactic Council.

But it was no longer the heart that controlled everything.

It was only one voice among many.

—

### **The Last of the Jedi**

The cost of Order 66 lingered.

The Jedi Order—once thousands strong—was gone.

Yoda had passed. Obi-Wan had become one with the Force.

Anakin could no longer wield it.

That left one.

Luke Skywalker.

He did not try to rebuild what had been.

He tried to build what should have been.

—

### **Dagobah: A New Beginning**

On Dagobah, where Yoda had lived and died, Luke established a new Jedi academy.

It began with almost nothing.

No grand temple. No archives of endless knowledge.

Just one teacher.

And a belief that the Force could still guide others.

Students came slowly at first. Then more.

Younglings from distant systems. Survivors. Seekers.

Luke trained them all.

Patiently.

Carefully.

And differently.

—

### **A New Jedi Philosophy**

One evening, beneath the thick canopy, Luke spoke with Anakin.

“The old rules…” Luke said. “They weren’t all wrong. But they weren’t all right either.”

Anakin nodded. “They feared attachment.”

“And you were destroyed by it,” Luke said quietly.

Anakin didn’t deny it.

Luke looked out into the swamp.

“But I don’t think the answer is to forbid it,” he continued. “I think it’s to understand it.”

No more absolute prohibition.

No more rigid detachment.

The new Jedi would learn balance—not by denying connection, but by mastering it.

It was a risk.

But so had everything worth doing.

—

### **Family on Naboo**

Life, at last, began to resemble something like peace.

Leia remained on Naboo, learning under Padmé’s guidance.

Politics. Diplomacy. Leadership.

One day, she would rule.

Han Solo remained at her side.

Still gruff. Still irreverent.

Still very much Han.

He clashed with palace staff more than once.

“Captain Solo, you cannot park the Falcon *there*—”

“Watch me.”

But over time, the palace adjusted.

They knew who he was.

What he had done.

And who he loved.

Their marriage was not grand—but it was genuine.

Chewbacca, too, made Naboo his home.

A special chamber within the palace was built just for him—though he preferred the open air whenever possible.

—

### **The Millennium Falcon**

The Falcon never stopped flying.

Though the war was over, there were still problems—smugglers, rogue elements, those who hadn’t accepted the new order.

Padmé occasionally called on Han, Leia, and Chewbacca.

“Just a small matter,” she would say.

Han would grin. “Those are always the worst kind.”

They handled them anyway.

And when the Falcon rested, it became something else.

A symbol.

Visitors came to see it—to stand inside the ship that had helped save the galaxy.

Han pretended to hate that part.

He didn’t.

—

### **A Living Legacy**

Luke visited when he could.

But his place was on Dagobah.

Anakin remained on Naboo.

Sometimes he would stand at the palace balcony, watching the horizon.

Not as the man he had been.

Not as the man he almost became.

But as the man who stayed.

Padmé would join him.

“Do you ever think about it?” she asked once.

“The past?” he said.

“The other path.”

Anakin was quiet for a long moment.

Then he shook his head.

“No,” he said. “I think about this one.”

He took her hand.

Below them, Naboo lived again.

And across the galaxy, something new—fragile, imperfect, but real—began to take shape.

Not an empire.

Not a republic.

A union.

And for the first time in a long time—

The future was open.

# Additional Details

For twenty-three years, the Skywalkers lived in hiding.

Not in palaces.

Not in senatorial towers.

Not beneath banners or titles.

But in a secluded residence buried deep within the remote regions of Naboo—far from major cities, shielded from orbital scans, hidden beneath thick terrain and careful concealment systems assembled gradually by Rebel engineers and Naboo loyalists.

It was never meant to be permanent.

And yet, over time, it became home.

—

### **The Hidden Years**

Padmé had once lived in grandeur.

First within the elegant halls of Theed Palace during her years as Queen. Later, among the towering skylines of Coruscant as a senator. Occasionally she escaped to the peaceful retreat of Varykino Villa.

But after Mustafar, none of those places were safe.

When she brought the injured Anakin and Obi-Wan back to Naboo, they understood immediately:
they could never openly return to public life while the Empire hunted them.

So they disappeared.

The Empire soon seized Naboo’s formal government, removing the existing monarch from Theed Palace and installing an Imperial-aligned ruler in their place.

The people of Naboo answered with revolt.

The imposed monarch was overthrown.

But afterward, no replacement was chosen.

Everyone understood the danger. Any visible ruler would immediately become a target for Imperial retaliation—or assassination.

So Naboo adapted.

Local governance fractured into regional councils and decentralized civic networks. Communities largely managed themselves while quietly supporting the growing rebellion.

The Empire never forgave them for it.

Especially not when rumors spread that Padmé Amidala herself still lived somewhere on the planet.

—

### **A Planet Under Siege**

The punishment came in waves.

First, the Death Star targeted Naboo repeatedly.

Then, after its destruction, came years of conventional assaults:
orbital bombardments,
blockades,
ground invasions,
sieges.

Naboo suffered terribly.

Yet somehow—

the hidden residence remained undiscovered.

Imperial intelligence searched relentlessly for Anakin Skywalker, Padmé Amidala, Obi-Wan Kenobi, and eventually Luke.

They never found them.

Over the years, the secluded residence evolved from hiding place into something larger:
an unofficial Rebel command center.

Pilots came and went quietly.

Encrypted transmissions passed through hidden underground rooms.

Supply caches accumulated beneath the surrounding terrain.

And within it all, life continued.

—

### **Living Together**

The quarters were modest.

Tight by royal standards.
Small by palace standards.
But after years, none of them cared.

They adapted.

Shared meals.
Shared hallways.
Shared mornings.

Luke and Leia grew up not as distant royal children, but as siblings raised side by side within a constantly shifting rebel household.

Obi-Wan lived nearby for many years, helping guide Luke’s Jedi training before his eventual sacrifice.

As Luke grew older and later departed for Dagobah, and as Leia spent more time on Rebel missions, the rhythm of the residence changed.

But for Anakin and Padmé, the routine remained.

After everything they had lost, simplicity became comforting.

And in that simplicity, they quietly rediscovered each other.

—

### **After the Empire**

When the Empire finally fell, the galaxy celebrated.

Naboo did not celebrate immediately.

It staggered.

The planet had endured too much damage over too many years. Infrastructure was fractured. Cities struggled to function. There was no central government left to coordinate recovery.

Meanwhile, Padmé and Anakin traveled to Coruscant to help shape the post-Empire order.

For a short time, Naboo existed in uncertainty.

But not chaos.

The people trusted that she would return.

And she did.

—

### **The Queen Returns Home**

At first, Padmé and Anakin returned quietly to their old hidden residence.

By then it had become legendary among Rebel veterans.

The place where the Rebellion had endured.
Where Luke and Leia had been raised.
Where strategies had been planned beneath the Empire’s nose for decades.

Visitors soon began gathering nearby simply to glimpse it from afar.

But the people of Naboo wanted more than symbolism.

They wanted Padmé back.

Not temporarily.
Not ceremonially.

Permanently.

—

### **The End of Elections**

The support was overwhelming.

To the people of Naboo, Padmé represented:

* stability
* resilience
* competence
* survival

They remembered her as the young Queen who defended Naboo decades earlier.
As the senator who resisted corruption.
As the Rebel architect who stood against the Empire.
As the woman who walked into the second Death Star itself alongside Luke and Anakin.

When discussions began about restoring Naboo’s monarchy, public opinion shifted decisively.

The old elected monarchy system—the rotating democratic selection of monarchs—felt too unstable now.

The galaxy had changed.

Under the new Galactic Union, planetary governments carried more responsibility than before. Naboo’s people no longer wanted constant political turnover.

They wanted continuity.

So the monarchy itself changed.

A lineage-based royal system replaced the old elective model.

Padmé had reservations about it.

But she understood why the people desired it.

And ultimately, she accepted.

—

### **The Palace Decision**

Even then, she hesitated about leaving the secluded residence.

Anakin understood immediately.

“You’re thinking about staying here,” he said one evening.

Padmé looked around the modest home that had protected them for so long.

“A part of me wants to.”

“But?”

She smiled faintly.

“But a Queen can’t hide forever.”

The old Theed Palace still stood—but barely.

After the Naboo uprising had overthrown the Imperial-installed monarch decades earlier, the remaining palace staff abandoned it entirely. Then came years of bombardment and siege damage.

By the end of the war, entire sections were uninhabitable.

Cracked towers.
Flooded chambers.
Collapsed wings.

No one could live there anymore.

Leia stepped beside her mother. “You’re thinking about rebuilding it already.”

Padmé smiled faintly. “I’m thinking about where to even begin.”

—

### **The First Planning Session**

A week later, the surviving architects of Naboo gathered within a partially restored chamber of the palace.

Ancient blueprints covered a long table.

“The people expect restoration,” one architect said carefully. “We can rebuild according to the original royal schematics.”

Padmé studied the plans.

The old palace had been designed for ceremony above all else:

* enormous isolated royal suites
* separate wings for political guests
* formal reception corridors
* layers of unnecessary separation between royal residents

It had been built for monarchy as institution.

Not family.

Anakin leaned beside her, arms folded. “Feels too large.”

“It *is* too large,” Padmé agreed.

One architect blinked. “Your Majesty?”

Padmé pointed to the central residence wing.

“This entire section can be simplified.”

The room went still.

“Simplified?” another architect repeated cautiously, as though the word itself might offend the building.

Padmé nodded.

“We’re rebuilding a home,” she said. “Not a monument to isolation.”

—

### **A Different Kind of Palace**

The discussions stretched over weeks.

At first, many officials expected Padmé to reclaim the vast royal master suite used by Naboo monarchs for generations.

Instead, she rejected it almost immediately.

“It separates everything too much,” she explained.

Anakin looked over one proposal. “There are four antechambers just to reach the bedroom.”

“Exactly.”

Leia, seated nearby with Han, smirked. “Very practical during an emergency evacuation.”

Han looked at the schematic. “I got lost halfway through this thing.”

One elderly architect looked mildly horrified. “The complexity is intentional.”

“That’s the problem,” Padmé replied.

Slowly, a new layout emerged.

The rebuilt palace would preserve the grand exterior and ceremonial halls familiar to Naboo’s people—but the private residential wing would become far more personal.

At its center:

A single shared main chamber for Padmé and Anakin.

Not separate royal quarters.

Not divided suites.

One chamber.

—

### **Padmé and Anakin’s Chamber**

The design became surprisingly collaborative.

Padmé favored elegance and openness.

Anakin favored simplicity.

“You don’t need six side rooms,” Anakin said during one discussion.

Padmé raised an eyebrow. “I used to have twelve.”

“That’s worse.”

She laughed softly.

In the end, their chamber was designed with:

* a single large bed
* wide windows overlooking the Naboo lakes
* attached study areas for Padmé’s political work
* a smaller adjoining meditation alcove for Anakin
* warm lighting and open architecture rather than ceremonial excess

No throne platform.

No elevated royal sleeping dais.

Just a room meant to be lived in.

—

### **Leia and Han**

Leia’s chamber planning proved… more complicated.

“You’re staying in the palace,” Padmé informed her one morning.

Leia nodded. “I assumed so.”

Han, nearby, frowned slightly. “Wait, permanently?”

Padmé looked at him calmly. “Unless you intend to vanish into smuggling again.”

Leia crossed her arms immediately. “He’s not.”

Han lifted his hands defensively. “I didn’t say I was!”

“You thought about it.”

“I think about lots of things.”

Eventually, Padmé assigned them a side chamber adjoining the main family wing.

Not enormous.

Not ceremonial.

Comfortable.

Han inspected the plans skeptically.

“There’s only one bed.”

Leia looked at him flatly. “Yes, Han.”

He pretended to study the blueprint very seriously. “Just making sure that wasn’t an architectural mistake.”

—

### **Chewbacca’s Chamber**

The architects were entirely unprepared for Chewbacca.

“His… room?” one asked uncertainly.

Chewbacca growled.

Han translated casually. “He says if you call it a kennel he’s throwing somebody into the lake.”

That clarified matters quickly.

Wookiee physiology required entirely different accommodations:

* lower sleeping structures
* reinforced support frames
* greater vertical clearance
* warmer environmental controls
* durable flooring

Eventually, Naboo craftsmen worked directly with Chewbacca to create a custom chamber.

The centerpiece became a woven Wookiee sleep mat specially designed for his size and comfort.

When it was finally installed, Chewbacca inspected it carefully.

Then gave an approving roar that echoed through the hallways.

The workers took this as high praise.

—

### **Luke’s Place**

Luke visited periodically during construction, usually between journeys to and from Dagobah.

“You should have your own chamber,” Padmé told him.

Luke shook his head gently. “I’m not here often enough.”

“You’re still family.”

In the end, several guest chambers were constructed within the residential wing—comfortable but modest.

One unofficially became “Luke’s room,” though nobody formally called it that.

Whenever he visited, palace staff quietly prepared the same chamber for him automatically.

—

### **Balancing Palace and Home**

One of the greatest challenges was balancing public symbolism with private practicality.

The people of Naboo wanted grandeur restored.

Padmé understood that.

So externally, the palace remained magnificent:

* restored domes
* sweeping staircases
* vast audience halls
* reflective pools and gardens

But internally—especially in the residential sections—it became warmer, quieter, more human.

Less a fortress of royalty.

More a living household.

—

### **Completion**

Years after reconstruction began, the final scaffolding came down.

The restored Theed Palace gleamed in the sunlight.

Citizens gathered once more in celebration as Padmé formally resumed residence.

Inside, workers carried the last furnishings into place.

Han dropped a bag near the side chamber entrance.

“So this is really happening.”

Leia smiled. “You sound nervous.”

“I fought the Empire. This is worse.”

Chewbacca barked laughter.

In the main chamber, Padmé stood near the window overlooking Theed.

Anakin entered quietly behind her.

“It’s finished,” he said.

Padmé looked around slowly.

Not at a throne.

Not at symbols of power.

At home.

Leia and Han arguing faintly somewhere down the corridor.

Chewbacca roaring approvingly at something.

Palace staff moving about.

Life.

She took Anakin’s hand.

“Yes,” she said softly.

“It is.”

—

The old hidden residence was eventually preserved as a Rebel Alliance museum. Visitors from across the galaxy came to see where the resistance had survived for so many years.

And more quietly, away from public attention, Varykino Villa was restored as well.

When the burdens of rebuilding became too heavy, Padmé and Anakin occasionally escaped there together for brief periods of peace.

Not as Queen and consort.

Not as war heroes.

Just as themselves.

For the first time in a very long while,
the galaxy allowed them that much.
