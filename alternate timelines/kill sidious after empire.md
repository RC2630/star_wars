# Part 1

The underground chamber beneath Sundari had once been designed as a continuity bunker — a final refuge for Mandalore’s government in the event of siege. The architects had imagined orbital bombardment, collapsing domes, poisoned atmosphere.

They had not imagined the Empire.

Muted lights glowed along the curved metallic walls, low and amber to avoid detection from thermal scans above. A single circular table occupied the center of the room. No banners. No insignias. No ceremonial displays.

Only six people.

Satine sat upright at the head of the table, pale blue robes draped with the severe elegance expected of Mandalore’s ruler. But there was strain around her eyes now that had not been there years ago. The strain of maintaining a lie large enough to protect an entire world.

Beside her sat Obi-Wan.

Not General Kenobi. Not Master Kenobi.

Simply Obi-Wan.

The armor and war robes were gone. He wore dark civilian layers beneath a Mandalorian cloak clasped loosely at the shoulder. His beard had begun to silver faintly near the chin. He looked older than thirty-nine. Not broken. Just weathered.

Across from them sat Padmé and Anakin.

Padmé retained the composed gravity of a senator, though motherhood had softened certain edges in her expression. She looked tired in the way people did when they carried both hope and fear simultaneously.

Anakin sat beside her, broad-shouldered now, his expression intent and restless beneath the dim lighting. The years had transformed the slave boy from Tatooine into something difficult to categorize. Not Jedi. Not politician. Not soldier.

Something in between.

At the far end of the table sat Yoda and Mace Windu.

The last senior remnants of the old Order.

For a long moment, nobody spoke.

Then Satine broke the silence.

“The Imperial Security Bureau expanded its surveillance network again last month,” she said quietly. “Three more Mid Rim systems placed under direct military oversight. Dissident arrests are increasing.”

Padmé folded her hands together. “The Senate still pretends the emergency measures are temporary.”

Mace gave a humorless look.

“The Senate is irrelevant now. Palpatine leaves it standing because it comforts people.”

“Afraid they are,” Yoda murmured. “Afraid to see already dead, the Republic is.”

The room fell quiet again.

Anakin leaned back slightly, jaw tight.

“Then we stop pretending this can be managed politically.”

Padmé glanced toward him, not disagreeing exactly, but measuring the tone.

Obi-Wan spoke carefully. “We all understand what’s at stake, Anakin.”

“No,” Anakin said. “I don’t think we do.”

His voice stayed calm, which somehow made the intensity beneath it sharper.

“We keep talking about resistance networks, supply lines, sympathetic senators. Meanwhile Sidious is consolidating everything faster than we expected. Every month he survives, more systems normalize the Empire.”

Mace studied him across the table.

“And your solution?”

Anakin met his gaze directly.

“We kill him.”

No one reacted visibly. The statement had already existed in the room before he spoke it aloud.

Still, hearing it settled something final into the atmosphere.

Padmé exhaled slowly.

“We all know that may become necessary,” she said. “But assassination alone doesn’t solve the problem. If Palpatine dies without preparation, the Empire could fracture into military rule. Or martyr him.”

“Correct,” said Mace immediately. “Removing Sidious is necessary. Not sufficient.”

Satine turned toward Yoda.

“You’ve faced Sith before. Both of you have. Is there any scenario where he can be arrested?”

Mace answered before Yoda could.

“No.”

Flat. Absolute.

“The Jedi tried legality already. Sidious turned the entire system against us in hours.”

Obi-Wan lowered his eyes briefly.

“We underestimated how completely he’d prepared the Republic to accept authoritarian rule.”

“Prepared it?” Anakin said quietly. “People were begging for it by the end.”

No one contradicted him.

He stood and began pacing slowly around the perimeter of the chamber.

“The war exhausted everyone. Fear exhausted everyone. Sidious didn’t create that fear from nothing. He weaponized what was already there.”

“And now?” Satine asked.

“Now he needs permanence,” Anakin replied. “That’s the weak point.”

The others watched him carefully.

“He can’t rule indefinitely through transition. Eventually the Empire has to become emotionally normal to people. Stable. Predictable.” He looked toward Padmé. “That takes time.”

Padmé nodded slowly, following his thought immediately.

“So if we move, we move before consolidation finishes.”

“Yes.”

Mace folded his arms.

“And how exactly do you propose reaching the most protected man in the galaxy?”

Anakin stopped pacing.

“Not through Coruscant security.”

Obi-Wan’s expression sharpened faintly.

“You’re thinking about him personally.”

“I’m thinking about patterns.”

Anakin stepped toward the table again.

“Sidious trusts systems less now than he used to. Order Sixty-Six worked, but surviving Jedi changed the equation. He knows Yoda survived. He knows you survived,” he said to Mace. “He doesn’t know where Obi-Wan is. He doesn’t know what I’m doing.”

“And that troubles him,” Yoda said softly.

Anakin nodded.

“He’ll become more isolated over time. More paranoid. More dependent on fewer people.”

“Dooku,” Obi-Wan said.

The room shifted subtly at the name.

“Yes,” Anakin replied.

Mace frowned.

“You believe Dooku is exploitable?”

“No,” Anakin said. “I think Dooku believes he’s Sidious’s partner. Eventually he’ll realize he’s disposable.”

Satine leaned forward slightly.

“You think they’ll fracture.”

“I think Sith always fracture.”

Yoda gave the faintest hint of grim amusement.

“Right, you are.”

Padmé spoke next.

“Even if that’s true, we still need a viable operation. We can’t build the entire future of the galaxy around waiting for two Sith Lords to distrust each other.”

“No,” said Obi-Wan quietly. “But we may be able to accelerate it.”

Everyone looked at him.

Obi-Wan rested his forearms against the table, thinking aloud carefully.

“Dooku’s pride is immense. But unlike Sidious, he still believes his actions serve a philosophical purpose. If evidence emerges that the Empire is becoming precisely the tyranny he once opposed…”

“He may hesitate,” Satine finished.

“Or overreach,” Mace said.

Anakin looked toward Obi-Wan thoughtfully.

“You think he can still be reached.”

Obi-Wan was silent for a moment.

“When I was younger,” he said at last, “I believed people either stood in the light or fell into darkness permanently.” A faint sadness crossed his face. “I don’t think reality is that clean anymore.”

Yoda studied him with quiet approval.

Padmé turned toward Mace.

“If direct military confrontation is impossible right now, and political opposition alone is too weak… then our path is destabilization.”

Mace nodded once.

“Intelligence. Exposure. Fragmentation.”

Satine added:
“And survival.”

That word lingered heavily.

Because all of them understood the reality beneath the discussion:
if Mandalore were exposed, the planet would burn.

Anakin looked around the room slowly.

“We can’t fight the Empire conventionally. Not yet. So we target the structure holding it together.”

“The Emperor himself,” said Mace.

“And the fear around him,” Padmé added.

Silence settled again.

Then Yoda spoke, slow and deliberate.

“Failed, the Jedi did.”

No one interrupted him.

“Blind, we became. Certain. Believed we alone understood the Force.” His ancient eyes moved from Obi-Wan to Anakin. “Different, this path is.”

Obi-Wan looked toward Anakin with the faintest trace of a smile.

“Fortunately, none of us are very good at orthodoxy anymore.”

A quiet laugh escaped Satine despite herself.

Even Mace’s expression loosened slightly.

Then the room became serious again.

Padmé straightened.

“So let’s say it plainly.”

She looked at each of them in turn.

“The goal is no longer survival.”

No one disagreed.

“The goal is the removal of Emperor Palpatine before the Empire becomes irreversible.”

Mace placed both hands on the table.

“Agreed.”

“Agreed,” Obi-Wan said.

Satine nodded once.

Yoda closed his eyes briefly.

“Agreed.”

Finally all eyes turned toward Anakin.

He stood still for a moment beneath the low amber light.

Not a Jedi.

Not a general.

Not a chosen weapon.

Just a man who had seen slavery, war, corruption, and now empire — and had decided none of them were inevitable.

When he spoke, his voice was calm.

“Then we end this.”

# Part 2

The rain came hard against the domes of Sundari.

Thin silver sheets swept across the capital’s curved transparisteel skyline while thunder rolled through the hollow wastelands beyond the city shields. Night had settled over Mandalore, but the palace beneath the storm remained awake.

Deep below the royal residence, in chambers hidden beneath layers of sealed alloy and false infrastructure records, the rebellion’s leaders had gathered again.

They never heard the first alarms.

Because the first warning did not come from sensors.

It came through the Force.

Yoda’s eyes snapped open.

At nearly the same instant, across the chamber, Anakin stiffened sharply. Obi-Wan’s head turned toward the ceiling. Mace Windu rose from his seat without a word.

The room fell instantly still.

Padmé saw it immediately in their faces.

“What is it?”

Yoda’s ears lowered.

“Found us… they have.”

Anakin closed his eyes briefly.

Cold presences.

Several.

One towering above the others like a sharpened blade wrapped in aristocratic control.

Dooku.

And with him—

“Inquisitors,” Obi-Wan said grimly.

Satine stood immediately. “How?”

“They sensed us,” Mace replied. “Too many Force users concentrated in one place.”

Anakin was already moving toward the exit.

“How many?”

“Six inquisitors,” said Yoda softly. “And Dooku.”

Obi-Wan looked toward Satine and Padmé.

“They cannot be allowed underground.”

Satine understood instantly. If imperial forces discovered the hidden resistance base beneath the palace, Mandalore would not survive the week.

Padmé stepped toward Anakin. “If this becomes public—”

“It already has,” he said quietly.

For one brief moment their eyes met.

Everything unspoken passed between them.

Then Obi-Wan ignited his lightsaber.

Blue light flooded the chamber.

Anakin’s followed a heartbeat later.

Mace Windu’s violet blade hissed alive beside them.

Yoda drew his smaller saber last, emerald light reflecting across the metallic walls.

The four of them looked less like the remnants of a dead order than something older. Sharper.

Not Jedi bound by doctrine.

Warriors united by choice.

“We finish this fast,” Mace said.

Then they were gone.

—

The palace landing platforms above Sundari shook under the storm winds.

A black Imperial shuttle rested upon the rain-slick durasteel, engines still glowing red-hot from descent.

Arrayed before it stood the inquisitors — dark armored hunters carrying spinning sabers and masked menace — while Count Dooku stood at their center beneath a dark cloak untouched by the rain.

He looked old now.

Not weak.

But tired in a way the dark side could not fully conceal.

The palace guards lay unconscious around the platform. None dead. Dooku had never enjoyed waste for its own sake.

The lead inquisitor stepped forward suddenly.

“My lord,” she hissed. “They’re here.”

Dooku had already felt them.

Four presences rising together through the structure below.

One ancient and luminous.

One controlled like compressed thunder.

One familiar as memory.

And one—

Dooku’s eyes narrowed.

Skywalker.

Not fallen.

Not broken.

Strong.

The blast doors behind the platform opened.

The four emerged together into the storm.

Rain hissed into steam against ignited lightsabers.

Blue.
Blue.
Purple.
Green.

The inquisitors shifted uneasily.

Dooku merely watched.

Obi-Wan stepped forward first, rain soaking through his cloak.

“Count Dooku.”

“Obi-Wan.” Dooku’s expression darkened faintly. “You continue to disappoint your former Order in increasingly creative ways.”

Obi-Wan gave a tired half-smile.

“There isn’t much Order left to disappoint.”

Anakin moved slightly ahead.

Dooku’s eyes settled on him.

“The famous Skywalker.”

“The disappointed replacement,” Anakin replied coldly.

That struck deeper than the others expected.

Dooku’s expression tightened almost invisibly.

He knew.

Of course he knew.

Sidious had spoken often enough of the boy he once intended to claim.

The inquisitors ignited their sabers almost simultaneously.

Circular crimson blades spun violently in the rain.

Mace stepped forward.

“You’re not leaving Mandalore alive.”

The lead inquisitor lunged instantly.

A mistake.

Mace met her attack with Vaapad’s terrifying precision — no wasted motion, no flourish. Purple light crashed once against red before his blade sheared through her saber guard and opened her chest in the same movement.

She collapsed before the others fully reacted.

Then chaos erupted.

The platform exploded into motion.

Two inquisitors rushed Yoda simultaneously, spinning sabers carving arcs through the rain. Yoda became green light and velocity, impossibly fast despite his age. Ataru transformed his small frame into a storm of kinetic unpredictability — flipping between strikes, rebounding from walls, using momentum itself as a weapon.

One inquisitor lost an arm before realizing Yoda had moved.

The second died seconds later with a precise thrust through the throat.

Nearby, Obi-Wan entered Soresu’s perfect defensive flow against another pair. Calm. Impenetrable. Their aggressive assaults crashed harmlessly against his blade control. He gave ground deliberately, guiding them into overextension.

Then suddenly he stopped retreating.

One precise pivot.

One angled deflection.

An inquisitor’s spinning saber cut directly into her partner.

Obi-Wan’s blade passed cleanly through them both before either hit the floor.

Anakin fought differently.

Violently.

Directly.

His opponent attacked with elaborate spinning intimidation tactics common among the inquisitors, but Anakin immediately overwhelmed the rhythm with powerful Djem So strikes that battered through defenses faster than the hunter could compensate.

The inquisitor attempted aerial repositioning.

Anakin ripped him out of the air with the Force and drove his saber through his chest before he hit the ground.

Only one remained.

She backed toward Dooku instinctively.

Fear radiated from her.

Dooku looked almost disgusted.

“So this,” he murmured, “is what the Sith reduced Force training to.”

The final inquisitor screamed and charged.

Mace intercepted her.

The duel lasted less than five seconds.

Then only Dooku remained.

Thunder rolled above the platform.

Rain hammered against black stone.

The old Count slowly ignited his curved-hilt saber.

Red light emerged into the storm.

At last.

A real duel.

Dooku’s gaze moved across them carefully.

Yoda.
Windu.
Kenobi.
Skywalker.

Four of the most formidable Force users alive.

And strangely…

For the first time in many years…

He felt clarity.

“You’ve become something unexpected,” Dooku said quietly.

Obi-Wan raised his blade.

“You should have joined us.”

Dooku almost smiled.

“No. I made my choice long ago.”

Then he attacked.

He moved first toward Yoda.

Of course he did.

Old rivalries pulled deepest.

Their blades collided in a blur of elegant violence — Makashi against Ataru, aristocratic precision against acrobatic ferocity. Dooku’s fencing mastery remained extraordinary even now. Every movement economical. Minimal. Refined.

Then Mace joined.

Vaapad crashed into the duel like controlled chaos, forcing Dooku immediately onto the defensive. Purple and red and green light strobed across the rain-soaked platform.

Obi-Wan circled wide, waiting.

Anakin did not wait.

He struck from Dooku’s blind side with overwhelming force, driving heavy two-handed blows meant not for finesse but collapse.

For a moment Dooku held.

Brilliantly.

Four masters pressing him from every angle, and still he adapted — redirecting momentum, dividing their timing, exploiting inches of positioning.

But age was real.

Fatigue was real.

And this was not Geonosis.

These opponents were united.

Anakin and Obi-Wan unconsciously fell into complementary rhythm despite years apart. Defense and aggression. Patience and momentum.

The old partnership still existed somewhere beneath everything.

Dooku realized it too late.

Obi-Wan locked his blade high.

Mace hammered low.

Yoda struck from above.

And Anakin—

Anakin drove forward with pure irresistible power.

Dooku’s saber tore free from his grasp.

The Count staggered backward to one knee.

Four blades surrounded him instantly.

Rain poured down his face.

For several seconds no one moved.

Then Dooku laughed softly.

Not bitterly.

Just tired.

“So,” he said, breathing heavily, “that is how it ends.”

Anakin stepped closer.

“Sidious betrayed you from the beginning.”

Dooku looked up at him.

“Yes,” he whispered.

At last, he understood it completely.

Not intellectually.

Personally.

Sidious had never intended partnership.
Never intended succession.
Never intended anything except use.

Tyranus had only been another tool.

Another disposable apprentice.

A flicker of grief crossed Dooku’s face then — not for himself, but perhaps for the man he once believed he was.

“The Republic was diseased,” he said weakly. “But this…” He coughed harshly. “This Empire is worse.”

Obi-Wan lowered his blade slightly.

“It’s not too late to help us.”

Dooku gave him an almost pitying look.

“For me? Yes.” His eyes shifted toward Anakin. “But perhaps not for all of you.”

His breathing grew shallow.

Then, quietly:

“Sidious fears you, Skywalker.”

Anakin’s expression hardened.

“He should.”

A faint smile touched Dooku’s mouth.

“Yes,” he said. “He should.”

Then the Count of Serenno died beneath the Mandalorian rain.

Silence followed.

Only thunder remained.

Yoda’s expression darkened suddenly.

“Gone,” he murmured.

Mace looked toward him immediately.

“What?”

“The Emperor knows.”

Of course he did.

Dooku’s death.
The inquisitors destroyed.
Four powerful Force users gathered openly on Mandalore.

There would be no hiding now.

No more secrecy.

No more preparation time.

The Empire would come.

And when it came, it would come with overwhelming force.

Orbital bombardment.
Legions.
Star destroyers.

Mandalore would burn.

Obi-Wan turned immediately toward the others.

“Then we move now.”

Padmé and Satine emerged onto the platform from the palace entrance, both having watched the final moments through security feeds below.

Padmé saw Dooku’s body.

Saw the expressions on the others’ faces.

And understood instantly.

“There’s no more time,” she said.

“No,” Mace agreed.

Anakin extinguished his saber slowly.

Rain streamed down his face as he looked toward the black sky above Sundari’s dome.

Somewhere beyond it, on Coruscant, Sidious still believed himself untouchable.

Still believed fear had already won.

Anakin turned back toward the others.

“We end this tonight.”

# Part 3

The decision was made within minutes.

No debate.
No ceremony.
No hesitation.

There would never be a better opportunity than this.

Before Dooku’s shuttle failed to report.
Before Imperial Intelligence traced the inquisitors’ disappearance.
Before Palpatine understood Mandalore had become the center of organized resistance.

They had one narrow opening.

And all of them knew it.

The storm still raged over Sundari when the four departed.

Padmé stood with Satine beneath the palace overhang as the transport lifted into the black Mandalorian sky. Neither woman spoke as the ship vanished beyond the clouds.

There was nothing left to say.

—

Hyperspace blurred blue around them.

Inside the dim transport cabin, no one rested.

Anakin sat forward near the cockpit, elbows on knees, staring at nothing.

Obi-Wan watched him quietly from across the compartment.

Years ago, during the Clone Wars, there would have been tension in a moment like this. Friction. Unspoken hierarchy. The strain of master and apprentice trapped inside roles they no longer fit.

Now there was none.

Only trust.

Mace stood near the rear bulkhead, eyes closed in meditation.

Yoda sat cross-legged on the deck plating, cane laid beside him.

At last Obi-Wan spoke.

“Once we land, we have perhaps a few hours before Coruscant begins receiving reports.”

“Less,” Mace said without opening his eyes. “Sidious built redundancies into everything.”

Anakin looked toward him.

“You really think we can do this?”

Mace finally opened his eyes.

“Yes.”

Not confidence.

Certainty.

That mattered.

Yoda’s voice came softly through the hum of hyperspace.

“Certain of victory, do not become.”

Anakin smirked faintly.

“Wouldn’t dream of it.”

But underneath the humor lay tension sharp enough to cut.

Because all of them understood the truth:

If this failed, the Empire became permanent.

—

Coruscant greeted them beneath endless rain and neon.

The city-world stretched infinitely beneath the night, towers piercing cloud layers like metallic mountains. Traffic flowed through the skies in rivers of white and red light while propaganda holograms glowed across skyscrapers:

THE EMPIRE BRINGS SECURITY.

The words felt obscene now.

Their transport descended silently into the lower industrial sectors far beneath the Senate district. Anonymous. Forgettable. One vessel among billions.

The four moved quickly through the undercity.

Anakin pulled his hood lower as clone veterans in white armor marched past nearby intersections. Stormtroopers now. The same men. Different symbols.

Obi-Wan noticed him watching them.

“You still feel responsible for them.”

“They were bred for obedience,” Anakin said quietly. “None of them chose this.”

Mace’s expression remained hard.

“They’ll still kill us.”

“Yes,” Anakin replied. “They will.”

Eventually they found refuge in an abandoned maintenance structure deep beneath the old Works district — rusted, forgotten, buried beneath layers of industrial noise and neglected infrastructure.

Safe enough for one night.

No more.

Inside the dark chamber, Yoda settled slowly to the floor.

“Find us through the Force, Sidious will try.”

Mace nodded once. “He’ll sense disruption the moment Dooku disappears.”

“Then disappear, we must.”

The others sat around him.

Yoda closed his eyes.

“Clear your thoughts. Release fear. Release intent.”

The meditation that followed was unlike ordinary Jedi practice.

Not tranquility.

Concealment.

The four Force presences gradually folded inward, compressing themselves into stillness so deep that even breathing seemed to fade into the structure around them.

Anakin struggled first.

Not with discipline.

With intensity.

His presence in the Force burned naturally bright, difficult to suppress entirely. But slowly, guided by Yoda and reinforced unconsciously by the others, even he disappeared beneath the currents of Coruscant’s endless living noise.

By dawn, they had become shadows.

And above them, unaware, Emperor Palpatine awoke to another ordinary morning.

—

The Imperial Palace had once been the Jedi Temple.

That fact lingered like a wound.

Its great corridors now gleamed with polished black stone and severe Imperial architecture, but beneath the redesign the old structure remained. The bones of the Jedi still held up the Empire.

Fitting, Anakin thought bitterly.

The four waited in silence high within an abandoned service passage overlooking one of the private executive corridors near the Emperor’s chambers.

No guards yet.

Too early.

Palpatine preferred privacy during his morning movements through the inner palace.

Mace crouched nearest the opening.

“Once it begins,” he said quietly, “we do not stop.”

No one answered.

Then—

Footsteps.

Measured.
Calm.
Unhurried.

Palpatine emerged into the corridor below draped in dark Imperial robes, hands clasped behind his back as though nothing in the galaxy could possibly threaten him.

For one impossible instant, he looked ordinary.

An old man walking through a hallway.

Then his eyes narrowed.

Too late.

The ambush detonated around him.

Mace dropped first from above, violet blade igniting mid-fall.

Palpatine spun with horrifying speed.

Red light exploded into existence.

The collision shook the corridor.

At the same instant Anakin and Obi-Wan descended from opposite flanks while Yoda launched forward in a blur of green.

Sidious moved like nightmare incarnate.

The dark side erupted outward violently enough to crack walls and throw debris through the corridor. His scream became Force lightning before his hands even fully rose.

Anakin intercepted it.

Blue light caught the torrent, electricity screaming across his blade as he drove forward through sheer power.

Obi-Wan circled immediately into defensive positioning, Form III anchoring the group against Sidious’s impossible aggression.

Yoda attacked low and fast.
Mace attacked relentlessly.

The Emperor laughed.

Actually laughed.

“At last!” he snarled.

His blade became crimson chaos.

He drove Mace backward with impossible speed, pivoted instantly to intercept Yoda mid-leap, then hurled a shockwave toward Obi-Wan hard enough to crater the wall behind him.

Anakin came straight through the debris.

Djem So crashed against Sidious’s style with overwhelming force. Not elegant. Not restrained. Pure pressure meant to break defenses through intensity.

Sidious’s yellow eyes locked onto him.

“There you are.”

Their blades collided violently.

For a moment the corridor became only red and blue light.

Anakin felt it then — the raw magnitude of Sidious’s power. Vast. Ancient. Corrupt beyond comprehension.

And underneath it—

Fear.

Not of death.

Of failure.

The duel spread through the corridor system like a natural disaster.

Walls split apart.
Statues shattered.
Windows burst outward into Coruscant’s skyline.

Then alarms erupted across the palace.

Too late now.

Stormtroopers flooded the surrounding sectors.

The first squad rounded the corridor corner at full sprint.

“CONTACT—”

Mace’s Force push hit them like an explosion.

Armor crumpled against walls.

But more followed immediately.

Blasterfire filled the air.

Obi-Wan shifted seamlessly, blade becoming a wall of blue defense as bolts ricocheted harmlessly away from the group.

Sidious attacked through the distraction instantly.

Lightning erupted from his hands in massive arcs.

Yoda absorbed part of it.
Mace endured the rest.

Both staggered.

Anakin lunged directly through the chaos, forcing Sidious backward for the first time.

The Emperor’s expression twisted with fury.

Then new figures emerged into the battle.

Battle droids.

Dozens of them.

Heavy Imperial security models accompanied by Separatist tactical droids still secretly preserved within Palpatine’s hidden arsenals.

Even now, after all these years, he had prepared contingencies upon contingencies.

Blasterfire intensified from every direction.

The corridor became war.

Anakin cut through droids almost mechanically now, movements flowing instinctively between combatants. Obi-Wan anchored the group repeatedly whenever the pressure threatened to overwhelm them.

Yoda moved like living Force energy itself.

Mace became terrifying.

Vaapad fed upon the surrounding darkness and chaos, transforming Sidious’s fury into fuel for his own assault. Purple light hammered against crimson again and again, driving the Emperor steadily backward through collapsing corridors.

Still Sidious endured.

Still he smiled through blood and rage.

“You cannot stop what I have created!”

“Yes,” Anakin said, breathing hard, “we can.”

Together the four pressed him.

Not as master and apprentice.
Not as council and subordinate.

As equals.

That was the difference.

Sidious had spent decades exploiting division.

But there was none here.

Obi-Wan anticipated Anakin’s movement instinctively.
Yoda coordinated effortlessly with Mace.
Anakin covered openings before they fully formed.

The Emperor began losing ground.

For the first time, genuine alarm entered his expression.

The battle finally burst into one of the great open Senate overlook chambers high within the palace structure. Vast windows revealed all of Coruscant beyond — endless city lights stretching beneath storm clouds.

Sidious stood cornered now.

Breathing hard.
Robes scorched.
Face twisted into something truly monstrous.

Stormtroopers littered the chamber floor.
Smoking droid parts burned nearby.

Silence fell briefly except for distant alarms.

Sidious looked at Anakin.

“One final chance.”

Anakin said nothing.

“You know the Republic was weak. You know peace requires power.”

Anakin stepped forward slowly, blue blade steady in his hand.

“No,” he said quietly.

“You were just afraid people would choose each other over fear.”

Something changed in Sidious’s face then.

Not rage.

Hatred.

Pure concentrated hatred.

Lightning exploded outward one final time.

All four met it together.

Blue.
Green.
Purple.

The dark side torrent bent backward under combined resistance.

Sidious screamed.

Mace struck first, severing the Emperor’s saber hand.

Yoda’s Force push drove him to his knees.

Obi-Wan pinned him there.

And Anakin—

Anakin delivered the final blow.

Clean.
Certain.
Without hesitation.

The blue blade passed through Darth Sidious’s chest.

The Emperor froze.

For one long moment, no one moved.

Then the dark side presence that had haunted the galaxy for decades suddenly vanished.

Just… vanished.

Palpatine collapsed to the floor.

Dead.

Darth Sidious.
Emperor of the Galactic Empire.
Destroyer of the Republic.

Gone.

The chamber fell silent except for distant alarms echoing across Coruscant.

The four survivors stood together over the body.

Breathing hard.
Exhausted.
Victorious.

And somewhere far below them, across a trillion lives and countless worlds, the future abruptly became uncertain again.

# Part 4

The four stood in silence over Sidious’s body.

The shattered Senate overlook chamber groaned around them. Fires burned in broken wall panels. Smoke drifted upward into the fractured heights of the old Jedi Temple — now wounded again by another battle for the fate of the galaxy.

Outside the vast windows, Coruscant continued moving.

Traffic streamed endlessly between towers.
Billboards flashed.
Millions went about their morning unaware that the Emperor was dead.

Anakin looked down at the corpse.

No triumph came.

Only absence.

The oppressive pressure that had weighed on the Force for decades had vanished so suddenly that the silence felt unnatural.

Mace deactivated his lightsaber first.

“So,” he said quietly, breathing hard, “what now?”

No one answered immediately.

It was Obi-Wan who finally spoke.

“We could announce it ourselves.”

Mace frowned slightly. “To whom?”

“The Senate. The military. Whoever’s left.”

“And say what?” Anakin asked. “‘We killed the Emperor, trust us with the transition’?”

The implications settled heavily over the room.

Four Jedi — or former Jedi — standing in the ruins of the Imperial Palace over a dead head of state.

No matter the truth, it could become something dangerous very quickly.

Then, softly—

“Must not lead the galaxy… you should.”

The voice moved through the room like distant wind.

All four froze.

Qui-Gon.

Not fully present. Not visible.

But unmistakable.

“Stay away from politics,” the voice continued gently. “Different failures… otherwise become.”

Yoda lowered his head slightly.

“Understand,” he murmured.

Mace looked toward the others.

And for once, he did not argue.

Obi-Wan gave a faint tired smile.

“Qui-Gon always did enjoy appearing after all the difficult parts were finished.”

Anakin extinguished his blade and turned toward the corridor.

“Then we leave.”

The alarms were growing louder now.

More troops approaching.

More confusion.

Soon the entire palace would lock down completely.

The four moved immediately.

—

The escape from the palace became battle once more.

Stormtroopers flooded the lower corridors in growing numbers now that central command had collapsed into panic. Some fought with discipline. Others fired wildly without orders, overwhelmed by contradictory signals and fragmenting communications.

The Emperor was dead.

Nobody knew who was in charge.

The four carved their way downward through the chaos.

Obi-Wan led defensively through narrow corridors, redirecting blasterfire with almost impossible precision.

Mace moved like controlled destruction, direct and overwhelming whenever squads attempted to encircle them.

Yoda used the Force itself as battlefield control, collapsing bulkheads behind them and hurling entire troop formations aside without slowing.

And Anakin—

Anakin fought with frightening efficiency now.

Not rage.

Clarity.

Every movement precise. Every strike purposeful. The years outside the Jedi Order had sanded away much of the instability that once lived inside him, but the intensity remained. Against stormtroopers advancing through smoke-filled corridors, he became almost unstoppable.

At last they reached the lower landing sectors.

Their transport still waited.

Miraculously.

Or perhaps the Force had simply decided enough history had already occurred here.

The ship lifted into Coruscant’s endless skyline just as emergency broadcasts began erupting across civilian channels.

IMPERIAL PALACE ATTACKED.

STATUS OF EMPEROR UNKNOWN.

MILITARY LOCKDOWN IN EFFECT.

The four said little during the return to Mandalore.

Exhaustion settled over them at last.

Not merely physical exhaustion.

Historical exhaustion.

They had spent years fighting toward a moment none of them fully expected to survive.

And now it was over.

Which meant the truly difficult part was beginning.

—

The Imperial Senate convened in chaos.

Thousands of senators packed the great chamber beneath its enormous domed ceiling while emergency alerts flashed across every console.

At first there had only been delay.

Confusion.

Palpatine was late to deliver a scheduled address regarding “security matters.”

That alone was unprecedented.

Then whispers spread.

Then contradictory reports.

Then finally—

Confirmation.

The Emperor was dead.

The Imperial Palace heavily damaged.

Communications fractured.

No successor named.

The Senate exploded.

Hardline Imperial factions immediately demanded emergency transfer of authority to military leadership.

Others denounced them as opportunists.

Regional blocs accused each other openly.
Old rivalries resurfaced overnight.
Former allies turned hostile within minutes.

And beneath it all lurked naked ambition.

Because Palpatine had made one catastrophic mistake:

He never imagined dying.

He had never formally designated an heir because the Empire had never truly been intended to outlive him. It was an extension of his own will.

Now the structure remained without its center.

And ambitious men rushed instantly to fill the void.

Bail Organa and Mon Mothma fought relentlessly from the reformist side of the chamber, attempting to prevent emergency dictatorship from simply reproducing itself under a new face.

The debates became screaming matches.

Accusations of treason filled the air.

At one point several senators nearly came to blows physically on the central platforms.

Then suddenly the chamber shifted.

A ripple moved outward through the Senate floor.

People were staring toward the main entrance.

Padmé Amidala had entered the chamber.

And beside her walked Anakin Skywalker.

The reaction was immediate.

Shock.
Relief.
Hope.

Even after years of forced exile from the Senate, Padmé still carried enormous moral authority. Many senators had quietly admired her resistance to Imperial overreach during the Empire’s earliest years. She had fought constantly for civil protections and senatorial limits on executive power until Palpatine himself finally ordered her removal.

Most had lacked the courage to stop him.

Now she had returned.

And she looked furious.

The chamber gradually quieted as Padmé ascended toward the central speaking platform.

Anakin remained beside her the entire time, calm but watchful.

The Senate waited.

Padmé looked around slowly at the chamber.

At the men and women arguing over the remains of dictatorship.

Then she began speaking.

“The Empire,” she said clearly, “was built on fear.”

Silence spread further.

“It promised security while dismantling liberty. It promised order while concentrating power into a single man beyond accountability.”

Her voice sharpened.

“And many of you allowed it because you were tired. Because the Clone Wars frightened you. Because surrendering responsibility felt easier than preserving democracy.”

Several hardline senators shifted angrily.

Padmé did not care.

“Palpatine exploited every weakness in the Republic — corruption, paralysis, inequality, fear. But the answer to a flawed republic was never empire.”

The chamber remained utterly still now.

“No government should survive the death of one man by collapsing into panic. No leader should possess authority so absolute that his removal threatens civilization itself.”

Her gaze swept across the Senate.

“That ends now.”

A wave of murmurs followed.

Padmé raised her voice slightly.

“A transitional authority must be established immediately to prevent military fragmentation and civil war. But no permanent emperor must ever exist again.”

One hardliner rose furiously.

“You speak treason against the lawful structure of the Empire!”

Padmé turned toward him without hesitation.

“Yes,” she said.

The chamber erupted.

She pressed forward through the noise.

“A provisional emperor should be elected by majority vote of this Senate with one explicit mandate: dismantle the Empire lawfully and oversee the transition into a reformed republic.”

That stunned even many reformists.

Not revolution through chaos.

Transformation through legality.

It was brilliant.

Because it denied the hardliners their strongest argument: that opposing the Empire meant disorder.

Padmé continued:

“The institutions of centralized authoritarianism must be dismantled deliberately and transparently. Emergency powers permanently restricted. Military authority decentralized. Executive oversight expanded. Anti-corruption reforms enacted. No future chancellor—or emperor—must ever again accumulate unchecked power.”

The chamber thundered with argument again.

But now momentum had shifted.

The vote was called hours later after brutal procedural conflict.

The result was decisive.

Bail Organa was elected provisional emperor.

Not because senators desired another emperor.

But because Bail was trusted by nearly every anti-authoritarian faction remaining in government.

The hardliners realized too late they had lost.

One of them snapped.

A senior Imperial loyalist from the Core Worlds suddenly surged to his feet near the central aisle, face twisted with fury.

“If I cannot save the Empire,” he shouted, “then neither will you!”

A blaster appeared in his hand.

He fired directly at Padmé.

The chamber screamed.

But Anakin had already moved.

Blue light exploded beside Padmé as his lightsaber ignited.

The bolt ricocheted harmlessly away.

Gasps spread across the Senate.

The hardliner stared in horror for one stunned second before security forces slammed him to the ground and dragged him away struggling.

Silence followed.

Anakin remained standing beside Padmé, blade still humming softly.

Not hidden anymore.

Not pretending anymore.

For a brief moment the entire Senate simply stared at him.

Then slowly, quietly, senators began applauding.

Not the weapon.

The protection.

The choice.

The years that followed were difficult.

The Empire did not vanish overnight. Entire sectors resisted reform. Some moffs attempted regional secessions. Several military governors refused civilian authority outright.

But without Palpatine, the system lacked cohesion.

And Bail Organa proved exactly the transitional leader Padmé had hoped for.

Under his provisional rule, the Imperial structure was dismantled piece by piece while a new constitutional framework emerged in its place.

Not the old Republic reborn unchanged.

Something wiser.

Emergency powers became heavily constrained.
Executive authority divided.
Military command decentralized.
Anti-corruption oversight strengthened.
Planetary representation expanded.

The Jedi Order did not formally return.

Nor did the Sith.

Instead, slowly, new Force traditions began emerging from the survivors and their students — less dogmatic, less institutional, more connected to ordinary life.

On Mandalore, Satine continued ruling beside Obi-Wan openly now at last.

No more secrecy.
No more hiding.

And Anakin Skywalker, once feared as the possible destroyer of the Republic, spent his later years helping rebuild it beside Padmé and their children.

Not as a Jedi.
Not as a conqueror.
Not as Darth Vader.

Simply as himself.

And because of that, the galaxy survived.
