# Part 1

*(42 BBY)*

Count Dooku left the Jedi Order in silence.

There was no dramatic denunciation in the Senate rotunda, no public scandal, no attempt to rally followers. One morning, he simply surrendered his seat, departed Coruscant, and returned to Serenno. To the Jedi Council, it was another sorrowful confirmation that age and pride could pull even great Masters away from the light. To Qui-Gon Jinn, it felt like a warning the Order refused to hear.

Dooku had once told him that the Jedi no longer served peace—they served stability. There was a difference. The Republic was rotting in plain sight, corruption spreading from the Core Worlds into the Senate itself, and still the Council spoke endlessly of patience, restraint, procedure.

Qui-Gon remembered the conversation often over the next two years.

*(40 BBY)*

Then Obi-Wan Kenobi fell in love.

The Mandalorian Civil War had become a brutal thing by then: cities burned beneath domes, clans turned on one another, and every attempt at peace seemed to collapse into another cycle of revenge. Qui-Gon and his young Padawan had been sent there as protectors for Duchess Satine Kryze during the worst of it. Somewhere between the firefights, the evacuations, the nights hiding in ruined settlements while blaster fire echoed over the snowfields, Obi-Wan and Satine became inseparable.

The Jedi Code allowed compassion, but only in the abstract. Not devotion. Not attachment. Not love with a name and a face.

Qui-Gon watched Obi-Wan struggle against it for months, watched him try to cut pieces of himself away in order to remain what the Order demanded. In the end, it was Satine who forced honesty into the open.

“If I asked for it,” she told him quietly one night beneath the shattered lights of Sundari’s outer districts, “I think you would leave the Jedi Order for me.”

Obi-Wan stared at her for a long time before answering.

“Yes.”

When word reached Coruscant that Obi-Wan Kenobi intended to resign from the Jedi Order and marry the Duchess of Mandalore, the Council reacted with cold disbelief. Some argued he should be cast out entirely. Others insisted Qui-Gon had failed him as a Master. Windu called it a dangerous precedent.

Qui-Gon listened to all of it in silence.

Then he stood, unclipped his lightsaber from his belt, and placed it before the Council.

“You speak of compassion,” he said, “but fear love. You speak of peace while raising children to know nothing else but obedience. You take infants before they are old enough to choose their own lives and call it wisdom.”

No one answered him.

Even Yoda looked tired.

Qui-Gon bowed once, turned, and walked out of the Jedi Temple beside Obi-Wan Kenobi for the last time.

What emerged afterward was not a rebellion against the Force, nor a crude imitation of the Sith. Qui-Gon called the new tradition the Dabi, an old word from a language spoken on forgotten frontier worlds. It meant something close to “those who listen while living.”

The Dabi rejected many of the Jedi Order’s oldest rules. They permitted attachment, family, marriage, and emotional honesty. They did not recruit toddlers or sever children from their parents. Training began only when someone was old enough to understand the choice being offered. The Force, Qui-Gon taught, flowed through living beings—not institutions—and fear of love was itself a path toward imbalance.

Their home became Mandalore.

Satine granted them sanctuary openly. To her, the Jedi had always claimed to be guardians of peace while too often arriving as instruments of distant political interests. The Dabi were different. Smaller. Personal. Present.

Obi-Wan remained Qui-Gon’s student even after his marriage, though the relationship between Master and apprentice became less rigid with time. Satine traveled often with them, refusing to become a ruler isolated behind palace walls. The people of Mandalore slowly grew used to the strange sight of a former Jedi Padawan walking the streets of Sundari beside the duchess, robes stirring in the desert wind while clansmen saluted him as family rather than outsider.

*(38 BBY)*

Two years later, another familiar figure arrived.

Dooku came to Mandalore alone.

He stepped from his shuttle into the pale light of Sundari’s landing platforms wearing no aristocratic ornaments despite reclaiming his title on Serenno. The years since leaving Coruscant had sharpened him somehow. He looked less burdened.

“You were right,” Qui-Gon told him after their first long conversation.

“No,” Dooku replied. “I was merely early.”

He joined the Dabi soon afterward.

The movement remained small, but its center hardened. Qui-Gon brought spiritual conviction. Dooku brought political understanding and aristocratic influence. Obi-Wan brought discipline and patience. Satine anchored them all with her belief that power existed to protect ordinary people rather than rule them.

For the first time in years, Qui-Gon felt hope instead of frustration.

Then, on a world at the edge of Hutt Space, he met a slave boy named Anakin Skywalker.

The boy was too old by Jedi standards. Too emotional. Too attached to his mother. Qui-Gon found the objections absurd now.

Anakin was gifted beyond anything he had ever encountered, but more than that, he was kind. Recklessly kind. The Force moved around him like a storm barely learning its own shape.

After winning the Boonta Eve podrace, Anakin earned his freedom. Qui-Gon offered him a choice—not recruitment, not destiny, simply a choice.

“Come with us,” he said. “Learn what you are.”

Anakin looked back toward his mother one last time before nodding.

The journey off Tatooine should have been simple.

Instead, Darth Maul found them.

The attack came at dusk near the edge of Mos Espa’s outskirts, where the desert flattened into open stone and the wind carried dust in long red veils across the ground. Maul emerged from nowhere, black robes snapping behind him as he ignited a double-bladed crimson lightsaber with a hiss like tearing metal.

Anakin froze.

Satine pulled him behind a cluster of vaporator machinery as Qui-Gon and Obi-Wan stepped forward together.

The Sith moved with terrifying speed.

Maul’s style was violent precision—constant motion, relentless pressure, every strike intended to drive opponents backward until panic opened them apart. Qui-Gon met him head-on, calm and measured, while Obi-Wan circled wide, searching for angles.

For a moment, it worked. The two fought with the instinctive coordination of years spent side by side. Qui-Gon absorbed Maul’s aggression while Obi-Wan disrupted his rhythm from the flanks.

Then Maul saw Satine.

The realization changed everything.

This was not a Jedi Master and apprentice. This was a bonded family.

And where orthodox Jedi protected life from principle, these two protected specific people.

Maul pivoted abruptly, breaking from Qui-Gon and lunging toward the vaporators where Satine shielded Anakin behind her.

Obi-Wan reacted before thought caught up.

The controlled restraint of Form III vanished instantly. His blade crashed against Maul’s in a sudden explosion of strength and forward momentum so fierce it nearly knocked the Sith off balance. Qui-Gon felt the shift immediately. Obi-Wan was no longer defending. He was hunting.

Blue light hammered against red in brutal arcs as Obi-Wan drove Maul backward across the stone. The Sith had expected serenity, detachment, caution. Instead he found rage sharpened into focus by fear for someone loved.

Obi-Wan’s stance changed mid-fight, abandoning the defensive precision Maul had studied. His strikes became heavier, more aggressive, echoing the raw power foundations of Form V in ways even Qui-Gon had never seen from him before.

For an instant, Maul misjudged the tempo.

That instant nearly killed him.

Obi-Wan battered one blade aside, stepped through the opening, and slashed close enough across Maul’s chest that sparks burst from scorched armor. Qui-Gon pressed simultaneously from the opposite flank, forcing Maul into a sudden retreat rather than a clean disengagement.

The Sith sprang backward onto a rocky rise, glaring at them through the desert wind.

Not afraid.

But uncertain.

This was wrong. Jedi were not supposed to fight like this. Their attachments were supposed to weaken them, cloud them, divide them. Instead the connection between Qui-Gon and Obi-Wan had made them more dangerous, not less.

For several long seconds, nobody moved.

Then Maul extinguished his saber and disappeared into the deepening dark beyond the dunes.

Anakin emerged slowly from cover after the silence settled, staring wide-eyed at Obi-Wan.

Obi-Wan himself looked shaken by what had happened. Satine touched his arm gently, grounding him before the adrenaline could carry him somewhere darker.

Qui-Gon said nothing at first.

But as they boarded their ship and left Tatooine behind, he found himself wondering whether the Jedi Order had misunderstood attachment for a thousand years.

# Part 2

*(22 BBY)*

Over the following decade, the Dabi ceased to be a curiosity.

What had begun as Qui-Gon Jinn and a fallen Padawan living in quiet exile on Mandalore slowly hardened into something undeniable: a functioning Force tradition entirely outside the authority of the Jedi Order. They trained students. They mediated disputes between clans and systems beyond Republic attention. Pilgrims and wanderers began seeking them out. More troubling still, they endured.

The Jedi Council had expected collapse.

Instead, the Dabi remained remarkably stable.

No fallen disciples. No dark side cults. No crusades against the Republic. The Order watched with increasing discomfort as one of their oldest assumptions—that attachment inevitably led to corruption—failed repeatedly in plain sight.

The matter became impossible to ignore once rumors began spreading through the Temple that Qui-Gon’s newest apprentice might be the Chosen One itself.

Anakin Skywalker’s existence circulated first as whispers among archivists and Masters who remembered the old prophecy. Then came reports from outer systems: a boy barely into adolescence piloting with impossible reflexes, sensing danger before it emerged, moving through the Force with unnerving instinctive power.

Some on the Council argued the Dabi had become dangerous.

They had already lost Count Dooku, one of the Order’s greatest Masters. They had lost Qui-Gon Jinn. They had lost Obi-Wan Kenobi, once considered among the most promising Jedi of his generation. And now the supposed Chosen One was growing up under their teachings instead of the Jedi’s.

Mace Windu privately suggested confronting them directly before the schism deepened further.

Yoda refused.

“Dark, they are not,” he said simply. “Watched them, we have. Consistent in the light side, remarkably so.”

That did not ease the Council’s unease.

But no action was taken.

By twenty-two BBY, Anakin Skywalker was nineteen years old, broad-shouldered and intense, carrying himself with the easy confidence of someone who had never been taught to fear his own emotions. He was still considered a Padawan within the Dabi tradition, though Qui-Gon supervised him less and less each year.

Anakin’s gifts were obvious to everyone now.

So were his flaws.

He was impatient, fiercely protective, impulsive when angry, and carried a dangerous tendency to throw himself entirely into whatever—or whoever—he loved. Yet unlike the Jedi Temple initiates, Anakin had grown up surrounded by people who confronted emotion openly instead of suppressing it. When he struggled, he was not isolated. Obi-Wan argued with him constantly. Satine counseled him gently. Dooku challenged him intellectually. Qui-Gon listened more than he lectured.

It made him powerful.

It also made him difficult to manipulate.

When Senator Padmé Amidala survived an assassination attempt shortly after arriving on Coruscant, the Dabi agreed to send Anakin to Naboo as her protector while the situation unfolded. Officially, it was a security arrangement. In practice, everyone except perhaps Qui-Gon understood immediately what would happen.

Anakin and Padmé recognized one another the moment they met again.

Ten years earlier, on Tatooine, she had seen a lonely slave boy staring up at the stars while talking about freedom as if it were something sacred. Now that boy had become a young man with intensity burning just beneath his composure.

Padmé had changed too. The queenly formality had softened into something wiser and sadder after years inside the Republic Senate.

They fell in love with startling speed.

Not secretly. Not shamefully.

Simply honestly.

While Anakin wandered Naboo’s lakes and forests with Padmé, another shadow arrived on Mandalore.

Maul came alone.

Darth Sidious had given him a single command:

“Eliminate all the Dabi.”

Maul obeyed without question.

He entered Sundari under cover of night and found exactly the targets he wanted: Qui-Gon, Obi-Wan, and Dooku together in one of the Dabi’s stone meditation chambers overlooking the city.

The duel that followed tore through the hall like a storm.

Maul attacked first, crimson blades spinning in lethal circles as he drove directly toward Qui-Gon. But the Dabi no longer fought like Jedi Masters paired beside temporary allies. They fought like family.

Dooku’s elegant Makashi precision hemmed Maul in from one side while Obi-Wan pressed from the other with increasingly aggressive power. Qui-Gon remained at the center, calm and immovable, forcing Maul constantly off balance.

The Sith adapted quickly.

He separated Dooku first, hammering at the older man with bursts of speed designed to exploit age and stamina. Then he turned suddenly toward Obi-Wan, recognizing him as the most emotionally reactive of the three.

It was a mistake.

Years earlier on Tatooine, Maul had glimpsed what happened when Obi-Wan fought to protect someone he loved. Now he encountered the perfected version of it.

Obi-Wan met Maul head-on with terrifying force.

Blue and red light crashed through the chamber in violent arcs. Obi-Wan’s style had evolved far beyond the restrained Soresu discipline he once cultivated as a Jedi Padawan. The defensive mastery remained, but now it merged fluidly into devastating counterattacks powered by Form V aggression whenever openings appeared.

Maul pressed harder, snarling with fury, but Obi-Wan refused to yield even a step.

Then Qui-Gon shifted Maul’s footing with a subtle Force push.

Dooku struck low.

Maul staggered for half a second.

And Obi-Wan took him apart.

The blue blade swept cleanly through Maul’s torso at the waist.

For a brief moment the Sith remained standing, disbelief burning in his yellow eyes. Then his body split in two and collapsed onto the stone floor of the chamber.

Silence followed.

Obi-Wan stood motionless, chest rising hard with breath, staring down at the remains of the man who had hunted them for over a decade.

“He was sent,” Dooku said quietly.

Nobody needed to ask by whom.

Not long afterward, Chancellor Palpatine himself arrived on Mandalore.

Officially, the visit concerned trade disputes and rising tensions within the Republic. In truth, Palpatine sought Dooku specifically.

The two men spoke privately for hours.

Palpatine was careful, patient, persuasive. He spoke of corruption in the Senate, of corporate interests strangling the Republic, of weak leadership allowing suffering to spread unchecked across the galaxy. He spoke about strength. Order. Necessary reform.

Dooku listened.

More than listened.

For a time, he was captivated.

Palpatine understood exactly which wounds to touch: Dooku’s disappointment in the Republic, his frustration with stagnation, his belief that civilization required firm guidance to survive.

Then Satine entered the room.

She took one look at the Chancellor and immediately sensed something wrong beneath the polished warmth.

“I don’t trust you,” she said bluntly.

Palpatine smiled pleasantly. “Duchess, I assure you—”

“No,” Satine interrupted. “Leave.”

Dooku looked between them in silence.

Palpatine tried once more, softer this time. “Surely Count Dooku understands the necessity of difficult decisions—”

A red lightsaber ignited beside the Chancellor’s throat.

Dooku stood from his chair slowly, expression unreadable.

“You heard the Duchess,” he said.

For the first time, something cold flickered behind Palpatine’s eyes.

Then it vanished beneath another practiced smile.

He departed Mandalore shortly afterward.

Not long after, on Naboo, Anakin Skywalker woke screaming from visions of his mother dying.

The Force hit him like a blade through the chest: pain, fear, endless desert darkness.

He and Padmé left immediately for Tatooine.

Back on Mandalore, the others felt it too. Not the details, but Anakin’s distress radiating violently through the Force. Qui-Gon sent Obi-Wan and Satine after him without hesitation.

They found Anakin at the Lars homestead, frantic and half-consumed by fear.

Anakin and Obi-Wan went into the desert together to search for Shmi Skywalker while Padmé and Satine remained behind.

They found her in a Tusken Raider camp.

Broken. Dying.

Shmi lived long enough to see her son one final time before the life left her eyes in Anakin’s arms.

The rage came instantly.

Obi-Wan felt it surge through the Force like fire breaking through dry brush. Years earlier, on Tatooine, he might have lost control entirely. Now he moved immediately beside Anakin, grounding him before grief transformed into slaughter.

“She’s gone,” Obi-Wan said quietly, one hand gripping Anakin’s shoulder hard enough to anchor him. “But you are still here.”

Anakin trembled violently.

For several terrible seconds Obi-Wan thought he might still snap.

Then the fury broke into grief instead.

Anakin buried his face against his mother’s body and wept.

The Tusken camp remained untouched.

Together, Obi-Wan and Anakin carried Shmi’s body back across the desert beneath the fading suns.

After the funeral, Anakin did not want to leave Padmé. Padmé did not want to leave him. So she traveled with the Dabi back to Mandalore alongside C-3PO and R2-D2.

There, she found herself spending long evenings in discussion with Dooku.

They spoke endlessly about the Republic’s failures: corruption in the Senate, the growing influence of corporate alliances, entire systems abandoned by distant politicians. By then the Trade Federation and its allies had begun consolidating into something larger, rebranding themselves openly as the Separatist movement.

But she understood more of Dooku’s criticism than she wanted to admit.

Then Qui-Gon disappeared investigating a bounty hunter named Jango Fett.

The trail led him to Geonosis.

It was a trap.

The Separatists captured him before he could escape and prepared him for public execution inside the vast Petranaki arena. When the rest of the Dabi learned what had happened, they immediately launched a rescue mission.

Anakin and Padmé insisted on joining despite every objection.

The Dabi fought together at the center of it all.

Qui-Gon moved with astonishing calm despite his age, his green blade flowing through droids and Geonosian guards alike. Obi-Wan fought beside Satine protectively whenever blaster fire closed near her position. Dooku carved elegant paths through enemies with precise economy.

At one point Qui-Gon became trapped beneath converging lines of battle droids. Blaster fire rained toward him from every direction.

Anakin hurled himself straight into the crossfire without hesitation.

His lightsaber became a blur of blue light. Droids fell apart around him in molten pieces as he drove forward with almost frightening ferocity, cutting a path wide enough to drag Qui-Gon free.

Then Dooku stumbled.

It was nothing dramatic. Just a single misstep from an old man fighting beyond his limits.

A dozen battle droids opened fire instantly.

Dooku destroyed several before the bolts struck him.

He collapsed without a sound.

The Force itself seemed to recoil.

Obi-Wan shouted his name. Qui-Gon froze in visible shock. Satine dropped beside Dooku’s body while Anakin’s fury exploded outward hard enough to stagger nearby droids.

The remaining Dabi fought harder after that.

Desperately.

But there were too many battle droids. Thousands.

Defeat closed around them—

—and then the Republic arrived.

Gunships screamed overhead. Clone troopers poured into the arena in disciplined waves while Master Yoda descended at the center of the assault.

The Clone Wars had begun.

After the battle, amid the smoke and ruins of Geonosis, Yoda met privately with the surviving Dabi.

Neither side pretended their philosophical differences had vanished. The Jedi still distrusted attachment. The Dabi still believed the Order’s rigidity had helped create the galaxy’s crisis.

But the war changed everything.

“We seek the same thing,” Qui-Gon said quietly.

Yoda nodded once. “Different paths. Shared goal.”

A formal truce was declared that day between the Jedi Order and the Dabi.

Later, after the dead had been counted and the survivors tended to, Yoda joined them beside Dooku’s covered body.

No politics remained then. No arguments.

Only grief.

“He was my Padawan,” Yoda said softly.

“And my teacher,” Qui-Gon replied.

For a long time, none of them spoke.

In the days that followed, the enormity of what had happened settled over all of them heavily. War had arrived at last. Dooku was dead. Entire systems were mobilizing for conflict.

Shaken by how close they had come to losing each other entirely, Anakin and Padmé invited the remaining Dabi to Naboo.

There, beneath the quiet skies and soft waters of Varykino, they married.

No secrecy.

No hiding.

Qui-Gon Jinn blessed the union himself while Obi-Wan stood smiling beside Satine, and for one brief moment before the darkness of war fully descended upon the galaxy, the future seemed almost peaceful.

# Part 3

*(22 BBY)*

The wedding on Naboo lasted long into the evening.

Lanterns floated over the lake outside Varykino while music drifted softly through the villa’s open halls. For the first time since Geonosis, there was laughter again. Not forced relief, but genuine warmth. Padmé moved among senators and Naboo guests with quiet happiness beside Anakin, whose usual restless intensity had softened into something almost peaceful.

Obi-Wan and Satine stood together on one of the balconies overlooking the water while Qui-Gon watched them all from nearby with the faintest expression of satisfaction.

For a brief moment, it felt as though the galaxy had paused.

Then the news arrived.

A servant rushed into the hall pale with shock, carrying a datapad broadcasting emergency transmissions from Mandalore. The music died almost immediately as conversations fell silent one by one.

Satine took the datapad first.

And went cold.

Darth Maul had returned.

No one understood how. Reports conflicted wildly: impossible survival stories, cybernetic reconstruction, dark side rituals whispered by terrified witnesses. But the details scarcely mattered compared to what followed.

Maul had emerged from the shadows more hateful than before, consumed utterly by vengeance. Worse still, he had found Mandalore vulnerable.

Over the years of Satine’s rule, the planet had demilitarized heavily in pursuit of lasting peace. Many Mandalorians had embraced it. Others never truly accepted it. Warrior clans, traditionalists, anti-pacifist factions long simmering beneath the surface—all of them now rallied behind Maul.

He united them into a single violent movement: the Shadow Collective.

Then he invaded Mandalore.

The footage spread across the galaxy within hours.

Cities burned beneath the domes. Civilians fled through crowded transit tunnels while armed militants poured into Sundari. Without the Dabi present and with Mandalore’s defenses gutted by decades of pacifist policy, organized resistance collapsed almost immediately. Panic consumed the capital.

Satine watched in horror as her life’s work unraveled in real time.

“They’re running,” she whispered, staring at the screen. “They’re running because there’s no one left to protect them…”

Then Maul himself appeared in the broadcast.

The transmission shifted violently through smoke-filled palace corridors as he personally stormed Sundari’s royal complex. Bodies of guards and staff littered the halls behind him. Blaster fire echoed somewhere in the distance.

He walked directly toward the duchess’s private office chambers.

The entire galaxy watched.

Maul stopped before the great doors and turned toward the holocamera, yellow eyes blazing with naked hatred.

“Once,” he said, voice low and venomous, “I was sliced in half by Obi-Wan Kenobi.”

He ignited his lightsaber.

“Today, I have come to exact my revenge on him.”

The crimson blades hissed through the darkened corridor.

“I will make him pay the price for my crippling by personally executing his eternal love.”

With a violent gesture of the Force, the doors exploded inward.

Maul strode into the office.

And found it empty.

Nothing moved except the drifting smoke.

Back on Naboo, silence hung over the room.

Satine closed her eyes shakily.

Beside her, Obi-Wan let out a breath he did not realize he had been holding.

Beyond the fear and grief and disbelief, there was the smallest flicker of relief between them.

If they had returned to Mandalore after the wedding as originally planned, Satine would already be dead.

The realization settled over all of them heavily.

And with it came another truth:

They could no longer simply go home.

The Dabi turned to the Jedi Order for help.

The appeal was not easy. The Clone Wars had already begun stretching Republic resources thin, and Mandalore officially remained outside Republic jurisdiction. Some within the Jedi Council argued this was precisely the kind of political entanglement they should avoid.

But the presence of Maul changed everything.

A Sith now ruled Mandalore openly.

Qui-Gon spoke before the Council personally beside Obi-Wan and Satine.

“This is no longer about Mandalorian politics,” he said. “This is the dark side consolidating power.”

Mace Windu remained wary throughout the discussions. The relationship between the Jedi and the Dabi had improved after Geonosis, but distrust still lingered beneath the truce.

Yoda listened quietly for a long time before finally speaking.

“Divided against the Sith, we cannot remain.”

At last, both he and Windu agreed.

A joint operation was assembled: Jedi and Dabi fighting side by side alongside several clone regiments in an effort to liberate Mandalore from the Shadow Collective.

The assault began in fire.

Republic gunships screamed over Sundari while clone troopers descended into streets already scarred by weeks of urban warfare. Mandalorian loyalists emerged from hiding to support the attack, and soon the entire capital became a maze of blaster fire, collapsing infrastructure, and close-quarters combat.

The Shadow Collective fought savagely.

These were not frightened conscripts or distant Separatist droids. They were hardened warriors fighting for conquest, vengeance, or old Mandalorian ideals twisted by Maul into fanaticism.

And at the center of it all was Maul himself.

The Sith moved through the battlefield like hatred given form, striking unpredictably and vanishing before Republic forces could pin him down. He targeted not clone commanders or supply lines, but relationships.

He taunted the Jedi about the Dabi’s attachments.

He mocked the Dabi for fighting beside the very Order they once abandoned.

He tried constantly to fracture the alliance.

But neither side yielded.

Obi-Wan fought beside Windu without hesitation. Anakin coordinated naturally with clone commanders while Yoda protected civilians trapped beneath collapsing districts. Whatever philosophical differences remained between Jedi and Dabi, Maul’s brutality burned them away in the middle of battle.

The light side remained united.

Midway through the campaign, Qui-Gon finally confronted Maul directly.

They met in the shattered remains of one of Sundari’s outer transit hubs beneath flickering emergency lights and smoke-choked ceilings.

Qui-Gon fought beautifully.

Age had slowed him, but not diminished him. His connection to the Living Force flowed through every movement, calm and centered even amid chaos.

But Maul fought like a creature with nothing left except vengeance.

The duel ended brutally.

Maul drove his saber through Qui-Gon’s chest before anyone else could reach them.

For a moment, the battlefield itself seemed to stop.

Qui-Gon staggered backward, shock flickering across Obi-Wan’s face as he arrived too late. Anakin shouted in raw disbelief. Even Maul looked almost stunned by the enormity of what he had done.

Qui-Gon collapsed slowly into Obi-Wan’s arms.

There were words then, though only Obi-Wan truly heard them.

Something soft.

Something forgiving.

Then Qui-Gon Jinn was gone.

The battle afterward became merciless.

Obi-Wan buried his grief beneath absolute focus, fighting almost entirely defensively as though terrified of what would happen if he let emotion fully loose again. Anakin became frighteningly aggressive, forcing himself constantly back toward discipline whenever anger threatened to overtake him.

Maul noticed both.

And he adapted.

If Obi-Wan could not be broken directly, then Satine would be the weakness instead.

During one of the final clashes near Sundari’s central districts, elite Shadow Collective operatives struck suddenly through the chaos and seized Satine despite every effort to protect her. Clone troopers fell around her position while Mandalorian commandos dragged her away toward an extraction transport.

“Obi-Wan!” she cried.

Everything changed instantly.

Until that moment Obi-Wan had fought like a wall: controlled, measured, defensive.

Then the ground erupted.

The Force exploded outward from him in a violent shockwave that cracked durasteel beneath everyone nearby. Buildings trembled. Streets split open. Entire sections of the battlefield shook under a sudden localized earthquake born purely from emotion.

The operatives carrying Satine were thrown violently off their feet.

For a heartbeat, everyone froze.

Anakin moved first.

He launched himself through the chaos at impossible speed, cutting down two stunned commandos before they could recover and pulling Satine back toward Republic lines.

But Obi-Wan remained standing at the center of the fractured street, breathing hard, surrounded by trembling debris.

Something dark flickered around him.

Maul saw it immediately.

The Sith smiled.

“Come to the dark side,” he sneered. “You already stand at its edge.”

For one terrible instant, the temptation was real.

Obi-Wan’s grief, fear, rage, and desperation churned together violently through the Force. Qui-Gon was dead. Satine had nearly been taken. Mandalore was burning again despite everything they had built.

It would have been easy to let go.

But Anakin stepped beside him.

Not as a student.

As family.

And through the chaos of battle, Yoda’s voice reached him too.

“Calm,” the old Master said firmly. “In the center of darkness, find it.”

Obi-Wan closed his eyes.

The tremors slowly stopped.

When he opened them again, the darkness had receded.

Nearby, Mace Windu looked grimly toward Yoda.

“This is exactly why—”

Yoda raised a hand before he could finish.

“Now is not the time.”

The battle raged on.

Clone troopers engaged the Shadow Collective’s remaining elite warriors across the city while the Jedi and Dabi converged on Maul together at last.

Yoda.

Windu.

Obi-Wan.

Anakin.

The surviving defenders of Mandalore pressed him from every side through a final prolonged lightsaber duel that tore across palace courtyards and burning transit bridges alike.

Even Maul could not withstand them forever.

Eventually, surrounded and facing certain defeat, he chose survival instead.

The Sith vanished into the chaos once more.

Leaderless and shattered, the remaining forces of the Shadow Collective surrendered shortly afterward.

The liberation of Mandalore was complete.

But victory felt hollow.

Padmé had carried the unconscious Satine away from the front lines during the final battle, sheltering with her inside an abandoned structure far from the worst fighting. Satine’s injuries were severe, worsened by the violence of her capture and the battle itself.

She regained consciousness only after the fighting ended.

Obi-Wan found her there.

For a long moment neither spoke.

Outside, distant fires still burned across Sundari’s skyline.

And once again, scarred but surviving, Mandalore began the long process of rebuilding from war.

# Part 4

*(22 BBY)*

Polis Massa was quiet in a way that unsettled all of them.

The medical facility drifted through darkness suspended over the barren asteroid’s surface, far from the great hyperspace routes and far from Mandalore’s fires. Sterile corridors hummed softly with machinery while pale lights reflected against polished durasteel walls. Outside the transparisteel observation windows, stars hung motionless over endless black.

None of them believed it was.

The Sith had already returned once from impossible death.

No one intended to underestimate him again.

“You don’t have to guard me every second,” she told him one evening softly.

“Your breathing is uneven.”

The room was empty.

Qui-Gon.

Through deep study of the Living Force, he had discovered a way to preserve consciousness after death—not as a ghost bound unnaturally to the world, but as something quieter. A presence carried within the Cosmic Force itself.

The revelation stunned both of them.

Death itself suddenly seemed less absolute than they had ever believed.

“The light side belongs to all of us,” he argued. “Not just the Dabi.”

Qui-Gon agreed without hesitation.

And so, slowly, the knowledge began passing outward.

Their conversations drifted into profound territory: the relationship between the Living Force and the Cosmic Force, the danger of trying to dominate destiny rather than flow within it, the difference between serenity and emotional suppression.

Anakin absorbed the teachings eagerly.

Obi-Wan absorbed them carefully.

Qui-Gon’s guidance slowly reshaped both of them.

Qui-Gon’s voice flowed steadily through their thoughts.

“The Force does not divide life and death as sharply as beings do. Fear creates that division. The Cosmic Force—”

“Watch out!”

Calm. Steady.

“Patience.”

“Don’t chase his anger.”

Maul noticed too.

No armies.

No witnesses.

Only the three of them beneath cold starlight.

Not passive.

Immovable.

Steady.

Certain.

Weak.

Fear.

Not rage.

Not hatred.

Fear.

Qui-Gon’s voice returned softly through the Force.

“Spare him.”

Obi-Wan hesitated only briefly before extinguishing his blade.

The silence afterward felt enormous.

Maul stared upward at him in disbelief.

For several long moments, no one spoke.

Then something inside the Sith finally broke.

“Vengeance kept me alive. Rage. The need to make you suffer.”

“By Sidious.”

Maul’s eyes flickered toward him.

Dooku’s fascination with the Chancellor.

The Separatist crisis.

Maul’s survival.

The war itself.

All of it.

Maul exhaled shakily one final time.

And died.

For a long while, neither Obi-Wan nor Anakin moved.

The stars stretched endlessly around them while the body of Darth Maul lay still at their feet.

Then the two Dabi looked at each other.

And suddenly, everything made sense.

# Part 5

*(21 BBY)*

The moment Maul died, the Force convulsed.

Not violently, but deeply—like the galaxy itself inhaling sharply.

Obi-Wan stiffened first. Anakin felt it a heartbeat later.

Something immense had shifted.

Far away, cold fury ignited through the Force like a star collapsing inward.

Sidious knew.

The two Dabi looked at each other without needing words.

“He’s coming,” Obi-Wan said quietly.

Obi-Wan spoke next, direct and unembellished.

“There is absolutely no chance we defeat him in a duel.”

No one argued.

They had all felt glimpses of Palpatine’s power over the years without understanding what they were sensing. Now that the truth had surfaced fully, those fragments suddenly aligned into something terrifying.

Sidious was not merely dangerous.

He was likely the most powerful Sith Lord in generations.

“Then don’t fight him that way.”

Anakin looked at her.

Padmé’s eyes narrowed with the focus she usually reserved for Senate debates.

“I would personally appreciate any solution that doesn’t involve collapsing the entire facility around me.”

So did Anakin.

Then the alarms began.

A deep vibration rolled through the station as emergency systems activated across the corridors.

“He’s here,” Obi-Wan said.

The doors at the far end of the chamber slid open.

Palpatine entered without disguise now.

No kindly Chancellor’s smile. No warm public mask.

Only Darth Sidious.

“You failed.”

Sidious blinked.

Not from fear.

From surprise.

Padmé’s voice sharpened immediately.

“You spent decades engineering this galaxy into chaos and still lost control of it.”

The lightning flickered slightly.

“You failed as Chancellor,” she continued coldly. “The Republic rotted under your watch. You failed as a Sith because your own apprentice exposed you. And you failed as a mastermind because in the end everyone around you turned against you.”

Satine joined in before Sidious could respond.

“You know what’s remarkable?” she asked quietly. “After all your manipulation… nobody actually loves you.”

That struck deeper.

Sidious’s expression twitched.

“You ruled through fear,” Satine continued. “Maul feared you. Dooku feared becoming useless to you. The Senate feared your authority. But fear isn’t loyalty.”

The Sith Lord stared at them both now with visible disbelief.

Not because the words hurt him directly.

Because almost nobody had ever dared speak to him this way.

Padmé stepped closer.

“If you’re truly as powerful and inevitable as you think you are,” she said, “then stop hiding. Go back to Coruscant. Declare the Jedi enemies publicly. Tell the galaxy exactly who you are and see what happens.”

For a long moment, silence hung in the room.

Sidious’s eyes narrowed.

He was suspicious enough to sense danger somewhere beneath the challenge.

But his pride was stronger.

Always stronger.

“You think the Jedi can stop me?” he asked softly.

“No,” Padmé replied.

That answer pleased him.

And so the trap closed.

Sidious smiled faintly, turned, and departed the station without another strike.

The instant his presence vanished into hyperspace, Obi-Wan staggered slightly as another wave moved through the Force.

This time it carried not fury—

—but connection.

Maul’s death had torn open something strange between living minds already bound deeply through the Force. Without fully understanding how, Obi-Wan suddenly felt Yoda’s awareness brush against his own across impossible distance.

Then Mace Windu.

Thoughts flowed half-formed between them: Sidious exposed. Returning to Coruscant. Immediate action required.

No words were truly spoken.

The Jedi simply understood.

On Mandalore, Yoda and Windu reacted immediately.

No Council meeting. No Senate approval. No hesitation.

There was no time.

Together they assembled a small strike team consisting of themselves and three other Jedi Masters. Quietly, without alerting Republic command structures, they departed for Coruscant.

The infiltration relied almost entirely on the Force.

Yoda guided them through currents of perception so subtle that even the clone guards outside the Chancellor’s complex barely registered their passage. Once inside, the Jedi entered deep meditative concealment, masking not only their physical presence but their Force signatures as well.

The office became silent.

Waiting.

Then Sidious arrived.

The doors opened.

Palpatine strode inward confidently, cloak sweeping behind him as he entered his private chambers—

—and five Jedi attacked simultaneously.

Even Sidious nearly failed to react in time.

The opening strike came within inches of killing him outright before his crimson blade ignited in a violent blur.

The office exploded into chaos.

Sidious moved with horrifying speed.

Within seconds, three Jedi Masters were dead.

Lightning scorched entire chambers while Yoda’s green blade flashed through debris and Windu’s Vaapad pressed relentlessly against Sidious’s fury.

For the first time in decades, the Sith Lord was cornered.

Truly cornered.

At last, battered and exhausted, Sidious fell beneath their blades.

But as he died, he smiled.

And with his final breath, he spoke two words into the galaxy.

“Order 66.”

Instantly, Yoda, Windu, Obi-Wan, and Anakin all felt it.

A massive disturbance rippled outward through the Force as clone troopers across thousands of battlefronts suddenly turned on their Jedi commanders.

Blasters rose.

Fear exploded.

Betrayal ignited.

Then—

five seconds later—

it stopped.

Completely.

Across the galaxy, clone troopers froze in confusion as their weapons locked rigidly in place.

On Kamino, one scientist watched events unfold with grim satisfaction.

Months earlier, this Kaminoan had grown increasingly suspicious of the Republic’s true intentions regarding the clone army. Quietly examining the neural architecture of the inhibitor chips, he eventually uncovered their hidden purpose.

Order 66.

The systematic extermination of the Jedi.

Disillusioned with both Palpatine and the Republic that enabled him, the scientist devised a countermeasure in total secrecy.

He created a second command: Order 67.

Unlike the original inhibitor chip, this new mechanism did not compel violence. It froze it. The moment activated, affected clones became physically incapable of hostile action. More ingeniously still, the modification spread contagiously between nearby clones through subtle neurological transmission systems hidden during standard maintenance cycles.

Within a year, nearly the entire clone army unknowingly carried it.

Only the oldest units and the newest generations remained untouched.

The Kaminoan had told no one.

Not the Jedi.

Not the Senate.

No one.

And when he saw nearby clones suddenly tense in preparation for Order 66, he simply said aloud:

“Order 67.”

The Republic’s greatest purge collapsed before it truly began.

In the chaotic days that followed, the galaxy nearly tore itself apart anyway.

News spread rapidly that Jedi Masters had assassinated Chancellor Palpatine inside his own office. Many systems immediately branded the Jedi traitors attempting a coup against the Republic.

Then the truth began surfacing.

One Separatist leader after another quietly corroborated the impossible story: Palpatine had indeed been Darth Sidious. The war itself had been manipulated from both sides by the Sith.

Ironically, many Separatist leaders felt relief upon learning of his death.

For years they had believed themselves freedom fighters struggling against Republic corruption. Sidious had twisted their movement constantly toward greater violence and authoritarianism.

Now, without him controlling events from above, the Separatists could finally pursue what many of them had genuinely wanted from the beginning:

Independence.

The Republic itself barely remained stable.

War exhaustion, the revelation of Sith infiltration, Maul’s devastation of Mandalore, and the near-collapse caused by Order 66 left the Senate desperate for someone trusted.

In the end, they elevated Bail Organa as emergency Chancellor.

Bail moved carefully but decisively.

Rather than prolong the Clone Wars indefinitely, he formally recognized Separatist sovereignty over systems already under their control. Negotiations followed. Borders stabilized. Fighting gradually slowed across the galaxy.

Not everyone accepted the peace.

But enough did.

And so, after years of manipulation, war, and fear, the galaxy finally began the slow work of rebuilding itself.

# Part 6

*(21 BBY)*

Peace did not arrive all at once.

It came unevenly, awkwardly, through reconstruction projects, ceasefire negotiations, reopened hyperspace lanes, and worlds slowly realizing they could sleep through the night again without hearing news of another battlefront collapsing.

The galaxy had been wounded too deeply for simple endings.

But for the first time in generations, the future no longer felt trapped inside someone else’s manipulation.

The Dabi changed too.

With Qui-Gon gone physically and Dooku dead, the tradition could not realistically survive forever as a completely separate order sustained only by Anakin and Obi-Wan. Both of them understood that eventually. More importantly, the Jedi Order itself had begun changing in ways neither of them once thought possible.

Yoda changed most of all.

The old Grand Master never fully abandoned the core of Jedi discipline, but after the war—and after witnessing the Dabi endure tragedy without falling into darkness—he slowly began admitting truths the Order had resisted for centuries.

Attachment was dangerous.

But so was fear of attachment.

The difference mattered.

Qui-Gon’s continued presence through the Force accelerated everything. First as a voice, then eventually as a fully manifested Force spirit, he became impossible for the Jedi Council to dismiss. Even the more conservative Masters found themselves instinctively respecting him. After all, Qui-Gon had independently discovered a path beyond death itself through the light side alone.

That carried enormous weight.

And so reforms slowly began.

The Jedi loosened their restrictions surrounding family and attachment. Recruitment practices changed gradually as well; fewer infants were taken permanently from their homes, and more emphasis was placed on consent and connection rather than institutional ownership. The Order also stepped back from direct political entanglement with the Republic, determined never again to become blind instruments of state power.

Not every Jedi agreed.

But enough did.

*(20 BBY)*

In time, the Dabi formally rejoined the Jedi Order—not as defeated dissidents returning in shame, but as a respected branch whose ideas had helped transform the greater whole.

The old divide faded.

Padmé Amidala returned to politics almost immediately after Bail Organa began stabilizing the Republic. She understood too clearly how fragile the galaxy remained to stay away for long.

“The Senate still needs voices willing to tell the truth,” she told Anakin one evening.

Anakin smiled faintly.

“Then I suppose Coruscant needs me too.”

He followed her gladly.

Their life together never became quiet exactly, but it became real in ways Anakin once thought impossible. No secrecy. No fear of discovery. No double life tearing him apart from within.

When their twins were born soon afterward, there was no tragedy attached to it. No desperate final words. No funeral pyres.

Only joy.

Obi-Wan remained on Polis Massa with Satine for many more months after everyone else departed. The medical specialists eventually concluded that they could do no more for her physically. Satine recovered enough to walk, govern, and live independently again, but the injuries she sustained during Mandalore’s liberation left lingering weakness that would likely never disappear completely.

She accepted that more calmly than Obi-Wan did.

Over the years that followed, Obi-Wan’s path changed quietly. He became increasingly introspective, drawn deeper into meditation and defensive Force disciplines. His mastery of Soresu eventually became legendary within the reformed Jedi Order—not simply as combat technique, but as philosophy.

Endurance.

Patience.

Stillness without passivity.

At the same time, he immersed himself in the study of Force healing traditions across the galaxy, searching endlessly for ways to ease Satine’s lingering pain and restore what war had taken from her.

Satine eventually returned to Mandalore.

The planet had rebuilt itself once again under her leadership, scarred but enduring as it always had. Even weakened physically, she remained wildly beloved by her people. Many Mandalorians who once opposed her pacifism came to see her survival itself as proof of strength.

Obi-Wan remained beside her always after that.

Not as exile.

Not as failed Jedi.

Simply as her husband.

*(17 BBY)*

Years later, after the galaxy had grown calmer and safer, they decided to have a child.

A daughter.

They named her Stella.

And so Obi-Wan Kenobi—once the solemn Padawan expected to embody perfect Jedi detachment—spent much of his later life tending gardens within the palace courtyards of Sundari, carrying his daughter through echoing hallways, studying ancient Force philosophies late into the night, and quietly ensuring no harm ever came near his family again.

It suited him far more than he once would have admitted.

Meanwhile, Qui-Gon Jinn’s reputation within the Jedi Order only grew with time.

At first he was respected.

Then revered.

Younger Jedi studied his teachings constantly. Older Masters increasingly admitted that many of the reforms preserving the Order had originated with him decades earlier. Even those who once considered him dangerously unorthodox gradually accepted that Qui-Gon had understood something essential about the Force long before the rest of them did.

Not control.

Not suppression.

Balance.

*(14 BBY)*

Years later, after Bail Organa finally stepped down from the Chancellorship, Padmé Amidala succeeded him.

Bail himself returned happily to Alderaan.

Publicly, he cited exhaustion from years spent rebuilding the Republic after the Clone Wars and the Sith crisis. Privately, most suspected he simply wanted to spend the remainder of his life with his queen and daughters away from galactic catastrophe for once.

No one blamed him.

Under Padmé’s leadership, the Republic remained imperfect—as all governments are—but more cautious about centralized power than before. The memory of Palpatine lingered like a permanent scar across galactic politics.

And slowly, over the decades, the war generation grew old.

*(4 BBY)*

At the very end of his life, Yoda retreated increasingly into contemplation. By then the Jedi Order had transformed almost beyond recognition from the institution he once led during the Republic’s decline.

One evening, surrounded by students beneath the twilight canopy of the Temple gardens, Yoda simply smiled softly and said:

“Fully one with the Force, I have become.”

Then he closed his eyes.

And peacefully passed beyond death.

Not long afterward, his spirit returned.

The second fully realized Force spirit the Jedi had ever known.

There is an old story told among later generations of Jedi about what happened afterward. Some say that deep within the luminous currents of the Cosmic Force, the spirit of Qui-Gon Jinn encountered the spirit of Yoda once more.

No arguments remained between them then.

No councils. No schisms. No war.

Only understanding.

And somewhere beyond the veil of death itself, the old Jedi and old Dabi traditions finally met not as rivals, but as companions walking the same endless path together.

The Force remained.

And the galaxy endured.

The end.

# Interlude 1.1

*(32 BBY)*

The request reached Mandalore under official Republic seal.

A Trade Federation blockade had surrounded Naboo under the pretense of a taxation dispute. Negotiations had stalled. Communications from the planet were fragmentary and increasingly desperate. From the Dabi.

“Exactly.”

“Yes,” Satine and Qui-Gon answered together.

Theed had already fallen.

Battle droids patrolled elegant streets while smoke curled upward from isolated fires across the city. The occupation looked efficient rather than chaotic, which somehow made it worse.

At first glance she appeared younger than Obi-Wan expected.

At second glance, far more dangerous.

Even surrounded by armed droids and Federation officials, Padmé Amidala carried herself with cold composure. Her handmaidens mirrored her perfectly, impossible to distinguish at a glance beneath ceremonial makeup and royal clothing.

Qui-Gon cut through blast doors while Obi-Wan cleared corridors ahead of the group. Satine coordinated escape routes with Captain Panaka as palace guards regrouped around them. Amidala herself remained unnervingly calm throughout the firefight.

The droid emitted an indignant series of beeps.

“My people are still there.”

“Not ideal,” Obi-Wan admitted.

“Understatement,” Satine muttered.

The silver starship altered course and vanished into hyperspace toward the desert world waiting ahead.

# Interlude 1.2

The Nubian royal starship lifted out of Mos Espa beneath a haze of heat and engine glare, its silver hull catching the last red light of Tatooine’s evening. Inside, the atmosphere was quieter than before they had landed there.

They departed Coruscant two days later.

The atmosphere shifted because of it. Panaka spoke to her more formally. Sabé relaxed slightly without the constant strain of performance. ### Section 1.2.4

Naboo greeted them with smoke.

# Interlude 2.1

*(22 BBY)*

Rain swept softly against the high windows of Sundari’s western gallery, turning the lights of the city beyond into long silver smears across the glass. Mandalore at night always felt strangely suspended from the rest of the galaxy—quiet in a way Coruscant never was.

Padmé Amidala stood near the window with a cup of untouched tea cooling in her hands.

Across from her, Count Dooku sat perfectly composed in a curved stone chair, fingers folded over the silver head of his cane. Age had slowed him physically but sharpened him everywhere else. Even at rest he carried the unnerving stillness of someone perpetually measuring the world.

Satine sat between them on a low couch, though “between” was becoming increasingly literal. The discussions had been happening for days now—after dinners, after council meetings, sometimes deep into the night—and they always seemed to settle into the same gravitational pull: Padmé defending the Republic, Dooku dismantling it piece by piece, Satine refusing to fully surrender to either side.

Anakin lingered nearby beside Obi-Wan and Qui-Gon, pretending not to listen while very obviously listening.

“You still believe the Senate can correct itself,” Dooku said to Padmé at last.

“I believe institutions matter,” Padmé replied evenly. “The Republic is flawed, not irredeemable.”

Dooku gave a faint smile.

“The Republic is not merely flawed. It is structurally captured. Entire sectors are governed by commercial interests while senators debate procedure for months at a time. Corporations possess armies. Banking clans influence legislation openly. Slavery persists in the Outer Rim because the Core Worlds find abolition inconvenient.”

Padmé’s expression tightened slightly at that.

“You think I don’t know that?”

“I think,” Dooku said calmly, “that you still mistake decency within individuals for health within systems.”

Satine leaned back, watching them both carefully.

“The problem,” she said, “is that both of you are arguing as though centralized power naturally trends toward virtue if the correct people hold it.”

Dooku glanced toward her. “And you believe it does not?”

“I believe power expands to justify itself. Always.” Satine folded one leg beneath herself. “The Republic claims to preserve peace while continuously empowering institutions capable of coercion on a galactic scale. The Separatists claim to desire freedom while consolidating military authority under corporate leadership. They are reflections of one another.”

Padmé shook her head. “That’s too cynical.”

“No,” Satine said quietly. “It’s historical.”

The rain deepened outside.

Padmé crossed the room slowly before answering.

“The Republic has prevented galaxy-wide war for a thousand years.”

“Has it?” Dooku asked. “Or has it merely displaced suffering far enough from Coruscant that the political class can pretend stability exists?”

Padmé stopped walking.

Dooku’s voice remained maddeningly calm.

“You have seen the Mid Rim worlds. You have seen the Outer Rim. Entire populations abandoned by senatorial bureaucracy while trade guilds dictate local survival. The Republic protects commerce far more reliably than people.”

“That doesn’t justify the Separatists,” Padmé shot back immediately. “The Trade Federation invaded Naboo. The Commerce Guild exploits entire systems. The corporations gathering around this movement aren’t idealists, they’re opportunists.”

“Of course they are,” Dooku replied.

The bluntness caught even Anakin off guard.

Dooku continued before anyone could interrupt.

“Political movements are never composed solely of idealists. The Republic itself certainly is not. But beneath the corporate interests lies something real: planetary systems no longer believe Coruscant represents them.”

“That’s because men like you encourage secession every time the Senate fails,” Padmé said.

“And men like me emerge because the Senate fails.”

The words landed heavily.

Satine watched Padmé closely before speaking again.

“You’re both trapped inside the same assumption,” she said. “You both think galactic peace must be engineered from above.”

Padmé frowned slightly. “And what do you propose instead?”

“Smaller sovereignties. Strong local governments. Mutual defense compacts without centralized domination.” Satine gestured toward the city beyond the window. “Mandalore survived because we stopped waiting for distant institutions to save us.”

Dooku’s expression sharpened faintly.

“And when neighboring systems threaten you? When piracy rises? When resources become scarce? Decentralization sounds noble until hard decisions become necessary.”

“It becomes dangerous when people believe hard decisions require authoritarian structures,” Satine replied.

Dooku leaned forward slightly now, the old aristocratic intensity emerging through his composure.

“You mistake discipline for authoritarianism. Civilization cannot survive indefinitely on idealism alone. The Republic is paralyzed precisely because nobody possesses the authority to act decisively.”

Padmé answered before Satine could.

“And who should possess that authority? You?”

The room went still for a moment.

Dooku smiled faintly again, but there was sadness in it now.

“That,” he said softly, “is the galaxy’s problem. Every conversation about reform eventually collapses into fear of tyranny, and not without reason. History is full of men convinced they alone could save civilization.”

“Usually while destroying it,” Padmé said.

“Sometimes while preserving it.”

Obi-Wan shifted slightly against the far wall, arms folded.

Anakin noticed Qui-Gon watching Dooku very carefully now.

Satine broke the silence first.

“The Republic’s greatest failure,” she said, “is not corruption. Corruption exists everywhere. It’s that ordinary people no longer feel seen by it. You cannot govern trillions through abstraction forever.”

Padmé nodded reluctantly at that.

“I agree with you there.”

“But your solution,” Dooku interjected, “is sentiment. Localism. Moral appeals. The galaxy is too large for governance built entirely on trust.”

“And yours,” Satine replied, “risks becoming rule by enlightened elites who believe themselves wiser than everyone else.”

Dooku did not deny it immediately.

That was the troubling part.

Finally he said, “Most societies are already governed that way. The difference is whether the elites are competent.”

Padmé exhaled sharply through her nose.

“That may be the most aristocratic thing I’ve ever heard.”

“It is also historically accurate.”

She looked genuinely frustrated now.

“You speak about democracy as though it’s naive.”

“No,” Dooku said. “I speak about democracy as though it is fragile. Because it is.”

The rain softened again outside.

For a while nobody spoke.

Then Padmé said quietly, “The Republic still matters to people. Despite everything. It represents the idea that worlds can cooperate without conquest.”

Dooku’s expression softened for the first time that evening.

“And that,” he admitted, “is worth preserving if it can be saved.”

The words surprised everyone slightly.

Even Satine looked at him differently after that.

Padmé studied him for a long moment.

“Then why do I get the feeling,” she asked carefully, “that part of you has already stopped believing it can be?”

Dooku looked toward the rain-streaked skyline of Sundari.

For the first time all night, he seemed tired.

“Because I have lived long enough,” he said quietly, “to watch institutions choose survival over virtue again and again.”

Silence settled over the room.

Anakin looked between them thoughtfully. Padmé still believed systems could be repaired from within. Satine believed concentrated power itself was the danger. Dooku believed civilization eventually required hard authority or it decayed into paralysis.

None of them were entirely wrong.

That was what made the conversation unsettling.

# Interlude 2.2

*(22 BBY)*

### Section 2.2.1

The Aldera Royal Palace seemed less constructed than composed.

White towers rose from the mountainsides like carved moonlight, connected by suspended bridges and terraces overflowing with gardens that glowed beneath the evening lanterns. Water moved everywhere on Alderaan—through narrow channels along polished stone paths, through quiet reflecting pools beneath archways, down silver waterfalls spilling into the valleys below.

Anakin understood immediately why Padmé loved it.

The invitation from Bail and Breha Organa had arrived only a week earlier: a formal celebration for Queen Breha’s birthday at the Grand Ballroom of the royal palace. Diplomats, artists, senators, scholars, nobles, and planetary delegates from across the Republic would attend.

The Dabi were specifically invited by name.

That alone said something about how much their reputation had changed.

Years ago, the Jedi Order would likely have viewed such an event with suspicion or mild disdain—a distraction at best, political entanglement at worst. But the Dabi moved through society differently. They did not isolate themselves from ordinary life, and so evenings like this became part of their world.

Even Dooku agreed to come.

Especially Dooku, if Anakin were being honest.

The older man carried himself through aristocratic settings with almost predatory ease, as though the entire architecture of galactic nobility had once been built specifically for him.

The Grand Ballroom opened beneath enormous crystal arches suspended high overhead like frozen waves. Music drifted through the chamber from a live ensemble stationed along one side of the hall—strings, soft percussion, long Alderaanian wind instruments that gave everything a strangely floating feeling.

Hundreds of guests moved across the polished floor.

Some danced formally. Others lingered in conversation beneath the chandeliers or out on the moonlit balconies overlooking the mountains.

Anakin hated formalwear almost immediately.

“This collar is an assassination attempt,” he muttered under his breath.

Padmé smirked beside him without looking up from adjusting one of his sleeves.

“You’re surviving heroically.”

“I’ve fought battle droids more comfortable than this.”

“You haven’t fought battle droids.”

“Fine,” Anakin admitted reluctantly. “Hypothetical battle droids.”

Nearby, Obi-Wan was faring only slightly better.

Unlike Anakin, he wore elegance naturally when forced into it, but there remained something faintly uncomfortable about seeing a man who once slept in caves during the Mandalorian Civil War now standing beneath crystal chandeliers in dark formal robes beside the Duchess of Mandalore.

Satine, naturally, looked completely at home.

“You’re doing the face,” she informed him quietly.

Obi-Wan blinked. “What face?”

“The diplomatic hostage face.”

“I do not have a diplomatic hostage face.”

“You absolutely do.”

Qui-Gon, standing nearby with a glass of wine he had no intention of finishing, looked deeply amused by the entire exchange.

Dooku looked offended on Obi-Wan’s behalf.

“The boy is perfectly composed,” he said.

“You taught him aristocratic posture for six months,” Satine replied.

“And it worked.”

Across the ballroom, Bail Organa approached through the crowd with the easy warmth that made him unusually well-liked even among ideological rivals.

“Master Qui-Gon,” he greeted, inclining his head respectfully. “Duchess Satine. Senator Amidala. Count Dooku.”

Then he looked toward Anakin and Obi-Wan.

“And the troublemakers.”

Anakin grinned immediately.

Obi-Wan sighed. “We are never escaping that reputation, are we?”

“No,” Bail said pleasantly.

Breha arrived moments later, drawing Satine and Padmé into conversation almost instantly. The three women disappeared together toward one of the balcony gardens while Bail began discussing Outer Rim shipping disputes with Dooku in tones suggesting he genuinely wanted Dooku’s opinion rather than merely tolerating it.

That left Anakin, Obi-Wan, and Qui-Gon temporarily abandoned near the ballroom floor.

Anakin watched Padmé laughing softly at something Breha said.

“She likes it here,” he said quietly.

Qui-Gon glanced toward him.

“Yes.”

Anakin hesitated before speaking again.

“Do you ever think about how strange this all is?”

“Constantly,” Obi-Wan answered before Qui-Gon could.

Anakin gestured vaguely around the ballroom.

“I mean… this. Us.” He lowered his voice slightly. “The Jedi would probably consider half of this scandalous.”

“Only half?” Obi-Wan asked dryly.

Qui-Gon smiled faintly into his wine.

“The Jedi spent centuries fearing attachment because attachment creates vulnerability,” he said. “They were not entirely wrong.”

Anakin frowned slightly. “But they thought vulnerability itself was failure.”

“Yes,” Qui-Gon said quietly. “And that was their mistake.”

Before Anakin could answer, the music shifted.

A slower arrangement drifted through the ballroom now, softer and more intimate than the earlier ceremonial pieces.

Padmé reappeared almost immediately.

“Anakin,” she said, extending a hand toward him.

He stared at it for half a second too long.

Obi-Wan closed his eyes briefly in secondhand embarrassment.

“Go,” he muttered.

Anakin recovered quickly enough to take her hand and allow himself to be pulled onto the dance floor.

He was not terrible at dancing.

This surprised everyone, including Anakin.

“You knew how to do this?” Padmé asked suspiciously as they moved into rhythm.

“No.”

“You’re lying.”

“Dooku made me learn.”

Padmé laughed softly. “Of course he did.”

Around them, the ballroom blurred gradually into music and motion.

For perhaps the first time in years, Anakin felt entirely present in his own life instead of chasing something just beyond reach.

No secrecy.
No fear.
No hiding.

Just Padmé’s hand in his and warm Alderaanian light reflecting across polished marble.

At the edge of the floor, Obi-Wan watched them with folded arms.

Satine appeared beside him quietly.

“You’re smiling.”

“I am not.”

“You are, actually.”

Obi-Wan exhaled softly through his nose.

“He’s happy,” he admitted.

“Yes.”

“That terrifies me a little.”

Satine’s expression softened.

“Because now he has something to lose?”

Obi-Wan nodded once.

For a moment neither spoke.

Then Satine slipped her hand into his.

“You know,” she said lightly, “most people at dances eventually dance.”

“I was hoping we could avoid that tradition.”

“Kenobi.”

He sighed with theatrical resignation.

“You wield your authority irresponsibly, Duchess.”

“I learned from politicians.”

She led him gently onto the floor before he could invent another excuse.

Nearby, Qui-Gon observed all of this with quiet satisfaction.

Dooku eventually returned to stand beside him, carrying two untouched drinks because he considered social rituals important even when he had no intention of participating sincerely in them.

“They seem well,” Dooku remarked.

Qui-Gon nodded.

“For now.”

Dooku studied the ballroom for a long moment.

Music echoed beneath the crystal ceiling. Senators laughed beside military officers. Nobles danced with diplomats. Padmé rested her head briefly against Anakin’s shoulder while Obi-Wan murmured something dry enough to make Satine roll her eyes affectionately.

It looked peaceful.

Almost deceptively so.

“The Republic is dying,” Dooku said quietly.

Qui-Gon did not answer immediately.

Finally he said, “Perhaps. But civilizations are not saved only by institutions.”

Dooku glanced toward him.

“Then by what?”

Qui-Gon looked back toward the dance floor.

“By people continuing to love one another while history tries to convince them not to.”

For once, Dooku had no argument ready.

### Section 2.2.2

For a while the evening settled into something unusually gentle.

The orchestra shifted between formal Alderaanian compositions and older Core World arrangements while servants drifted soundlessly through the ballroom carrying silver trays of wine and pale fruit desserts. Beyond the towering windows, the mountains stood dark beneath a sky flooded with stars.

Anakin had stopped pretending not to enjoy himself.

That alone amused Obi-Wan enormously.

“You realize,” Obi-Wan observed as Anakin and Padmé returned briefly from the dance floor, “that ten years ago you were living in a junk shop on Tatooine.”

“And now I’m being insulted by former Jedi at royal functions,” Anakin replied. “Life’s strange.”

“Try not to track sand onto the palace floor.”

Padmé laughed into her drink while Anakin looked personally betrayed.

“I told you that once in confidence.”

“You said it loudly in front of several people.”

Before Anakin could retaliate verbally, Bail Organa approached again with unmistakably dangerous diplomatic enthusiasm.

“Master Qui-Gon,” he said, “you haven’t danced once.”

Qui-Gon looked up slowly from his conversation with Breha.

“I was hoping to preserve my dignity this evening.”

“Far too late for that,” Breha informed him.

Satine smiled immediately. “I like her.”

“Everyone likes her,” Bail said.

Dooku, unfortunately, made the mistake of standing nearby.

Bail turned toward him next.

“And Count Dooku appears determined to spend the entire evening glaring aristocratically at architecture.”

“I am appreciating the engineering.”

“You’re avoiding participation.”

“I am seventy-eight years old.”

Breha folded her arms. “That is not remotely a valid excuse on Alderaan.”

Dooku looked genuinely offended by the conspiracy forming around him.

Qui-Gon, meanwhile, had become visibly entertained.

“I warned you,” he said mildly.

“You said nothing.”

“I implied it spiritually.”

Anakin nearly choked trying not to laugh.

A few minutes later, through a combination of social pressure, strategic diplomacy, and Satine openly declaring that both men were being ridiculous, Qui-Gon and Dooku found themselves standing at the edge of the ballroom floor while the orchestra began another slower arrangement.

“This,” Dooku informed Qui-Gon quietly, “is entirely your fault.”

“My fault?”

“You encouraged them.”

“I merely failed to rescue you.”

Dooku sighed the sigh of a man who had once ruled planets and now found himself socially cornered by idealists.

Then Breha offered him a hand.

To everyone’s surprise except perhaps Bail’s, Dooku accepted with perfect aristocratic grace.

Of course he could dance.

Anakin immediately muttered, “That’s infuriating.”

Obi-Wan nodded solemnly. “I know.”

Dooku moved across the ballroom with such effortless precision that it became obvious he had spent decades inside courts and noble functions long before becoming a Jedi. Every motion was economical. Controlled. Elegant without showiness.

Breha matched him easily.

“You’ve done this before,” she observed.

“A distressing number of times.”

“You hide it well.”

“I try to hide most things well.”

Nearby, Qui-Gon was faring differently.

Satine had apparently decided that if she was going to drag Obi-Wan onto the floor, she might as well finish the job properly. So now Qui-Gon found himself opposite her while Obi-Wan stood temporarily abandoned beside Padmé and Anakin.

“You planned this,” Qui-Gon said.

“Obviously.”

“You realize I spent most of my Jedi career specifically avoiding events like this.”

“Yes,” Satine replied. “That’s why this is funny.”

Qui-Gon shook his head with quiet amusement before finally allowing himself to follow her lead into the dance.

Unlike Dooku, Qui-Gon moved without polished courtliness. There was no aristocratic perfection to him. But there was something deeply calm about the way he carried himself, something unselfconscious and grounded that fit the music strangely well.

Satine noticed it immediately.

“You’re actually relaxed,” she said softly.

“Rarely in ballrooms.”

“No,” she corrected gently. “In general.”

Qui-Gon considered that.

The music drifted around them while the great chandeliers reflected warm gold light across the polished floor.

Perhaps she was right.

For years, before leaving the Jedi Order, he had carried constant tension beneath everything: frustration with the Council, fear for Obi-Wan, disappointment in the Republic, unease about the future. Even his spirituality had become tangled with institutional conflict.

But here—

Obi-Wan dancing awkwardly but sincerely with the woman he loved.
Anakin laughing openly beside Padmé.
Dooku somehow becoming the most elegant person in the room despite actively resenting the experience.
Friends rather than subordinates. Family rather than hierarchy.

Yes.

Relaxed was probably the right word.

At the edge of the ballroom, Anakin watched the older generation with growing delight.

“I cannot believe Dooku’s this good at dancing.”

Padmé smiled. “You sound offended.”

“I am offended.”

“He’s a count, Anakin.”

“He’s also terrifying with a lightsaber. One person shouldn’t be allowed both.”

Obi-Wan glanced toward Qui-Gon and Satine.

“It’s strange seeing him like this.”

“Qui-Gon?”

Obi-Wan nodded.

“He used to feel…” He searched briefly for the word. “Restless. Even when standing still.”

Padmé followed his gaze.

“And now?”

Obi-Wan watched his old master carefully.

“Now he looks like someone who finally stopped arguing with himself.”

The music slowed further as the dance neared its end.

Across the ballroom, Dooku and Qui-Gon eventually drifted near one another again while their partners exchanged amused glances.

“You survived,” Qui-Gon observed.

“Barely.”

“You’re smiling.”

“I most certainly am not.”

Qui-Gon raised an eyebrow.

Dooku paused.

Then, very slightly, against all personal instinct and dignity, he admitted:

“…perhaps marginally.”

Qui-Gon laughed outright.

Not the restrained polite laugh of a diplomat or teacher.

A real one.

The sound startled several nearby senators who had likely never imagined Qui-Gon Jinn capable of such a thing.

For a brief moment, all six of them stood together near the center of the ballroom beneath the crystal lights:

* Qui-Gon, finally at peace with the life he chose,
* Dooku, still sharp-edged but no longer alone,
* Obi-Wan and Satine quietly inseparable,
* Anakin and Padmé young and radiant with impossible hope.

And though none of them knew it yet, years later the memory of that evening would return to the survivors with almost unbearable clarity.

Because it would come to represent the last moment before the galaxy began to break apart.
