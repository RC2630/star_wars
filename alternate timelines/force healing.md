# Part 1

The red blade slid through Qui-Gon Jinn’s chest with a hiss that seemed to cut through the Force itself.

Obi-Wan Kenobi screamed and threw himself against the energy barrier that separated him from his master. On the other side of the field, Qui-Gon collapsed to one knee beneath the towering horns of the Sith warrior who had ambushed them on Naboo. The chamber around them thundered with machinery and heat. Far above, generators pulsed with violent light.

Qui-Gon looked toward Obi-Wan one last time. Not with fear. With sorrow.

And with acceptance.

That acceptance haunted Obi-Wan for the rest of his life.

The barrier dropped. Obi-Wan attacked with desperate fury, and the duel that followed would become legend among the Jedi. When it ended, the Sith lay dead in the reactor shaft below, cut in half by Obi-Wan’s blade. But victory brought no triumph. Obi-Wan knelt beside Qui-Gon as the older Jedi’s breathing faded.

Nearby stood a frightened slave boy from Tatooine named Anakin Skywalker.

Qui-Gon had believed the boy was destined for greatness. He had freed him from slavery, brought him before the Jedi Council, and insisted he be trained despite the Council’s fears. Qui-Gon’s final request, spoken with failing breath, was simple.

“Train the boy.”

Obi-Wan promised he would.

But the two survivors carried Qui-Gon’s death in very different ways.

Obi-Wan responded by turning inward. He buried grief beneath discipline and structure. If Qui-Gon had died because his defense failed for even a moment, then Obi-Wan would become impossible to break. Year by year, he devoted himself completely to mastery of Soresu, the defensive form of lightsaber combat. His blade became economical, precise, patient. Where other Jedi attacked, Obi-Wan endured. Where others sought victory, Obi-Wan sought survival.

Anakin Skywalker responded differently.

He could not accept what he had witnessed.

He had seen Qui-Gon alive one moment and dead the next. The transition felt wrong to him. Incomplete. The Force bound all living things together—he could feel that even as a child. If the Force connected life so completely, why was death so absolute? Why had Qui-Gon simply slipped away while Obi-Wan held him?

The question never left him.

By the age of fourteen, Anakin had become one of the most gifted Padawans in the Temple. He was brilliant with machines, frighteningly talented as a pilot, and immensely powerful in the Force. But he had also become secretive.

At night, after his formal studies ended, he disappeared into the Jedi Archives.

The Archives contained forgotten corners few Jedi visited anymore: old records from healers, philosophers, mystics, and ancient sects whose practices bordered on forbidden. Jedi healing itself was respected, but most practitioners focused on meditation, pain relief, or accelerating natural recovery. The deeper arts were viewed with suspicion. Too many healers had lost themselves trying to conquer death itself.

Anakin read everything.

Treatises from the Old Republic. Fragments from worlds long destroyed. Accounts of Jedi who could stabilize mortally wounded allies for moments at a time. Ancient theories claiming emotional bonds altered the flow of the Force between living beings.

Again and again, he returned to one forbidden conclusion.

Attachment created pathways.

The Jedi taught that attachment led to fear and suffering. Anakin did not entirely disagree. But every text he studied suggested the same thing: deep emotional bonds generated extraordinary resonance in the Force. The stronger the connection, the stronger the flow between two lives.

For years he experimented in secret.

At first he practiced on plants, then animals, then small injuries of his own. He discovered he could transfer vitality through direct physical contact. Not healing exactly—more like anchoring another being against death. He learned that the process required complete concentration and emotional openness. Distance weakened it. Indifference made it impossible. But attachment—true attachment—strengthened it dramatically.

Eventually he understood the central principle.

A sufficiently powerful Force user could prevent someone deeply bonded to them from dying, so long as they physically held that person and continuously sustained the connection through the Force.

It was exhausting. Dangerous. Intimate in a way Jedi teachings discouraged.

And Anakin mastered it alone.

He told no one.

Not Obi-Wan. Not the Council. Not even Chancellor Palpatine, who had taken increasing interest in the young Jedi over the years.

Then came Tatooine.

Ten years after leaving his homeworld, Anakin returned under grim circumstances. He and Senator Padmé Amidala had traveled there while Obi-Wan pursued a growing Separatist conspiracy threatening the Republic. During the journey, Anakin had been plagued by visions of his mother suffering.

Shmi Skywalker had remained on Tatooine after Anakin’s departure, eventually marrying Cliegg Lars, a moisture farmer who treated her with kindness. But shortly before Anakin arrived, Tusken Raiders abducted her into the desert.

Anakin found the camp at dusk.

The tents stood beneath blood-red skies. Smoke drifted over the dunes. He cut through the guards in desperate panic and reached the central hut where his mother had been imprisoned.

Shmi barely recognized him.

She was bruised, feverish, dying from torture and exhaustion. Her hands trembled as she touched his face.

“Anakin…”

In that moment, terror nearly consumed him. The same helplessness from Naboo returned in full force. Qui-Gon dying while Obi-Wan held him. Life slipping away despite love, despite power, despite the Force itself.

Not again.

Anakin gathered his mother into his arms and closed his eyes.

He reached inward.

Then outward.

The Force flooded through him with shocking intensity. He felt Shmi’s life flickering like a candle in violent wind. Instinctively he wrapped himself around that fading light—not physically, but spiritually. Holding her body became inseparable from holding her existence itself.

For one terrible second he thought it had failed.

Then her breathing steadied.

Weak. Fragile. But stable.

Shmi looked at him in confusion.

“I… I can breathe…”

Anakin stared down at her, stunned.

He could feel the connection sustaining her. Every moment required effort. If he loosened his concentration even slightly, her life immediately began slipping away again. But as long as he held her, she remained alive.

Padmé arrived moments later with the droids and stopped in disbelief at the sight before her. Anakin sat in the sand cradling his mother, utterly motionless, sweat pouring down his face.

“She’s dying,” Padmé whispered.

“She was,” Anakin replied. “I’m keeping her here.”

He refused to release her for even a second.

C-3PO and R2-D2 helped prepare transport while Padmé guided them back to the ship. Anakin carried Shmi aboard himself and maintained the connection through the entire flight off-world. He did not sleep. Did not eat. Barely spoke.

Only when they reached a proper medical facility on Naboo did he finally lower his mother onto the surgical bed.

The instant his hands left her shoulders, the medical droids surged forward. Machines stabilized what Anakin had preserved. Bacta treatments repaired internal damage. Exhaustion and infection were purged from her system.

Hours later, the lead physician emerged.

“She will recover fully.”

Anakin nearly collapsed.

When Obi-Wan reunited with them days later, he expected grief and tragedy. Instead he found Shmi Skywalker alive and recovering comfortably in a Naboo clinic garden.

At first he assumed the reports were mistaken.

Then Anakin explained everything.

They spoke privately late into the night aboard Padmé’s starship while stars drifted beyond the viewport.

“You kept her alive yourself?” Obi-Wan asked quietly.

Anakin nodded. “As long as I held her.”

“That’s impossible.”

“I thought so too.”

Obi-Wan listened as Anakin described years of secret study in the Archives, ancient healing records, theories about attachment and the Force. Obi-Wan expected himself to react with alarm. The Council certainly would have. Emotional attachment lay at the center of nearly every Jedi cautionary tale.

Yet he could not ignore the simple truth before him.

Shmi Skywalker lived.

Obi-Wan remembered kneeling beside Qui-Gon’s body on Naboo. He remembered the unbearable certainty that nothing could be done. And now his former Padawan sat across from him calmly describing a way death itself might be resisted.

“Why didn’t you tell me?” Obi-Wan finally asked.

Anakin looked down. “Because I thought you’d forbid it.”

Obi-Wan almost said that he should have.

Instead, after a long silence, he asked:

“Can you teach me?”

Anakin looked genuinely shocked.

“You want to learn it?”

“Yes.”

Obi-Wan folded his hands inside his robes, gaze distant.

“My master died in my arms. I have spent ten years learning how to ensure no enemy ever breaks my defense again.” He paused. “But defense alone wasn’t enough to save him.”

For the first time since Qui-Gon’s death, Obi-Wan allowed himself to imagine another possibility. Not merely surviving loss.

Preventing it.

Their lessons began in secret.

At first Obi-Wan struggled badly. The technique demanded something profoundly uncomfortable for him: emotional vulnerability. Soresu had taught him control, calmness, distance. The healing art required the opposite. It demanded sincerity of feeling without restraint.

“Stop thinking about the Force as a wall,” Anakin told him during one meditation session deep within the Temple’s unused archive chambers. “You keep trying to contain everything.”

“That usually works.”

“This isn’t defense. It’s connection.”

Obi-Wan frowned but tried again.

Slowly, painstakingly, he improved.

Master and former Padawan became collaborators in secrecy. Together they refined the art beyond what Anakin had discovered alone. Obi-Wan’s discipline stabilized the technique; Anakin’s intuition expanded it. They learned that emotional honesty mattered more than raw passion. Compassion worked better than fear. Calm attachment proved stronger than desperation.

Eventually Obi-Wan succeeded in preserving a dying bird whose neck had been broken during a storm over Coruscant. He held the tiny creature carefully in his palms while Anakin guided him through the connection.

The bird survived long enough for proper healing treatment.

Obi-Wan stared at the sleeping creature afterward for a very long time.

“This changes everything,” he murmured.

Anakin looked uncertain. “Or destroys everything.”

Because both of them understood the danger immediately.

If the Jedi Council learned of this ability, reactions would split sharply. Some would see miraculous healing. Others would see a direct path toward possessiveness, fear of loss, and obsession with preventing death at any cost.

And beyond the Temple, the implications were even greater.

The Republic was drifting toward war. Systems were seceding. Assassinations multiplied. Rumors spread of Sith influence moving unseen through the galaxy once more.

In such a galaxy, the ability to hold death itself at bay would become the most coveted power imaginable.

# Part 2

The first rumors spread quietly through the Jedi Temple.

At first they sounded impossible.

A wounded initiate surviving injuries that should have killed him after being held by a senior Jedi in meditation. A battlefield healer stabilizing clone troopers long enough for evacuation despite catastrophic wounds. Jedi speaking in hushed voices about “anchoring” life through the Force.

Most dismissed the stories as exaggeration born from war.

But the stories continued.

And at the center of all of them stood Obi-Wan Kenobi and Anakin Skywalker.

The two worked carefully, almost obsessively, refining the art they had uncovered together. Their private experiments gradually transformed into structured study. They mapped emotional resonance patterns through meditation. They documented how physical contact affected Force flow. They discovered the technique worked most strongly through genuine attachment, but not exclusively through romance or family. Deep friendship could sustain it. So could mentorship. Loyalty. Compassion.

Anakin once stabilized a clone captain he barely knew simply because the troopers under his command trusted him so completely that a reciprocal bond had formed without him realizing it.

That discovery unsettled the Council more than anyone admitted openly.

Because it suggested attachment was not merely temptation.

It was part of life itself.

Yoda became the first Council member to approach the matter without fear.

The ancient Jedi Master began attending some of their sessions personally. He asked difficult questions. Tested boundaries. Challenged assumptions.

“Dangerous, this path remains,” Yoda warned during one gathering in the Temple gardens. “Strong emotion, powerful attachment. Consume a Jedi, these things easily can.”

“But compassion is attachment too,” Anakin argued. “We already teach that.”

“Different, they are.”

“Are they?” Obi-Wan asked quietly.

Yoda studied him for a long moment.

War had aged Obi-Wan. The defensive serenity he once cultivated now carried visible strain beneath it, as though he spent every waking moment holding pressure inside himself.

“Changed, you have,” Yoda finally observed.

Obi-Wan looked away slightly. “I’ve had reason to.”

Despite his caution, even Yoda became fascinated by the implications. He meditated on the technique himself, though he never fully practiced it. He sensed both great light and terrible danger surrounding it.

The Council allowed the research to continue under supervision.

Some Masters supported it enthusiastically, particularly the healers. Others deeply distrusted it. Mace Windu remained openly skeptical.

“The Jedi exist to preserve peace,” he said during one Council session. “Not to wage war against mortality itself.”

“We already do that every time we save a life,” Obi-Wan replied.

The room fell silent.

Obi-Wan rarely challenged the Council directly.

Anakin noticed the exchange carefully.

So did Yoda.

The war continued to spread.

The Confederacy tightened its grip on the Outer Rim while Republic fleets burned across dozens of systems. Jedi generals became symbols of hope throughout the galaxy, and among them none were more famous than Obi-Wan Kenobi, Anakin Skywalker, and Anakin’s Padawan learner, Ahsoka Tano.

The three became inseparable on campaign.

Obi-Wan brought calm strategy and discipline. Anakin brought fearless aggression and instinctive brilliance. Ahsoka brought speed, unpredictability, and a growing confidence that made clone troopers adore her.

Together they won battles that should have been impossible.

And then came Mandalore.

The world had once stood proudly neutral during the Clone Wars under the leadership of Duchess Satine Kryze. Satine believed fiercely in peace and had resisted pressure from both the Republic and Separatists alike. But neutrality had made Mandalore vulnerable. Criminal syndicates and political corruption hollowed the system from within until Darth Maul emerged from the shadows to seize control personally.

By the time Obi-Wan arrived secretly on Mandalore, the capital had become a trap.

He came because Satine asked for help.

He stayed because he still loved her.

Maul knew that too.

The former Sith lord had changed since Naboo. Years of rage and survival had sharpened him into something colder and crueler. He understood Obi-Wan intimately. Understood precisely where to strike.

Obi-Wan was captured quickly after entering the palace.

They brought him bound before the throne room while Satine struggled helplessly against guards nearby. Maul paced slowly before them with the darksaber in hand.

“You survive everything, Kenobi,” Maul said softly. “Master after master. Friend after friend. Yet still the Force leaves you alive.”

Obi-Wan met his gaze evenly. “Whatever you think this accomplishes, you’ve already lost.”

Maul smiled faintly.

“No. You simply haven’t understood the lesson yet.”

Then he turned and drove the darksaber through Satine’s abdomen.

The sound Obi-Wan made was not a scream.

It was worse.

For a moment the entire room vanished beneath raw horror. Satine gasped in shock as the blade withdrew and she collapsed toward him. Guards released Obi-Wan just as Maul intended, allowing him to catch her body.

“This time,” Maul whispered, “you will watch.”

Satine trembled violently in Obi-Wan’s arms.

Blood spread across her pale clothing.

And then Obi-Wan reached into the Force.

Not with panic.

Not with rage.

With absolute refusal.

The connection ignited between them instantly. Stronger than anything he had ever experienced. Years of restrained love, buried longing, and unspoken devotion surged outward all at once.

Satine’s fading life stabilized.

Maul frowned.

Obi-Wan pulled her closer and descended deeper into the Force than he ever had before. Somewhere instinct took over. He stopped focusing solely on preserving Satine and instead wrapped the connection outward around both of them simultaneously.

The air shuddered.

A translucent field erupted around Obi-Wan and Satine like glass formed from light itself.

Maul’s eyes narrowed.

He struck immediately.

The darksaber crashed against the shield and rebounded violently.

The chamber shook.

Obi-Wan barely noticed. Sweat poured down his face as he maintained the connection. Satine stared up at him weakly, breathing shallow but alive.

“Obi-Wan…” she whispered.

“I’ve got you,” he said hoarsely.

Maul attacked again.

And again.

Each strike failed.

The shield bent under the assaults but did not break. Blaster fire from the guards splashed harmlessly against it. Maul’s frustration darkened into fury.

“Impossible,” he snarled.

Obi-Wan did not answer.

Far away, aboard a Republic cruiser preparing for deployment, Anakin Skywalker suddenly froze mid-conversation.

A vision slammed into him without warning.

Obi-Wan kneeling in darkness. Satine dying in his arms. The sensation of crushing strain. Maul’s hatred burning like fire.

Anakin stood immediately.

“Ahsoka,” he said sharply. “We’re leaving.”

Within hours they reached Mandalore.

Officially, the Republic had no authorization to intervene. Anakin ignored that completely.

He and Ahsoka infiltrated Sundari through maintenance sectors beneath the city while clone forces created diversions outside. Fighting erupted through the palace complex as Mandalorian loyalists seized the opportunity to rebel against Maul’s occupation.

Anakin finally confronted Maul in the throne chamber corridors.

Their lightsabers collided with explosive force.

“You,” Maul hissed. “The favored child.”

“You picked the wrong day,” Anakin replied.

The duel tore through the palace, but Maul quickly realized something dangerous: Anakin Skywalker had become far more powerful than expected. Worse, Ahsoka fought beside him with complete trust and coordination. Together they drove Maul steadily backward.

But they could not trap him.

Maul escaped through hidden transit tunnels beneath the city, vanishing once more into the underworld he knew so well.

Only after the fighting ended did Anakin finally reach Obi-Wan.

He found him exactly where the vision had shown: seated against the throne room wall, arms wrapped tightly around Satine while the shimmering Force shield flickered around them both.

Obi-Wan looked exhausted beyond words.

“She’s alive,” he managed quietly.

Anakin knelt beside him in disbelief.

“You held this the entire time?”

Obi-Wan gave the faintest nod.

They evacuated immediately.

Throughout the journey to a Republic medical frigate, Obi-Wan never released Satine. Not once. Clone troopers rotated around him silently, protecting the corridor outside the medbay with unusual solemnity.

The surgery lasted eleven hours.

Satine survived.

But the damage was irreversible.

The darksaber had destroyed too much tissue along her spine. Even advanced reconstruction could not fully restore mobility. When Satine finally awakened days later, medical droids explained gently that she would require a repulsorlift hoverchair permanently.

Obi-Wan sat beside her in silence for a long time after the doctors left.

“I’m sorry,” he whispered eventually.

Satine looked at him with tired affection.

“You carried me through death itself, Obi-Wan. I think you’re allowed to stop apologizing.”

But Maul’s attack had broken something inside him.

Obi-Wan realized with painful clarity that he could no longer continue pretending his attachment to Satine was temporary, containable, or secondary to the Order. He had spent years obeying every rule expected of him while quietly sacrificing the life he actually wanted.

And still he nearly lost her.

This time he refused to let go.

He formally petitioned the Jedi Council for permission to marry Satine openly while remaining within the Order. His case was difficult to dismiss. He was one of the Republic’s greatest generals, deeply respected throughout the galaxy, and his conduct throughout the war had remained honorable despite extraordinary strain.

More importantly, he did not ask lightly.

“This is not rebellion,” Obi-Wan told the Council. “Nor is it secrecy. I am asking honestly.”

Mace Windu remained unmoved.

“If we allow exceptions whenever attachment becomes difficult,” he said, “the Code loses meaning.”

“The Code was written to guide us,” Obi-Wan replied quietly. “Not imprison us.”

The debate lasted hours.

Yoda argued for compassion but hesitated to fully support changing centuries of tradition during wartime. Several Masters feared the precedent. Others privately sympathized but lacked courage to oppose the orthodox majority.

In the end, the request was denied.

Obi-Wan accepted the answer calmly.

Then he resigned from the Jedi Order.

The announcement shook the Republic.

Clone commanders across multiple sectors reacted with open disbelief. Senators protested publicly. Even many Jedi were stunned. Obi-Wan Kenobi had embodied Jedi discipline for nearly his entire life. If someone like him could no longer remain within the Order, many began quietly wondering what exactly the Order had become.

Anakin took the news personally.

Not because Obi-Wan left.

Because the Council forced him to.

And then, only weeks later, Ahsoka Tano was arrested for the bombing of the Jedi Temple.

The accusations spread rapidly across Coruscant. Military investigators claimed evidence linked her directly to the attack. Despite Anakin’s insistence that something was wrong, the Council expelled Ahsoka from the Order before her trial concluded.

Anakin watched his Padawan walk away in chains while Masters he had trusted stood in silence.

Something inside him hardened permanently.

Though Ahsoka was eventually proven innocent after the true culprit was exposed, the damage was done. She refused the Council’s invitation to return.

“I know what I am to them now,” she told Anakin quietly.

Soon afterward, Anakin Skywalker resigned from the Jedi Order as well.

Not dramatically.

Not angrily.

Simply with exhausted certainty.

He had lost his mother once already. Nearly lost Padmé repeatedly during the war. Seen Obi-Wan driven out for loving Satine openly while he himself hid a secret marriage to Padmé Amidala out of fear.

And now Ahsoka too.

The Jedi Order increasingly felt less like a family and more like an institution terrified of human attachment despite depending upon it constantly.

Anakin would not abandon Obi-Wan or Ahsoka.

So he left with them.

At first the Republic High Command refused to allow former Jedi to continue commanding clone forces. Regulations technically prohibited it. The GAR depended heavily on Jedi authority structures.

But the clones themselves complicated everything.

The 501st refused reassignment quietly but firmly. Cody and the 212th became equally resistant. Across multiple deployments, clone officers submitted formal petitions requesting continued service under Skywalker, Kenobi, and Tano.

The public supported them overwhelmingly.

To ordinary citizens, the three were heroes who had sacrificed everything for the Republic while the Jedi Council clung to outdated rigidity. Senators began publicly pressuring Chancellor Palpatine to retain them as independent military commanders attached directly to the Republic rather than the Jedi Order.

Palpatine considered the situation carefully.

Privately, he disliked how unpredictable matters had become. Anakin drifting away from the Jedi had advantages, but Obi-Wan and Ahsoka remaining close to him complicated future plans. Still, rejecting the request outright risked political backlash and potential unrest among clone units already fiercely loyal to their commanders.

So Palpatine approved the arrangement.

Officially, Obi-Wan Kenobi, Anakin Skywalker, and Ahsoka Tano became special wartime commanders operating independently alongside Republic forces.

Unofficially, Palpatine decided it would make eliminating them easier later.

And so the strange new family moved from battlefield to battlefield together.

Anakin. Padmé in secret whenever possible. Obi-Wan. Satine. Ahsoka. Their clone troops.

Satine traveled aboard their flagship because nowhere else was truly safe. Mandalore remained under Maul’s fractured control, and Coruscant was politically dangerous for someone so closely associated with former Jedi leadership.

So Satine stayed with them.

While gunships launched toward warzones and starfighters screamed into battle overhead, Satine often remained aboard the command vessel in her hoverchair reviewing intelligence reports beside clone officers who had grown fiercely protective of her.

The clones began calling her “the Duchess” with quiet affection.

Sometimes Obi-Wan returned from battle exhausted and bloodied only to find Satine waiting awake in the dim ship lighting. He would kneel beside her chair, forehead resting gently against her hand, and for a few moments the war disappeared.

And gradually, without intending to, the group began becoming something the Jedi Order had never truly known how to create.

Not an institution.

A family.

# Part 3

The assault on Mandalore began beneath a sky full of fire.

Republic cruisers emerged from hyperspace over the planet in disciplined formation while drop ships descended through anti-aircraft fire toward the domed cities below. The war had reached its final, brutal phase across the galaxy, but for Obi-Wan, Anakin, Ahsoka, and Satine, Mandalore was personal.

This was not simply another campaign.

It was home reclaimed from a tyrant.

Satine remained aboard the flagship during the initial assault, coordinating civilian evacuation routes from the command deck beside clone officers and Mandalorian resistance leaders. Her hoverchair glided silently between tactical holotables while alarms flashed crimson across the bridge.

No one aboard treated her disability with pity anymore.

They had watched her survive Maul.

That alone commanded respect.

Below, fighting erupted through Sundari’s streets as loyalist Mandalorians rose against the Shadow Collective occupying the capital. Clone troopers of the 501st and 212th advanced sector by sector while Ahsoka led strike teams through the upper districts.

Anakin drove straight toward the palace.

He could feel Maul waiting.

The former Sith had changed again since their last confrontation. His rule over Mandalore had become increasingly unstable, poisoned by paranoia and rage. Entire districts lived in fear of random executions. Even his own followers trusted him only because they feared him more than anyone else.

And yet when Anakin finally confronted him in the throne room, Maul did not seem afraid.

He seemed desperate.

“You have no idea what’s coming,” Maul hissed as their blades crashed together. “The Republic is already dead.”

Anakin pressed harder. “You said that last time.”

“You think Sidious cares about any of you?” Maul snarled. “The Jedi, the Senate, this war—it’s all—”

Anakin slammed him backward through the throne dais before he could finish.

The duel raged through shattered chambers while Mandalorian warriors fought below. Maul was still deadly, still fast beyond human limits, but he no longer held the advantage he once had. Anakin fought with frightening precision now, sharpened by years of war and tempered by responsibilities larger than himself.

At the same time, Ahsoka cornered Maul’s remaining loyalists across the transit tunnels beneath the city.

By the end of the day, Mandalore belonged to its people again.

Maul was captured alive.

The news spread across the Republic within hours. Citizens celebrated in the streets of Coruscant. The liberation of Mandalore felt like proof that the war might finally be ending.

But peace did not come easily.

When Satine formally announced her intention to reclaim leadership of Mandalore, resistance emerged almost immediately from within the clans themselves.

Many supported her return enthusiastically. Others questioned whether a ruler confined to a hoverchair could govern a warrior culture fractured by civil war.

And then Bo-Katan Kryze challenged her openly.

The sisters stood facing one another inside the palace council chamber while clan representatives watched in tense silence. Obi-Wan stood quietly beside Satine’s chair, hands folded within his sleeves.

“You cannot lead Mandalore like this,” Bo-Katan said bluntly. “The clans won’t follow weakness.”

Satine met her gaze steadily.

“I survived Maul. Mandalore survived Maul. If you still believe strength is measured only by who can stand tallest in armor, then our people learned nothing.”

Bo-Katan’s jaw tightened.

“You always believed words were enough.”

“And you always believed violence solved things.”

The room grew dangerously tense.

Several clans clearly favored Bo-Katan. Others remained loyal to Satine. After years of occupation and civil conflict, Mandalore stood dangerously close to tearing itself apart again.

Obi-Wan finally stepped forward.

“You both fought to save this world,” he said calmly. “If Mandalore descends into another internal war now, Maul wins even in defeat.”

Bo-Katan looked toward him sharply.

“You’re not Mandalorian.”

“No,” Obi-Wan agreed. “But I love one.”

The honesty of it seemed to disarm the room more effectively than any political maneuver.

Over the following weeks Obi-Wan remained on Mandalore constantly, helping mediate disputes between clans while Satine slowly rebuilt a functioning government. To the surprise of many traditionalists, she proved extraordinarily effective despite her injuries. She delegated military responsibilities carefully, relied heavily on local leaders, and governed with a patience Mandalore desperately needed after years of chaos.

Eventually even Bo-Katan relented.

Not completely.

But enough.

The sisters reached an uneasy understanding, and Bo-Katan formally pledged support to Satine’s restored government.

When it became clear Obi-Wan intended to remain permanently beside Satine as protector, advisor, and husband, he officially transferred command of his clone forces to Anakin.

Commander Cody accepted the change with visible emotion.

“It’s been an honor, sir,” he told Obi-Wan quietly.

“The honor was mine, Cody.”

Neither of them knew it would be the last peaceful conversation they would ever share.

Because while Mandalore rebuilt itself, the galaxy collapsed.

Anakin and Ahsoka departed shortly afterward aboard a Republic cruiser transporting the captured Maul under heavy guard. The atmosphere aboard the ship remained tense. Even imprisoned within ray shields, Maul radiated disturbing calmness.

“You still don’t see it,” he told Anakin through the containment field. “He’s already won.”

Anakin ignored him.

Then Order 66 activated.

The command arrived silently through the inhibitor chips embedded within the clone army. Across thousands of battlefields, clone troopers turned on Jedi generals without warning.

On the cruiser above Mandalore, everything erupted into chaos within seconds.

Troopers Anakin had fought beside for years suddenly raised rifles against him.

Ahsoka sensed it first.

Something in the Force twisted violently—like a scream spreading across the galaxy all at once.

“Anakin—!”

Blaster fire exploded through the corridor.

Anakin’s lightsaber ignited instantly as clone troopers opened fire from both ends of the passage. The betrayal hit him almost physically. Faces he knew. Men who had trusted him. Men he trusted.

All trying to kill him.

“What are you doing?!” he shouted.

No answer came beyond cold military precision.

The inhibitor chips had overridden everything else.

During the confusion, Maul escaped exactly as he had hoped. He slaughtered guards, tore through sections of the cruiser, and vanished into the chaos while Anakin and Ahsoka fought desperately simply to survive.

Not all the clones obeyed.

A handful resisted long enough to hesitate. Others managed partial control over themselves after witnessing troopers they considered brothers suddenly acting like machines. Rex, after agonizing struggle, finally revealed the existence of the inhibitor chips to Ahsoka.

The realization horrified everyone.

The clones had never truly been free.

The ship descended into civil war. Loyalist troopers fought alongside Anakin and Ahsoka against those fully consumed by the order. Corridors burned. Systems failed. Entire compartments depressurized during firefights.

Eventually the surviving group managed to escape aboard emergency transports and return to Mandalore’s surface.

They arrived exhausted, wounded, and carrying the collapse of the Republic with them.

Satine received them personally at the palace landing platform.

When Ahsoka stepped off the shuttle covered in soot and blood, Satine immediately embraced her.

“You’re safe,” she whispered.

For a moment, Ahsoka nearly broke apart entirely.

Anakin looked worse.

Not physically.

The Force around him felt hollowed out.

“The Jedi are gone,” he said quietly to Obi-Wan later that night.

Across the galaxy, the purge spread rapidly. Temple broadcasts vanished. Republic channels became Imperial declarations almost overnight. Palpatine proclaimed the Jedi traitors responsible for an attempted coup and reorganized the Republic into the first Galactic Empire.

Most citizens accepted it out of fear, exhaustion, or confusion.

The Jedi Order shattered within days.

Some survived in hiding. Others died defending civilians. Entire generations of knowledge vanished in fire and blood.

On Mandalore, the survivors gathered in silence.

Obi-Wan. Satine. Anakin. Ahsoka. A small number of clones who had removed or resisted their chips. Bo-Katan and several Mandalorian commanders.

None of them fully belonged anywhere anymore.

Not Jedi.

Not Republic.

Not Empire.

For weeks they debated what came next.

At first survival alone seemed difficult enough. Imperial influence spread rapidly across nearby systems, and Mandalore itself remained vulnerable after years of war. But gradually another realization emerged.

They could not simply hide.

The Jedi Order was broken not only by betrayal, but by its own rigidity. The Republic had fallen because too many people surrendered responsibility to institutions that stopped questioning themselves.

None of them wanted to repeat that mistake.

The discussions eventually led to a decision.

They would build something new.

Not a replacement for the Jedi.

A departure from them.

They chose the name Dabi, drawn from an ancient word found in forgotten Force manuscripts Anakin once studied in the Temple Archives. It carried several meanings simultaneously: bond, ember, continuity.

The symbolism felt appropriate.

The Dabi rejected the old Jedi prohibition against attachment entirely. Emotional bonds were not viewed as weaknesses to suppress but responsibilities to navigate honestly. Love, loyalty, grief, family—these things shaped the Force whether acknowledged or not.

The goal was not detachment.

It was balance without denial.

They also rejected the Sith obsession with domination through passion. Emotion could strengthen connection, but unchecked fear and possessiveness still corrupted. Anakin himself insisted on that distinction more strongly than anyone.

“We don’t control people because we love them,” he told the first small gathering of followers months later. “We protect them.”

Training changed as well.

Students learned healing and emotional discipline side by side. Combat emphasized defense and preservation over conquest. Force bonds were studied openly rather than hidden in shame.

Anakin continued training Ahsoka for a time, though increasingly their relationship resembled equals more than master and apprentice.

One evening after a sparring session overlooking Sundari’s white towers, Anakin finally deactivated his blade and shook his head.

“You know I can’t really teach you much anymore.”

Ahsoka smirked faintly. “Took you long enough to admit it.”

“You’re already a knight.”

The words landed harder than either expected.

For years Ahsoka had imagined hearing them from the Jedi Council someday.

Instead they came here, under Mandalorian stars, from the man who had refused to abandon her when everyone else did.

She bowed her head quietly.

“Thank you.”

Mandalore gradually became the center of their new life.

Satine rebuilt the government carefully with Bo-Katan’s support. Obi-Wan remained constantly at her side, no longer torn between duty and love. He became something unusual among Mandalorians: an outsider fully accepted into clan leadership through loyalty rather than blood.

Bo-Katan herself slowly transformed from wary rival into trusted ally. Though she still distrusted many mystical aspects of the Dabi, she respected the people behind it fiercely.

And Anakin—

Anakin finally stopped living divided against himself.

No more lies about Padmé. No more pretending attachment did not define him. No more forcing himself into the shape of a Jedi who no longer existed.

Yet peace still eluded him sometimes.

At night he often stood alone on the palace balconies overlooking Sundari while Imperial broadcasts spread across the stars. Entire systems disappeared into silence. Rumors emerged of a dark enforcer hunting surviving Jedi for the Emperor.

And somewhere out there, Maul still lived.

The galaxy had not finished breaking yet.

Not even close.

# Part 4

Palpatine’s victory unraveled almost immediately.

He had expected panic after Order 66. Shock. Confusion. A galaxy exhausted enough by war to accept any promise of stability. He believed the Jedi would die isolated and hunted, their reputation shattered before they could organize resistance.

Instead, the Temple assault became a disaster.

When clone troopers stormed the Jedi Temple under emergency orders, they met coordinated resistance almost at once. Several Council Masters had sensed something wrong in the Force moments before the attack began. Temple guards sealed entire wings while senior Jedi rallied defenders around the younglings.

The fighting inside the Temple became desperate and chaotic.

Master Shaak Ti coordinated evacuations through hidden transit tunnels beneath Coruscant while Plo Koon and Ki-Adi-Mundi led defensive actions in the main halls. Masters who would have fallen alone on distant battlefields instead survived because they were together.

The clones still inflicted terrible casualties.

But the Jedi were not annihilated.

By the time Coruscant security forces fully surrounded the district, hundreds of Jedi had already escaped with nearly all surviving younglings. Rather than scattering blindly across the galaxy, the survivors regrouped in secrecy. Guided by Yoda, they fled beyond Republic shipping lanes to the ancient swamp world of Dagobah.

There, hidden beneath dense fog and powerful vergences in the Force, the Jedi Order endured.

Broken.

Wounded.

But alive.

And meanwhile, the Republic itself refused to die as obediently as Palpatine expected.

His declaration of the Galactic Empire triggered immediate outrage across countless systems. Entire sectors already distrusted the sudden concentration of executive power after years of emergency wartime authority. Now, with clone troopers openly attacking Jedi across the galaxy, many senators demanded evidence rather than accepting Palpatine’s accusations blindly.

Then Mandalore intervened.

The first counter-broadcast aired only sixteen hours after Palpatine’s Imperial declaration.

Citizens across the HoloNet expecting triumphant Imperial messaging instead found Duchess Satine Kryze appearing from Sundari beside Obi-Wan Kenobi, Anakin Skywalker, Ahsoka Tano, and several clone officers who had removed their inhibitor chips.

The statement was calm.

Measured.

Devastating.

Anakin openly declared that clone behavior had been altered through hidden bioengineering. Rex testified personally regarding the inhibitor chips. Obi-Wan demanded an independent Senate investigation into Palpatine’s emergency powers and the attack on the Jedi Temple.

Most importantly, Satine framed the crisis not as a Jedi issue, but as a constitutional one.

“If an elected leader may secretly weaponize an army against political opponents without oversight,” she said evenly, “then the Republic no longer exists.”

The broadcast spread everywhere.

Palpatine attempted to suppress it immediately, but Mandalore’s communications network had become extraordinarily resilient during Maul’s occupation. Independent worlds rebroadcast the transmission faster than Imperial censors could contain it.

Suddenly the galaxy was divided.

Coruscant claimed the Jedi had attempted treason.

Mandalore claimed Palpatine had staged a coup.

And unlike previous crises, neither side could fully silence the other.

Anakin Skywalker’s presence proved especially catastrophic for Palpatine’s narrative. The Hero With No Fear was one of the most beloved figures in the Republic. Citizens had watched him fight for years across every major front of the Clone Wars. Seeing him stand beside former Jedi, clone veterans, and Mandalorian leaders shattered the image of a unified Jedi betrayal.

Worse still, Anakin himself finally understood the truth.

Palpatine was not merely manipulative.

He was the enemy.

The realization poisoned every memory they shared. Every conversation. Every carefully cultivated moment of mentorship stretching back to Anakin’s childhood.

“You knew,” Anakin said quietly one night after another Imperial broadcast. “You knew exactly what you were building the entire time.”

His anger terrified even himself.

But unlike before, he was no longer isolated inside it.

Obi-Wan stood beside him. Ahsoka did too. So did Padmé.

And Padmé herself was now in danger.

Her opposition to Palpatine inside the Senate had become increasingly public before the Empire declaration. Worse, she remained personally connected to nearly everyone now resisting him.

Anakin refused to leave her on Coruscant.

The extraction mission became one of the most dangerous flights of his life.

Imperial security had already tightened around the capital world by the time he infiltrated the planet using old smuggling corridors from the undercity levels. Padmé met him secretly inside her apartment while Senate surveillance intensified throughout the district.

“You shouldn’t have come,” she whispered the moment she saw him.

“You think I’m leaving you here?”

She tried arguing at first. The Senate still needed opposition voices. Publicly fleeing with Anakin risked validating Imperial suspicions.

Then Anakin placed a hand gently against her stomach.

Their children moved beneath it.

Nothing else mattered after that.

The escape nearly failed twice.

Imperial patrols intercepted them above the cityscape, and only Anakin’s impossible piloting carried them through the traffic canyons of Coruscant alive. By the time they finally jumped to hyperspace, both of them sat shaking in silence inside the cockpit.

Padmé laughed first.

Mostly from exhaustion.

“You know,” she said weakly, “normal couples usually choose quieter lives.”

Anakin rested his forehead against hers.

“Too late now.”

Back on Mandalore, the political alliance deepened rapidly.

Padmé and Satine became the center of it.

Privately, they disagreed constantly.

Padmé believed democratic institutions had to survive even if imperfect. Satine distrusted centralized military authority profoundly after watching the Republic descend into authoritarianism during the war. They argued over pacifism, interventionism, emergency powers, planetary sovereignty, and whether violence could ever truly preserve peace.

But the arguments remained respectful.

And strategically, they understood one another perfectly.

Publicly, Mandalore projected total unity.

Together they coordinated broadcasts exposing inconsistencies in Imperial claims. Former Separatist senators began cautiously contacting them. Independent journalists uncovered evidence linking Palpatine to increasingly suspicious wartime decisions. More clone veterans came forward about behavioral abnormalities surrounding Order 66.

Gradually public opinion shifted.

Palpatine’s greatest mistake had been moving too quickly.

He expected fear to overwhelm scrutiny.

Instead the galaxy had time to question him.

Then the Jedi struck back.

From Dagobah, the surviving Council debated their options for weeks. Some argued they should remain hidden and rebuild slowly. Others believed Palpatine’s grip strengthened every day he remained alive.

Mace Windu ended the debate personally.

“He is Sith,” Windu said flatly. “If we delay, more worlds fall.”

In the end four Jedi Masters infiltrated Coruscant secretly and entered the Chancellor’s office carrying a small hovercam recorder.

Mace insisted on the recording himself.

“If we fail,” he said, “the galaxy deserves proof.”

Palpatine sensed them before they entered.

“So,” he murmured from his office window as the doors opened behind him. “The Jedi return.”

Mace Windu stepped forward.

“You are under arrest, Chancellor.”

Palpatine smiled slowly.

For a moment he still considered deception. Political maneuvering. One final manipulation.

Then he saw the hovercam.

And realized the performance was over.

The transformation into Darth Sidious unfolded before the recording in horrifying clarity. His voice darkened. His face twisted beneath years of corruption finally unleashed openly.

The duel began instantly.

Three Jedi Masters died within seconds.

Sidious moved like living violence, his crimson blade carving through the office in flashes of impossible speed. But Mace Windu endured.

Vaapad met Sith fury directly.

The duel shattered windows overlooking the Senate district while storms gathered over Coruscant. Sidious attacked relentlessly, drawing on hatred powerful enough to warp the Force itself around him.

But Mace refused to break.

Again and again he redirected Sidious’s aggression back upon him. The Sith Lord’s power became a storm feeding its own destruction.

Finally, after nearly collapsing the entire office tower around them, Windu disarmed him.

Sidious screamed with rage and unleashed torrents of Force lightning.

Mace advanced through it.

One step.

Then another.

Then his blade pierced Sidious’s chest.

Darth Sidious died staring at the man he had underestimated for decades.

Mace Windu emerged from the Chancellor’s office alone.

And the hovercam recorded everything.

The footage detonated across the galaxy like a supernova.

Citizens watched Palpatine reveal himself willingly as a Sith Lord. They watched him murder Jedi Masters before any attack against him occurred. They watched Mace Windu fight openly in defense of the Republic.

There was no surviving the truth after that.

The Empire collapsed almost overnight.

Some sectors attempted to continue supporting Imperial authority, but momentum shattered immediately once the deception became undeniable. Senators who had remained cautious now openly denounced Palpatine. Military officers abandoned Imperial command structures. Entire fleets stood down rather than continue fighting for a dead Sith.

Bail Organa moved fastest.

Working alongside Mon Mothma and surviving constitutional loyalists, he restored the Republic under emergency transitional authority and became provisional Chancellor until proper elections could be organized.

Meanwhile, Padmé accomplished something few believed possible.

She ended the Clone Wars.

The Confederacy of Independent Systems had continued fighting largely because neither side trusted the other’s leadership anymore. Once Sidious’s manipulation became public, everything changed. Separatist leaders finally understood they too had been used.

Padmé negotiated directly with surviving CIS representatives on neutral worlds while Mandalore guaranteed safe passage.

The peace talks were tense.

Painful.

But genuine.

At last, after years of galactic war, a formal armistice was signed.

The Clone Wars ended not with total victory, but with mutual exhaustion and shared betrayal.

And slowly, life resumed.

Not perfectly.

Never cleanly.

But honestly.

Ahsoka eventually reconnected with Lux Bonteri during postwar reconstruction efforts on former Separatist worlds. Their earlier affection had survived years of war, separation, and political chaos surprisingly intact.

This time neither of them belonged to opposing sides anymore.

Raxus Secundus transformed from a wartime capital into the administrative center of a newly demilitarized Confederacy existing peacefully alongside the restored Republic. Lux became one of the leading voices advocating reconciliation between former enemies.

Ahsoka moved there eventually.

Not as a Jedi.

Not as a soldier.

Simply as herself.

Anakin teased her mercilessly before she departed Mandalore.

“You know, for someone who spent years pretending not to like politicians—”

“Oh, be quiet,” Ahsoka muttered while hugging him goodbye.

Padmé returned to Coruscant once the political climate stabilized enough for safety. She resumed her Senate career immediately, determined to help rebuild democratic institutions before complacency allowed another Palpatine to rise.

This time Anakin went with her openly.

No lies.

No secrecy.

Their twins, Luke and Leia Skywalker, were born under the lights of Galactic City rather than in hiding or fear. Obi-Wan visited often enough that hospital staff began recognizing him on sight. Satine accompanied him whenever her health allowed.

The children adored her immediately.

Obi-Wan remained on Mandalore permanently afterward.

Though peace had returned, he never stopped searching for ways to heal Satine fully. The Force techniques he and Anakin discovered years earlier continued evolving through careful study. He explored ancient healing sites, consulted surviving Jedi healers, even corresponded cautiously with scholars from worlds outside Republic space.

Satine sometimes accused him gently of being impossible.

“You already saved my life,” she told him one evening while they watched sunset over Sundari.

Obi-Wan smiled faintly.

“I’m greedy.”

The Dabi itself gradually dissolved naturally.

Without war or persecution binding them together, its members followed different paths. The philosophy remained influential among younger Force-sensitive communities, particularly its teachings on emotional honesty and healing, but it never hardened into a formal order.

Perhaps that was for the best.

No one among them wanted another rigid institution.

Yet despite peace, despite rebuilding, despite children and diplomacy and hope returning slowly to the galaxy—

One shadow remained.

Maul.

No one knew exactly where he had gone after escaping during Order 66. Rumors surfaced occasionally from criminal syndicates in the Outer Rim. Entire pirate fleets disappearing overnight. Underworld factions reorganizing beneath some unseen hand.

Every few months Obi-Wan still sensed him faintly through the Force.

Waiting.

Wounded pride and hatred kept alive long past reason.

For Maul had lost everything now.

His empire.

His revenge.

His war.

And somewhere deep within the darkness consuming him, one truth burned hotter than all others:

Kenobi still lived.

# Part 5

Peace changed Mandalore slowly.

The scars of occupation remained visible for years after the war ended. Entire districts of Sundari still carried structural damage from the siege. Clan rivalries simmered beneath formal unity. Smugglers and former Shadow Collective remnants lingered at the edges of Mandalorian space waiting for weakness.

And somewhere beyond the Outer Rim, Maul still lived.

Obi-Wan never forgot that.

Even after the Dabi dissolved. Even after Anakin and Padmé settled on Coruscant. Even after Ahsoka departed for Raxus Secundus with Lux Bonteri. Even during the quietest evenings beside Satine beneath Mandalore’s pale skies, part of Obi-Wan remained listening for the echo of Maul through the Force.

Because he understood something most people did not.

Maul would come back.

Not for power alone.

For closure.

Hatred had sustained Maul for too long to end in retreat. He would not accept losing Mandalore, losing his revenge, losing the chance to finally break Obi-Wan completely.

And if he returned, he would strike at Satine first.

The knowledge terrified Obi-Wan in ways he rarely admitted even to himself.

Not the kind of fear that caused panic.

The colder kind. The exhausting kind. The kind that lingered constantly beneath every peaceful moment, waiting.

But unlike years earlier, Obi-Wan no longer tried to suppress the feeling entirely. He had learned too much by now about the dangers of denial. Fear acknowledged could become caution. Love acknowledged could become strength.

So he prepared.

Mandalore prepared with him.

The irony was not lost on anyone that Satine Kryze—the woman who had spent much of her life opposing militarization—now ruled a world steadily rebuilding its defenses under the guidance of Obi-Wan Kenobi and Bo-Katan Kryze.

At first Satine resisted fiercely.

“We cannot become what we spent decades fighting against,” she argued during one late-night strategy meeting.

Bo-Katan folded her arms. “And if Maul returns tomorrow?”

“He won’t rule Mandalore again.”

“That isn’t an answer,” Obi-Wan said quietly.

Satine looked toward him sharply.

Obi-Wan rarely contradicted her publicly unless he felt absolutely necessary.

He stepped closer to her hoverchair, kneeling beside it so they spoke eye-to-eye rather than across the room.

“If we dismantle our defenses now,” he said gently, “we are betting the lives of everyone on this world on Maul deciding not to return.”

“He’s weakened.”

“He’s obsessed.”

That silenced her.

Because she knew he was right.

Eventually Satine relented reluctantly. Mandalore would not expand militarily beyond necessity, but it would remain armed and organized until Maul was dealt with permanently.

Bo-Katan took command of much of the planetary defense structure afterward. The sisters still argued often, but there was affection underneath it now rather than resentment. Years of survival had taught both of them the value of compromise.

Meanwhile Obi-Wan continued studying healing.

What had begun years ago as desperate experimentation alongside Anakin had evolved into something astonishingly advanced. Obi-Wan explored ancient Force vergences beneath Mandalore, corresponded carefully with surviving Jedi healers from Dagobah, and meditated for days at a time searching through the living Force itself.

Satine often worried he pushed himself too hard.

“You know,” she told him once while watching him pore over ancient medical holocrons, “most husbands buy flowers during middle age crises.”

Obi-Wan looked up with complete seriousness.

“I can get flowers too.”

She laughed so hard she nearly fell sideways out of her chair.

But beneath the warmth remained a harder truth neither could escape.

Satine still suffered.

The hoverchair gave her mobility, and she refused to let others pity her, but pain lingered constantly beneath the surface. Some mornings she woke unable to move comfortably at all. Certain injuries the darksaber inflicted never truly stopped burning.

And Obi-Wan could not accept that as final.

Then Maul returned.

The alarms began shortly before dawn.

Long-range patrol craft detected unauthorized hyperspace entries near Mandalorian orbit, followed by attacks on outer defense stations. Within minutes emergency sirens spread through Sundari while military channels exploded with reports.

Bo-Katan entered the palace command center already wearing armor.

“It’s him,” she said immediately.

Obi-Wan had known before she spoke.

He could feel Maul approaching through the Force like an old wound reopening.

Satine’s expression hardened quietly.

“Then let’s finish this.”

Maul descended upon Mandalore at the head of a brutal but comparatively small force: fanatics, mercenaries, surviving Shadow Collective loyalists. He no longer possessed the resources for occupation. This was not conquest.

It was vengeance.

And at his side he carried the darksaber once more.

The fighting across Sundari became savage but controlled. Mandalore had prepared exactly for this moment. Defense batteries locked down major transit routes while Bo-Katan’s forces isolated Maul’s landing zones before his followers could spread chaos through civilian sectors.

Maul himself carved directly toward the palace.

Toward Satine.

He reached the central plaza at dusk.

Obi-Wan waited there alone.

The city around them burned with reflected firelight while Mandalorian gunships roared overhead. Citizens watched from distant balconies in tense silence as the two old enemies faced one another one final time.

Maul ignited both weapons simultaneously.

The darksaber hissed to life in one hand.

His double-bladed crimson lightsaber erupted in the other.

“You’ve grown old, Kenobi,” Maul said softly.

Obi-Wan drew his own blade.

“So have you.”

Then they collided.

The duel surpassed everything between them before.

Maul fought like a creature trying to outrun extinction itself. Rage poured through every strike. He attacked relentlessly with both weapons simultaneously, shifting between impossible angles designed to overwhelm Obi-Wan’s defenses through sheer pressure.

Years earlier such aggression might have worked.

Not anymore.

Obi-Wan’s Soresu had become something extraordinary over time. His defense no longer resembled passive survival. It flowed around attacks with almost frightening calmness, conserving energy while forcing opponents deeper into frustration and exhaustion.

And unlike before, Obi-Wan no longer fought divided against himself.

No guilt.

No repression.

No uncertainty about what mattered.

Maul sensed it eventually.

“You’ve changed,” he snarled during one violent exchange.

“Yes,” Obi-Wan replied.

“And she changed you.”

Obi-Wan’s eyes hardened.

“Yes.”

The answer enraged Maul more than any insult could have.

He attacked furiously, red and black blades crashing against Obi-Wan’s defense like storms breaking against stone. Entire sections of the plaza shattered beneath missed strikes.

But gradually the pattern shifted.

Maul grew slower.

Obi-Wan grew clearer.

Years of fear transformed into focus. Every nightmare about losing Satine. Every moment of helplessness on Naboo and Mandalore. Every lesson learned beside Anakin and through the Force itself.

All of it converged here.

Finally Maul lunged too aggressively with the darksaber.

Obi-Wan pivoted.

One clean motion.

His blade pierced Maul through the chest.

Silence fell across the plaza.

Maul staggered backward, staring downward in disbelief.

The darksaber slipped from his grasp and clattered across the stone.

For a moment the hatred in his eyes faded, replaced by exhaustion deeper than pain.

“So this…” he whispered weakly, “…is how it ends.”

Obi-Wan lowered him carefully to the ground.

“Yes.”

Maul looked up at him one last time.

Not with peace.

But with emptiness.

Then Darth Maul died at last beneath Mandalorian skies.

The war he began decades earlier finally ended with him.

Obi-Wan stood slowly afterward and picked up the darksaber.

Around him, Mandalorian warriors knelt.

Not because they worshipped him.

Because he had defended their world when it mattered most.

And because Mandalore remembered.

In the weeks following Maul’s death, relief spread through the planet like sunlight after years of storm.

For the first time in a very long while, Obi-Wan truly relaxed.

Only then did he finally act upon the conclusion he had spent years fearing.

Because through all his studies, all his meditation, all his healing research, he had uncovered a truth both elegant and terrible.

Satine’s injuries could be healed fully.

But only if the original wound itself was undone.

Not metaphorically.

Precisely.

The same weapon.

The same path.

The same moment reversed through the Force.

When Obi-Wan first explained the theory, Satine stared at him for nearly a full minute.

“You want to stab me again.”

Obi-Wan grimaced slightly. “When you phrase it like that—”

“How else would one phrase it?”

“It would be carefully controlled.”

“With the ancient murder sword.”

Obi-Wan actually laughed despite himself.

Then his expression sobered.

“It’s dangerous.”

Satine studied him quietly.

“Do you believe it will work?”

“Yes.”

“And if it doesn’t?”

Obi-Wan’s silence answered clearly enough.

Satine reached out and touched his face gently.

“I trust you.”

The procedure took place privately within the palace medical chamber.

No crowds. No ceremony.

Only Obi-Wan, Satine, a handful of medical droids, and the darksaber resting between them.

Satine positioned herself upright from her hoverchair as Obi-Wan steadied his breathing.

“You can still change your mind,” he whispered.

“So can you.”

He smiled faintly.

Then he ignited the blade.

The black energy hummed softly in the room.

Obi-Wan’s hands trembled only once before he steadied them completely. He positioned the blade carefully against the old scar tissue exactly where Maul had struck years earlier.

Satine nodded once.

Obi-Wan drove the darksaber through her body.

Satine cried out in agony, gripping the chair hard enough to crack the armrests. Fresh pain exploded through old trauma all at once. Tears streamed down her face instantly.

But Obi-Wan held her firmly.

And reached into the Force.

The healing state overtook him completely.

He saw the old wound as it truly existed—not merely flesh damaged, but a moment frozen inside Satine’s body and spirit. The injury had never fully ended. Part of her remained trapped inside that throne room with Maul standing over her.

Carefully, impossibly carefully, Obi-Wan began withdrawing the darksaber backward along the exact path it had originally traveled.

As the blade moved, the Force flowed behind it.

Healing.

Restoring.

Undoing.

Satine gasped sharply.

The pain changed.

Not disappearing suddenly, but unraveling.

Scar tissue vanished beneath the retreating blade. Nerve damage reformed. Destroyed pathways reconnected. Years of agony dissolved piece by piece into warmth.

Obi-Wan focused so deeply he barely breathed.

Finally the darksaber cleared her body completely.

The wound was gone.

Not healed.

Gone.

Satine collapsed forward into Obi-Wan’s arms shaking violently while medical droids scanned her in stunned silence.

Every reading came back normal.

Obi-Wan deactivated the darksaber slowly.

“Satine…” he whispered.

She looked at him uncertainly.

Then, very carefully, she stood.

No hoverchair.

No support.

No pain.

For the first time in years, her legs held her weight naturally.

Satine took one hesitant step.

Then another.

Her breathing broke halfway into a laugh and halfway into tears.

“It doesn’t hurt,” she whispered.

Obi-Wan looked as though he might collapse from relief alone.

Satine reached for him immediately, and he caught her as she wrapped both arms around his neck.

Outside the palace windows, Mandalore’s lights stretched endlessly beneath the night sky.

And for the first time since Maul’s blade pierced her body years ago, Satine Kryze finally felt whole again.

# Epilogue

The Jedi did not return to Coruscant immediately.

Even after Palpatine’s death and the restoration of the Republic, many within the Order understood that simply moving back into the old Temple and resuming life exactly as before would be a catastrophic mistake. Too much had happened. Too many had died. And, uncomfortably, too many criticisms raised by former Jedi and by the Dabi had proven valid.

Dagobah changed them.

Living together in hardship stripped away much of the ceremonial distance the Jedi Order had accumulated over centuries. Masters cooked meals beside initiates. Younglings grew up seeing Council members argue openly, apologize openly, grieve openly. There was less grandeur there, but more honesty.

Eventually the Jedi established a compromise.

The Coruscant Temple was reopened, but not as the unquestioned center of the Order anymore. Smaller enclaves spread across the galaxy gained greater autonomy. Attachment rules were revised heavily after years of bitter internal debate. Marriage was no longer forbidden outright, though emotional discipline remained central to training.

Mace Windu opposed some of the reforms at first.

Yoda surprised everyone by supporting most of them.

“Too afraid of loss, we became,” he admitted quietly during one Council gathering. “And because of that fear, blind we were.”

Not all Jedi agreed. Some left voluntarily to form smaller, stricter traditions elsewhere. Others embraced the changes eagerly. The rebuilt Order became less unified philosophically than before, but perhaps more resilient because of it.

And interestingly, the Jedi never fully dismissed the Dabi’s influence.

Though the organization itself dissolved, its teachings spread quietly through younger generations of Force users. Healing techniques developed by Anakin and Obi-Wan entered mainstream Jedi study eventually, albeit under careful supervision. Emotional honesty stopped being treated as dangerous by default.

No one said the old Order had failed entirely.

But almost no one believed it should remain unchanged either.

—————

The darksaber became Obi-Wan’s problem almost immediately.

At first he simply intended to lock the weapon away somewhere secure and never think about it again. To him, it was less a symbol of leadership than an extremely traumatic piece of metal associated with Maul ruining his life repeatedly.

Unfortunately, Mandalorians had opinions.

Very strong opinions.

The fact that Obi-Wan defeated Maul in direct combat while Maul wielded the darksaber triggered ancient cultural traditions many clans still took seriously. Within months, rumors spread through Mandalorian space referring to Obi-Wan as Mand’alor.

This horrified Obi-Wan personally.

“I absolutely do not want that title,” he told Bo-Katan immediately.

Bo-Katan, deeply amused by the entire situation, leaned back in her chair.

“You know that refusing it makes them respect you more.”

“Of course it does.”

“It’s very annoying.”

Satine handled the matter with significantly more composure than Obi-Wan.

Publicly, she clarified repeatedly that she remained Mandalore’s legitimate ruler through constitutional authority, while Obi-Wan held no official political office beyond advisory duties.

Privately, she found the entire thing hilarious.

Especially because certain traditionalist clans clearly admired Obi-Wan now in ways they never entirely admitted before. He had defeated Maul, protected Mandalore repeatedly, survived impossible odds, and apparently wanted power so little that he actively recoiled from it.

To many Mandalorians, this accidentally made him ideal leadership material.

The compromise that eventually emerged was awkward but functional: Satine remained ruler of Mandalore politically, while Obi-Wan became an unofficial symbolic figure associated with the defense and unity of the planet.

The darksaber itself stayed in his possession.

Mostly because nobody else could convince him to hand it over safely.

Obi-Wan kept it locked securely in the palace under multiple layers of security and Force protections. He rarely used it. In fact, he visibly disliked igniting it at all.

But occasionally, during ceremonies or moments of crisis, the black blade reappeared at his side.

And every single time, Mandalorians around him stood a little straighter.

—————

Life on Raxus Secundus surprised Ahsoka.

After years of war, military command, fugitives, and galactic collapse, peace initially felt unnatural to her. Lux adapted much faster. He genuinely enjoyed governance, diplomacy, and reconstruction work. Ahsoka took longer to stop expecting disaster every time alarms sounded somewhere in the city.

But eventually she settled into it.

The new Confederacy became something radically different from the wartime Separatist movement. Without Sith manipulation driving conflict, many CIS worlds actually developed into remarkably functional local democracies with strong anti-authoritarian traditions. Raxus itself transformed into a center for interplanetary reconciliation efforts.

Ahsoka worked extensively with refugee programs and postwar trauma recovery.

She turned out to be extremely good at helping former child soldiers.

Probably because she had been one herself.

She and Lux never married formally for several years. Neither cared much about ceremony after everything they survived together. But they built a life piece by piece anyway: shared apartments that slowly became homes, late-night political arguments, quiet mornings overlooking the Raxus skyline.

The children question arrived gradually.

Then painfully.

Togruta and humans could not naturally reproduce together.

At first neither wanted to discuss how deeply that hurt.

Lux blamed himself privately for a while. Ahsoka did too, though she hid it better. Years earlier she would have simply buried the grief and moved on automatically.

Now she didn’t want to do that anymore.

Eventually they chose adoption.

Not symbolically.

Not reluctantly.

Wholeheartedly.

The first child they adopted was a war orphan from an Outer Rim colony devastated during the Clone Wars. Then another years later. Their home slowly became chaotic, loud, politically argumentative, and full of children who inherited Ahsoka’s talent for climbing things they absolutely should not climb.

Lux once walked into his office to discover one of their daughters hanging upside down from the ceiling lights while Ahsoka casually continued reading reports beneath her.

“You know,” Lux said carefully, “most parents stop this behavior.”

Ahsoka glanced upward.

“She’s fine.”

“She’s upside down.”

“She’s very coordinated upside down.”

Their children adored her completely.

The confrontation over Luke and Leia became legendary within certain Jedi circles afterward because of how catastrophically it went.

The twins were still young when several senior Jedi arrived on Coruscant hoping to discuss formal Jedi training arrangements with Padmé and Anakin. Officially the conversation was meant to be respectful and diplomatic.

Unfortunately, one of the visiting Masters used the phrase “the children should be brought to the Temple while they’re still young enough.”

That wording turned the entire meeting into a disaster instantly.

Anakin’s expression became so dangerous so quickly that Obi-Wan later described it as “watching a reactor overload in real time.”

“You are not taking my children anywhere,” Anakin said flatly.

The Jedi Master tried to clarify that no one intended force or coercion—

Which only made things worse because now Padmé became angry too.

“What exactly,” she asked with terrifying calmness, “makes you think the Jedi Order has authority over our children?”

The discussion deteriorated rapidly after that.

Obi-Wan eventually intervened before someone said something irreversible.

In the end a compromise emerged months later after cooler heads prevailed. Luke and Leia would receive Force education voluntarily through a hybrid approach involving both Jedi teachers and family oversight rather than traditional Temple separation.

The twins themselves later exploited this arrangement shamelessly.

Especially Leia.

At age eight she once informed a frustrated Jedi instructor:

“My father says questioning authority is healthy.”

The instructor closed his eyes briefly.

“Yes. That does sound like your father.”

The joke happened during dinner.

Which somehow made it worse.

Years had passed since Satine’s healing, enough that the trauma surrounding the darksaber no longer dominated every conversation involving it. Obi-Wan had even developed the ability to occasionally speak about the entire ordeal without looking emotionally devastated afterward.

This proved dangerous.

Because Obi-Wan’s sense of humor, long suppressed beneath Jedi discipline for decades, became increasingly reckless once relaxed domestic happiness entered his life.

The conversation itself had nothing to do with Maul initially. They were discussing some Mandalorian ceremonial nonsense involving ancient weapons when Satine made an offhand comment about how much trouble one sword had caused over the years.

Without thinking, Obi-Wan replied:

“Well, to be fair, you are one of the few people qualified to compare being stabbed by the darksaber twice.”

Silence.

Absolute silence.

Satine stared at him.

Bo-Katan slowly lowered her drink.

Even the nearby servants stopped moving.

Obi-Wan realized his mistake immediately.

“Oh no,” he whispered.

Satine continued staring at him for several long seconds.

Then she burst into helpless laughter so violent she nearly fell sideways out of her chair again.

Bo-Katan followed immediately afterward, laughing hard enough she had to stand up.

Obi-Wan sat there looking deeply ashamed of himself while both sisters completely lost composure for nearly five full minutes.

Eventually Satine managed to wipe tears from her eyes.

“You,” she gasped between laughs, “are a terrible human being.”

Obi-Wan nodded solemnly.

“Yes. I think that’s been conclusively established.”
