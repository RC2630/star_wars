# Part 1

The battlefield was already in motion when Anakin Skywalker first felt it.

Blaster fire stitched across the broken ground of a half-built settlement, Republic walkers trading shots with Separatist armor that crawled over the ridgelines like metallic insects. Dust hung in the air, lit orange by distant explosions. Commander Rex was barking orders into his helmet comms, and Ahsoka Tano was already moving ahead of the line, deflecting incoming fire with her blades as she pressed the enemy back.

Anakin stood at the ridge, one hand lifted slightly, as if listening to something no one else could hear.

“General?” Rex’s voice crackled through the comm. “We’ve got their flank breaking. If we push now—”

Anakin didn’t answer immediately.

His eyes had gone distant.

The noise of battle didn’t fade, but it became… secondary. Like it was happening far away, behind thick glass. Something else was pressing in. Not words. Not images at first. A pull, sharp and undeniable, like standing at the edge of a cliff and feeling gravity tilt toward the sky instead of the ground.

Then it came.

A planet. Remote. Quiet. Unnamed in his mind, but certain in its existence. He saw himself standing there. He saw Padmé beside him. Not in battle. Not in Senate halls. Just walking across pale ground under a wide, unfamiliar sky.

And then a scroll.

Old. Physical. Important in a way he could not explain.

The vision ended as abruptly as it had come, snapping him back into the roar of blaster fire and the shouts of clones.

Rex was still speaking. “General Skywalker?”

Anakin lowered his hand.

“I need to leave,” he said.

Ahsoka had just leapt back to the ridge, landing in a crouch beside him. “Leave? We’re in the middle of—”

“I know,” Anakin cut in. His tone wasn’t loud, but it had weight. Finality. “You’ve got this, Ahsoka. Rex. Finish it.”

Rex straightened slightly. “General, respectfully, we need you here to—”

“I said you’ve got it.”

Ahsoka’s expression shifted. “Anakin, what happened?”

He hesitated for the briefest moment, like he was trying to find words that didn’t exist.

“A Force vision,” he said. “I need to get back to Coruscant. Now.”

That alone changed the air around them. Ahsoka’s posture softened slightly, but her eyes stayed sharp.

“Is it urgent enough to abandon the operation?”

“Yes,” Anakin said immediately.

Rex looked out over the battlefield, then back at him. “We can hold the line, General. But this had better be worth it.”

“It is,” Anakin replied.

And then he was gone from the ridge, already moving toward his ship.

—

Coruscant was loud in a different way.

Not blaster fire and collapsing metal, but endless motion—traffic lanes, Senate spires, engines cutting through layered skies. Senator Padmé Amidala was in the middle of reviewing reports in her office when the doors opened without announcement.

Anakin walked straight in.

Her assistant started to protest. “You can’t just—”

“It’s fine,” Padmé said quickly, recognizing him immediately. Then, more carefully, “Anakin… what’s wrong?”

He didn’t sit.

“I had a vision,” he said.

That alone made her expression shift. “Another one?”

“Yes. And I need you to come with me.”

Padmé blinked once. “Come with you where?”

“I don’t know the name of the place,” Anakin admitted. “But I can find it.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s a Force vision,” he said, like that explained everything. “We were there. You and me. On a planet. Remote. We found something. A scroll. It matters.”

Padmé stared at him for a long moment.

“Anakin, I have Senate duties. There are delegations arriving tomorrow, and I have—”

“I don’t care,” he said, then immediately softened it, but only slightly. “Padmé, I can feel it. The place is calling to me. I can find it without charts. Without navigation. I just need you there.”

Her eyes narrowed. “You are asking me to abandon the Senate because of a dream.”

“It wasn’t a dream.”

“And how do you intend to explain this to anyone?”

“I don’t.”

That earned him a long silence.

Finally, Padmé exhaled slowly. “If I go with you, this has to be controlled. No recklessness.”

Anakin’s expression brightened just slightly. “So you’re coming.”

“I didn’t say that.”

But she was already reaching for her cloak.

—

The Naboo skiff lifted off from Coruscant with minimal ceremony and far too little explanation for anyone on board to feel comfortable.

C-3PO stood near the rear compartment, hands clasped tightly. “I must express my considerable concern, Master Anakin. This entire undertaking lacks all standard navigational procedure, not to mention Senate clearance, and—oh dear—”

R2-D2 beeped from beside the controls.

“Yes, I am aware that Master Skywalker is piloting manually,” Threepio continued. “That is precisely the issue.”

Padmé stood near the cockpit entrance, watching Anakin at the controls. “You didn’t bring charts.”

“I don’t need them.”

“That’s not comforting.”

“It should be,” he said.

The skiff angled into open space. Coruscant’s layered lights fell away behind them, replaced by the dark stretch of orbit.

Anakin’s hands barely moved on the controls. The ship drifted at first, then corrected, then drifted again—but never according to standard navigation patterns. There was no plotted hyperspace route visible on any display.

Padmé stepped closer. “Anakin, where are we going?”

He didn’t look away from the void ahead.

“Straight,” he said.

“That’s not a direction in space.”

“It is today.”

C-3PO made a soft whimpering sound that he would later deny ever producing.

R2-D2 emitted a questioning series of tones, as if offering assistance.

Anakin shook his head once. “No. Just… let me feel it.”

And then something changed.

The ship stopped drifting.

It began to move with certainty.

Not through mapped lanes, not through calculated jumps, but as if the void itself had a hidden current and Anakin had finally found it.

Padmé looked at him with growing unease. “This shouldn’t be possible.”

Anakin’s voice was quiet. “It is.”

—

Time became difficult to measure.

There were no confirmations from instruments that made sense to anyone but him. No reassuring markers of passing systems. Only the steady sensation in Anakin’s mind growing sharper, like approaching a sound that had been distant for a very long time.

Eventually, the skiff slowed.

Ahead: a planet.

Unmarked. Unclaimed. Quiet in a way that felt almost deliberate.

R2-D2 gave a low whistle.

C-3PO leaned forward slightly. “I do not recognize any records of this world whatsoever. That is… deeply unsettling.”

Anakin stood.

“This is it.”

Padmé studied the planet. “There’s no sign of habitation.”

“There will be,” he said, already moving.

The skiff descended.

It landed on pale ground beneath a sky that felt too still to be comfortable.

No cities. No structures. Just distant terrain and silence broken only by wind.

Anakin turned to the droids. “Stay with the ship.”

C-3PO looked offended and relieved at the same time. “Oh—well, of course. That is entirely within acceptable parameters of droid safety protocols—”

R2-D2 let out a skeptical chirp.

Anakin had already stepped away.

Padmé followed.

—

They walked for a long time.

The ground was firm, almost stone-like, but dust shifted under each step. The air carried no scent she could identify. No landmarks guided them, yet Anakin never hesitated.

Padmé finally spoke. “You said you’ve never been here.”

“I haven’t.”

“Then how do you know where you’re going?”

Anakin didn’t slow. “Because it’s already in my head. It’s like… remembering something I never experienced.”

“That’s not reassuring.”

“It’s accurate.”

Eventually, the landscape changed slightly. A shallow rise of terrain, then a break in it—like something had once been buried and revealed again by time or wind.

Anakin stopped.

“This is it.”

Padmé stepped beside him.

There, partially embedded in the ground, was a scroll.

Not digital. Not holographic. Physical, as if it had been deliberately placed and preserved. The material looked old, but intact. Covered in unfamiliar markings that did not match any script she recognized.

Padmé crouched slightly. “This is what you saw?”

Anakin nodded once.

He reached down and lifted it carefully.

For a moment, neither of them spoke.

Then Padmé said, quietly, “We’re going to need the droids.”

—

Back at the skiff, R2-D2 immediately extended a data probe into the scroll’s surface when they returned.

C-3PO hovered anxiously. “I must warn you, Master R2, any attempt to decode unknown linguistic structures without contextual databanks could result in catastrophic mistranslation—”

R2-D2 beeped dismissively and continued scanning.

Lines of shifting symbols appeared on a small projection.

Padmé leaned in. “Can it be translated?”

C-3PO hesitated. “With sufficient time and cross-referencing, I may be able to construct a partial linguistic bridge. But I cannot guarantee accuracy.”

“Do it,” Anakin said.

Time passed in uneven stretches of silence, broken only by the soft beeps of R2-D2 and the increasingly agitated muttering of C-3PO as he attempted to reconcile patterns that did not match anything in his memory banks.

At last, a coherent structure emerged on the projection.

C-3PO straightened slightly. “I believe I have it. Or at least… a version of it that appears internally consistent.”

Padmé looked at Anakin. “Read it.”

C-3PO adjusted his stance, then began.

Anakin and Padmé stood side by side as the words formed on the display, waiting to understand what had been waiting here in silence.

---

# Part 2

The translated words hung in the air long after C-3PO stopped speaking.

For a moment, nobody moved.

The skiff’s interior felt suddenly smaller, as if the walls had closed in just slightly without anyone noticing. Outside, the unnamed planet remained silent and unmoving, its pale horizon stretching in every direction like it had been waiting for something it already knew would happen.

C-3PO stood rigidly beside the display. “I must stress again, this is an extremely unusual textual structure. The section labeled ‘Attack of the Clones’ appears to be a complete narrative record of prior events involving both of you. In precise detail. Including… private conversational exchanges. I find this deeply inappropriate.”

R2-D2 let out a low, uncertain tone.

Padmé didn’t look away from the projection. “Read it again.”

“I would prefer not to,” C-3PO said immediately.

“Read the next part,” she repeated, firmer.

C-3PO hesitated, then continued.

As the words were spoken, Anakin’s expression slowly changed. Not shock at first—something more like resistance. Like his mind trying to reject what his ears were hearing while his instincts kept confirming it.

The script described a sequence of events that matched what he and Padmé had lived through with exactness that should not have been possible. Not broad strokes. Not general outcomes. Specific conversations. Private moments. The kind of things that were never recorded, never spoken aloud in any official report, never witnessed by anyone else.

Then came the references to their time at Varykino Villa.

C-3PO’s voice faltered slightly as he read certain lines. Even the droid seemed to recognize the weight of what he was saying.

Padmé’s hands tightened at her sides. “Stop.”

C-3PO stopped instantly.

Anakin stared at the projection. “That wasn’t public knowledge.”

Padmé looked at him. “No one else was there for most of that time. Not even Senate staff. Not even… security.”

R2-D2 emitted a soft series of tones, subdued in a way that suggested he had no explanation either.

Anakin shook his head once. “Then it’s a trick.”

C-3PO straightened slightly, almost offended despite himself. “I must point out that the translated sections contain references to events and conversations that neither I nor R2-D2 were present for. If this were some form of deception, it would require access to information beyond any reasonable computational inference.”

Silence returned.

Padmé’s voice lowered. “So what are you saying?”

C-3PO hesitated again. “I am saying… I do not understand how this could exist.”

Anakin exhaled slowly. “Neither do I.”

He stepped closer to the scroll, eyes narrowing. “Flip it.”

Padmé looked at him. “Anakin—”

“Flip it.”

After a pause, she did.

The back side of the scroll revealed a second title, etched in the same unfamiliar script, already partially translated by R2-D2’s ongoing work.

C-3PO leaned in reluctantly. “The second section is titled… ‘Revenge of the Sith.’”

The name meant nothing to the droids.

But it landed differently on Anakin and Padmé.

C-3PO continued reading.

At first, the language described events that had not yet happened. Political shifts. Rising tension. The Chancellor’s growing influence. The Jedi becoming increasingly entangled in conflict. The words were structured like history, but they pointed forward instead of backward.

Then the tone shifted.

Anakin Skywalker would fall.

The statement was simple. Direct. Not metaphorical.

Padmé’s gaze didn’t move from the projection, but her breathing changed slightly.

C-3PO continued, slower now, as if even his processing systems were resisting the content.

The Chancellor was identified in the script as something more than he appeared. A hidden force guiding events from within the Republic itself. The name “Palpatine” appeared in the translation, and for the first time C-3PO paused on it.

“Shall I continue?” he asked, unusually quiet.

Padmé answered immediately. “Yes.”

Anakin did not speak.

The script continued.

Jedi would be betrayed. The temple would fall. Younglings would be killed.

The words were not embellished. Not emotional. They were written as statements of occurrence.

C-3PO’s voice became increasingly strained as he progressed, as if even reading it was uncomfortable. At one point he stopped entirely.

“I must note,” he said, “that I am detecting extreme psychological distress indicators in both of you, and I strongly recommend—”

“Keep reading,” Anakin said.

C-3PO obeyed.

Padmé’s hands were clenched now, but her voice remained steady. “Continue.”

Then came the final sections.

Anakin Skywalker, Jedi Knight, would become the instrument of those killings.

Padmé would die during childbirth.

The final line lingered after it was spoken.

C-3PO stopped reading. “That concludes the deciphered portion.”

No one responded.

Even R2-D2 remained silent.

The only sound was the faint wind outside the skiff hull.

Anakin stepped back slowly from the projection.

For a moment, his face was unreadable. Not calm, not angry—something heavier than both, as if every possible reaction had collided and canceled each other out.

“That’s not… possible,” he said finally.

Padmé looked at him. “After what we just heard, I don’t think we can say that anymore.”

Anakin turned sharply toward her. “You believe it?”

“I believe the first part,” she said quietly. “Because it matched everything we lived through.”

“That doesn’t mean the second part is real.”

Padmé’s eyes stayed on him. “It means someone, or something, knew exactly what we did. In detail we cannot explain.”

Anakin paced once, then stopped. “So what, this is some kind of prediction?”

C-3PO spoke carefully. “If I may… the structure resembles an absolute record. Not prediction in the conventional sense, but… a fixed account.”

Anakin shook his head. “No. If we know about it, we can change it.”

Padmé’s expression tightened slightly. “Anakin.”

He turned back to her. “If this is real, then it only happens because we don’t know about it. Now we do.”

“That assumes knowledge is enough,” she said. “What if it isn’t?”

Anakin’s voice rose slightly. “Then what? We just accept it?”

Padmé didn’t answer immediately. When she did, her tone was careful.

“If it is fixed,” she said, “then fighting it in the wrong way may only lead us back to it.”

Silence again.

Anakin looked at the scroll, then back at her.

“So what do you suggest?”

Padmé stepped closer to the projection, studying the strange script still glowing faintly with partial translation. “We change the source.”

Anakin frowned. “The source?”

“The scroll itself,” she said. “If it contains what happens, then perhaps it can be altered before those events occur.”

C-3PO made a distressed sound. “I must advise extreme caution. Interacting with unknown written systems of this complexity could result in irreversible misinterpretation of causality structures.”

Anakin ignored him. “We don’t even know how to write in it.”

R2-D2 emitted a short series of tones, and a small segment of the scroll’s decoded structure highlighted itself on the display.

C-3PO translated automatically, reluctantly. “R2-D2 indicates that the script is… partially consistent. He believes pattern learning may allow reconstruction of writing functions.”

Anakin turned back toward the scroll. “So we learn it.”

Padmé looked at him. “And then we decide what to change.”

Anakin nodded slowly. “We stop this before it happens.”

Padmé did not immediately agree or disagree.

Instead, she looked at the words still hovering on the projection.

Anakin Skywalker would fall.

Then she said, quietly, “If we are going to try to change it, we need to understand exactly what it is telling us. Not just the outcome. Every step.”

Anakin reached out and steadied the scroll on the table.

“Then we start here,” he said. “And we learn how to rewrite it.”

# Part 3

The silence that followed their decision gradually gave way to purpose.

Padmé sat at the small table inside the skiff with the scroll laid carefully across it. Anakin stood beside her, arms folded, while C-3PO and R2-D2 remained nearby.

Finally, Padmé looked up.

"We need to know when this happens."

Anakin nodded. "Right now all we know is that it hasn't happened yet."

C-3PO's shoulders drooped.

"Oh... must I read it again?"

"I'm afraid so," Padmé said apologetically.

The protocol droid sighed.

"I never imagined that perfect linguistic translation would become such a burdensome talent."

R2-D2 gave an amused whistle.

"You needn't sound so pleased," C-3PO muttered.

This time, as C-3PO began reading the translated text again from the beginning of the section titled *Revenge of the Sith*, R2-D2 connected a cable to a datapad resting on the table.

Each translated line was copied into the datapad as it was spoken, producing a searchable Galactic Basic transcript.

When the reading finally concluded once more, C-3PO looked positively exhausted.

"I sincerely hope that was the final recitation."

Anakin picked up the datapad.

"It won't be."

C-3PO let out a tiny groan.

Over the next several hours, the couple combed through the text line by line.

Padmé searched for dates.

Anakin searched for military references.

They looked for explicit years.

They looked for calendars.

Planetary cycles.

Birthdays.

Mentions of ages.

Anniversaries.

Anything that could anchor the script to a point in time.

Again and again they came up empty.

Padmé rubbed her forehead.

"Nothing."

Anakin frowned as he continued scrolling.

"No dates."

"No."

"No references to how long the war has lasted."

"No."

"No mention of anyone's age."

Padmé leaned back.

"It's almost as though whoever wrote this assumed the reader already knew when it was."

Hours continued to pass.

Eventually Anakin stopped scrolling.

"Wait."

Padmé looked over.

"What?"

He began reading portions of the script again, not looking for explicit dates anymore but reconstructing the sequence of events.

"The opening battle."

He continued.

"The rescue."

"The Council."

"The Chancellor."

"Utapau."

"The arrest attempt."

"The Temple."

"Mustafar."

He paused.

"This isn't very long."

Padmé leaned over the datapad.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean..." He traced his finger down the entries. "There's barely any downtime. One event flows almost directly into the next."

He thought for another moment.

"I don't think this takes months."

"How long?"

"I don't know exactly."

He continued mentally piecing together troop movements, hyperspace travel, military deployments and political meetings.

Finally he looked up.

"Less than a month."

Padmé blinked.

"You're certain?"

"As certain as I can be."

He pointed near the end of the transcript.

"You give birth here."

She nodded slowly.

"Yes."

"You'd already have to be heavily pregnant when all this starts."

Padmé instinctively looked down at herself.

Then she looked back at him.

"I don't think I'm pregnant."

"You don't know?"

She gave him an almost incredulous look.

"We're on an unnamed world with two droids and no medical facilities."

"...Good point."

He looked carefully at her stomach.

There was no visible sign whatsoever.

Padmé noticed where he was looking and folded her arms.

"Well?"

"You don't look pregnant."

"I should hope not."

"So..."

He thought for another few seconds.

"If this script is accurate... then these events can't begin immediately."

Padmé slowly nodded.

"There has to be time first."

"Months."

"At least."

For the first time since they had discovered the scroll, both of them visibly relaxed.

Not because the future seemed any less dangerous.

But because it no longer felt imminent.

Padmé released a slow breath.

"We have time to think."

"We don't have to solve everything today."

Anakin nodded.

"We can do this properly."

He looked toward the scroll.

"We learn the language."

Padmé followed his gaze.

"We're not going to start changing symbols we barely understand."

"No."

"We work from what we know."

She smiled faintly.

"Galactic Basic first."

"Aurebesh after that."

"And only then..."

"...do we translate our changes back into whatever language this scroll uses."

C-3PO brightened slightly.

"An entirely sensible methodology."

R2-D2 beeped approvingly.

With the immediate urgency eased, the discussion shifted toward the changes themselves.

Anakin activated the datapad again.

"The obvious ones first."

Padmé nodded.

"No argument there."

He began listing them.

"I don't fall."

She answered immediately.

"You don't fall."

"I don't become..."

He couldn't quite bring himself to say the rest.

Padmé finished quietly.

"You don't become the person described here."

He nodded gratefully.

"No slaughter at the Temple."

"No deaths caused by your fall."

Padmé continued.

"I survive."

"You survive."

"And..."

She hesitated only briefly.

"Mace Windu kills Palpatine."

Anakin looked at the corresponding section of the transcript.

"If that happens..."

"A great deal changes."

Neither of them needed to say more.

They continued making notes.

Then Anakin suddenly stopped.

His eyes fixed on one particular exchange near the beginning of the script.

He read it aloud.

"'All right. But you owe me... and not for saving your skin for the tenth time...'"

He continued.

"'Ninth time... that business on Cato Nemoidia doesn't count. I'll see you at the briefing.'"

He frowned.

Padmé looked over.

"What is it?"

Anakin lowered the datapad.

"I can use this."

"Use what?"

"The rescues."

She looked confused.

"What rescues?"

"Obi-Wan."

He began counting aloud.

"The mission on Carnelion IV."

He raised one finger.

"The pirate blockade."

A second.

"The refinery collapse."

A third.

"The moon over Vanqor."

A fourth.

"The ambush on..."

He continued carefully until he reached the end.

"...Seven."

Padmé watched him.

"You've counted before."

"I've had to."

She smiled faintly.

"Of course you have."

Anakin looked back at the transcript.

"The script says I joke about saving him for the tenth time."

"But Obi-Wan corrects you."

"Ninth."

"And then he says..."

Anakin pointed to the line.

"'That business on Cato Nemoidia doesn't count.'"

He looked up.

"I've never saved him on Cato Nemoidia."

Padmé thought for a moment.

"So whatever that refers to hasn't happened."

"Exactly."

He paced once around the table.

"I've saved him seven times."

"The script implies I'll save him an eighth time."

He pointed farther down.

"Then the Battle of Coruscant happens."

"The Invisible Hand."

"That rescue becomes the ninth."

Padmé nodded slowly.

"And afterward..."

"Something happens on Cato Nemoidia."

He looked thoughtfully at the datapad.

"We don't know what."

"But it happens before the opening conversation."

Padmé smiled slightly.

"So now we have a chronometer."

Anakin tilted his head.

"A what?"

"A way of measuring how close we are."

He considered it.

"I like that."

The realization gave them another reference point.

They no longer had only Padmé's pregnancy as an indicator.

Now Anakin simply had to keep track of how many times he had rescued Obi-Wan.

As the discussion continued, Anakin's expression gradually became more determined.

He scrolled through the transcript again.

There were battle reports.

Starfighter engagements.

Explosions.

Clone casualties.

Ships lost.

He quietly said,

"Look at this."

Padmé followed his finger.

"So many clones."

"They're everywhere."

"They die throughout this."

He remained silent for several moments.

Then he looked up.

"If we're changing things..."

She already knew what he was about to say.

"...we should save them too."

Padmé nodded.

"I'd like that."

"Not just us."

He pointed farther up the transcript.

"If we can change anything..."

His eyes drifted toward the very beginning of the script.

"...what if we start before all this?"

Padmé immediately looked at him.

"What do you mean?"

"What if we insert something before the opening?"

He pointed at the first page.

"If changing the script changes events..."

"...then maybe adding something here changes everything before the Battle of Coruscant."

Padmé's expression hardened instantly.

"No."

Anakin blinked.

"No?"

She shook her head.

"Absolutely not."

He looked surprised.

"Why?"

"Because we have no idea how this works."

She tapped the opening lines.

"These are the foundation."

"If we alter the very beginning..."

"...we may change the entire structure in ways we cannot predict."

She looked directly at him.

"The first lines stay exactly as they are."

"No additions before them."

"No deletions."

"No substitutions."

Anakin thought for a long moment.

Then he nodded.

"You're right."

He set the datapad back down.

"We don't touch the opening."

"Not until we understand far more than we do now."

Padmé smiled with relief.

"Agreed."

While they had spent the afternoon debating possibilities and carefully working through the logic of the mysterious document, the two droids had quietly devoted themselves to a different challenge.

R2-D2 projected increasingly sophisticated analyses of the unknown writing system.

C-3PO cross-referenced the recurring grammatical structures, reconstructed symbol usage, and gradually built a complete functional understanding of the script's written form.

Hours later, C-3PO straightened with unmistakable satisfaction.

"I believe..."

He looked at the scroll.

"...we have done it."

Anakin turned.

"You can write it now?"

"I believe so."

R2-D2 extended a small precision tool from one of his compartments.

Another attachment unfolded beside it.

One produced microscopic engravings.

The other carefully smoothed existing markings away without visibly damaging the material beneath.

C-3PO watched R2-D2 demonstrate on a tiny unused margin before translating.

"R2-D2 confirms that he can create, erase, and modify individual symbols with a degree of precision exceeding biological capability."

Anakin stared at the scroll.

"So we can actually change it."

"It would appear so."

Padmé slowly reached out and rested one hand beside the ancient document.

The impossible object that had first confronted them with a future they could scarcely believe had now become something else entirely.

Not merely a record to be feared.

But a text that could be edited.

And now the greatest challenge before them was no longer *whether* they could alter the words engraved upon it.

It was deciding exactly what those new words should be.

---

# Part 4

The datapad lay between them, filled with notes, revisions, and increasingly elaborate discussions.

Anakin leaned over the translated script.

"I think we should change the Invisible Hand."

Padmé looked up.

"The battle?"

"No. What happens aboard it."

He tapped the relevant section.

"Look."

She moved beside him.

"Palpatine is already restrained aboard Grievous' flagship."

"Yes."

"We should just kill him there."

Padmé considered that for several moments.

"What if Count Dooku kills him?"

Anakin immediately shook his head.

"No."

"Why not?"

He scrolled back to the transcript of *Attack of the Clones*.

"Remember the ending?"

Padmé thought for a moment.

"...Dooku reports to Darth Sidious."

"Exactly."

He opened the other transcript again.

"And this script says Palpatine is Darth Sidious."

The realization settled over both of them again, no less unsettling for having been repeated.

"So Dooku's Sith master is Palpatine," Anakin continued.

"He'd never kill him."

Padmé nodded.

"You're right."

She thought quietly.

Then her eyes brightened slightly.

"What if you kill Palpatine instead?"

Anakin stared at her.

"...Then I'll be prosecuted."

"He is the Chancellor."

"I don't think 'he secretly turned out to be a Sith Lord because an ancient scroll told me so' is going to satisfy a tribunal."

Padmé reluctantly admitted the point.

"Hm."

She folded her arms.

"What if you kill him..."

She looked back down at the script.

"...and afterward everyone believes Count Dooku did it?"

Anakin blinked.

"...Padmé."

"What?"

"That is spectacularly dishonest."

"It solves the legal problem."

"What if people discover the truth?"

She looked at the mysterious scroll.

"Then we write that they don't."

Anakin couldn't help smiling despite himself.

"You've become remarkably comfortable rewriting reality."

She smiled back.

"We've already accepted that we're trying."

He laughed quietly.

"Fair enough."

His attention returned to the script.

"What about Dooku?"

"We still kill him."

"Definitely?"

"The Clone Wars can't end while he's still leading the Separatists."

Anakin nodded.

"I agree."

He began speaking more quickly as the revised sequence assembled itself in his mind.

"I kill Dooku."

Padmé nodded.

"Then?"

"I immediately execute Palpatine while he's still restrained."

"Instead of freeing him."

"Exactly."

He continued.

"Then Obi-Wan and I leave aboard an escape pod."

Padmé leaned closer.

"And afterward?"

"The Invisible Hand explodes."

He tapped the screen.

"Everyone still aboard dies."

Padmé's eyes widened.

"...Including Grievous."

Anakin grinned.

"Exactly."

She looked back down at the script.

"So if anyone asks why both Dooku and Palpatine are dead..."

"...they were aboard a ship that exploded."

She smiled.

"That is surprisingly believable."

"It also removes Grievous."

"The entire Separatist military leadership in one moment."

Anakin nodded.

"We'll have to make some edits."

He pointed at several paragraphs.

"Delete the section where Grievous jettisons all the escape pods."

Padmé nodded while making notes.

"So an escape pod remains available."

"Right."

He pointed farther down.

"And remove the part where Dooku knocks Obi-Wan unconscious."

"So Obi-Wan comes with you."

"Yes."

Padmé continued writing.

"We leave your execution of Dooku unchanged?"

"I think so."

"It already happens."

She nodded.

"After that..."

She paused to think.

"...we add a line."

Anakin looked over.

"'Anakin immediately executed Palpatine instead of releasing him from his restraints.'"

He smiled.

"Very direct."

"I'd rather not leave room for misunderstanding."

He laughed.

"Fair."

Padmé continued.

"Then you and Obi-Wan reach an escape pod."

"The Invisible Hand explodes."

"Grievous dies aboard."

"Done."

They both looked farther down the transcript.

Hundreds of lines still remained.

Padmé quietly said,

"I don't think we need any of this anymore."

Anakin followed her gaze.

The Temple.

Mustafar.

The birth.

The funeral.

None of it made sense if the chain of events had already been broken.

He nodded.

"I think we erase everything after that."

"Everything?"

"I don't see how any of it still happens."

Padmé considered it carefully.

"...Neither do I."

Anakin paused on another section.

"This."

"'Execute Order 66.'"

He frowned.

"The script never explains why that command works."

Padmé searched through the text herself.

"No."

"It only shows the results."

"We never learn the mechanism."

Anakin sighed.

"I guess we leave that alone."

Padmé nodded.

"If Palpatine dies before giving the order..."

"...then perhaps nobody ever needs to know."

He looked thoughtful.

"Someone else can figure out someday why the clones would suddenly attack the Jedi after hearing a single command."

Padmé made one final note.

"We don't have enough information to rewrite something we don't understand."

After removing nearly everything that followed the destruction of the Invisible Hand, they found themselves staring at an unexpectedly blank ending.

Padmé rested the stylus on the table.

"We need an aftermath."

Anakin nodded.

"What happens politically?"

She thought for a while.

"The Clone Wars end."

He agreed immediately.

"The Separatists surrender."

"No endless fighting."

"No."

Anakin pointed at another note.

"What about the Separatist Council?"

Padmé considered it.

"I don't think they all need to die."

"You don't?"

She shook her head.

"If they surrender..."

"...they should stand trial."

Anakin nodded.

"Nute Gunray?"

"A prison cell."

He smiled.

"I can live with that."

Padmé continued.

"We also need a new Chancellor."

Anakin leaned back.

"This is definitely your area."

She smiled faintly.

"I appreciate your confidence."

She thought quietly for several moments.

Finally she spoke.

"Bail Organa."

Anakin nodded without hesitation.

"If you think he'd do well..."

"I do."

"Then Bail Organa becomes Chancellor."

She wrote it down.

Hours passed as they refined wording, checked consistency, and slowly rebuilt the remainder of the story into something entirely different.

At one point Padmé stopped unexpectedly.

"Oh."

Anakin looked over.

"What?"

She pointed to one particular line they had almost overlooked.

"'My wife and I will take the girl. We've always talked of adopting a baby girl. She will be loved with us.'"

Padmé looked thoughtful.

"Bail wants a daughter."

Anakin blinked.

"Oh."

She smiled.

"If we're not giving our child away anymore..."

"...then perhaps they should have one of their own."

He laughed.

"You want to rewrite reality so Bail's wife becomes pregnant?"

Padmé shrugged.

"It seems kinder."

Anakin grinned.

"All right."

He leaned over the draft.

"Surprise pregnancy."

Padmé raised a finger.

"Make sure it's a girl."

"Done."

Some time later, after another lengthy round of revisions, Anakin suddenly looked up.

"I've got another one."

Padmé smiled cautiously.

"Should I be worried?"

"The Jedi Council."

"What about them?"

He pointed toward the ending.

"We add a Council meeting."

She waited.

"Yoda announces that Jedi are permitted to marry."

Padmé stared at him.

Then she broke into a genuine smile.

"...That's actually a wonderful idea."

"No more hiding."

"No more secret marriage."

She nodded enthusiastically.

"Yes."

"We're absolutely doing that."

They carefully inserted the new scene.

Later still, Anakin developed a very different idea.

He looked unusually pleased with himself.

"Padmé."

She looked up.

"What now?"

He pointed toward several quiet moments after the war.

"We should add some kiss scenes."

She blinked.

"...What?"

"A couple."

He smiled innocently.

"Really over the top ones."

She stared at him in disbelief.

"Anakin."

"What?"

"What is wrong with you?"

He shrugged.

"Nothing."

Then, with complete sincerity, he added,

"If we're already rewriting the future..."

"...we might as well have some fun."

Padmé covered her face with one hand.

"I cannot believe I'm hearing this."

He looked entirely unashamed.

"So?"

She slowly lowered her hand.

"...Fine."

He grinned triumphantly.

"But only because apparently we're rewriting everything else."

"I'll take it."

"And no making them ridiculous."

"No promises."

She gave him a look.

"...Reasonably ridiculous."

She sighed.

"I suppose that's the best compromise I'm going to get."

A little while afterward Anakin spoke again.

"Oh."

Padmé looked up with mock suspicion.

"What now?"

"I make myself a Jedi Master."

She laughed.

"Of course."

"I've earned it."

"You'll remain on the Council?"

"Yes."

She tilted her head.

"Why stop there?"

He looked confused.

"What do you mean?"

"If we're editing the future..."

"...why not make yourself Grand Master?"

Anakin immediately shook his head.

"No."

She blinked.

"No?"

"I don't think I know enough to be Grand Master."

He smiled sheepishly.

"Master is enough."

Padmé nodded approvingly.

"I think that's a surprisingly modest decision."

"I have my moments."

As the revisions neared completion, Anakin reached the end of the original script one final time.

Something finally occurred to him.

"Padmé."

"Yes?"

"Where's Ahsoka?"

She frowned.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean..."

He searched the transcript again.

"...she isn't here."

Padmé leaned over.

Together they searched every page.

Nothing.

Not a single mention.

Anakin looked increasingly unsettled.

"That's strange."

"Very strange."

He looked toward Padmé.

"Should we add her?"

She was quiet for a long time.

Finally she shook her head.

"No."

He looked surprised.

"No?"

"If she's completely absent..."

"...there may be a reason."

She folded her hands together.

"We don't know what that reason is."

"So..."

"...we leave her alone."

Anakin considered it.

Then nodded.

"Playing it safe."

"Exactly."

"No unnecessary changes."

He smiled.

"All right."

At last there were no more revisions to debate.

The droids began the painstaking work.

Guided by Anakin and Padmé's carefully prepared Basic draft, C-3PO translated each new sentence into the mysterious language of the scroll.

R2-D2 precisely erased obsolete engravings.

Then, with microscopic accuracy, he inscribed the replacement text into the ancient material.

Line after line disappeared.

Line after line took its place.

The destruction of the Invisible Hand.

The deaths of Dooku, Palpatine, and Grievous.

The end of the Clone Wars.

The Separatist surrender.

Trials instead of massacres.

Bail Organa becoming Chancellor.

A daughter born into Bail Organa's family.

A Jedi Council that accepted marriage.

Anakin Skywalker receiving the rank of Jedi Master.

Several unmistakably affectionate scenes that caused Padmé to roll her eyes while Anakin looked entirely satisfied.

Hours later, C-3PO finally stepped back.

"It is complete."

Anakin looked over the rewritten scroll.

"So..."

Padmé smiled.

"So."

The impossible task was finished.

The two of them carefully packed away the datapad, secured the scroll, and prepared to depart the unnamed world.

As they boarded the Naboo skiff, Anakin became unusually serious.

"When we get back..."

Padmé looked at him.

"...we have to pretend."

She nodded slowly.

"As though we've never seen any of this."

"I think otherwise we'll accidentally change things before we're supposed to."

Padmé smiled faintly.

"That will be much easier said than done."

"I know."

She reached over and took his hand.

"But we'll try."

He squeezed it gently.

"We live normally."

"One day at a time."

"As though none of this happened."

Padmé nodded.

"As though none of this happened."

The skiff lifted away from the silent planet and disappeared into hyperspace.

When they returned to Coruscant, both of them resumed their ordinary responsibilities.

Anakin returned to the war.

Padmé returned to the Senate.

Neither spoke publicly of the unnamed world.

Neither mentioned the scroll.

Neither revealed the rewritten future.

And every day afterward, they did their best to live exactly as they would have if they had never found it at all.

# Part 5

The months passed more peacefully than either of them had dared to hope.

Outwardly, Anakin Skywalker and Senator Padmé Amidala lived exactly as they had promised one another aboard the Naboo skiff.

They spoke of the scroll only in private.

They never mentioned the unnamed planet.

They never hinted to anyone that they believed the future had once been written.

Instead, they watched.

And they counted.

One evening, Padmé's private comlink chirped with Anakin's encrypted signal.

She answered immediately.

"Anakin?"

His voice came through, tired but unmistakably pleased.

"It happened."

Padmé smiled.

"The eighth rescue?"

"Yes."

"What happened?"

"I'll tell you everything when I get back."

She laughed softly.

"So the chronometer still works."

"It does."

He paused.

"One more milestone passed."

Some weeks later, her private comlink sounded again.

This time Anakin's voice carried more amusement than before.

"You'll like this."

"What is it?"

"Cato Nemoidia."

Padmé sat upright.

"You've been there?"

"I have."

"And?"

Anakin laughed.

"I finally understand the line."

"What happened?"

He sighed.

"Obi-Wan insists I didn't actually save him."

"So it really doesn't count?"

"Apparently not."

She smiled.

"The script was right."

"It was."

His tone became more serious.

"Which means..."

"...the next rescue is aboard the Invisible Hand."

Neither of them needed to say anything further.

They both knew what was approaching.

Not long afterward, Coruscant itself came under attack.

Separatist warships emerged from hyperspace in overwhelming numbers.

The Battle of Coruscant had begun.

Reports flooded the Jedi Temple.

The Chancellor had been captured.

Held aboard General Grievous' flagship, the Invisible Hand.

The Council responded immediately.

Anakin and Obi-Wan launched without delay.

As Anakin's fighter accelerated toward the immense warship, he felt an odd calm settle over him.

He already knew nearly every corridor he would walk.

Nearly every conversation he would have.

Not because he had lived them before.

Because he had read them.

Obi-Wan glanced toward him.

"You seem unusually calm."

Anakin smiled faintly.

"I've had a feeling we'd make it."

"I certainly hope you're right."

They landed.

Boarded.

Fought through droids.

Advanced deeper into the flagship.

Every step matched the rewritten script.

When they entered the observation chamber, Count Dooku stood waiting.

"So," Dooku said, igniting his lightsaber, "you have come."

The duel unfolded almost exactly as described.

Only one crucial difference remained.

Obi-Wan never lost consciousness.

The revised script had removed that moment.

He fought beside Anakin throughout the engagement.

Eventually Dooku lay defeated.

His lightsaber gone.

Anakin looked toward the restrained Chancellor.

Then back toward Dooku.

Without hesitation, he ended the Count's life.

Silence followed.

Palpatine looked at him.

"Anakin..."

The Chancellor's voice was controlled.

"Release me."

Anakin met his eyes.

"No."

Palpatine frowned.

"What are you doing?"

Anakin drew a slow breath.

"I know who you are."

Something changed in Palpatine's expression.

Not fear.

Recognition that something had gone terribly wrong.

"I don't know what deception you've fallen for—"

"You are Darth Sidious."

The room became utterly still.

Palpatine's eyes narrowed.

"Who told you that?"

Anakin said nothing.

Palpatine continued quietly.

"This is a mistake."

"No."

Anakin raised his lightsaber.

"This is the end."

For the first time, genuine alarm crossed Palpatine's face.

"You fool—"

The blade swept downward.

When it was over, neither Sith Lord remained alive.

Obi-Wan stared at him.

"Anakin..."

Anakin lowered his weapon.

"I'll explain later."

Alarms suddenly echoed throughout the ship.

Grievous had begun withdrawing surviving forces.

Obi-Wan looked around.

"We need to leave."

"I know."

The two Jedi ran through corridors exactly as they had planned months before.

This time escape pods remained available.

Grievous had never ordered them jettisoned.

Anakin and Obi-Wan entered one together.

The pod blasted clear of the dying flagship.

Moments later, the Invisible Hand tore itself apart.

Fire consumed its fractured hull.

The explosion spread from bow to stern.

General Grievous never escaped.

The flagship vanished into expanding clouds of debris above Coruscant.

Watching from the escape pod, Obi-Wan quietly said,

"I suppose no one aboard survived."

Anakin looked back at the fading explosion.

"No."

The days that followed unfolded with remarkable fidelity to the rewritten scroll.

Without Count Dooku.

Without General Grievous.

Without Supreme Chancellor Palpatine.

The Separatist Alliance rapidly collapsed.

Its leadership surrendered.

The Clone Wars officially ended.

The surviving members of the Separatist Council were arrested and brought before Republic courts.

Nute Gunray entered a prison cell instead of a battlefield grave.

The Galactic Senate elected Bail Organa as Chancellor.

The Jedi Council convened.

Yoda slowly looked around the assembled Masters.

"The war ended, it has."

Silence filled the chamber.

"Much reflected, we have."

Another pause.

"Changed, the Order must."

Master Windu inclined his head.

"What changes do you propose?"

Yoda smiled gently.

"Forbidden, marriage no longer shall be."

Several Masters exchanged surprised glances.

"The Jedi," Yoda continued, "must not fear attachment itself."

The decision passed.

The Order changed.

No longer did Anakin and Padmé need secrecy.

Their marriage became publicly acknowledged.

Anakin received the rank of Jedi Master.

He retained his seat upon the Council.

Days later, Padmé gave birth safely to twins.

A son.

And a daughter.

Elsewhere, Queen Breha Organa of Alderaan became pregnant with a baby girl of her own after Chancellor Bail Organa took a brief vacation to return home to Alderaan.

Exactly as Anakin and Padmé had written.

Life gradually settled into peace.

One afternoon, inside the Jedi Temple itself, Anakin found Padmé waiting in one of its quiet gardens.

She smiled.

"So, Master Skywalker."

He laughed.

"I'll never get tired of hearing that."

She stepped closer.

"I know."

He wrapped his arms around her.

"And now?"

"Now," she replied, "we simply live."

They kissed.

At a respectful distance, Obi-Wan stood with Yoda.

Obi-Wan smiled.

"I suppose they no longer have to hide."

Yoda's ears twitched slightly.

"Hmm."

After a moment, Yoda smiled as well.

"Good. That is."

The moment lingered peacefully.

Then it was over.

The rewritten scroll contained no further words.

The future, at last, was unwritten once more.

# Part 6

Some weeks later, Anakin approached Padmé with an idea.

"I want to go back."

She looked up from the children.

"The planet?"

He nodded.

"I need answers."

Padmé looked toward the twins sleeping peacefully.

"Then we all go."

Before long, the Skywalker family departed together.

Anakin.

Padmé.

Their infant twins.

R2-D2.

C-3PO.

Once again Anakin relied on no charts.

The same mysterious pull through the Force guided him unerringly back to the unnamed world.

After landing, they wandered across the silent landscape for hours.

Nothing had changed.

Until suddenly someone spoke.

"I wondered when you'd return."

The voice came from nowhere.

Then, before them, a translucent figure slowly appeared.

He was not luminous like a Jedi Force spirit.

Nor did he resemble any being either Anakin or Padmé had ever encountered.

He possessed a strange, ghost-like presence unlike anything known in the Republic.

The figure smiled politely.

"My name is George Lucas."

Silence followed.

Anakin instinctively stepped in front of Padmé and the children.

"Who are you?"

"I told you."

"No."

Anakin's voice hardened.

"Who are you really?"

George smiled faintly.

"I wrote the scroll."

The words hung in the air.

Anakin stared at him.

"...You."

"Yes."

"You wrote all of it?"

"I did."

Anakin's expression hardened immediately.

"Why?"

George regarded him quietly.

"Long before I wrote the scroll you found, I wrote others."

He raised one hand.

"They remained outside your world."

"I never sent them here."

"What were they called?" Padmé asked.

George answered calmly.

"'A New Hope.'"

"'The Empire Strikes Back.'"

"'Return of the Jedi.'"

Anakin frowned.

"What are they about?"

George smiled.

"They describe events occurring after the story recorded in the scroll you altered."

"Tell us."

"I won't."

"Why not?"

"Because they are no longer relevant."

Anakin took a step forward.

"Then why did you write *Revenge of the Sith* like that?"

George folded his hands.

"Because I wrote *A New Hope* first."

Anakin looked confused.

"But you said—"

"I wrote the later events before I wrote the earlier ones."

George nodded.

"Once *A New Hope* existed, the earlier story needed to lead into it."

"So you doomed us."

"I did."

"You made me become..."

He could not finish the sentence.

George lowered his eyes.

"I know."

"You made Padmé die."

"I know."

"You made me murder children."

George remained silent for a long moment.

Finally he said quietly,

"I am sorry."

Anakin's anger did not disappear.

"Sorry?"

"I believed it was necessary."

"For what?"

"For the story."

Anakin shook his head.

"There had to be another way."

"Perhaps."

"Then why didn't you choose it?"

George answered simply.

"Because that was not the story I chose to write."

Padmé spoke next.

"If that is the case..."

"...why send us the scroll?"

George smiled.

"Because eventually I regretted what I had written."

He looked toward Anakin.

"I wanted to know what you would do if I gave you the chance."

"So you sent the vision."

"Yes."

"And the feeling leading me here."

"Yes."

"I merely placed the scroll where you would find it."

Anakin thought for a moment.

"And *Attack of the Clones*?"

George nodded.

"You would never have believed the second script."

"So I included the first."

"The details."

"The private conversations."

"Everything only the two of you could know."

Padmé slowly nodded.

"So we'd recognize it as supernatural."

"Exactly."

Anakin looked at George for a long time.

Then his expression softened.

"There is one thing."

George waited.

"You gave me Padmé."

He reached for her hand.

"For that..."

He smiled warmly.

"...I'll always be grateful."

Padmé smiled back.

George inclined his head.

"I'm glad."

Anakin's smile faded again.

"But."

George waited.

"I didn't appreciate growing up as a slave on Tatooine."

George answered almost automatically.

"It was necessary for the plot."

Anakin frowned.

"No."

"It wasn't."

"The story could have happened another way."

George smiled faintly.

"I am not going to debate story construction with a mere mortal."

Anakin opened his mouth.

Then closed it again.

Padmé decided to ask a different question.

"What about those other scrolls?"

George looked toward her.

"The ones you called *A New Hope*, *The Empire Strikes Back*, and *Return of the Jedi*."

"Yes?"

"Will those events still happen?"

George shook his head.

"No."

"Why not?"

"You changed the earlier scroll."

He gestured gently.

"Those later writings depended upon the original version of *Revenge of the Sith*."

"You altered it."

"They no longer apply."

Padmé looked at Anakin.

"So..."

George smiled.

"You succeeded."

"The future truly changed."

"You need not fear those unwritten events."

He looked at the young family.

"Go home."

"Live."

"Enjoy the life you created."

Before either of them could ask another question, George's ghostly form slowly faded.

Within moments nothing remained.

Only empty air.

For a long time, no one spoke.

Finally Anakin quietly said,

"So."

Padmé nodded.

"So."

He looked upward.

"The Force isn't the whole story."

"No."

"There was... something above even that."

Padmé squeezed his hand.

"Maybe."

They stood silently with their children between them.

Eventually Anakin smiled.

"I don't think I want to spend my life thinking about it."

Padmé smiled too.

"Neither do I."

"We have children to raise."

"A Republic to serve."

"A Jedi Order to help rebuild."

She nodded.

"And a family."

He laughed softly.

"I think that's enough."

Together they returned to their ship.

They left the unnamed world behind once again.

Back on Coruscant, life settled into an ordinary rhythm.

Anakin Skywalker served openly as a respected Jedi Master and member of the Jedi Council.

Padmé Amidala continued her work in the Senate under Chancellor Bail Organa's administration.

Their marriage was no longer a secret.

Their children grew surrounded by both parents.

R2-D2 and C-3PO remained faithful members of the household.

Although Anakin and Padmé never forgot the impossible encounter on the unnamed planet, they chose not to let it define their lives.

Whatever powers might exist beyond the Force, whatever mysteries lay beyond mortal understanding, they concluded that the best response was not endless speculation.

It was to cherish the peace they had fought to preserve, to raise their family with love, and to live each new day as though it belonged to them at last.
