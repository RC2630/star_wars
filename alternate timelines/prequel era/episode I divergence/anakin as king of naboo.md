# Part 1 - The Boy Who Stayed

The funeral pyres on Naboo burned long into the night.

The Gungan soldiers had already withdrawn to the swamps, the Federation army had been deactivated, and the palace balconies once draped in fear now glittered again beneath soft Naboo lanterns. Musicians played in the streets of Theed while civilians embraced one another beneath fountains and marble arches.

Yet inside the Jedi Temple ship preparing to depart Naboo, there was no celebration.

Obi-Wan Kenobi stood before the Jedi Council with exhaustion carved into every line of his face.

Qui-Gon Jinn was dead.

The memory refused to leave him: Qui-Gon collapsed against the cold floor of the reactor chamber, the crimson blade withdrawn from his body, his final breaths ragged but focused.

Train the boy.

Obi-Wan had given his word.

Now the Council sat above him in silence.

“The boy is dangerous,” Master Mace Windu said at last. “His future remains clouded.”

“He is too old,” added Master Ki-Adi-Mundi. “Too much fear, too much attachment.”

Obi-Wan struggled to remain calm. “Qui-Gon believed otherwise.”

“Qui-Gon was a wise Jedi,” Master Yoda replied, his ears drooping slightly. “But even wise Jedi may disagree with the Council.”

Obi-Wan stepped forward.

“He was the one who discovered the Sith. He followed the will of the Force when no one else would. He believed this boy mattered.”

“The matter is settled,” Windu said.

Obi-Wan’s jaw tightened.

“You have granted me the rank of Jedi Knight,” he said carefully. “Then permit me to honor my Master’s final request.”

“No.”

The answer came instantly.

The chamber seemed colder after that.

Obi-Wan looked from face to face, searching for hesitation, uncertainty, compassion—anything.

He found none.

“He is alone,” Obi-Wan said quietly. “He has nowhere else to go.”

“The Republic is vast,” Yoda answered. “His path need not be the Jedi path.”

Obi-Wan’s voice hardened.

“I gave Qui-Gon my word.”

“And the Jedi Order overrides your word,” Windu replied. “You are a Jedi Knight. You will obey the Council, not Qui-Gon.”

For the first time in his life, Obi-Wan felt anger toward the Order.

“If you refuse,” he said, “then I will train him without your permission.”

The chamber fell silent.

Several Council members exchanged looks.

When Yoda spoke again, disappointment weighed heavily in his ancient voice.

“Careful, Obi-Wan. Dangerous this path is.”

“Dangerous is abandoning him.”

Windu folded his hands into his robes.

“You are not prepared to train a boy of such uncertainty, especially while grieving your Master. We will not permit it.”

“And if I choose otherwise?”

“Then you will force the Council to act.”

Obi-Wan stared at them for several long seconds.

Then he bowed stiffly and departed.

The Council’s concerns deepened after that meeting.

Obi-Wan continued petitioning them over the following days. Again and again he argued that Anakin Skywalker deserved guidance, stability, and training. Again and again the Council refused.

Eventually they began quietly assigning Jedi to observe him.

Nothing overt. Nothing hostile.

But Obi-Wan noticed.

A familiar face in the Temple corridors.

Questions regarding his movements.

Subtle inquiries into whether he had met with the boy.

The Council feared he would defy them.

For a brief time, Obi-Wan considered doing exactly that.

He thought of leaving Coruscant entirely. Taking a transport and disappearing into the Outer Rim with Anakin.

But every path he imagined ended badly.

The Jedi would pursue them.

Anakin would grow up hunted.

And Obi-Wan himself—newly knighted, grieving, angry, uncertain—could not deny that the Council’s warnings troubled him. He had seen Anakin’s fear. His possessiveness. His desperate attachment to his mother.

The burden felt immense.

Weeks passed.

Eventually Obi-Wan stopped arguing.

The Council interpreted his silence as acceptance.

It was not acceptance.

It was surrender.

—

Anakin Skywalker remained on Naboo.

At first, he hated the idea.

Theed was beautiful beyond imagination, but every marble corridor and shining garden reminded him that he did not belong there.

He asked repeatedly to return to Tatooine.

“My mom’s still there,” he told Obi-Wan one evening beside the palace landing platforms. “She’s alone.”

The wind carried the scent of rain through the city.

Obi-Wan looked tired.

“Your mother loves you,” he said gently. “Which is why she wanted you free.”

“She didn’t want me gone forever.”

“No.” Obi-Wan hesitated. “But if you return to Tatooine, your life may become very small.”

Anakin frowned.

“What’s wrong with small?”

“There is nothing wrong with it,” Obi-Wan answered. “But you have talents that should not be buried in a junk shop beneath a desert sun.”

Anakin kicked lightly at the stone beneath his feet.

“I could free her someday.”

“You could,” Obi-Wan said. “And perhaps one day you will. But not by returning to chains yourself.”

Anakin was silent.

“You really won’t train me?” he finally asked.

Pain crossed Obi-Wan’s expression.

“I cannot.”

The answer hurt more than Anakin expected.

He had dreamed constantly of becoming a Jedi since Qui-Gon first spoke of it.

Now the dream was dissolving.

Queen Padmé Amidala found him later that night sitting alone beside one of the palace fountains.

The Queen of Naboo still wore simple ceremonial clothing despite the celebrations around her. She seemed older now than when they had first met, as though her assault on the Royal Palace had forced years onto her shoulders.

“You should be celebrating,” she told him.

“I’m thinking.”

“That sounds dangerous.”

Anakin smiled faintly.

“I want to go back to Tatooine.”

Padmé sat beside him.

“You don’t have to decide tonight.”

“I don’t belong here.”

“That’s not true.”

He glanced at her skeptically.

“I’m just some slave kid.”

Padmé looked almost offended.

“You helped save Naboo.”

“I blew up one ship.”

“You flew into a battle no trained pilot would have survived.”

“That was luck.”

“You rebuilt half the palace maintenance droids in three days because our technicians couldn’t keep up after the occupation.”

Anakin shrugged.

“That part wasn’t luck.”

Padmé smiled.

Over the next hour she asked him questions—not about the Force or the Jedi, but about engines, navigation systems, hyperdrives, droid logic, and podracing.

By the end of the conversation, she had made her decision.

“You should stay,” she said.

Anakin blinked.

“Here?”

“Yes.”

“What would I even do?”

“Help us rebuild. Work with the pilots. Work with the engineers. Learn.”

“I’m nine years old.”

“You say that as though Naboo has no young people.”

He looked uncertain.

Padmé’s voice softened.

“You have a future here, Anakin.”

No one had ever said those words to him before.

Not like that.

And so he stayed.

—

The years that followed changed both Naboo and Anakin Skywalker.

Padmé remained deeply involved in his upbringing despite her constant obligations as Queen of Naboo.

The relationship never resembled formal adoption. Naboo law, royal expectations, and Padmé’s political responsibilities made such a thing impractical.

Yet in practice, much of the palace came to see him as hers.

She supervised his education personally whenever time allowed.

He learned galactic history from Naboo scholars, diplomacy from observing ministers and ambassadors, economics from trade officials rebuilding after the Federation blockade, and statecraft from watching Padmé navigate endless political tensions with remarkable patience.

Anakin absorbed knowledge at astonishing speed.

He was restless in ordinary classrooms but brilliant when allowed to engage directly.

Within a year, palace engineers discovered the former slave child from Tatooine could diagnose reactor inconsistencies faster than senior technicians.

Within two years, he had redesigned maintenance protocols for entire sectors of Theed’s transportation systems.

By twelve, he was personally test-flying prototype N-1 modifications despite the horrified objections of Naboo security staff.

He crashed only twice.

The palace staff considered that miraculous.

Anakin’s charm proved equally disruptive.

He spoke directly to everyone.

Guards.

Pilots.

Servants.

Mechanics.

Politicians.

Unlike many aristocrats on Naboo, he had grown up among laborers and ordinary people. He listened to them naturally.

Citizens noticed.

Stories spread quickly.

The boy hero who had destroyed the droid control ship.

The slave child welcomed into the royal palace.

The gifted mechanic rebuilding systems older engineers struggled to maintain.

The fearless pilot who treated security escorts like racing rivals.

The awkward but sincere youth who wandered marketplaces talking to merchants for hours.

Naboo adored him.

Not everyone approved.

Some old noble families quietly disliked his growing popularity.

Others distrusted his origins.

A few worried that Padmé had become too emotionally attached to him.

But every attempt to diminish him failed.

Anakin possessed an unusual quality difficult to resist: people believed he genuinely cared.

Because he did.

He remembered hunger.

He remembered fear.

He remembered what it felt like to belong to someone else.

When labor disputes emerged during reconstruction projects, he sided publicly with workers demanding fair compensation.

When refugee communities displaced by the invasion struggled financially, he spent weeks helping coordinate supply deliveries himself.

Once, to the absolute horror of palace officials, he disappeared for nearly three days into Naboo’s rural lake country because he had heard farmers were suffering from damaged irrigation systems.

He returned exhausted, soaked, and triumphant after repairing the pumps personally.

Padmé scolded him for frightening everyone.

Then she hugged him.

By the time he was thirteen, the distinction between national hero and political figure had begun to blur.

Padmé noticed it first.

Citizens no longer treated Anakin merely as the child who had helped save Naboo years earlier.

They sought his opinions.

Local leaders invited him to civic functions.

Trade representatives asked for meetings.

University students requested speeches.

Anakin found the attention bewildering.

“I’m not a politician,” he insisted after one particularly large public gathering.

Padmé laughed softly.

“You spend your mornings debating infrastructure policy with ministers.”

“That’s different.”

“You argued for increased educational funding for six hours yesterday.”

“They were wrong.”

“You negotiated a compromise.”

“They were still wrong.”

Padmé smiled.

He reminded her increasingly of Qui-Gon Jinn in certain moments: stubborn, instinctive, impossible to intimidate.

Yet there was warmth in him too. A brightness.

The darkness Obi-Wan and the Jedi Council had feared seemed distant from the young man growing within Naboo’s shining capital.

Obi-Wan himself visited occasionally during those years.

The meetings were always complicated.

Anakin greeted him eagerly every time.

Part of him never stopped hoping the Jedi Knight might change his mind.

But Obi-Wan remained careful.

He offered advice, encouragement, and occasional stories of Qui-Gon, yet he never trained him.

Never spoke of the Force beyond vague spiritual guidance.

Never stayed too long.

The Council still watched.

Obi-Wan could feel it.

During one visit, he found Anakin in a palace hangar rebuilding the internals of a damaged astromech droid while simultaneously lecturing three Naboo officials about taxation policy.

“How do you know so much about taxes?” Obi-Wan asked in disbelief.

Anakin looked up from inside the droid.

“Because everybody complains about them.”

“That is not generally considered expertise.”

“It should be.”

Obi-Wan laughed despite himself.

Then the laughter faded.

Anakin noticed.

“What?”

Obi-Wan studied him quietly.

“You seem happy here.”

Anakin paused.

“I am.”

The answer brought Obi-Wan both relief and sadness.

Qui-Gon would have wanted more for him.

Yet perhaps this life—peaceful, meaningful, loved—was enough.

Perhaps it was better.

—

When Padmé Amidala’s second elected term as Queen of Naboo neared its end, public anxiety spread across the planet.

Naboo’s constitution prevented any monarch from serving more than two terms.

Padmé remained enormously popular.

Many wished she could continue indefinitely.

Instead, preparations began for the next election.

Potential candidates emerged from noble houses, academic circles, and regional governments.

Political analysts debated policy constantly.

Then Anakin Skywalker announced his candidacy.

The galaxy laughed.

Naboo did not.

He made the announcement in the palace gardens beneath a crowd far larger than expected.

Padmé stood several meters behind him among the officials, stunned almost speechless.

Anakin himself looked oddly calm.

“I know what people are going to say,” he began. “They’re going to say I’m too young.”

Murmurs and scattered laughter moved through the audience. Padmé gently reminded him that he is only one year younger than when she was first elected as queen. He ignored her.

“They’re probably right,” he continued. “But four years ago this planet gave me a chance when I had nothing. Naboo gave me a home. Queen Amidala taught me what leadership means. The people here taught me what service means.”

His voice strengthened.

“I don’t think leadership belongs only to the old, or the rich, or the powerful. I think it belongs to people willing to help.”

The applause lasted several minutes.

His opponents underestimated him immediately.

That proved disastrous.

Anakin campaigned with relentless energy.

He traveled constantly across Naboo by starfighter, skiff, or public transport.

He visited villages ignored by traditional candidates.

He spoke passionately about infrastructure modernization, expanded technical education, labor protections, and stronger cooperation between Naboo and the Gungans.

Most importantly, he spoke naturally.

He lacked the polished caution of career politicians.

Sometimes he misspoke.

Sometimes he became too emotional.

Sometimes he argued openly with critics.

But citizens trusted him because every reaction appeared genuine.

Padmé watched the campaign unfold with growing amazement.

“You’re actually winning,” she told him one evening after new polling data arrived.

Anakin looked equally shocked.

“I know.”

“You didn’t expect this?”

“I thought maybe people would listen.” He rubbed his forehead. “I didn’t think they’d vote for me.”

Padmé studied him fondly.

“You remind people of what Naboo survived.”

“That was years ago.”

“For you,” she said softly. “Not for them.”

His final debate secured the election.

One opponent questioned his background directly.

“You are asking Naboo to entrust its future to a former slave child with no aristocratic lineage and no formal governing experience.”

The statement was intended to destroy him.

Instead, Anakin answered without hesitation.

“You’re right.”

The audience quieted.

“I was a slave.”

His voice carried across the chamber.

“I know what power looks like when it serves only itself. I know what it feels like when ordinary people are ignored because they’re poor or weak or inconvenient. If that experience disqualifies me from leadership in your eyes, then don’t vote for me.”

He stepped forward.

“But if you believe Naboo should belong to all its people—not just the fortunate—then I promise I will spend every day I have serving this world the way it once served me.”

The election ended in a landslide.

At thirteen years old, Anakin Skywalker became King of Naboo.

The coronation ceremony transformed Theed into a sea of silver banners and luminous flowers.

Citizens filled every balcony and avenue.

Gungan delegations marched proudly beside Naboo officials.

Pilots from the Royal Security Forces flew precision formations overhead.

Anakin stood at the center of it all wearing formal royal robes tailored hastily enough that the sleeves still fit imperfectly.

He looked overwhelmed.

Padmé approached him shortly before the ceremony began.

For the first time in years, she was no longer queen.

Soon she would depart for Coruscant to serve as Naboo’s senator in the Galactic Senate.

“You’re nervous,” she observed.

“I’m about to govern a planet.”

“That is generally considered stressful.”

“What if I fail?”

Padmé smiled gently.

“You will.”

Anakin stared at her.

“What?”

“You’ll fail sometimes,” she clarified. “Every leader does. The important thing is what you do afterward.”

He absorbed that quietly.

Then he looked toward the enormous crowds waiting beyond the palace doors.

“I still feel like that kid from Tatooine.”

Padmé rested a hand on his shoulder.

“Good,” she said. “Never lose him.”

The doors opened.

Sunlight flooded the chamber.

The people of Naboo began to cheer.

And Anakin Skywalker walked forward to become their king.

# Part 2 - The Liberation of Tatooine

Two years into Anakin Skywalker’s reign, the royal palace at Theed had grown accustomed to movement.

Messengers hurried through polished corridors carrying reports from across Naboo. Diplomats from neighboring systems waited beneath painted domes for audiences with the young king. Engineers, pilots, ministers, scholars, and military officers flowed endlessly through the palace like currents through water.

And somewhere within that motion, Anakin thrived.

At fifteen, he governed with an intensity that exhausted nearly everyone around him.

He still flew regularly with the Royal Security Forces despite constant objections from advisors. He still disappeared into hangars to help technicians repair starfighters. He still argued directly with ministers twice his age whenever he believed they were wrong.

Yet Naboo prospered.

Trade expanded rapidly under his infrastructure reforms. Relations with the Gungans deepened further than at any point in recorded history. Educational initiatives produced new generations of engineers and pilots inspired by the king himself.

Even many former critics had been won over.

The former slave boy from Tatooine had become something unprecedented: a monarch who genuinely believed government existed to improve ordinary lives.

Then Obi-Wan Kenobi visited Naboo again.

The Jedi Knight arrived quietly aboard a Republic transport and was escorted to the palace overlooking Theed’s vast waterfalls and shining towers. Years had sharpened him. The youthful uncertainty he once carried had hardened into calm discipline, though traces of old sadness remained in his eyes.

Anakin greeted him warmly.

“Master Obi-Wan.”

Obi-Wan raised an eyebrow.

“I’m fairly certain I’m not allowed to be called that.”

“You’re still going to correct me after all this time?”

“It’s practically my defining trait.”

Anakin laughed and embraced him briefly.

For several days Obi-Wan remained as an honored guest. He attended diplomatic functions, toured Naboo reconstruction projects, and watched with growing amazement as Anakin navigated affairs of state with startling confidence.

One evening they stood together on a palace balcony overlooking Theed.

Lights shimmered across the city below like constellations reflected upon water.

“You’ve built something remarkable here,” Obi-Wan admitted.

“We built it,” Anakin corrected. “Padmé started most of this.”

“She would say you carried it further.”

Anakin leaned against the balcony railing.

“How is Coruscant?”

“Complicated.”

“That sounds miserable.”

“It often is.”

They shared a quiet smile.

Then Obi-Wan noticed the expression changing on Anakin’s face.

A heaviness.

A distance.

“What is it?” Obi-Wan asked.

Anakin did not answer immediately.

Finally he spoke softly.

“I still think about her.”

Obi-Wan understood instantly.

Shmi Skywalker.

Tatooine.

The promise.

Years earlier, Anakin had departed the desert world believing he would someday return to free his mother from slavery.

Now he was king.

And he had begun to realize what that meant.

“I used to think becoming a Jedi would give me the power to help people,” Anakin said quietly. “But maybe power was always something else.”

Obi-Wan studied him carefully.

“What are you planning?”

Anakin turned toward him.

“I’m going back to Tatooine.”

—

The proposal shocked Naboo.

An emergency royal assembly convened within days after Anakin publicly announced his intentions. Senators of multiple worlds arrived from Coruscant to understand and mediate the situation.

Ministers erupted in protest.

Military officials questioned logistics.

Economic advisors warned of catastrophic expense.

Legal scholars argued that Tatooine lay outside Republic jurisdiction and therefore beyond Naboo’s responsibility.

Even loyal supporters appeared uneasy.

Inside the great assembly chamber beneath Theed’s towering arches, debate raged for hours.

Finally Anakin rose from the royal platform.

Silence spread slowly across the chamber.

He looked very young in that moment.

And utterly certain.

“When I was a child,” he began, “I believed slavery was simply the way the galaxy worked. Some people were free. Some weren’t.”

He walked slowly down the center aisle.

“No one came to help us. No Republic ships. No senators. No Jedi.” His voice hardened slightly. “People knew slavery existed on Tatooine. They simply decided it wasn’t their problem.”

Many senators shifted uncomfortably.

Anakin continued.

“Naboo fought for its own freedom when the Trade Federation invaded this world. We were brave then because injustice was happening to us.”

He looked around the chamber.

“But what does our freedom mean if we only value liberty for ourselves?”

The room remained completely silent now.

“We rebuilt our cities. We restored our prosperity. We speak proudly of peace and civilization.” His eyes sharpened. “Yet only a few hyperspace routes away, children are still bought and sold.”

The words landed heavily.

“If we ignore suffering simply because it belongs to someone else, then our progress means nothing.”

He stopped at the center of the chamber.

“I will not pretend Tatooine is not our concern simply because it is inconvenient.”

Somewhere in the assembly, applause began.

Then more joined it.

Then more.

Anakin raised a hand for silence.

“I am not asking Naboo to conquer for wealth. Or territory. Or pride.” His voice rang through the chamber. “I am asking Naboo to stand for something larger than itself.”

The debate changed after that.

Citizens across Naboo argued passionately for weeks. Holonet discussions exploded with controversy. Some still opposed intervention entirely.

But gradually, public sentiment shifted.

Anakin’s sincerity proved impossible to ignore.

Naboo had once stood helpless before oppression and survived only because people had chosen courage over fear. Many citizens began to believe their world now possessed a responsibility to extend that same courage elsewhere.

Others recognized the growing political reality: if Naboo succeeded, the small peaceful world would become a symbol across the galaxy.

Not merely prosperous.

Noble.

Visionary.

A planet willing to sacrifice for justice beyond its own borders.

Public support surged.

The campaign was approved.

—

The military preparations transformed Naboo.

Royal Security Forces mobilized on an unprecedented scale. Starfighters filled palace hangars. Troop transports gathered above Naboo in orbiting formations. Volunteer support organizations emerged across major cities.

Many officials begged Anakin not to personally participate in combat operations.

He ignored them.

“You are the king,” one minister protested during a strategic briefing.

“Yes,” Anakin replied. “Which means if people are risking their lives, I should be willing to risk mine too.”

Security advisors proposed elaborate precautions.

One particularly nervous official suggested disguising Anakin as a page while elevating a royal page into a ceremonial decoy monarch.

Anakin rejected the idea immediately.

“I’m not hiding behind another person.”

“But Your Majesty—”

“My senior page can oversee civilian administration while I’m gone,” Anakin interrupted. “Grant him provisional authority where necessary. But he remains a page, not a fake king.”

The decision horrified the palace.

It also made him even more popular.

Meanwhile on Coruscant, Senator Padmé Amidala stunned the Galactic Senate by publicly supporting the campaign.

Many expected her to distance herself from the controversial operation.

Instead she delivered one of the defining speeches of her political career.

“Naboo is not acting from greed,” she declared before the Senate. “Nor conquest. Nor expansion. It acts because suffering ignored becomes injustice accepted.”

Her voice carried firmly across the chamber.

“My world has chosen to bear costs and risks not for itself alone, but for people who possess no voice within this Republic.”

The Senate listened carefully.

“In an age where systems increasingly retreat inward and protect only their own interests, Naboo has chosen responsibility instead.”

The speech transformed galactic perception overnight.

What many had initially dismissed as reckless idealism now appeared profoundly moral.

The Senate formally voted to support Naboo politically and diplomatically. Direct military aid failed to pass, but the symbolic backing carried enormous weight.

Suddenly the liberation of Tatooine no longer appeared to be the isolated crusade of a young king.

It had become a galactic cause.

—

The campaign began with expectations of brutal resistance.

Instead, Tatooine erupted.

Not against Naboo.

Against the Hutts.

For generations the planet’s ordinary citizens had endured cartel violence, extortion, corruption, slavery, and fear. Entire settlements existed effectively under criminal domination.

When Naboo forces landed, many locals saw opportunity instead of invasion.

Workers sabotaged Hutt supply lines.

Mechanics disabled cartel transports.

Slaves openly revolted against masters across Mos Espa, Mos Eisley, and dozens of smaller settlements.

Formerly hidden resistance groups emerged almost overnight.

Then the Tusken Raiders entered the war.

At first, they attacked everyone.

Several Naboo patrols vanished entirely in the deep deserts. Hutt convoys disappeared beneath violent ambushes. Entire dune regions became death traps.

Anakin insisted on negotiating personally.

Most advisors considered the idea suicidal.

Nevertheless he traveled deep into Tusken territory under guarded escort and met with tribal leaders beneath the burning twin suns.

The negotiations lasted nearly twelve hours.

Anakin spoke plainly.

“The Hutts poison this world,” he told them. “They take from everyone. Settlers. Workers. Tribes.”

The Tuskens distrusted outsiders instinctively.

But Anakin understood Tatooine.

Understood survival.

Understood humiliation.

He promised territorial protections once the Hutts fell. Recognition of Tusken sovereignty over ancestral lands. Material support. Defensive weapons to secure their villages from future incursions.

Most importantly, he treated them not as savages, but as people.

The alliance changed the war entirely.

Tusken fighters began striking Hutt fortifications with terrifying effectiveness. Caravan routes collapsed. Hutt weapon transports vanished into desert ambushes.

Caught between Naboo forces, local uprisings, slave revolts, and Tusken attacks, Hutt control began unraveling faster than anyone expected.

The Hutts rallied what strength they could.

Slave owners armed private militias.

Mercenaries and bounty hunters flooded onto Tatooine seeking profit.

The entire planet descended into warfare.

And at the center of it all flew Anakin Skywalker.

He led squadrons personally through blistering desert skies in his N-1 starfighter, often ignoring direct retreat orders from horrified commanders.

Stories spread rapidly among troops.

The young king diving directly into anti-aircraft fire to rescue pinned infantry.

The king landing inside active combat zones to evacuate civilians.

The king personally coordinating slave uprisings through underground networks.

His presence became both military and symbolic.

For many on Tatooine, he represented proof that freedom was actually possible.

The final battles around Mos Espa ended the conflict.

Combined resistance forces overwhelmed the remaining Hutt defenses after nearly three weeks of continuous fighting. Several prominent Hutts attempted escape offworld and were intercepted by Naboo starfighters.

Others surrendered.

The surrender treaty formally dissolved Hutt authority over Tatooine.

Anakin immediately established a provisional occupation government dedicated to democratic transition.

Then he issued the decree that echoed across the galaxy.

All slaves on Tatooine were free.

Immediately.

Without exception.

Celebrations erupted across entire settlements.

Families separated for decades reunited openly in the streets.

Slave compounds emptied almost overnight.

Many former owners complied reluctantly.

Some did not.

Anakin answered swiftly.

Any owner refusing emancipation would be personally pursued by occupation forces.

“If they surrender their slaves peacefully,” he announced publicly, “they may continue their lives in peace. If they refuse, we will remove them ourselves.”

Most complied after that.

Watto did not.

At least not initially.

The Toydarian barricaded himself inside his junk shop and loudly insisted that no “offworld king” would tell him how to conduct business.

Then Anakin arrived personally.

A platoon of elite Naboo infantry accompanied him into Mos Espa.

Watto reportedly turned pale the moment he saw who stood at his doorway.

Anakin entered the shop slowly, gazing around at shelves crowded with old machinery and scavenged parts.

“So,” he said calmly, “we’re doing this the difficult way.”

Watto attempted bravado.

It failed completely.

Within the hour, Shmi Skywalker’s slave transmitter had been removed.

She walked out of the shop free.

For the first time in her life.

—

The transition on Tatooine continued for months.

Naboo administrators supervised elections for a new planetary government. Infrastructure projects began almost immediately. Trade routes stabilized without cartel interference.

The Tusken tribes received protected territories where outsiders were forbidden from trespassing. Supplies, defensive equipment, and formal agreements reduced conflict dramatically.

Without Hutt domination poisoning every aspect of life, Tatooine entered one of the most peaceful periods in its history.

Captured Hutt leaders were transported to Coruscant under heavy guard.

Their trials became galactic spectacles.

For perhaps the first time, powerful criminal rulers were being publicly held accountable for systematic enslavement and abuse on an interstellar scale.

Then Anakin returned home.

Shmi Skywalker arrived at Theed beside him beneath enormous cheering crowds.

The former slave woman who had once lived beneath desert suns now entered one of the most beautiful cities in the galaxy as the honored mother of its king.

The contrast overwhelmed her.

Servants bowed.

Officials greeted her respectfully.

Luxurious chambers awaited her inside the royal palace.

For days she seemed unable to believe any of it was real.

Anakin visited her constantly during those first weeks, almost afraid the moment might vanish if he looked away too long.

“You kept your promise,” she told him quietly one evening.

Anakin smiled.

“I know.”

—

Across the galaxy, his reputation transformed completely.

He was no longer merely the boy king of Naboo.

He had become something far larger.

To some, he was an idealist.

To others, a liberator.

To many ordinary citizens living under corrupt systems, he became a symbol of hope dangerous enough to frighten governments and criminals alike.

Several Hutts on other worlds threatened retaliation against Naboo.

Anakin answered publicly and without hesitation.

“If they attempt to invade Naboo,” he declared, “they will meet the same fate as Tatooine.”

After witnessing the collapse of Hutt rule on Tatooine, most chose silence instead.

Then the Republic Senate intervened decisively.

In a historic resolution, the Senate announced that any organized retaliation against Naboo would be treated as aggression against a member world of the Republic itself.

The implication was unmistakable.

If the Hutts attacked Naboo, they would face the Republic military.

The remaining Hutt leadership fell quiet almost immediately.

And so, at fifteen years old, Anakin Skywalker fulfilled the promise he had carried since childhood beneath Tatooine’s burning suns.

His mother was free.

The slaves were free.

And for the first time in generations, Tatooine belonged to its people.
