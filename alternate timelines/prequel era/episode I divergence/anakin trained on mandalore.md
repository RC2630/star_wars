# Part 1

The pyres burned long into the Naboo night.

Along the lake country outside Theed, the people of Naboo stood in silence beneath darkening skies as Jedi Master Qui-Gon Jinn was committed to the Force. The orange glow of the flames reflected across polished marble and still water alike. Queen Amidala stood with her hands folded before her, solemn and unmoving. Nearby, Captain Panaka and the surviving Naboo guards maintained a respectful distance.

The Jedi Council had come in person.

Mace Windu stood with his cloak hanging heavily around his shoulders. Beside him, Ki-Adi-Mundi watched the flames in thoughtful silence. Plo Koon bowed his head. Yoda leaned upon his cane, ancient eyes fixed not on the fire itself, but somewhere beyond it.

Obi-Wan Kenobi stood closest of all.

He wore the exhaustion of the last days openly now. His master was dead. The Sith had returned. And somewhere inside himself, beneath grief and anger and shock, he could still hear Qui-Gon’s final request.

Train the boy.

Anakin Skywalker stood several paces behind him. The boy’s face was tense and uncertain. He did not fully understand Jedi customs or funeral rites, but he understood enough to know that the man who had freed him from slavery was gone forever.

Obi-Wan did not look back at him once during the ceremony.

If he had, he might not have remained standing.

When the funeral ended and the crowds slowly dispersed, the Council summoned Obi-Wan privately to the royal palace.

The chamber they chose overlooked the waterfalls of Theed. Tall windows stood open to the evening breeze. The room itself was quiet enough that Obi-Wan could hear the distant rush of water below the cliffs.

Yoda regarded him carefully.

“Great loss, this is,” the old master said softly.

Obi-Wan bowed his head. “Yes, Master.”

Mace Windu stepped forward. “The Council has reached its decision. In light of your actions on Naboo, and the defeat of the Sith warrior, you are granted the rank of Jedi Knight.”

For the first time since Qui-Gon’s death, Obi-Wan felt almost nothing at all.

This was supposed to have been the moment he dreamed of.

Instead it felt hollow.

He bowed automatically. “Thank you, Masters.”

Yoda studied him for a long moment. “Ask something more, you will.”

Obi-Wan lifted his eyes.

“I wish to train Anakin Skywalker.”

The room became still.

Ki-Adi-Mundi folded his hands into his sleeves. “The boy is dangerous.”

“He is gifted,” Obi-Wan replied immediately.

“He is too old,” Mace Windu said. “Too much fear already lives within him.”

“Qui-Gon believed—”

“Qui-Gon was mistaken,” Windu interrupted.

The words struck harder than Obi-Wan expected.

For a moment, anger flashed across his face before he mastered it.

“With respect, Master Windu,” he said carefully, “Qui-Gon gave his life believing this boy mattered. He believed Anakin was the Chosen One.”

“The prophecy remains uncertain,” said Yoda. “Clouded, the future is.”

Obi-Wan took a step forward.

“I gave Qui-Gon my word.”

“And your word to the Order?” Windu asked evenly.

Obi-Wan hesitated.

The silence answered for him.

Mace Windu’s expression hardened slightly. “You are a Jedi Knight now. Your duty is to the Council and to the Order itself. You will not train this boy.”

Obi-Wan stared at him in disbelief.

“He has no one,” Obi-Wan said. “His mother is still enslaved on Tatooine. Qui-Gon brought him here because he believed the Force guided him.”

“The decision is final,” said Ki-Adi-Mundi.

Obi-Wan looked from one master to another.

No one moved.

No one yielded.

The grief he had spent hours suppressing suddenly sharpened into something dangerous.

“You would have me break my promise to my master only hours after his death?”

Yoda’s ears lowered slightly. “Difficult, this is. But obedience, a Jedi must learn.”

Obi-Wan straightened slowly.

“If I train him without the Council’s permission?”

Mace Windu answered immediately.

“You will not.”

“And if I do?”

“Then you will be acting against the authority of the Jedi Council.”

Obi-Wan’s jaw tightened.

“Qui-Gon was my master.”

“And the Council is your master now,” Windu said.

Something in Obi-Wan finally snapped into clarity then.

Not rage. Not rebellion for its own sake.

Certainty.

He breathed once, slowly.

Then he unclasped the Jedi braid from behind his shoulder.

The thin lock of hair fell into his hand.

“I will honor Qui-Gon’s dying wish,” he said quietly. “If I cannot do so as a Jedi… then I will no longer be a Jedi.”

For the first time, genuine surprise crossed several faces in the chamber.

Even Yoda looked startled.

Obi-Wan placed the severed braid upon a nearby table.

The gesture felt strangely irreversible.

Mace Windu watched him carefully. “Consider this carefully, Obi-Wan.”

“I already have.”

“You would abandon the Order for this?”

“I would not abandon Qui-Gon.”

Silence filled the room again.

Finally, Yoda closed his eyes briefly.

“Sad, this is.”

Obi-Wan bowed one final time.

Then Windu spoke again.

“The lightsaber.”

Obi-Wan blinked.

“You carry Qui-Gon Jinn’s weapon,” Windu said. “It belongs to the Order.”

Slowly, Obi-Wan reached beneath his robe and removed the green-bladed lightsaber he had carried since the battle with Darth Maul. He stared at it for several seconds.

Qui-Gon’s weapon.

Qui-Gon’s legacy.

Then, reluctantly, he placed it beside the braid.

For a fleeting instant, he looked very young.

He turned and walked out without another word.

—

Anakin was waiting in one of the palace corridors, sitting anxiously atop a wide windowsill overlooking the city.

The boy jumped to his feet the moment he saw Obi-Wan approaching.

“Well?” he asked. “What happened?”

Obi-Wan stopped before him.

“The Council promoted me to Jedi Knight.”

Anakin’s face lit up instantly.

“I knew they would!”

“But they refused to let me train you.”

The excitement vanished just as quickly.

“Oh.”

Obi-Wan knelt slightly so they were eye level.

“I argued with them.”

Anakin searched his face nervously. “Did they change their minds?”

“No.”

The boy looked downward.

For several seconds neither of them spoke.

Then Obi-Wan continued quietly, “So I left the Jedi Order.”

Anakin looked up so fast Obi-Wan almost smiled.

“You what?”

“I’m no longer a Jedi.”

Anakin stared at him in utter shock.

“But… then what happens to me?”

Obi-Wan rested a hand on his shoulder.

“I’m still going to train you.”

Relief flooded across Anakin’s face immediately.

Then confusion followed right behind it.

“But if you’re not a Jedi… then I won’t be a Jedi either.”

A shadow of disappointment crossed the boy’s features.

Obi-Wan understood it completely. The Temple had represented something wondrous to Anakin. Purpose. Belonging. A future greater than slavery and dust and hunger.

Obi-Wan squeezed his shoulder gently.

“We’ll figure it out.”

Anakin searched his face carefully.

Obi-Wan looked tired, grieving, uncertain about nearly everything ahead of him.

But not about this.

That certainty was enough for the boy.

“Okay,” Anakin said finally.

Obi-Wan rose to his feet.

“Come on. We’re leaving Naboo.”

“Where are we going?”

For the first time in days, something almost warm appeared in Obi-Wan’s expression.

“Mandalore.”

—

The journey to Mandalore was quiet.

Anakin spent much of it asking questions Obi-Wan could barely answer.

What happens now?

Where will we live?

Can people leave the Jedi Order whenever they want?

Will the Jedi come after us?

Obi-Wan answered honestly whenever he could.

Sometimes he simply said, “I don’t know.”

The truth was that he had spent almost his entire life inside the structure of the Jedi Order. The Temple had defined every day since childhood. Now, suddenly, all of that was gone.

No rank.

No council.

No place in the Republic’s oldest institution.

Just a twenty-five-year-old former Jedi Knight, a Force-sensitive child depending entirely upon him, and a promise made beside a dying man.

Yet beneath the uncertainty there was another feeling too.

Relief.

The closer they came to Mandalore, the more Obi-Wan felt something inside himself loosening for the first time in years.

When their ship finally descended through the atmosphere toward Sundari, Anakin pressed against the cockpit glass in fascination.

The domed cities gleamed beneath the pale desert sun like polished pearls against endless white sands.

“It doesn’t look anything like Tatooine,” Anakin murmured.

“No,” Obi-Wan agreed softly. “It doesn’t.”

Their arrival caused immediate confusion among the palace guards.

The Duchess had not seen Obi-Wan Kenobi in nearly eight years.

Not since the violence of the Mandalorian Civil War, when they had both been impossibly young and trying very hard not to acknowledge feelings neither of them truly understood.

Now he stood once again at the gates of Sundari.

Only this time he was no longer a Jedi.

Satine Kryze came to meet him personally.

She crossed the great palace corridor in flowing silver and blue, composed and regal as ever — until she saw him clearly.

Then the composure cracked.

“Obi-Wan.”

He smiled faintly.

“Hello, Satine.”

For a long moment they simply looked at one another.

Eight years vanished in an instant.

She saw the grief in his eyes immediately.

“He’s dead,” she said quietly.

Obi-Wan nodded once.

“I’m sorry,” she whispered.

Then her gaze shifted to the boy standing beside him.

Anakin looked between them curiously.

“Who’s that?” he asked.

Obi-Wan glanced at Satine.

For the first time in many days, genuine warmth reached his face.

“That,” he said, “is your future aunt.”

Satine blinked in complete astonishment.

“Your— Obi-Wan!”

Anakin burst out laughing.

Even Obi-Wan looked briefly pleased with himself.

Satine stared at him another second before a helpless smile finally broke through her disbelief.

“You disappeared for eight years,” she said, “and this is how you return?”

“I thought it was charming.”

“It was reckless.”

“I learned from the best.”

She rolled her eyes despite herself.

Then, more softly: “You really left the Order?”

“Yes.”

The humor faded between them.

Satine stepped closer.

“For him?”

Obi-Wan looked at Anakin.

“For Qui-Gon,” he said. “And for him.”

Satine studied him carefully.

Years ago, she had known a young Padawan who buried his feelings beneath discipline and duty so deeply he scarcely allowed himself to breathe.

The man standing before her now still carried that discipline.

But the chains around it were gone.

At last, she smiled gently.

“Then welcome home, Obi-Wan.”

—

The days that followed felt almost unreal.

Anakin adapted to palace life with astonishing speed. One moment he was marveling at polished corridors and Mandalorian architecture; the next he was asking palace engineers endless questions about power systems and ventilation grids.

The guards quickly learned that if equipment malfunctioned anywhere near the royal residence, the strange Tatooine boy would somehow already be halfway inside the machinery trying to fix it.

Obi-Wan, meanwhile, found himself rediscovering parts of himself he had ignored for years.

He and Satine spoke late into the nights about everything they had missed. The war. Qui-Gon. The Jedi. Politics. Loneliness.

About the years they had spent pretending duty mattered more than love.

One evening, standing together on a balcony overlooking Sundari’s lights, Satine finally asked the question waiting between them since his arrival.

“What happens now?”

Obi-Wan looked out across the city.

“For the first time in my life,” he admitted quietly, “I have absolutely no idea.”

Satine slipped her hand into his.

“Good.”

He looked at her, surprised.

“You were always miserable when you had everything planned.”

Obi-Wan laughed softly.

Then, after a silence:

“I spent years telling myself leaving the Order for you would have been selfish.”

Satine held his gaze steadily.

“And now?”

He looked down at her hand in his.

“Now I think I was afraid.”

She stepped closer.

“Are you still?”

Obi-Wan answered by kissing her.

A few days later, they were married quietly within the royal palace of Sundari.

No grand galactic ceremony.

No Jedi representatives.

No Senate delegation.

Only a small gathering of trusted friends, Mandalorian officials, and one intensely excited nine-year-old boy who insisted on watching everything from the very front row.

As Satine and Obi-Wan stood together beneath the pale lights of the palace hall, Anakin grinned so hard his face hurt.

For the first time since leaving Tatooine, he felt something he had not realized he was searching for.

A family.

# Korkie Interlude: Part 1

Life inside the Sundari royal palace settled into rhythms none of them could have imagined only weeks earlier.

For Obi-Wan, the adjustment remained surreal at times. He would wake before dawn out of old Jedi habit, briefly expecting the distant bells of the Temple or the quiet movement of initiates through Coruscant corridors. Instead he found soft desert light filtering through Mandalorian glass, the stillness of Satine sleeping beside him, and the strange realization that his life belonged entirely to himself now.

It frightened him sometimes.

It also made him happier than he had ever expected to be.

A few days after the wedding, Satine brought Obi-Wan and Anakin deeper into the palace residential quarters, away from the public halls and government chambers. The section was quieter there, warmer somehow. Less royal. More lived-in.

Anakin noticed the toys scattered near one doorway first.

Then a small boy darted around the corner at full speed before skidding to a halt upon seeing strangers.

He could not have been older than four.

Blond-haired, sharp-eyed, and dressed in miniature Mandalorian formal clothes that already looked slightly disheveled from climbing somewhere he was probably not supposed to climb.

The child stared at Obi-Wan suspiciously.

Then at Anakin.

Then at Satine.

“Auntie Satine,” he declared with complete seriousness, “the guards said you got married.”

Satine smiled immediately.

“I did.”

The boy considered this.

“Why?”

Obi-Wan nearly choked trying not to laugh.

Satine folded her arms. “A very diplomatic question.”

“I’m practicing.”

“You’re four.”

“That’s when diplomacy starts.”

Obi-Wan exchanged a look with Satine that said very clearly: yes, this child belongs in your family.

Satine knelt beside the boy.

“Korkie, this is Obi-Wan.”

Korkie looked him up and down carefully.

“You’re very tall.”

“So I’ve been told.”

“And this,” Satine continued, “is Anakin.”

Anakin grinned immediately. “Hi.”

Korkie narrowed his eyes.

“You’re not tall.”

“Give me a few years.”

Korkie appeared to accept this answer.

Satine rested a hand gently on the boy’s shoulder.

“Korkie is my nephew,” she explained quietly to Obi-Wan as the children continued studying one another. “His parents were killed shortly after he was born. Political violence. There were still factions after the war who wanted leverage against House Kryze.”

Her voice hardened slightly at the memory.

“I brought him into the palace after that. Officially as ward and heir under my protection. Unofficially…” Her expression softened as she looked at the child. “As my son.”

Obi-Wan understood immediately.

The galaxy was full of children left vulnerable by conflict, ambition, and power struggles. The Jedi raised many such children themselves.

But this was different.

This was personal.

He placed a hand gently over Satine’s.

“You saved him.”

Satine gave a faint smile. “I tried.”

Obi-Wan looked at Korkie again.

The child was now circling Anakin cautiously.

“Do you know how to fight?” Korkie asked.

“A little.”

“Can you fly ships?”

“Yes.”

Korkie gasped softly.

Obi-Wan smiled despite himself.

He already knew exactly how this was going to unfold.

By the end of the week, the two boys were nearly inseparable.

Korkie followed Anakin everywhere through the palace. Anakin, who had spent most of his life either alone or surrounded by adults, discovered very quickly that he enjoyed having someone younger looking up to him.

He taught Korkie games from Tatooine.

Korkie taught Anakin how to navigate the endless palace corridors without getting lost.

Together they managed to concern the palace staff on an almost daily basis.

One afternoon, Satine discovered them both halfway inside a maintenance shaft after Anakin insisted he could “improve airflow efficiency” in one wing of the palace.

Another time, Obi-Wan found Korkie proudly wearing a pair of oversized flight goggles Anakin had modified for him out of spare parts.

“They make him look more aerodynamic,” Anakin explained.

Korkie nodded solemnly. “Very aerodynamic.”

Obi-Wan had absolutely no response prepared for this kind of parenting.

The realization struck him often now: the Jedi Order had trained him to negotiate disputes, survive combat, and resist temptation.

It had not trained him for children.

Especially not two of them.

Still, he tried.

Anakin’s Force training began slowly.

Without the structure of the Temple, Obi-Wan was forced to build lessons almost entirely from instinct and memory. Mornings often began with meditation exercises on palace balconies overlooking Sundari’s endless white horizons. Obi-Wan taught patience first. Breathing. Awareness. Control.

Anakin tolerated meditation the way a starfighter tolerated atmospheric drag.

Barely.

The boy wanted movement. Action. Lightsabers. Flying. Speed.

Yet despite his impatience, he learned astonishingly quickly.

Sometimes too quickly.

Obi-Wan began to understand at last why the Council had feared him.

The Force moved around Anakin with unnatural intensity. Skills that took Jedi initiates years to grasp sometimes came to him in days.

Korkie often watched these lessons from nearby.

At first Obi-Wan assumed the child was merely curious.

Then one morning, while Anakin practiced telekinetically lifting small training spheres, Korkie marched directly into the exercise room and sat down beside him.

Anakin looked over. “What are you doing?”

“Training.”

Obi-Wan blinked.

“Korkie…”

The child held out both hands toward one of the spheres with tremendous concentration.

Nothing happened.

Korkie squinted harder.

Still nothing.

Anakin immediately became concerned.

“Maybe you’re doing it wrong.”

“I’m not.”

“You look like you’re trying to eat it with your mind.”

“That’s not what I’m doing.”

Obi-Wan turned away briefly to hide a smile.

For several days afterward, Anakin became determined to help Korkie “unlock” his abilities.

“Try feeling the room,” he instructed one afternoon.

“I feel bored,” Korkie replied.

“No, with the Force.”

“Oh.”

Korkie frowned intensely at a nearby cup.

The cup remained entirely unimpressed.

Eventually Obi-Wan sat both boys down.

“The Force doesn’t work that way,” he explained gently. “Not everyone can use it.”

Anakin looked troubled by this revelation.

“But why not?”

Obi-Wan considered the question carefully.

“The Force exists in all living things,” he said. “But people connect to it differently. Some strongly. Some quietly. Some not at all.”

Korkie brightened immediately. “Good. Meditation is terrible.”

Anakin still looked uncertain.

For much of his life, extraordinary ability had been the thing that separated him from everyone around him. His reflexes. His instincts. His impossible piloting.

Now the Force had become another dividing line.

It bothered him more than he expected.

But children adapt quickly.

Within a few weeks, Anakin stopped trying to involve Korkie in Force exercises altogether. Instead, he began sharing the things he loved most.

Podracing stories became Korkie’s absolute favorite.

Anakin described Mos Espa in vivid detail — the heat shimmering above the streets, the roar of engines echoing through canyons, crowds screaming as racers flew past at deadly speeds.

“You actually raced those?” Korkie asked in disbelief.

“Yep.”

“Weren’t you scared?”

“All the time.”

“Then why do it?”

Anakin grinned.

“Because it’s fun.”

Korkie appeared to consider this flawless logic.

The stories expanded from there.

Droids.

Scrapyards.

Flying.

Tusken Raiders.

Sandstorms.

Starships.

Anakin explained hyperdrives to a fascinated four-year-old using cups, fruit, and eventually an entire stolen dinner tray as demonstration pieces.

The palace staff became increasingly resigned to finding mechanical parts in places mechanical parts absolutely should not be.

Satine watched all of this with quiet amazement.

One evening she found Obi-Wan standing outside a doorway listening to the boys inside.

Anakin was animatedly describing how to rebuild a podracer engine while Korkie interrupted every few seconds with increasingly absurd questions.

“Can a podracer fly into space?”

“No.”

“Can you put lasers on it?”

“I mean… probably.”

“Can it have eight engines?”

“That sounds unsafe.”

“Can it have ten?”

Obi-Wan was smiling.

Not politely.

Not carefully.

Openly.

Satine slipped beside him.

“You’re happy,” she observed softly.

He looked into the room again.

Anakin was now trying to explain torque ratios to a child who clearly thought torque was some kind of animal.

“Yes,” Obi-Wan admitted quietly.

For a moment he simply stood there listening to the sound of their family becoming real.

# Korkie Interlude: Part 2

The peace they found in Sundari lasted only a short while.

The capital remained beautiful, ordered, and calm on its surface. Its gleaming domes stood against the endless white desert as symbols of the New Mandalorian dream: reason over violence, law over conquest, dialogue over bloodshed.

Obi-Wan admired that dream.

He also noticed, as the weeks passed, how many guards surrounded the Duchess wherever she went.

One afternoon, while walking through one of Sundari's open plazas, he finally asked about it.

Satine gave a faint smile.

"There are still those who believe peace is weakness."

"I've noticed."

"They're quieter than they used to be."

"But not gone."

"No."

Anakin walked several paces ahead, enthusiastically explaining to Korkie why a podracer with six engines would actually corner worse than one with two.

Korkie was unconvinced.

"What if all six were *really* fast?"

"That isn't how engines work."

"I think it should be."

Obi-Wan smiled.

Then the world exploded into motion.

Helmeted warriors burst from adjoining streets with military precision.

They wore dark armor without identifying markings. Jetpacks roared to life as stun bolts ripped through the air.

"Down!" Obi-Wan shouted.

Instinct took over.

He thrust both hands forward.

An invisible wave of Force energy blasted the first attackers backward before they could fire again.

But there were more.

Far more.

The ambushers ignored Obi-Wan entirely.

Their objective was obvious.

Two warriors seized Satine before nearby palace guards could react. Another grabbed Korkie, lifting the struggling child effortlessly.

"Korkie!" Satine cried.

Anakin started running toward them.

"No!"

Obi-Wan lunged first.

He caught Anakin around the waist just as another warrior reached for the boy.

Then Obi-Wan gathered the Force beneath him.

The world blurred.

He burst away in a Force-enhanced dash that carried them across the plaza faster than any ordinary sprint. Stone shattered beneath his feet as he accelerated, putting precious distance between Anakin and the kidnappers.

He came to a stop atop a nearby terrace.

Too late.

The attackers had already reached their extraction craft.

Its engines ignited.

Satine fought furiously despite being restrained.

Korkie kicked and screamed.

The ship lifted into the sky.

"No..." Obi-Wan whispered.

His eyes swept desperately across the street.

A civilian landspeeder.

Abandoned.

Good enough.

He lifted Anakin onto the passenger seat without breaking stride.

"Hold on."

Before Anakin could answer, Obi-Wan had already accelerated.

The speeder shot through Sundari's traffic lanes with reckless speed.

Buildings flashed past.

Warning alarms blared as other vehicles scattered out of his path.

Ahead, the kidnappers' ship climbed higher.

Obi-Wan pushed the civilian engine far beyond anything its designers had intended.

The speeder shook violently.

Panels rattled loose.

Smoke began pouring from the rear housing.

Still he drove faster.

"We're gaining!" Anakin shouted.

"I know."

The distance closed.

Twenty meters.

Ten.

Five.

Obi-Wan pulled alongside.

The transport banked sharply.

He matched it.

Then, at precisely the right instant, he drove the speeder upward.

Metal scraped violently against metal.

The civilian craft slammed onto the transport's upper hull.

Obi-Wan killed the engines.

"We're here."

He jumped onto the ship's exterior.

The wind tore at his robes.

The transport continued flying at high speed over Mandalore's deserts.

Without thinking, Obi-Wan reached toward his belt.

His hand found nothing.

For one impossible second his mind still expected the familiar weight of a lightsaber.

Only then did reality catch up.

He had surrendered Qui-Gon's weapon.

He no longer carried one himself.

There would be no blade cutting effortlessly through durasteel.

He looked down at the armored hull beneath his feet.

"So be it."

He planted both feet firmly.

Raised his hands.

And struck.

The first Force Push echoed like a hammer blow.

The hull groaned.

But held.

Again.

Another tremendous impact slammed into the metal.

Still it resisted.

Obi-Wan closed his eyes briefly.

He reached deeper.

Not into anger.

Into determination.

Satine was inside.

Korkie was inside.

He struck again.

The hull buckled.

A visible dent appeared.

Again.

The durasteel bent inward with a shriek of tortured metal.

Again.

Again.

Again.

Each impact landed like artillery.

Finally, the weakened plating gave way.

Metal burst inward.

A jagged opening tore through the transport's roof.

Without hesitation Obi-Wan dropped through.

He landed hard inside a cargo corridor.

Blaster fire greeted him immediately.

He thrust out one hand.

The nearest guard flew backward into a bulkhead with enough force to crack the wall before collapsing unconscious.

A second attacker rushed him.

Obi-Wan seized the warrior telekinetically and hurled him sideways into a third.

Both crashed together and fell still.

More guards poured into the corridor.

He searched through the Force.

Satine.

Korkie.

There.

Toward the bow.

He sprinted.

Blaster bolts ricocheted around him.

He ducked one.

Another scorched the wall inches from his shoulder.

He burst into a larger compartment.

Satine and Korkie sat restrained in heavy chairs.

Half a dozen armored warriors stood around them.

One guard immediately raised her rifle.

Obi-Wan reached out.

The weapon tore free from her hands.

It flew across the room directly into his waiting grip.

Years of Jedi training had never emphasized firearms.

But he had spent enough time beside soldiers during the Naboo crisis.

He fired.

One guard dropped.

A second.

A third.

The remaining warriors froze.

Their commander looked from the fallen troops to the former Jedi standing calmly with the captured blaster.

Then he slowly lowered his own weapon.

One after another, the others followed.

Their rifles clattered onto the deck.

Obi-Wan did not take his eyes off them.

"Move away from them."

They obeyed.

Only then did he hurry to Satine.

He broke her restraints first.

She immediately reached for Korkie.

Obi-Wan freed the child moments later.

Korkie threw his arms around both adults at once.

"I knew you'd come."

Obi-Wan managed a small smile.

"Always."

There was no time to celebrate.

"We have to leave," Satine said urgently.

Obi-Wan nodded.

He led them back toward the breach in the roof.

The four climbed onto the transport's exterior.

The abandoned speeder still rested where Obi-Wan had landed it.

Miraculously, it remained functional.

They climbed aboard.

Obi-Wan took the controls.

Satine held Korkie tightly beside her.

Anakin squeezed in beside them.

The engines roared.

The speeder leapt away.

Behind them, alarms echoed across the transport.

Then its weapon batteries opened fire.

Scarlet bolts streaked through the sky.

Obi-Wan twisted the speeder sharply.

One blast missed by centimeters.

Another exploded beneath them.

The fragile civilian craft shuddered violently.

"Hang on!" Obi-Wan shouted.

More fire.

He rolled.

Dove.

Climbed.

Every instinct from years as a Jedi pilot returned immediately.

The transport continued firing.

Another barrage erupted.

Obi-Wan banked hard.

Most of the bolts missed.

One did not.

The explosion came without warning.

There was no time to react.

No opportunity to shield anyone.

The bolt struck Korkie directly.

The child went limp instantly.

Satine screamed.

"Korkie!"

There was no response.

No movement.

Only stillness.

Anakin stared in horror.

Obi-Wan felt the life vanish through the Force in a single heartbreaking instant.

Every instinct demanded he stop.

Turn around.

Fight.

Grieve.

Instead he tightened his grip on the controls.

Not because he wanted to.

Because everyone else aboard depended upon him.

He pushed the damaged speeder even harder.

Minute after minute he flew through the desert canyons surrounding Sundari, weaving between rock formations until finally the larger transport could no longer follow safely.

Its engines faded into the distance.

Eventually it abandoned pursuit.

Only then did Obi-Wan slow.

Silence filled the speeder.

Satine cradled Korkie's small body throughout the flight home.

She never let go.

—

The funeral took place three days later.

Unlike the state ceremonies of Mandalorian nobles, this one was private.

Satine requested it herself.

Only family.

Only those who had truly loved him.

The white stone memorial overlooked the desert beyond Sundari's protective dome.

Flowers surrounded the bier.

Korkie's favorite toy—a small mechanical flyer Anakin had built for him only a week earlier—rested beside him.

Anakin stood motionless through the ceremony.

He had lost people before.

But never someone who had laughed with him only days earlier.

Never someone who had become his little brother.

When everyone else had spoken, Anakin stepped forward quietly.

"I was going to teach him how to fly someday."

His voice cracked.

"He would've been really good."

There was nothing more to say.

He stepped back.

Obi-Wan wrapped an arm around his shoulders.

Satine watched in silence as Korkie was laid to rest.

She did not cry again.

Her grief had become something quieter.

Deeper.

Afterward, the three returned to the palace.

No one spoke until evening.

Finally Satine broke the silence.

"I know who sent them."

Obi-Wan looked up.

"The factions that opposed the New Mandalorians."

She folded her hands tightly together.

"The warriors who never accepted our reforms. They believe peace has weakened Mandalore. They believe removing me would return power to them."

"They murdered his parents," Obi-Wan said softly.

"Yes."

"And now they murdered him."

She nodded once.

"I protected him for four years."

Her voice nearly failed.

"And in the end..."

Obi-Wan reached across the table and took her hand.

"You gave him four years of love he would never otherwise have known."

She closed her eyes.

After a long silence, Obi-Wan spoke again.

"This won't be the last attempt."

"No," Satine agreed.

"They've seen that kidnapping can work."

"They won't stop."

Obi-Wan looked toward the adjoining room, where Anakin sat alone, staring silently out a window toward the desert.

"They must never reach him."

Satine followed his gaze.

"The Jedi believed he is the Chosen One."

"They did."

"And whether that prophecy is true or not..." she said quietly, "...he is our family."

Obi-Wan nodded.

"They came for all of us today."

His expression hardened with resolve.

"They will not get another chance."

Together they began making plans.

The palace guard would be expanded.

Travel routes would become unpredictable.

Additional escorts would accompany every public appearance.

Secure rooms throughout the palace would be reinforced.

Obi-Wan himself would rarely allow Anakin out of his sight when beyond the palace walls.

He had once trusted the Jedi Order to provide safety.

Now there was only his own vigilance.

He intended to make it enough.

# Part 2

Life in Sundari settled into something neither Obi-Wan nor Satine had ever expected to possess.

Routine.

Not the rigid routine of the Jedi Temple, nor the ceremonial routine of Mandalorian government, but something quieter and more human. Shared breakfasts. Late-night conversations. Anakin tracking sand through palace corridors despite repeated requests not to. Satine pretending to be outraged while secretly finding it endearing. Obi-Wan discovering, to his own astonishment, that he enjoyed fixing broken training remotes with Anakin more than he had ever enjoyed most diplomatic receptions.

The palace staff adjusted gradually to the strange arrangement.

The former Jedi Knight.

The pacifist duchess.

The impossibly gifted Force-sensitive child they were apparently raising together.

It should have looked absurd.

Instead, somehow, it worked.

Very early on, Obi-Wan made one thing absolutely clear.

“No ‘Master,’” he told Anakin during a morning training session in one of the palace courtyards.

Anakin blinked. “But you’re teaching me.”

“Yes.”

“That means you’re my master.”

Obi-Wan shook his head.

“It means I’m responsible for you. That’s different.”

Anakin frowned, not entirely understanding.

Obi-Wan lowered the wooden practice staff he had been using.

“You’re family, Anakin. Satine and I are raising you. I want you to call us Aunt and Uncle.”

Anakin stared at him as though he had spoken another language.

“Really?”

“Yes.”

“But… Jedi don’t do that.”

Obi-Wan’s expression softened slightly.

“We’re not Jedi.”

The words still felt strange to say aloud.

Anakin looked uncertain for several moments.

Then, awkwardly:

“…Uncle Obi-Wan?”

Obi-Wan smiled.

“There you are.”

Satine nearly cried the first time Anakin called her Aunt Satine at dinner later that evening.

She managed to preserve her composure for almost six entire seconds.

—

Satine approached Anakin’s education seriously.

If Obi-Wan was teaching him how to listen to the Force, Satine intended to teach him how to listen to people.

She introduced him to the structure of governments, trade systems, planetary alliances, and the difficult mathematics of peacekeeping. She explained why systems fell into corruption, why wars began, and why charismatic leaders could become dangerous when institutions weakened around them.

Anakin absorbed it all with startling speed.

He asked difficult questions constantly.

“Why doesn’t the Republic just free slaves everywhere?”

“Because the Republic barely governs the Outer Rim at all,” Satine explained patiently.

“Then why do people listen to the Senate?”

“Sometimes they don’t.”

“That seems stupid.”

“It often is.”

Obi-Wan, overhearing from across the room, quietly hid a smile behind his tea.

Anakin loved the political lessons less than the combat training, but Satine quickly discovered that once discussions involved injustice or corruption, his attention became absolute.

He hated unfairness with frightening intensity.

Sometimes, when Anakin spoke about slavery, Obi-Wan noticed Satine watching him with concern.

Not because the boy was cruel.

Because his anger burned so hot.

—

The issue of weapons became unavoidable almost immediately.

Neither Obi-Wan nor Anakin possessed a lightsaber.

Obi-Wan had surrendered Qui-Gon’s weapon to the Jedi Council, and Anakin had never built one.

At first Obi-Wan attempted to focus entirely on Force training: meditation, concentration, awareness exercises, telekinesis, sensory expansion.

Anakin progressed at a rate Obi-Wan found genuinely unsettling.

Most initiates spent years learning basic telekinetic control.

Anakin casually lifted entire clusters of training spheres within days.

Precognitive reflexes came even more naturally. During sparring exercises, the boy often reacted before Obi-Wan himself had fully committed to an attack.

“He’s doing it instinctively,” Obi-Wan admitted one evening.

Satine leaned against the balcony beside him. “Is that unusual?”

Obi-Wan let out a quiet laugh.

“Satine, if the Council could see some of this, Master Windu might actually faint.”

Eventually Obi-Wan accepted reality.

Force abilities alone were not enough. Anakin needed combat discipline too.

So Obi-Wan acquired two traditional Mandalorian beskad swords.

The weapons were elegant in their brutality: single-edged iron blades designed for close combat, heavier than lightsabers and demanding proper footwork and timing.

One for himself.

One for Anakin.

The first time Satine discovered them sparring in the palace courtyard, she nearly had a diplomatic incident on the spot.

“Absolutely not!”

Obi-Wan lowered his sword immediately. “Satine—”

“You gave him a weapon?”

“It’s training.”

“He is nine!”

Anakin stepped in before Obi-Wan could answer.

“They’re for protection,” he said earnestly.

Satine crossed her arms. “Protection from what?”

Anakin thought carefully.

“…Things that need protecting against.”

Obi-Wan had to turn away to hide his expression.

Satine looked between the two of them suspiciously.

Then Anakin added, with complete sincerity:

“Uncle Obi-Wan says aggression is bad.”

“I did say that.”

“And these are defensive swords.”

Satine stared at them for several seconds longer.

Then, somehow, impossibly, she sighed in reluctant defeat.

“I cannot believe that explanation worked on me.”

“It was a very good explanation,” Obi-Wan said.

“You stay out of this.”

—

Months passed.

Anakin advanced rapidly.

Too rapidly.

The beskad swords became extensions of his body almost frighteningly quickly. His strikes carried unusual creativity and unpredictability, while his reflexes bordered on precognitive impossibility even without formal lightsaber training.

Obi-Wan recognized the problem before Anakin did.

The boy was outgrowing the limits of improvised instruction.

One evening after sparring, Obi-Wan lowered his blade slowly.

“You’re ready.”

Anakin immediately brightened. “For what?”

Obi-Wan looked toward the Sundari sunset.

“Ilum.”

—

The journey became something almost joyful.

For Anakin, Ilum was mythic long before he ever saw it. Obi-Wan told him stories during hyperspace travel: ancient crystal caves, Jedi pilgrimages, lightsabers assembled by hand over days of meditation and craftsmanship.

Satine listened quietly from nearby, occasionally smiling at how animated Obi-Wan became while describing Jedi traditions he supposedly no longer cared about.

When they finally arrived above the frozen world, Anakin practically vibrated with excitement.

Then they landed.

And found Jedi Temple Guards waiting outside the crystal caves.

Not one or two.

Dozens.

The masked sentinels stood in absolute silence beneath the icy winds, yellow lightsabers hanging motionless at their sides.

Obi-Wan slowed immediately.

“What is this?”

One of the guards stepped forward.

“By order of the Jedi Council, access to the crystal caves is restricted.”

Obi-Wan stared at him.

“Restricted from whom?”

“You and the boy.”

The cold suddenly felt much sharper.

Satine looked genuinely stunned. “You stationed guards on an entire planet?”

The guard remained unmoving.

“We were instructed to prevent unauthorized acquisition of kyber crystals.”

Anakin looked up at Obi-Wan, confused.

“They did this because of us?”

Obi-Wan felt anger rising in his chest.

Not merely disagreement anymore.

Surveillance. Containment. Fear.

The guard activated a holocommunicator without another word.

“The targets have arrived,” he said calmly.

Obi-Wan exhaled slowly.

“Oh, this is unbelievable.”

—

The Jedi Council came in person.

Their ships descended through Ilum’s storms within hours, pushing hyperspace routes as fast as possible.

When they arrived outside the crystal caves, the confrontation was immediate.

Snow whipped around robes and cloaks alike while Temple Guards formed silent lines nearby.

Mace Windu stepped forward first.

“You should not have come here.”

Obi-Wan folded his arms. “He needs a lightsaber.”

“He is not a Jedi.”

“He is my student.”

“He is dangerous.”

Anakin frowned openly now.

“I’m standing right here.”

Nobody acknowledged him.

Ki-Adi-Mundi looked toward the beskad sword at Anakin’s side with visible disapproval.

“You’ve trained him in Mandalorian combat?”

“Yes,” Obi-Wan said.

“That is not the Jedi way.”

Obi-Wan’s patience finally began to crack.

“You forfeited the right to dictate the Jedi way to me when you cast me out for honoring my master’s dying wish.”

“You chose to leave,” Windu said sharply.

“I chose not to abandon a child.”

The air became very still.

Obi-Wan stepped closer.

“Listen to me carefully. I have already trained him. Meditation. Telekinesis. Force awareness. Precognition. He’s progressing whether you approve or not.”

Mace Windu’s expression darkened.

“If you deny him lightsabers, then I will continue training him without them.”

“With Mandalorian swords?” Ki-Adi-Mundi said dismissively.

“Yes.”

Several council members exchanged skeptical glances.

“Child’s play,” one muttered.

“No match for true lightsaber combat.”

“Perhaps it is better this way,” another added. “Contain the damage.”

Anakin’s face changed instantly.

The insult landed hard.

He stepped forward before Obi-Wan could stop him.

“Then prove it.”

The snow blew sideways around him as every Jedi present looked down at the boy.

Anakin pulled the beskad sword from his belt.

The metal rang sharply in the frozen air.

“I challenge Master Yoda.”

Several Temple Guards visibly stiffened.

Even Obi-Wan blinked.

“Anakin—”

“If I win,” Anakin continued, “you let us into the caves.”

Yoda looked almost entertained.

“A bold challenge,” he said mildly.

Anakin lifted the blade into guard position.

“You said Mandalorian combat was child’s play.”

Slowly, Yoda stepped forward.

“Very well.”

—

The duel began carefully.

Yoda moved lightly through the snow, cane discarded, green lightsaber ignited with its familiar snap-hiss.

Anakin held only cold Mandalorian iron.

Most observers expected the fight to end instantly.

It did not.

From the opening exchange, Anakin’s reflexes shocked everyone watching.

He moved fast.

Not merely physically fast — Force fast.

He anticipated angles before they fully developed, shifted footing instinctively, and adapted mid-motion with startling creativity.

Yoda pressed harder.

Anakin adjusted.

Obi-Wan watched in growing disbelief.

He had known Anakin was gifted.

This was beyond gifted.

Yoda’s eyes narrowed thoughtfully during the duel.

Not fearful.

Interested.

The boy’s aggression was dangerous, yes. His emotions burned close to the surface.

But his instincts…

Extraordinary.

More importantly, Yoda suddenly understood something very clearly:

If denied proper guidance, Anakin truly would continue down another path entirely.

A Force-trained Mandalorian warrior raised outside Jedi philosophy.

The future branching from that possibility felt deeply unstable.

So, gradually, Yoda changed his approach.

He gave Anakin opportunities.

Tiny openings.

Moments just barely visible.

Anakin seized every single one.

Finally, with a powerful rotational strike aided by telekinetic momentum, Anakin knocked Yoda’s lightsaber clean from his hand into the snow.

Silence fell instantly across Ilum.

Anakin stood breathing hard, sword raised.

Yoda looked at him for several long seconds.

Then the old master smiled faintly.

“Strong, you are.”

Mace Windu looked utterly unconvinced.

But the duel had happened publicly.

The terms had been agreed upon.

At last Yoda turned toward the Temple Guards.

“Permit them entry.”

—

The crystal caves were silent beyond words.

Ancient.

Living.

The frozen blue corridors shimmered with refracted light as Obi-Wan, Satine, and Anakin ventured deeper into the heart of Ilum.

Anakin felt the crystals long before he saw them.

A strange resonance hummed through him, almost musical.

Then one crystal called.

Clear as a voice.

Nearby, Obi-Wan stopped as another answered him too.

For a moment master and student simply looked at one another in shared understanding.

Satine watched both of them quietly.

Not Jedi.

Not entirely.

But something meaningful nonetheless.

They returned to Mandalore with their crystals carefully protected.

Then came the construction.

Days of precision work spread across palace tables: circuitry, emitters, focusing arrays, polished metal components. Obi-Wan guided Anakin patiently through every step while building his own alongside him.

It became collaborative rather than ceremonial.

Satine frequently joined them, offering suggestions on grip design and balance despite openly claiming not to understand any of the engineering.

Finally, late one evening, both hilts lay completed before them.

Anakin looked almost unable to breathe.

Obi-Wan nodded once.

“Together?”

Anakin grinned.

“Together.”

They activated them simultaneously.

Two blue blades burst into existence with twin flashes of light, illuminating the workshop in brilliant azure.

Anakin stared at his lightsaber in awe.

Obi-Wan gazed at his own for several silent seconds.

The familiar hum filled the room like the return of an old voice.

Satine smiled softly as blue light reflected in all three of their eyes.

# Part 3

The first time Anakin ignited his lightsaber in combat practice, Obi-Wan understood immediately that ordinary instruction would never be enough.

The boy moved like someone rediscovering something rather than learning it for the first time.

Within months, Anakin’s proficiency surpassed what most Jedi initiates achieved in years. His grasp of bladework evolved at a pace that forced Obi-Wan to continuously reevaluate his own teaching methods. What began as foundational exercises in stance, movement, and control rapidly became advanced sparring.

Form I came first, as it always did: broad fundamentals, fluid transitions, discipline.

Anakin mastered it quickly.

Form II intrigued him for several weeks before he grew impatient with its elegance.

Form III frustrated him at first. Soresu required patience above all else, and patience remained one of Anakin’s weakest qualities. Obi-Wan insisted upon it anyway, forcing him through endless defensive drills until his reactions became instinctive.

Form IV delighted him.

Form V transformed him.

The moment Anakin fully encountered Djem So, something locked into place.

Power. Counterstrikes. Dominance through controlled aggression.

Unlike Ataru’s acrobatics or Makashi’s precision, Djem So felt honest to him. Direct. Unapologetic. It matched the intensity already burning inside him.

Obi-Wan noticed it immediately during sparring.

Anakin stopped merely reacting to attacks. He began controlling exchanges, redirecting force against force with frightening natural ability. His counters landed harder than they should have for someone his age. Every year that passed widened the gap further between Anakin and nearly everyone around him.

Except Obi-Wan.

Barely.

Their duels gradually evolved into spectacles within the palace training courtyards. Guards paused to watch. Palace staff lingered in nearby corridors pretending not to stare. Satine often observed from balconies overhead with a mixture of pride and concern.

Blue blades flashed beneath Mandalorian skies while master and student moved faster than ordinary eyes could comfortably follow.

Anakin laughed during combat.

Obi-Wan rarely did.

“You rely too much on overpowering your opponent,” Obi-Wan warned during one session after narrowly avoiding a crushing overhead strike.

“It works.”

“Until it doesn’t.”

Anakin spun his blade once. “You sound exactly like Aunt Satine.”

“I’ll take that as a compliment.”

“It wasn’t.”

Obi-Wan smiled faintly. “Again.”

—

The years passed gently on Mandalore.

Anakin grew taller, stronger, sharper. The lingering traces of the frightened slave boy from Tatooine slowly faded into memory.

But not entirely.

Certain things remained buried inside him no matter how much love surrounded him now.

Obi-Wan sensed them sometimes during meditation sessions: flashes of fear, anger, helplessness. Anakin rarely spoke directly about his mother anymore, yet Obi-Wan knew the wound had never fully healed.

Satine understood it too.

Which was why she paid such close attention when Chancellor Palpatine entered their lives.

At first, his interest appeared harmless.

The Supreme Chancellor began visiting Mandalore periodically under the broad justification of maintaining diplomatic relations with Duchess Satine’s government. Publicly, the meetings concerned trade routes, Senate negotiations, neutral system policies.

Privately, however, Palpatine always found time to speak with Anakin.

The Chancellor possessed an unsettling gift for understanding exactly what people wanted to hear.

He never pushed too hard initially.

He asked thoughtful questions.

He listened carefully.

He treated Anakin not as a child, but as someone important.

That alone made an impression.

Obi-Wan found Palpatine courteous enough, though slightly difficult to read. The Chancellor often praised Anakin’s intelligence and maturity in ways that felt flattering without seeming overtly manipulative.

Satine trusted him immediately less than she trusted a venomous serpent.

To her, Palpatine represented nearly every political instinct she opposed.

Centralized authority.

Accumulation of executive power.

Charismatic leadership overriding institutional restraint.

The Republic increasingly revolved around him personally, and Satine disliked leaders who became indispensable.

So whenever Palpatine spoke privately with Anakin, Satine listened.

Not secretly, exactly. She made little effort to conceal her presence nearby. Palace guards quickly learned not to interrupt her during those moments.

Most conversations remained innocuous enough.

Politics.

History.

Governance.

Philosophy.

Palpatine often encouraged Anakin to think beyond traditional systems and limitations. He praised independence. Strength. Vision.

Anakin enjoyed the discussions.

Then one afternoon, everything changed.

Satine stood outside a receiving chamber while Palpatine and Anakin spoke inside over tea.

“…people fear strong leadership,” Palpatine was saying calmly. “But history shows that decisive individuals are often the only thing preventing chaos.”

Anakin leaned forward slightly. “The Senate seems pretty chaotic.”

Palpatine smiled.

“Exactly. Endless committees. Endless debate. While innocent people suffer waiting for action. Sometimes democracy requires firmer guidance than idealists are willing to admit.”

Satine’s eyes narrowed immediately.

Inside, Palpatine continued smoothly.

“The galaxy changes because exceptional people seize responsibility others are too timid to accept.”

Anakin considered that carefully.

“So you think power is good?”

“I think power is a tool,” Palpatine replied. “And tools depend entirely upon who wields them.”

Then came the sentence that finally crossed Satine’s line.

“The mistake most governments make,” Palpatine said softly, “is allowing principles to interfere with necessary authority.”

The doors slid open instantly.

Satine entered like a storm.

Palpatine looked up in surprise.

Anakin straightened immediately, sensing the sudden tension.

Satine’s expression was ice.

“Chancellor Palpatine,” she said evenly, “I hereby order you to leave Mandalore and never return without my explicit permission.”

For perhaps the first time in many years, Palpatine looked genuinely caught off guard.

“I beg your pardon?”

“You heard me.”

The Chancellor slowly rose from his chair.

“You have no authority to evict a Chancellor.”

“And Mandalore is not under Republic jurisdiction,” Satine replied instantly. “It is an independent world. As Duchess of Mandalore, I order you to leave immediately. This is your final warning.”

The room became very still.

Anakin looked between them in confusion.

Palpatine’s expression remained outwardly composed, but something colder flickered briefly behind his eyes now.

“Duchess,” he said carefully, “surely this is unnecessary. I have merely been offering mentorship to a gifted young man.”

“You have been normalizing authoritarianism to an impressionable teenager.”

“A harsh interpretation.”

“An accurate one.”

Palpatine clasped his hands calmly behind his back.

“The Republic faces dangerous times. Surely even you understand that idealism alone cannot preserve peace.”

Satine stepped closer.

“And surely you understand that every tyrant in galactic history believed concentration of power was ‘necessary.’”

The Chancellor studied her silently.

Then he shifted tactics.

“Anakin is extraordinary,” he said gently. “He deserves guidance from those capable of recognizing his potential.”

“He already has guidance.”

“A former Jedi and a pacifist duchess?”

Satine’s voice hardened further.

“Yes.”

Palpatine glanced briefly toward Anakin.

“There are many in the Republic who would value your abilities properly.”

Satine cut him off immediately.

“No.”

The single word landed like a locked door.

For several long seconds neither moved.

Then, finally, Palpatine inclined his head slightly.

“As you wish, Duchess.”

He looked toward Anakin one last time.

“I hope we meet again someday.”

Satine answered before Anakin could.

“We won’t.”

—

Palpatine departed Mandalore within the hour.

He never returned.

After his shuttle disappeared into the evening sky, Anakin found Satine standing alone on one of the palace balconies overlooking Sundari.

The wind tugged lightly at her hair.

“You really hate him,” Anakin said.

Satine exhaled quietly.

“No,” she said. “Hatred clouds judgment. I distrust him.”

Anakin leaned against the railing beside her.

“He seemed nice.”

“That is part of why he worries me.”

Anakin frowned.

“I don’t understand.”

Satine turned toward him fully.

“Anakin, some of the most dangerous people in history were charming. Persuasive. Intelligent. They made people feel understood.”

“He never said anything evil.”

“No,” Satine agreed. “Because truly dangerous ideas rarely arrive sounding evil.”

The city lights shimmered below them.

Satine folded her arms lightly.

“Palpatine believes power should gather around strong individuals. He believes institutions become obstacles. That rules are inconveniences. That exceptional people deserve exceptional authority.”

Anakin considered this carefully.

“That sounds kind of reasonable.”

“It always does at first.”

He looked at her.

Satine’s expression softened slightly.

“Listen to me carefully. Never trust anyone who asks for power while claiming they alone can fix everything.”

Anakin remained quiet.

“People who centralize authority always insist it’s temporary,” Satine continued. “Necessary. Efficient. But power rarely gives itself back willingly once acquired.”

The words settled deeply into him.

Satine rested a hand gently on his shoulder.

“You are strong in the Force. Stronger than anyone I’ve ever met. Someday many people will try to influence you. Some will flatter you. Some will promise purpose. Some will tell you that because you are exceptional, ordinary limits should not apply to you.”

Anakin looked away toward the horizon.

“How do I know who to trust?”

Satine smiled sadly.

“You don’t. Not completely.”

Then she tapped lightly against his chest.

“But you can learn to recognize when someone is feeding your ego instead of your conscience.”

Anakin absorbed the lesson quietly.

Years later, he would still remember that conversation almost word for word.

And from that day onward, whenever powerful men spoke too confidently about necessary authority, a small part of Satine Kryze’s voice always echoed somewhere in the back of his mind.

Palpatine never managed to silence it.

# Part 4

Years passed quietly on Mandalore.

Not peacefully — the galaxy itself was growing steadily more fractured with every passing year — but within the walls of Sundari, Anakin’s life remained grounded by something few Force-sensitive prodigies ever truly received.

Stability.

Obi-Wan trained him relentlessly, but never coldly. Satine challenged him intellectually at every opportunity, refusing to let raw power become the center of his identity. Between them, Anakin matured into something unusual: immensely capable, fiercely emotional, deeply loyal, and far more politically aware than most Jedi ever became.

By the time he reached adulthood, even Obi-Wan had begun struggling to keep up with him in sparring.

Anakin’s mastery of lightsaber combat had become extraordinary.

His command of all seven forms bordered on obsessive study, though Form V remained unmistakably his own. Djem So suited the force of his personality perfectly. His counters struck with terrifying precision and overwhelming momentum, yet years of Obi-Wan’s discipline prevented him from descending into recklessness.

Usually.

One evening after a particularly intense duel in the palace training hall, Obi-Wan deactivated his lightsaber and stood silently for several moments.

Anakin lowered his own blade.

“What?”

Obi-Wan studied him carefully.

For the first time since Qui-Gon’s death, he heard his old master’s voice in memory without grief immediately following it.

Train the boy.

He had.

And now the boy was a man.

Obi-Wan clipped his saber to his belt.

“That’s enough.”

Anakin blinked. “Enough for today?”

Obi-Wan shook his head slowly.

“No. Enough entirely.”

The meaning settled gradually.

Anakin stared at him.

“You’re serious?”

“You no longer need my instruction.”

A strange expression crossed Anakin’s face then — pride mixed with uncertainty, accomplishment mixed with loss.

For years, training had defined his life.

Now suddenly there was no next lesson waiting.

Satine, watching nearby from the balcony, smiled softly.

Obi-Wan stepped forward and rested a hand against Anakin’s shoulder.

“You’ve surpassed what I can teach you.”

Anakin immediately smirked.

“I knew it.”

Obi-Wan sighed. “I regret saying anything.”

But beneath the dry humor, emotion lingered heavily between them.

Neither fully knew how to articulate it.

Obi-Wan had not merely trained Anakin.

He had raised him.

—

The announcement came less than a week later.

Anakin entered the palace dining room with the unmistakable expression of someone who had already made a decision several hours ago.

Obi-Wan noticed it immediately.

“That face worries me.”

Satine looked up from her datapad. “Which face?”

“The heroic face.”

“That is unfortunately several of his faces.”

Anakin sat down across from them.

“I’m going to Tatooine.”

Silence.

Obi-Wan already knew where this was heading.

“My mother is still there,” Anakin continued. “I should’ve gone back years ago.”

Satine nodded carefully. “That’s understandable.”

“And while I’m there,” Anakin added, “I’m going to free the slaves.”

The silence afterward became catastrophic.

Obi-Wan closed his eyes immediately.

Satine stared at Anakin as though he had just announced plans to personally fistfight a supernova.

“You’re going to what?”

“The slaves,” Anakin repeated. “I can help them.”

Satine leaned forward slowly.

“Anakin… Tatooine is controlled by the Hutts.”

“So?”

“So the Hutts are not local criminals. They are an interstellar cartel with private armies, political influence, and enough money to destabilize sectors.”

Anakin crossed his arms.

“That doesn’t make slavery okay.”

“No one said it did.”

Obi-Wan finally spoke.

“How exactly are you planning to accomplish this?”

Anakin shrugged.

“I don’t know yet.”

That answer somehow worried them even more.

Satine stood and began pacing.

“Anakin, if you start openly attacking slave operations on Tatooine, the Hutts won’t simply surrender. They’ll retaliate. Against slaves. Against civilians. Against anyone connected to you.”

“So we do nothing?”

“No,” Satine snapped. “But there is a difference between helping people and charging blindly into a planetary crisis because you’re angry.”

“I’m not angry.”

Obi-Wan raised an eyebrow.

Anakin reconsidered.

“…Okay, I’m angry.”

“Reasonably angry,” Obi-Wan said. “Still dangerous.”

Anakin stood now too.

“You both taught me to help people.”

“We taught you not to confuse power with omnipotence,” Satine replied immediately.

The room grew tense.

For several long moments nobody spoke.

Finally Obi-Wan sighed deeply.

“We’re not stopping you, are we?”

“No.”

Satine rubbed her temples.

“Wonderful.”

Obi-Wan looked toward her.

“We should go with him.”

“Yes,” Satine said immediately. “Before he accidentally declares war on the entire Hutt Cartel.”

“I heard that.”

“You were meant to.”

At last Anakin relented enough to accept the condition.

They would accompany him.

Supervise him.

Attempt to prevent galactic catastrophe.

It was, all things considered, probably the best available outcome.

—

Mos Espa looked smaller to Anakin than it had in memory.

Not physically smaller.

Emotionally.

As a child, the city had felt enormous and inescapable, every alleyway and market corridor part of an entire universe. Now he walked through it as a grown man carrying a lightsaber beneath his robes, and suddenly the place seemed fragile.

Dusty.

Cruel in mundane ways.

Obi-Wan watched him carefully as they moved through crowded streets.

Anakin’s jaw tightened repeatedly at the sight of slaves wearing transmitter collars.

Satine noticed too.

“Easy,” she murmured quietly.

“I am being easy.”

“That sentence alone concerns me.”

Without hesitation, Anakin led them directly toward Watto’s old shop.

“She’s… free?”

Watto squinted suspiciously.

“You really didn’t know?”

The Lars homestead stood beneath Tatooine’s twin suns exactly as harsh and weathered as the surrounding desert.

Everything else vanished.

“You’re so tall.”

“You’re free.”

“Oh my! Master Anakin!”

“Threepio!”

“I hardly recognized you, sir! You’re enormous now!”

Introductions.

Explanations.

Obi-Wan mostly observed quietly, deeply relieved by what might have been avoided here.

For years he had feared what unresolved guilt over Shmi might someday become inside Anakin.

Owen nearly choked on his drink.

Cliegg stared at him in disbelief.

Beru looked horrified.

Even Threepio seemed distressed.

“Son, people survive Tatooine by understanding its limits.”

“That’s exactly the problem.”

“I have the power to help.”

Obi-Wan’s gaze remained steady.

Anakin looked at her angrily.

“You don’t know that.”

Satine stood slowly.

“No, Anakin. You don’t know that.”

“But I can do something.”

An ecosystem of power, money, fear, dependence, violence, and corruption extending far beyond one planet.

For the first time, Anakin truly confronted a painful reality:

Some problems could not be solved by one powerful person arriving with righteous intentions.

Even him.

Especially him.

Slowly, he sat back down.

Threepio cautiously tilted his head.

“Are you feeling better, sir?”

Anakin sighed heavily.

“No.”

Satine rested a hand against his shoulder anyway.

“Good,” she said softly. “That means you’re finally thinking seriously about it.”

# Part 5

The message from Coruscant arrived during a quiet evening in Sundari.

Anakin was halfway through dinner when the report appeared across the holoprojector: another assassination attempt against Senator Padmé Amidala of Naboo.

The room immediately had his full attention.

“She survived?” he asked sharply.

“Yes,” Satine said, scanning the incoming details. “Barely.”

The report showed the aftermath: a destroyed landing platform, smoke rising into Coruscant’s endless skyline, emergency crews surrounding twisted metal.

Several people had died.

Padmé had not been among them.

Anakin stared at the projection in disbelief.

“But she’s an angel,” he said.

Obi-Wan looked up from across the table.

Anakin shook his head, genuinely confused.

“Why would anybody want to assassinate her?”

Obi-Wan exchanged a glance with Satine before answering.

“Your Aunt Satine is my angel too.”

“That hasn’t stopped people from trying to kill her over the years.”

Anakin looked horrified.

“What?!”

Satine leaned back calmly. “Occupational hazard.”

“Who tried to kill you?”

“All kinds of people,” Obi-Wan said. “Political extremists. Traditionalist factions. People who think pacifism weakened Mandalore. Criminal interests unhappy with her reforms.”

Anakin blinked.

That genuinely startled him.

“But you’re… you.”

Satine smiled sadly.

“That doesn’t protect anyone from politics.”

Being good did not make someone safe.

Being loved did not make someone untouchable.

Angels could bleed.

The next morning, Anakin announced he was leaving for Coruscant.

“To help Padmé,” he clarified immediately.

Neither Obi-Wan nor Satine objected.

This was different from Tatooine.

Different from attempting to dismantle slavery by sheer force of will.

A targeted threat against a single person was something concrete. Immediate. Understandable.

Obi-Wan merely said, “Be careful.”

Satine added, “And try not to terrify the entire Senate.”

“No promises.”

“You sound exactly like your uncle.”

“I’m standing right here,” Obi-Wan said.

Coruscant felt overwhelming after years on Mandalore.

Anakin navigated the endless city-world with growing unease until finally reaching the secure apartment where Padmé was staying under Republic protection.

When she saw him enter, surprise washed instantly across her face.

“Anakin?”

For a brief moment neither moved.

Ten years had changed both of them completely.

The little slave boy from Tatooine now stood tall and broad-shouldered, carrying himself with calm confidence and a lightsaber at his belt.

The young Queen of Naboo had become a senator hardened by years of politics and compromise.

Yet somehow recognition remained immediate.

Anakin smiled softly.

“Hello, Padmé.”

She crossed the room toward him slowly, still staring.

“You came all the way from Mandalore?”

“I heard someone was trying to kill my childhood angel of Naboo.”

Then her expression softened.

“You remembered that?”

“I remember everything.”

The words landed with surprising weight between them.

Padmé recovered first.

“What are you doing here?”

Anakin’s expression sharpened slightly.

“Protecting you.”

That night, the kouhuns came.

Tiny deadly creatures slipping silently into Padmé’s apartment under cover of darkness.

Anakin sensed them instantly.

His lightsaber ignited blue across the darkened room, illuminating the insects mid-attack.

Two swift strikes.

The creatures fell smoking to the floor before reaching Padmé’s bed.

By the time security forces burst into the room moments later, the danger was already over.

Padmé sat upright beneath tangled blankets, staring at Anakin standing protectively between her and the window.

The blue glow reflected in her eyes.

“You really did come to protect me.”

Anakin glanced back at her.

“Yes.”

The following day, Chancellor Palpatine suggested Padmé leave Coruscant entirely.

“Naboo would be safer,” he said carefully during a Senate security meeting.

Anakin did not miss the brief flicker of recognition in Palpatine’s eyes when they encountered one another again.

Nor did Palpatine miss the coldness with which Anakin regarded him now.

Satine’s warnings still lingered sharply in his mind.

If Palpatine noticed the difference, he concealed it well.

Padmé agreed reluctantly to temporary seclusion.

Anakin accompanied her.

Away from Coruscant’s noise and danger, something between them began unfolding almost immediately.

Perhaps it had always been waiting.

Intense.

“You speak like a senator.”

Anakin smirked slightly.

“I hope not.”

“My Aunt Satine.”

Padmé blinked.

“The Duchess of Mandalore?”

Padmé laughed softly.

“That explains a lot.”

Anakin looked pleased with himself.

“She says centralized authority always becomes dangerous if nobody questions it.”

Padmé smiled thoughtfully.

“I think I’d like her.”

“You would.”

“I’d like to meet her someday.”

“You probably will.”

Neither fully realized then how important that promise would become.

Then the dreams began.

Fragments.

Pain.

Sand.

His mother calling out somewhere far away.

But the visions intensified rapidly until sleep itself became unbearable.

Padmé noticed immediately.

“You’re terrified,” she said quietly one night.

Anakin stared out across the dark Naboo lake.

“It’s my mother.”

Within days, they were en route to Tatooine.

The Lars homestead greeted them with grief before words.

Cliegg looked exhausted beyond description.

Owen grim.

Beru heartbroken.

Anakin already knew before anyone spoke.

“What happened?”

Cliegg lowered his gaze.

“Tuskens took her. Nearly a month ago.”

Something inside Anakin went dangerously still.

“She’s alive?”

“We don’t know.”

But Anakin already felt the answer.

Alone.

He tracked the raiders deep into the desert through Force instinct more than physical evidence.

When he found the camp, he found Shmi too.

Broken.

Weak.

Barely conscious.

Yet somehow still able to recognize him.

“Anakin…”

He caught her gently as she collapsed against him.

“I’m here, Mom. I’m here.”

For a few brief moments, she smiled.

Then she died in his arms beneath Tatooine’s burning suns.

The grief that followed nearly consumed him.

Anakin sat beside her body for a long time while rage gathered inside him like a storm.

The Tusken village lay nearby.

He could hear them moving.

Talking.

Living.

Something savage rose within him then — a terrible desire to make them suffer exactly as he was suffering now.

He imagined igniting his lightsaber and simply walking through the camp until nothing remained alive.

Men.

Women.

Children.

The thought horrified him even as part of him wanted it desperately.

His breathing became ragged.

His grip tightened around his saber.

Then another voice rose in memory.

Satine’s.

Never trust the part of yourself that believes pain grants permission.

Then Obi-Wan’s:

You are responsible for what you become when angry.

Anakin closed his eyes.

Slowly, painfully, he let the rage loosen enough for thought to return.

The gathered Tuskens immediately reacted with alarm.

Anakin’s voice carried across the camp coldly.

“Who took my mother?”

Only movement.

Warriors stepping forward aggressively.

Weapons raising.

Then one attacked.

Anakin moved instantly.

Blue light flashed.

The attacker fell.

Another charged.

Then another.

Anakin cut through several warriors effortlessly before finally stopping.

The remaining Tuskens had backed away now.

Afraid.

For the first time since arriving, Anakin truly saw them not as faceless monsters but as frightened people realizing they had awakened something vastly more dangerous than themselves.

The lesson had landed.

More violence suddenly felt unnecessary.

Without another word, he lifted Shmi’s body and walked away from the village.

No one followed.

At the funeral, the desert wind blew softly across freshly disturbed sand.

Anakin stood beside Owen, Beru, Cliegg, and Padmé while Threepio quietly wept electronic sorrow nearby.

Afterward, long after the others had gone inside, Anakin remained staring at the grave.

“She was an angel too,” he said quietly.

Padmé slipped her hand into his.

Anakin looked downward.

“And it still happened.”

For the first time in his life, he fully understood what Obi-Wan and Satine had tried to teach him for years.

Goodness did not guarantee safety.

Love did not prevent loss.

Power could not shield everyone.

Not even the people who mattered most.

“I have to tell them what happened.”

Padmé went with him.

Satine met them personally upon arrival in Sundari.

The moment she saw Anakin’s face, her own expression changed immediately.

Obi-Wan embraced him without a word.

Later, in private, Anakin told them everything.

The visions.

Shmi’s death.

The rage.

The village.

The killings.

“So you entered the camp intending revenge.”

“Yes.”

“And then?”

Anakin explained carefully.

The confrontation.

The attack.

Stopping himself.

Leaving.

By the end, Satine exhaled slowly.

“That could have gone much worse.”

Anakin looked ashamed.

“I wanted it to.”

Obi-Wan rested a hand against his shoulder.

“But it didn’t.”

That distinction mattered enormously.

Political discussions stretched late into evenings. They debated Senate corruption, militarization, trade inequities, local sovereignty, humanitarian reform.

The Duchess possessed fierce principles tempered by realism rather than naivety.

“Stay.”

Padmé looked at him quietly.

“Here?”

“With us.”

He hesitated slightly.

“With me.”

Padmé smiled softly.

By then neither of them truly wanted separation anymore.

And honestly, Mandalore felt safer than Naboo.

Safer than Coruscant.

Safer than a Republic steadily drifting toward instability.

So she stayed.

This time there were no secrets.

No hidden vows.

Obi-Wan stood proudly beside Anakin throughout the ceremony while Satine embraced Padmé afterward like family already long accepted.

For a brief moment, life seemed astonishingly full.

Then Geonosis happened.

Reports spread rapidly across the galaxy.

Separatist movements.

Count Dooku.

Massive droid armies.

A clone army suddenly unveiled by the Republic.

The Battle of Geonosis.

War.

The Clone Wars had begun.

Mandalore responded immediately.

Standing before assembled reporters and holocameras, Duchess Satine Kryze addressed the galaxy calmly and firmly.

“Mandalore will remain neutral in this conflict.”

Others praised it.

But Satine remained unmoved.

Afterward, gathered privately together within the palace, Obi-Wan, Anakin, Padmé, and Satine discussed the war deep into the night.

“The Jedi are generals now,” Obi-Wan said quietly, still sounding unsettled by the idea.

“The Senate handed emergency powers to the Chancellor,” Padmé added grimly.

Satine’s expression darkened immediately.

“Of course they did.”

Anakin looked between them all.

“So what do we do?”

Obi-Wan answered first.

“We stay out of it.”

Padmé nodded slowly.

“This war feels wrong.”

Satine looked toward the distant stars beyond Sundari’s dome.

“It was designed to.”

# Part 6

Mandalore remained outside the Clone Wars.

At least officially.

While the Republic and Separatists tore apart entire sectors, Sundari endured in tense neutrality beneath its great protective domes. Life inside the royal palace settled into an unusual rhythm: strategy discussions over breakfast, Senate reports arriving alongside military casualty counts, Obi-Wan and Anakin continuing their sparring sessions while Padmé and Satine argued politics nearby.

They had become less a household and more a strange little government unto themselves.

A former Jedi Knight.

A Mandalorian duchess.

A Naboo senator.

The most gifted Force-user of his generation.

And somewhere within all of it, an actual family.

But even from neutrality, the war crept closer every month.

Trade disruptions.

Refugees.

Mercenary activity spreading across neutral systems.

Criminal syndicates growing bolder under cover of galactic chaos.

Satine watched all of it with increasing concern.

Then came the report about Pre Vizsla.

The palace communications room fell silent as the details arrived.

Dead.

Killed in single combat.

Rumored killer: Darth Maul.

“…Didn’t I kill him on Naboo over a decade ago?”

Padmé looked up immediately.

“Yes. You told me about that duel after Qui-Gon’s funeral.”

Anakin frowned.

“I was there too. Well, mostly I was busy blowing up the droid control ship. But afterward everyone said he was dead.”

Obi-Wan folded his arms tightly.

“The dark side of the Force is mysterious.”

“Somehow he survived.”

Satine leaned against the table, troubled.

“If he’s leading Death Watch now—”

“He won’t stop there,” Obi-Wan interrupted quietly.

Anakin noticed something unusual then.

Fear.

Not panic.

Not helplessness.

But genuine concern in Obi-Wan’s eyes.

“We need to be careful moving forward,” Obi-Wan said.

Not ceremonially.

Not diplomatically.

She stormed into Sundari still wearing full armor and helmet, moving with such urgency that palace guards barely had time to announce her presence.

“Satine.”

Her voice echoed sharply through the corridor.

Satine immediately rose from her chair.

Bo-Katan removed her helmet.

Her expression alone told them the situation was catastrophic.

“Listen to me,” she said. “Death Watch has been hijacked. I’m done with them.”

Anakin blinked.

“Who’s this?”

Satine sighed softly.

“My sister.”

Anakin’s eyes widened slightly.

“The warrior sister you said you didn’t see eye to eye on?”

“Yes.”

Bo-Katan pointed impatiently.

“No time for this nonsense. Maul’s coming.”

Obi-Wan stepped forward immediately.

“How many?”

“The full Shadow Collective. Black Sun, Pykes, syndicates, mercenaries — everyone he’s gathered.”

Her voice hardened further.

“And Maul is strong.”

Obi-Wan’s expression turned cold.

“I know.”

Satine looked directly at her sister.

“You’re certain?”

“Yes.”

A long silence followed.

Then Satine nodded once.

“Against outsiders like Maul, we unite.”

Bo-Katan looked almost relieved.

“Yes.”

Obi-Wan unclipped his lightsaber from his belt.

“I’m never letting a Sith Lord take Mandalore.”

His voice carried quiet fury now.

“Not after everything we built here.”

From that day onward, Bo-Katan remained inside the palace under Satine’s protection.

The sisters still argued.

Constantly.

But beneath the arguments lingered something undeniable now: fear of losing one another before reconciliation fully became possible.

Maul came at dawn.

And he came like a war.

Shadow Collective forces descended upon Sundari from orbit while alarms screamed across the domed city. Mandalorian defenders scrambled into position as transports flooded the streets with armed syndicate troops.

The palace shook from distant explosions.

Anakin stood at one of the observation platforms beside Obi-Wan, watching smoke rise through Sundari.

“There’s too many of them.”

Satine’s face remained pale but steady.

“Mandalore doesn’t maintain a wartime military.”

Bo-Katan cursed under her breath.

“We can’t hold the whole city.”

The situation deteriorated rapidly.

Street after street fell under Shadow Collective occupation. Palace defenses became increasingly isolated while reports flooded in of civilian panic throughout Sundari.

Then Padmé had an idea.

“Naboo.”

Everyone turned toward her.

She activated a holocommunicator immediately.

Queen Neeyutnee answered within minutes.

“Senator Amidala?”

“Your Majesty, Mandalore is under attack. We need assistance immediately.”

Neeyutnee looked grim already.

“The Naboo security forces are heavily committed supporting Republic operations. We have almost nothing available.”

Padmé thought quickly.

“Even the Gungans?”

The Queen paused.

Then realization crossed her face.

A moment later, another figure joined the holocall: Boss Lyonie of the Gungans.

Padmé stepped forward urgently.

“Boss Lyonie, Mandalore requests aid.”

The Gungan leader blinked in surprise.

“Mandalore?”

“We are under attack by criminal syndicates led by a Sith Lord.”

That got his attention immediately.

Padmé continued:

“Naboo once stood with the Gungans when it mattered most. I’m asking now for that same alliance.”

Boss Lyonie looked toward Neeyutnee.

Then back toward Padmé.

Slowly, he nodded.

“Da Gungans help.”

A massive Mandalorian transport launched for Naboo almost immediately.

Anakin watched it prepare from the hangar and instantly saw the problem.

“That thing’s a flying target.”

Bo-Katan crossed her arms.

“Correct.”

“I’m escorting it.”

Padmé stepped beside him.

“I’m coming too.”

“No.”

“Yes.”

Anakin opened his mouth to argue.

Padmé folded her arms.

“You think I’m safer here while the palace is under siege?”

He hesitated.

“…Fair point.”

Minutes later, they launched together in a starfighter escort formation while the transport jumped toward Naboo.

Back in Sundari, the battle worsened dramatically.

Shadow Collective troops breached outer palace defenses.

Entire districts fell.

Then Darth Maul himself entered the palace.

The duel between Maul and Obi-Wan erupted through the grand halls with violent intensity.

Blue and crimson clashed across polished stone while guards scattered from the path of destruction.

Years of hatred fueled Maul.

Years of experience steadied Obi-Wan.

But Maul was no longer the reckless apprentice from Naboo.

He fought like something sharpened by survival itself.

Eventually Obi-Wan faltered.

Maul struck hard, disarming him and sending him crashing across the throne chamber floor.

Moments later, Shadow Collective soldiers dragged Satine in as well.

Maul stood over them with the Darksaber ignited.

Black energy hissed through the chamber.

“How poetic,” Maul said coldly. “Kenobi forced to watch helplessly once again.”

Obi-Wan struggled against his restraints furiously.

“Don’t.”

Maul smiled.

“I think I shall kill her now.”

He raised the Darksaber toward Satine.

Then—

“STOP!”

The chamber exploded with blue light.

Anakin burst through the upper entrance mid-leap, lightsaber igniting in his hands as he came down directly toward Maul.

Blue met black in a violent crash inches before the killing strike landed.

The impact shook the floor.

Maul staggered backward in genuine surprise.

Anakin landed between him and Satine immediately.

“You’re done.”

Outside, thunderous war cries suddenly echoed through Sundari’s streets.

The Gungans had arrived.

—

The duel began instantly.

Maul attacked aggressively, Darksaber spinning through savage arcs while Anakin met him head-on with powerful Djem So counters.

Outside the throne chamber, chaos raged.

Bo-Katan grabbed two blasters from fallen guards and tossed one toward Padmé.

Padmé caught it smoothly.

“You know how to use one?”

Padmé gave her a look.

“I’m from Naboo.”

Together, they locked down the palace entrance, holding off incoming Shadow Collective reinforcements with disciplined precision.

Meanwhile, inside the chamber, Anakin and Maul pushed each other harder than either expected.

“You fight well,” Maul snarled during a vicious exchange.

Anakin shoved him backward.

“I had a good teacher.”

Maul’s eyes burned yellow.

“I killed your master’s master.”

The words hit like venom.

“What makes you think I won’t kill your master?”

A heavy strike.

“Or you?”

Anakin’s blade held firm.

“Ataru is an unfavorable matchup against Juyo.”

Maul blinked.

Anakin pressed forward.

“Djem So is far more effective.”

“You think lightsaber theory is enough to stop me?!”

Anakin’s eyes hardened.

“Well,” he said calmly, “you will see when you are defeated.”

“Which will never happen!”

Anakin drove Maul backward across the chamber.

“Which WILL happen.”

Their blades locked violently.

“And it will happen today.”

The duel exploded into a final furious exchange.

Maul attacked with raw dark side ferocity.

Anakin met him with relentless precision.

Then finally—

An opening.

Tiny.

Brief.

Enough.

Anakin struck.

Maul froze.

The Darksaber slipped from his grasp.

A moment later, the former Sith Lord collapsed lifelessly to the floor.

This time, Anakin made absolutely certain there would be no survival.

No return.

No miracle resurrection from hatred alone.

Darth Maul was dead.

Forever.

Outside, the battle still raged.

Anakin freed Obi-Wan and Satine immediately.

The moment Obi-Wan stood, Satine embraced him fiercely.

Neither bothered pretending composure anymore.

Then Obi-Wan reignited his lightsaber.

“Let’s finish this.”

Nearby, Anakin picked up the fallen Darksaber and turned toward Bo-Katan.

“Here.”

She stared at it.

Then recoiled slightly.

“I can’t.”

“What?”

“I didn’t defeat him.”

Anakin blinked incredulously.

“Bo-Katan—”

“The Darksaber has rules.”

“This is not the time for traditionalist nonsense.”

She hesitated.

“You’re the rightful wielder now.”

Anakin shoved the hilt into her hand.

“We need to liberate Sundari NOW.”

Bo-Katan looked at the weapon.

Then at the burning city outside.

Finally she ignited it.

Black energy burst to life.

Her expression changed instantly.

“…Right. Fine. Let’s go.”

The counterattack swept through Sundari like a tidal wave.

Anakin.

Obi-Wan.

Bo-Katan.

Three lightsabers cutting through occupied streets while Gungan forces surged behind them in organized formations.

Padmé fought alongside them with blaster precision learned long ago during Naboo’s own battles.

The tide shifted almost immediately.

Shadow Collective forces began losing ground district by district.

Then the news spread.

Maul was dead.

Killed personally by Anakin Skywalker.

Shock rippled through enemy ranks.

Morale shattered.

Within hours, organized resistance collapsed entirely.

The Shadow Collective surrendered.

—

Celebration erupted across Sundari.

Citizens flooded the streets despite ongoing repairs. Gungans were cheered openly as heroes throughout the city. Mandalorians embraced warriors they had once viewed as strange outsiders.

For the first time in years, Mandalore felt united.

Even Bo-Katan stood publicly beside Satine during reconstruction efforts.

“I was complicit,” she admitted quietly one evening while watching repair crews rebuild damaged sectors.

Satine looked toward her sister.

“Manipulated, the clones were too.”

The information spread immediately.

Republic technicians, medics, and engineers across the galaxy began deactivating inhibitor chips en masse.

Within a week, surviving clone units returned fully to themselves.

Shock and guilt spread rapidly through the army as countless troopers realized what they had been forced to do.

The war was finally ending.

—

Chancellor Bail Organa moved quickly.

Very quickly.

A peace treaty between the Republic and the Confederacy of Independent Systems was negotiated within weeks. After years of catastrophic warfare, both sides accepted exhaustion over annihilation.

The Republic formally recognized the CIS as a legitimate sovereign state controlling the systems it held at the moment peace was signed.

The Clone Wars were over.

Kamino’s cloning facilities were eventually shut down permanently.

Formal clone rights legislation followed soon afterward. Every clone trooper received legal personhood and citizenship protections.

Sweeping constitutional reforms reshaped the Republic itself.

Emergency powers were drastically restricted.

Executive authority weakened.

Democratic safeguards expanded.

Systems gained stronger protections against centralized abuse.

For the first time in years, the Republic began trying to heal instead of conquer.

A fragile bipolar peace emerged between the Republic and CIS.

Tense.

Uneasy.

But stable enough to inspire hope.

After three years of devastating war, very few in the galaxy wanted another conflict anytime soon.

—

The Jedi Order changed too.

Profoundly.

Yoda led the survivors through painful internal reform. The Temple was rebuilt slowly while surviving Jedi regrouped from across the galaxy.

Padawans who had lost masters were reassigned to surviving Knights and Masters grieving losses of their own.

The Jedi Council temporarily shrank from twelve members to six simply because too few Masters remained willing to serve.

More importantly, the Order confronted uncomfortable truths about itself.

How deeply it had become entangled in politics.

How willingly it had transformed into a military institution.

How blind it had become to the manipulations surrounding it.

The Jedi pledged never again to become soldiers for the Republic.

Yoda himself changed perhaps most of all.

In quiet moments, he often reflected on Qui-Gon Jinn.

On the Living Force.

On instincts the old Order had ignored for too long.

Slowly, the Jedi began rediscovering parts of themselves long buried beneath centuries of rigid doctrine.

—

Years later, the galaxy still carried scars.

Entire worlds remained devastated.

Families remained broken.

Nothing erased the suffering completely.

But there was peace.

Real peace.

And on Mandalore, inside the rebuilt royal palace of Sundari, life continued.

Anakin and Padmé remained together.

Obi-Wan and Satine ruled wisely side by side.

Bo-Katan served Mandalore loyally while maintaining her own fierce identity.

Sometimes Yoda visited.

Occasionally Bail arrived exhausted from Republic politics and stayed just long enough to recover his sanity before returning to Coruscant.

The palace remained what it had slowly become over the years:

A home.

Not perfect.

Not simple.

But whole.

And when Anakin occasionally stood atop Sundari’s towers at sunset, blue lightsaber at one side and the Darksaber at the other, he sometimes thought about how close the galaxy had come to darkness.

Then he would hear laughter somewhere inside the palace behind him.

Family.

Alive.

Safe.

And he would turn around and go back inside.

The end.
