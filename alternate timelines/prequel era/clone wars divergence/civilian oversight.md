# Part 1

The opening months of the Clone Wars forced the Galactic Republic to confront a problem it had never truly prepared for.

The Grand Army of the Republic had been created with astonishing speed. Millions of clone troopers now stood ready to fight across thousands of worlds, while the Jedi Order accepted military commissions as generals in order to coordinate the Republic's defense against the Confederacy of Independent Systems.

Yet the Senate was deeply divided over one question.

Who truly commanded the army?

The Jedi possessed centuries of experience in leadership, diplomacy, and combat, but they were neither elected nor directly accountable to the citizens of the Republic. Many senators feared creating an army whose highest authority rested entirely in the hands of an ancient religious order.

For days, debate echoed through the Senate Rotunda.

"The Republic has always been governed by civilian authority," Senator Fang Zar declared. "We cannot abandon that principle simply because we now face war."

Another senator rose.

"None question the honor of the Jedi. But military power without civilian oversight is dangerous regardless of who possesses it."

Others objected.

"The Jedi are already leading troops into battle. We cannot have politicians giving orders from Coruscant while battles rage light-years away."

Then Bail Organa stood.

"There is a compromise."

The chamber quieted.

"The Jedi possess the military expertise. The Senate represents the will of the Republic's citizens. Let them serve together."

He continued calmly.

"Assign every Jedi General a senator. The senator shall accompany the Jedi throughout the war and remain with that command. The senator shall represent the Republic's civilian government, oversee military decisions from the political perspective, and possess formal authority within the chain of command. The Jedi General shall direct military operations, advise the senator, and command the army in the field."

A murmur spread through the Senate.

Some senators exchanged uncertain looks.

Another asked, "You mean senators...on the front lines?"

"If we demand civilian oversight," Bail answered, "then oversight must be more than signatures on reports. It must be present where decisions are made."

The proposal grew over the following days.

The final legislation established a unique command structure.

Every Jedi General would be permanently paired with a senator.

They would live together, travel together, campaign together, and remain with the same military formation throughout the war.

The senator would formally outrank the Jedi General in the Republic's military chain of command, representing civilian authority over the armed forces.

The Jedi General would remain responsible for directing battles, advising the senator on military matters, and leading the clone forces.

To reduce friction, each Jedi and senator would be allowed some influence over whom they wished to serve alongside whenever practical.

The measure passed.

Not everyone celebrated.

Inside the Jedi Temple, some Masters questioned whether placing politicians directly into military campaigns was wise.

Others argued that civilian oversight reflected one of the Republic's oldest ideals.

Master Yoda simply nodded.

"If together they serve the Republic, stronger perhaps both shall become."

Soon afterward, pairings began across the Republic.

One of the first requests came from Bail Organa.

He requested General Obi-Wan Kenobi.

When informed, Obi-Wan smiled.

"I was hoping he might ask."

Later that afternoon, Bail visited the Temple.

The two men met in a quiet courtyard overlooking Coruscant.

"It appears we're partners now," Bail said with a smile.

"So it does."

"I've watched your work for years, General."

"And I've admired your integrity in the Senate."

Bail folded his hands behind his back.

"We won't always agree."

"I would be disappointed if we did."

Bail laughed.

"I suspect you'll argue every military point."

"I probably will."

"And I'll argue every political one."

Obi-Wan nodded.

"That sounds like an excellent arrangement."

Bail extended his hand.

"Then let us keep each other honest."

Obi-Wan shook it.

"For the Republic."

Their command structure became one of the first officially organized under the new system.

Senator Bail Organa stood at the top.

Below him served Jedi General Obi-Wan Kenobi.

Under Obi-Wan came Clone Commander Cody.

Then the clone troopers of the 212th Attack Battalion.

Everyone involved quickly realized why the arrangement had been created.

Bail never attempted to micromanage military tactics.

Obi-Wan never ignored the broader political consequences of victory.

Each respected the other's expertise.

Each understood where the other's authority began.

Elsewhere in the Temple, another pairing unfolded rather differently.

Padmé Amidala arrived carrying several datapads filled with newly approved regulations.

She found Anakin Skywalker outside one of the hangars, overseeing maintenance on his starfighter.

He looked up immediately.

"Padmé."

She smiled.

"General Skywalker."

"Don't call me that."

"It's your rank now."

"I still don't like how it sounds."

She walked over.

"The Senate finished the assignments."

"I heard."

Silence lingered for a moment.

Then Anakin looked directly at her.

"Padmé, let's work together in this war."

She stared at him for several seconds.

"I expected you to say that."

"So?"

She lowered her datapads slightly.

"Anakin, I'm happy to work with you, but..."

He tilted his head.

"I think you might regret this."

"Why?"

A faint smile appeared on her face.

"If we go through with this, you will see."

He folded his arms.

"Are you sure?"

She searched his expression one last time.

"Definitely."

She sighed.

"Alright then."

Anakin grinned so broadly that she couldn't help smiling back.

"Good."

"You sound far happier than someone about to spend months being supervised."

"I don't need supervising."

"You most certainly do."

"I do not."

She raised an eyebrow.

"You flew directly through a droid blockade over Geonosis."

"It worked."

"You jumped out of a moving transport."

"It worked."

"You challenged Count Dooku."

"...That one didn't work."

Padmé nodded.

"My point exactly."

Anakin rubbed the back of his neck.

"I suppose you'll remind me of these things often."

"Oh, constantly."

He laughed.

"I think I'm beginning to understand what you meant."

"You haven't even begun."

Within days, their command was officially established.

The chain of command placed Senator Padmé Amidala at its head.

Immediately beneath her served Jedi General Anakin Skywalker.

Under Anakin came his newly assigned Padawan, Ahsoka Tano.

Below Ahsoka stood Captain Rex.

Then came the clone troopers of the 501st Legion.

When Captain Rex received the finalized documentation, he read it twice before handing it back to Commander Appo.

"So Senator Amidala outranks General Skywalker."

"Looks that way."

Rex scratched his chin.

"I wonder how that's going to work."

Nearby, Ahsoka had just arrived and overheard.

"What are you two talking about?"

Rex handed her the datapad.

She scanned the chain of command.

"Huh."

"What do you think?" Rex asked.

She smiled mischievously.

"I think General Skywalker's about to discover that battlefield victories don't help much in arguments."

Rex blinked.

"Why?"

"You'll see."

The opportunity came sooner than anyone expected.

Their first deployment together involved liberating a Republic supply station that had fallen under Separatist attack.

Inside the flagship's briefing room, holographic maps filled the air.

Anakin pointed toward the enemy flank.

"We strike here."

Padmé examined the projection.

"What's here?"

"The fastest route."

She zoomed the image outward.

"And these buildings?"

"Civilian housing."

"Occupied?"

Anakin hesitated.

"...Probably."

Padmé looked at him.

"If we attack there, civilians will be caught in the fighting."

"We'll end the battle faster."

"And create a humanitarian disaster."

"It gives us the advantage."

"It also gives the Separatists a propaganda victory."

Anakin frowned.

"They're using civilians as cover."

"Which means we don't help them by proving their accusations."

The room became quiet.

Rex carefully studied the map while pretending not to listen.

Ahsoka crossed her arms with unmistakable interest.

Padmé tapped another section of the map.

"What about this route?"

"It's longer."

"How much longer?"

"Maybe two hours."

"And casualties?"

Anakin looked more closely.

"...Probably fewer."

"Civilian casualties?"

"...Much fewer."

Padmé folded her arms.

"Then that's our route."

Anakin opened his mouth.

Then closed it again.

She smiled.

"General?"

He exhaled.

"Yes...Senator."

Ahsoka immediately looked away to hide her grin.

After Padmé left the room to coordinate evacuation efforts with local officials, Ahsoka leaned toward Anakin.

"You know she's right."

"I know."

"You were still going to argue."

"I was."

"Why?"

"Because that's what I do."

She nodded thoughtfully.

"Fair enough."

Rex cleared his throat.

"If I may, General."

"Go ahead."

"I've served with Jedi for a while now."

"And?"

"I don't think I've ever seen anyone give one an order."

Anakin looked toward the door through which Padmé had departed.

A smile slowly spread across his face.

"She just did."

# Part 2

As the Clone Wars spread from sector to sector, the Republic's experiment with civilian oversight settled into daily routine.

Every campaign, every deployment, every fleet movement reinforced the new principle: senators and Jedi generals would command together.

Some partnerships became famous for their efficiency.

Others became famous simply because everyone wondered how they had not strangled each other yet.

Among the most respected was the command led by Senator Bail Organa and Jedi General Obi-Wan Kenobi.

Neither believed that authority alone guaranteed wisdom.

Whenever they disagreed, they argued their positions plainly, listened carefully, and accepted the final decision without resentment.

During one campaign on a Mid Rim colony, the Republic had broken the Separatist lines. Commander Cody's troops were prepared to continue pursuing the retreating droid army.

The command staff gathered around the tactical display.

Bail studied the reports arriving from the planet's surface.

"The evacuation convoys are behind schedule."

Obi-Wan looked at the retreating enemy formations.

"Commander Cody has an opportunity to press the attack."

Bail folded his arms.

"General, I believe we should prioritize the evacuation."

Obi-Wan remained silent for several moments, weighing the military situation.

"Militarily I'd prefer to continue the pursuit..."

He looked toward Bail.

"...but I believe your reasoning is sound, Senator."

Bail nodded.

"Thank you."

Obi-Wan activated his comlink.

"I'll inform Commander Cody."

"Cody here."

"Commander, we're suspending pursuit."

There was only the briefest pause.

"Understood, General."

"Our priority is escorting the evacuation."

"It will be done."

The order was carried out without complaint.

Neither Bail nor Obi-Wan viewed the conversation as a victory over the other.

It was simply how they worked.

Elsewhere in the Republic, another command was achieving equally impressive military results through considerably less peaceful discussions.

Official reports praised Senator Padmé Amidala and General Anakin Skywalker for their effectiveness.

Mission objectives were consistently achieved.

Republic forces under their command earned an enviable operational record.

The 501st Legion developed a reputation for impossible victories.

From the outside, they appeared to be one of the Republic's strongest command teams.

Inside briefing rooms, transports, command centers, and temporary headquarters, however, the atmosphere was often much less harmonious.

Several months into the war, intelligence located Count Dooku near a Separatist logistics hub.

The Republic operation had one primary objective.

Evacuate thousands of trapped refugees before the enemy could overrun the city.

As civilians boarded transports, Anakin watched the tactical display.

Dooku's escape route narrowed by the minute.

He looked toward Padmé.

"I have Dooku trapped. Give me permission to pursue."

Padmé did not even look up from the evacuation reports.

"Negative."

Anakin blinked.

"...What?"

"Our mission is to evacuate the refugees."

"We'll never get another chance."

Padmé finally turned to face him.

"General Skywalker, that is my decision."

Frustration escaped before Anakin could stop it.

"You're my wife."

Padmé's expression remained perfectly composed.

"Right now I'm Senator Amidala, your civilian commander. So you have to listen."

Several seconds passed.

Anakin slowly nodded.

"...Yes, Senator."

Padmé's expression softened just enough.

"Besides..."

She gave him an almost amused look.

"Even if I wasn't, 'you're my wife' isn't helping your case."

Anakin remained silent.

"What did you think marriage was?" she asked. "Me agreeing with you on everything?"

He had no answer.

She smiled faintly.

"There's a reason most people don't marry each other less than a month after meeting."

She returned to the tactical display.

"You don't seem to understand it yet."

Another transport icon disappeared from the map as it successfully escaped.

"Soon I think you will."

Anakin watched the display in silence while Dooku's signal finally vanished into hyperspace.

Some weeks later, another operation ended with another argument.

The mission had succeeded.

The objective had been secured.

No civilians had been lost.

No clone casualties had exceeded projections.

By every measurable standard, it had been an excellent day.

Anakin was still furious.

Back aboard their flagship, the briefing room doors closed.

He turned toward Padmé.

"Why won't you allow me to do anything??!"

She looked up from the after-action reports.

"Because your ideas are absolutely terrible sometimes and if they fail they will get all of us—yes, the clones too—into trouble."

"They won't fail!"

"How do you know that?"

Anakin hesitated.

"...Jedi intuition, I guess?"

Padmé sighed.

"And as a senator I cannot allow you to make decisions based on intuition alone."

She set the datapad aside.

"Even if you think it will work, if it fails the cost is too high for us to bear."

"Then we can never get anything done!"

Silence settled over the room.

Padmé leaned back in her chair.

"Anakin..."

Her voice became quieter.

"Ever since our conversation on Naboo during the picnic... where you told me that someone wise should make all the decisions, I knew that we don't think alike."

He looked away.

"I was going to address it, but we never had the chance."

She glanced toward the viewport, where clone technicians moved across the hangar below.

"Once Geonosis started, the galaxy became a blur of motion and war."

She looked back at him.

"But after the war, we have to talk about this."

"I want you to understand that the way you think and the way you make decisions..."

She chose her words carefully.

"...is actually quite problematic."

Anakin said nothing.

"You don't know me very well."

Her tone remained calm rather than accusing.

"You saw me for a few minutes on Tatooine and concluded that I was an angel."

"You saw me again for a few days after 10 years and decided you wanted to marry me."

"Then not even a month later you decided you want to be paired with me for the war."

She folded her arms.

"You think I will support your decisions and we will have a great time working together."

A small, sympathetic smile crossed her face.

"Ani, that's... quite a stretch of a conclusion to make after knowing me for just a few months."

He shifted uncomfortably.

"If the picnic conversation gave you any hints, it's that I don't approve of the way you think."

"And I never will."

She walked over and stood directly in front of him.

"So please stop thinking you will have an easy time here."

"I will continue to veto your decisions that threaten our objectives or put our people in harm."

"I will continue to oppose the reckless things you often do."

Then her expression brightened.

She reached out and playfully poked his arm.

"And you will continue to listen to me."

Anakin looked at the finger pressing lightly against his sleeve.

"You picked me to work with you; now you bear the consequences of that choice and you don't get to complain about it."

Despite himself, a reluctant smile tugged at the corner of his mouth.

"You're enjoying this."

"A little."

"A little?"

"Maybe more than a little."

Months passed.

The arguments never truly stopped.

Neither did the victories.

Following another successful campaign, the two of them found themselves walking through one of the Republic cruiser's quiet corridors after the evening briefing.

The day's reports had finally been filed.

No one else was nearby.

Anakin let out a long sigh.

"You know..."

Padmé looked over.

"You were right."

"Oh?"

"Maybe I should have picked a senator who doesn't have such strong opinions on everything and who would just let me do whatever I want."

Padmé laughed.

"I am sure 'pushover' senators are plentiful in the Senate."

"But that would defeat the point of 'civilian oversight,' Anakin."

"The point is precisely so you are NOT able to do whatever you want."

He shrugged.

"Still I would be able to fulfill all the mission objectives."

"Just in a way that suits my tastes better."

Padmé smiled knowingly.

"Do you realize that weak-willed senators can't actually be paired with Jedi generals?"

He looked at her.

"What?"

"We all had to go through Jedi mind trick resistance screening."

She continued walking.

"Only the ones that passed are allowed to be paired with a Jedi General."

"Otherwise a single mind trick is used and the whole chain of command and idea of civilian oversight would break down."

She shrugged lightly.

"So even if you went looking for a less opinionated senator, you won't find any pushovers."

"I know."

He sighed again.

"Still I wish you would just let me do things my way sometimes."

"And get yourself and your clones hurt?"

She looked directly at him.

"And get far more civilians killed than necessary?"

"Just to chase Dooku or Grievous?"

Anakin spread his hands.

"People are starting to call me the 'Hero With No Fear.'"

"I've got to live up to that reputation, you know."

Padmé stopped walking.

"And that's what precisely worries me."

She spoke gently.

"'No fear' is not necessarily a good thing."

She searched his face.

"Look what you did the first time you encountered Dooku."

"You ran up without a second thought and got yourself struck by lightning."

"Then you ended up with your arm cut off."

Anakin rubbed his mechanical forearm.

"Okay..."

He grimaced.

"That was maybe too reckless."

"I think I have learned since then."

Padmé's expression softened for only a moment before becoming serious again.

"And I definitely don't trust you enough to let you do whatever you want."

She met his eyes.

"You literally massacred an entire village of Tuskens."

Anakin's face fell.

She continued quietly.

"On some level I am glad that you wanted to be paired with me."

"So I can at least monitor and control some of your worst impulses."

He looked genuinely frustrated.

"But I don't like to be controlled!"

"I want to have freedom over my decisions."

"I know."

Her answer came immediately.

"But the Senate doesn't want the Jedi generals to have absolute freedom over their decisions for a good reason."

She stepped closer.

"And for you, specifically..."

She gently poked him in the shoulder once more.

"...there are even better reasons."

Anakin looked down at the finger against his tunic before meeting her eyes again.

This time, he did not argue.

# Part 3

As the war continued, Supreme Chancellor Palpatine maintained his longstanding habit of inviting Anakin Skywalker to private conversations.

Sometimes they discussed battles.

Sometimes politics.

Sometimes the burdens of command.

For years, Anakin had found it easy to speak openly with the Chancellor. During his years as Obi-Wan Kenobi's Padawan, he had often vented his frustrations over being held back, denied opportunities, or ordered to exercise patience when he wanted action.

Palpatine had always listened.

He had expected those conversations to continue.

Instead, he found something had changed.

One afternoon, after another Republic victory, Anakin arrived at the Chancellor's office looking noticeably tired.

Palpatine poured two cups of tea.

"You've had another successful campaign."

"We accomplished the mission."

"And yet you don't seem especially pleased."

Anakin shrugged.

"It was a long week."

Palpatine watched him carefully.

He decided to test the waters.

"Senator Amidala is holding you back, isn't she?"

The reaction was immediate.

Anakin's head snapped upward.

"These are private disagreements between her and me."

His voice rose sharply.

"You stay out of this!!"

Silence filled the office.

Palpatine had expected agreement.

Perhaps irritation.

Perhaps an invitation to continue.

Instead he found himself looking at a young Jedi whose expression made it unmistakably clear that he had crossed a line.

After several moments, Anakin looked away.

"...Sorry."

Palpatine smiled pleasantly.

"No apology is necessary."

But inwardly, he was already reconsidering everything.

Years earlier, Anakin would readily complain about Obi-Wan's caution.

Now Senator Amidala was imposing even tighter restraints upon him than Obi-Wan ever had.

Yet Anakin would not discuss those disagreements.

Not with him.

The Chancellor did not raise the subject again that afternoon.

Some days later, Anakin returned for another meeting.

This time Palpatine chose his words much more carefully.

They spoke first about fleet deployments and supply routes before the Chancellor allowed the conversation to drift naturally.

"Senator Amidala has only the Republic's interests at heart."

Anakin nodded.

"She does."

Palpatine continued in a sympathetic tone.

"It must simply be... difficult for a man of your abilities to be restrained by procedure."

Anakin let out a slow sigh.

"Yes."

For a moment Palpatine thought he had finally found the opening he wanted.

Then Anakin continued.

"Sometimes I do feel restrained."

Another pause.

"But I let her restrain me."

He looked down at his cup.

"It's probably for the good of both of us."

Palpatine remained outwardly composed.

"I see."

Anakin smiled faintly.

"I don't enjoy it."

"I imagine not."

"But she's usually thinking about consequences that I wasn't."

He stood.

"I should get back."

"Of course."

After Anakin departed, the Chancellor remained standing beside the window overlooking Coruscant.

His expression did not change.

But his thoughts did.

Padmé Amidala possessed far more influence over Anakin Skywalker than he had anticipated.

Not merely as a senator.

Not merely as a commander.

Anakin was accepting restraints from her that he rejected from almost everyone else.

The realization was deeply unsettling.

Elsewhere, aboard the flagship of the 501st, another person had discovered something about Anakin.

Ahsoka Tano had learned that disagreeing with her Master could be entertaining.

Agreeing with Senator Amidala instead was even more effective.

During one planning session, Anakin pointed toward a mountain pass.

"We attack through here."

Padmé shook her head.

"Too exposed."

"I disagree."

"So do I," Ahsoka said.

Anakin looked at her.

"You haven't even heard my full explanation."

"I've heard enough."

"You have not."

Padmé folded her arms.

"I agree with Ahsoka."

Anakin looked between the two of them.

"This is mutiny."

"No," Ahsoka replied cheerfully. "This is consensus."

Later that week, another discussion ended similarly.

"I think we should split the battalion."

"I disagree," Padmé said.

"So do I," Ahsoka added.

Anakin stared at his Padawan.

"You didn't even hesitate."

"I've been learning."

"Learning what?"

Ahsoka smiled.

"That siding with Senator Amidala instead of siding with you is a really effective way to make you angry."

"It absolutely is."

"Good."

"You realize I'm your Master."

"I do."

"I could assign you extra lightsaber drills."

Padmé looked up from her datapad.

"I overrule that."

Anakin threw his hands into the air.

"See?"

Ahsoka grinned.

"I told you it worked."

Despite the exasperation in his voice, Anakin never punished her for it.

Partly because she was Ahsoka.

Partly because Padmé could simply overrule him anyway.

The arguments became part of the rhythm of their command.

Then, one afternoon, Anakin finally went too far.

Republic intelligence had located General Grievous.

For the first time in months, the Separatist commander appeared vulnerable.

The command room buzzed with tactical analysis.

Anakin pointed toward the holographic display.

"No, listen. We have him boxed in."

Padmé studied the projected movements.

"No, that sounds like a trap."

"It isn't."

"Don't go pursue Grievous."

"But Grievous is a menace!"

He pointed at the map again.

"We have the manpower to stop him for once..."

Padmé remained unmoved.

"Alright."

She folded her arms.

"We have discussed this for 30 minutes already."

She looked directly at him.

"Clearly you can't convince me, so maybe just stop trying and spend your energy elsewhere."

She gathered her datapads.

"If you want me to change my mind, somebody else will have to convince me otherwise."

She left the room.

Anakin remained staring at the tactical display.

Somebody else...

Who could possibly change Padmé's mind?

He thought through every name he knew.

Other Jedi.

Other senators.

Clone officers.

Nothing seemed promising.

Then another idea occurred to him.

He activated a long-range communication channel.

The Naberrie household on Naboo answered.

Ruwee Naberrie appeared first, with Jobal Naberrie joining him a moment later.

"Anakin!" Jobal smiled warmly.

"It's good to see you."

"You too."

Ruwee noticed his expression.

"Is something wrong?"

"I need to ask a little favour from you."

The couple exchanged a glance.

"What is it?"

"I believe we currently have General Grievous in a position where we can take him out."

He immediately launched into an explanation.

"First, our reconnaissance shows his fleet is isolated."

"Second, our forces already control the surrounding hyperspace routes."

"Third, if we move immediately, we have enough troops to cut off his retreat."

He continued outlining his reasoning for several minutes.

"If we succeed, we could be much closer to ending the war."

He finally took a breath.

"But Padmé doesn't agree and says it's a bad idea."

He smiled hopefully.

"Could you help me convince her to let us pursue Grievous?"

Ruwee slowly looked toward Jobal.

She looked back at him.

Several silent seconds passed.

Finally Ruwee answered carefully.

"We... can try our best."

Jobal nodded.

"We will see what we can do."

Later that day, Padmé received an incoming call.

She accepted it immediately.

"Mother? Father?"

Jobal smiled.

"Hello, dear."

Ruwee looked slightly uncomfortable.

"Anakin called us to ask if we can change your mind about General Grievous..."

Padmé blinked.

"...Hang on."

She muted the transmission.

Then she turned toward the corridor.

Her voice echoed through the command deck.

"GENERAL ANAKIN SKYWALKER, GET YOUR ENTIRE TORSO OVER HERE RIGHT NOW!"

Clone troopers nearby very wisely found urgent reasons to be somewhere else.

Ahsoka quietly pretended to become fascinated by a nearby console.

Anakin emerged from another room almost immediately.

One look at Padmé's face told him everything.

He walked over with obvious caution.

"...What's up?"

Padmé stared at him.

"Did you really call my PARENTS to try and get them to help you change my mind?"

She threw both hands into the air.

"Are you serious?"

"Are you out of your mind?"

Anakin rubbed the back of his neck.

"...You told me to find someone else to convince you."

He offered an uncertain smile.

"I merely did what you asked."

Padmé closed her eyes for a long moment.

When she spoke again, her voice was calm.

Almost too calm.

"Well obviously I mean people actually involved in the operation."

She began counting on her fingers.

"Other generals."

"Other senators."

"Clone commanders."

"Intel officers."

"Et cetera."

She pointed emphatically.

"Not my parents."

"They barely follow the war."

"They have no clue what you are actually talking about."

"They barely even know who General Grievous is."

She looked at him in complete disbelief.

"How could you think recruiting them is the right idea?"

Anakin opened his mouth.

"I—"

Before he could continue, Padmé's expression shifted.

Understanding suddenly crossed her face.

"Oh."

She nodded slowly.

"I know."

She folded her arms.

"You asked them because you precisely know that they know nothing about the actual situation and thus you can easily convince them that you're in the right and make them support you."

She took one step closer.

"Don't you?"

Anakin froze.

"You think they are clueless and gullible enough that the instant you say 'we should pursue Grievous' they would just agree with you?"

He said nothing.

"And then you think they will be able to convince me because they are my parents and I listen to my parents?"

She shook her head.

"Oh, I can see through you, Anakin."

"I know exactly why you did what you did."

He lowered his eyes.

"I..."

"You made a mistake."

Her voice remained steady.

"A big one."

She held his gaze.

"I will be crystal clear about this."

"Never attempt that again."

"This is NOT cool."

"You need to learn some boundaries."

The silence that followed was far longer than any of their usual arguments.

When Padmé finally unmuted the call, she offered her parents an apologetic smile.

"I'm sorry about that."

Ruwee smiled gently.

"No apology necessary."

Jobal looked toward the edge of the screen, where Anakin was still standing quietly out of frame.

"We had a feeling there was probably more to this than we understood."

Padmé nodded.

"There was."

She glanced briefly toward Anakin before returning her attention to her parents.

"We'll handle it."

# Part 4

Months of campaigning eventually brought two of the Republic's most successful commands together.

The 212th Attack Battalion under Senator Bail Organa and General Obi-Wan Kenobi joined forces with the 501st Legion under Senator Padmé Amidala and General Anakin Skywalker for a major offensive against a fortified Separatist position.

The operation required constant coordination between the two commands.

Obi-Wan and Anakin handled the battlefield tactics.

Bail and Padmé coordinated logistics, diplomacy with local authorities, evacuation plans, and the broader strategic objectives expected by the Senate.

The campaign ended in a decisive Republic victory.

That evening, with reports finally completed and the fleet preparing to depart, the four commanders gathered in one of the flagship's conference rooms.

For once, no holographic battle maps floated above the table.

No urgent transmissions interrupted them.

Four years after Leia's rescue, Obi-Wan's Force spirit helped guide Luke and Leia toward a remarkable realization.

They were brother and sister.

The revelation answered one question while raising another.

Who were their parents?

At first, they found nothing.

Records had vanished.

Witnesses had died.

The Empire had erased countless traces of the past.

Only after the Galactic Civil War ended and peace returned did they finally have the freedom to devote themselves fully to the search.

The breakthrough came from an unexpected source.

R2-D2.

Unlike C-3PO, whose memory had been erased decades earlier, R2 still retained everything he had witnessed.

Luke and Leia carefully sifted through years upon years of archived recordings.

Battlefields.

Senate buildings.

Republic cruisers.

Hidden conversations.

Gradually, fragments assembled into a complete picture.

Their parents were named Anakin Skywalker and Padmé Amidala.

Neither of them had died when the Empire rose.

They had simply disappeared.

With renewed determination, Luke and Leia began searching the galaxy.

They followed every lead.

Asked every surviving witness.

Visited forgotten hideaways and old Rebel contacts.

News of their search eventually reached the very people they sought.

When Anakin learned what his children were doing, he sat quietly for a long time.

Padmé reached across the table and took his hand.

"I think..."

She smiled gently.

"...it's finally time."

He nodded.

"I think so too."

Rather than continue hiding, they reached out.

The meeting took place in a quiet place far from politics and ceremony.

Luke arrived first.

Leia stood beside him.

R2-D2 rolled forward with a series of excited whistles.

C-3PO stopped beside him.

"Oh my."

Anakin stepped into view.

Padmé followed.

For several seconds, nobody moved.

Twenty-five years.

Twenty-five years of separation.

Twenty-five years of wondering.

Then Luke crossed the distance first.

Padmé embraced him tightly.

A moment later Leia joined them.

Anakin wrapped his arms around all three of them.

No one wanted to let go.

Much later, once everyone had settled, Padmé quietly explained everything.

She spoke about the Empire's rise.

The failed attempt to hide safely on Naboo.

The attack that forced them to flee.

The meeting where Obi-Wan, Yoda, Bail, Ahsoka, and Rex convinced them that remaining together would endanger the twins.

She described the terrible decision to entrust Luke to the Lars family and Leia to Bail and Breha Organa.

Her voice occasionally trembled.

"It was the hardest decision we ever made."

Leia reached across and gently squeezed her mother's hand.

"I believe you."

Anakin then explained the years that followed.

"We wanted to find you."

"More times than I can count."

He looked at Luke.

"I wanted to know whether you were safe."

Then toward Leia.

"I wanted to know whether you were happy."

He smiled sadly.

"But every time..."

"We stopped ourselves."

"We believed that contacting either of you would put you in danger."

Luke nodded slowly.

"I understand."

Leia nodded as well.

"So do I."

She looked between them.

"We don't resent either of you."

"You made the best decision you could."

Luke smiled.

"You gave us a chance to survive."

Anakin lowered his head.

"Thank you."

As the weeks became months, the distance between them steadily disappeared.

One evening Anakin finally said what he had wanted to say since the reunion.

"We'd like to offer you something."

Luke looked up.

"What?"

"A family."

Silence followed.

Luke and Leia exchanged a glance.

The deaths of Owen and Beru had left a wound that never truly healed.

The destruction of Alderaan had taken Bail and Breha from Leia forever.

Neither of them had expected to find parents again.

Leia smiled first.

"I'd like that."

Luke nodded.

"So would I."

Over time, the family continued to grow.

Anakin and Padmé introduced Luke and Leia to Ahsoka.

"This is the little Skywalker I've heard so much about," Ahsoka teased.

Luke laughed.

"I've heard a lot about you too."

"And this," Anakin said with a smile, "is Captain Rex."

Rex rolled his eyes.

"Just Rex these days."

Leia embraced the old clone without hesitation.

"It's an honor."

Rex smiled.

"The honor's mine."

Luke and Leia likewise introduced their parents to their closest companions.

Han Solo extended a hand.

"So you're the famous parents."

Anakin shook it.

"So I've been told."

Chewbacca let out a welcoming roar.

Padmé smiled.

"It's wonderful to finally meet you."

Life slowly settled into something none of them had dared imagine for decades.

One quiet afternoon, Anakin and Padmé sat together with R2-D2.

"I want to see everything," Padmé said softly.

"The years we missed."

"So do I," Anakin agreed.

R2 obediently projected recording after recording.

Leia's childhood on Alderaan.

The twins meeting one another on the first Death Star.

Journeys.

Friendships.

Victories.

Losses.

Hours passed.

Eventually, one particularly interesting recording appeared.

Holographic Leia stood alone, speaking calmly but urgently.

"General Kenobi, years ago you served my father in the Clone Wars..."

Anakin suddenly interrupted.

"Pause."

R2 stopped the playback.

Leia's holographic image remained frozen in the air.

The sentence hung unfinished.

Anakin and Padmé looked at one another.

Both smiled.

There was sadness in the smile.

And gratitude.

And nostalgia.

Anakin broke the silence.

"He really did."

He looked toward the frozen image of Leia.

"Obi-Wan really did serve Bail in the Clone Wars."

Padmé nodded.

"And you served me in the Clone Wars."

They sat quietly.

Remembering.

The Senate debates that had created the system of civilian oversight.

Bail and Obi-Wan working together with quiet mutual respect.

Their own endless arguments.

The countless times Padmé had overruled Anakin.

The evenings spent discussing tactics instead of sleeping.

The difficult lessons they had learned together.

The victories.

The losses.

The people who were no longer there to remember those days with them.

Obi-Wan.

Yoda.

Bail.

Breha.

Owen.

Beru.

So many others.

The silence remained comfortable.

Anakin reached for Padmé's hand.

She intertwined her fingers with his.

After everything they had endured...

The war.

The nightmares.

The Empire.

The years in hiding.

The decades apart from their children.

They had survived.

They were still together.

Luke and Leia had survived as well.

And after all those years, they had finally come home.

Padmé leaned forward.

Anakin met her halfway.

They kissed.

Beside them, R2-D2 let out a cheerful whistle.

Outside, the galaxy continued rebuilding itself under the New Republic.

At long last, the fighting was over.

The family was together again.

And for the first time in many years, the future held not fear, but peace.
