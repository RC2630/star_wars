# Part 1

The fires of Geonosis still burned when Count Dooku’s ship vanished into the stars.

In the shattered hangar, the echoes of battle had not yet faded. Scorched metal smoked, droid parts lay scattered like broken bones, and the last of the clone troopers secured the perimeter with mechanical precision. In the center of it all, Anakin Skywalker sat on the ground, pale but conscious, cradling the stump where his right arm had been severed.

Padmé Amidala ran to him without hesitation.

“Anakin!” she cried, dropping to her knees beside him.

“I’m all right,” he said through clenched teeth, though the strain in his voice betrayed him. “It’s just… a scratch.”

She didn’t smile at the attempt. Instead, she took his hand—his remaining hand—and held it tightly, her face drawn with fear and relief.

Obi-Wan Kenobi stood nearby, watching. He said nothing, but his gaze lingered longer than it needed to. Beside him, Master Yoda leaned on his cane, ears angled forward, eyes half-lidded but attentive.

“Strong, their bond is,” Yoda murmured quietly.

Obi-Wan exhaled. “Yes. Stronger than it should be.”

Neither of them moved to interrupt.

When the transport ships arrived, the survivors boarded in silence. Anakin was taken immediately to the medbay upon reaching Coruscant, where skilled surgeons and droid specialists worked to save his life. In time, they fitted him with a prosthetic arm—sleek, responsive, and eerily lifelike beneath its synth-skin covering.

Padmé, too, underwent treatment. The claw marks from the nexu were deep, and the fall from the gunship had left her bruised and shaken. Still, she recovered quickly, her resolve as firm as ever.

During those days, Yoda and Obi-Wan spoke often in private chambers within the Jedi Temple.

“They hide nothing, yet say nothing,” Obi-Wan said one evening. “It’s as if they believe their feelings invisible.”

“Blind, young Skywalker is not,” Yoda replied. “But choose not to see, he does.”

“And confronting him?”

Yoda shook his head slowly. “Close himself off, he would. Deny, deflect… or worse.”

Obi-Wan folded his arms. “Then what do you suggest?”

Yoda’s eyes narrowed slightly. “Observe, we shall. Reveal themselves, they will—if patient, we are.”

It was not long before a quiet decision was made. R2-D2, the astromech who rarely left Anakin’s side, was taken briefly for “routine maintenance.” The procedure was uneventful, unnoticed by the droid itself. When it was returned, it carried something new within its casing—a small, expertly concealed device.

From that moment on, everything R2-D2 saw, heard, and transmitted was relayed to a secure channel within the Jedi Temple.

—

When Anakin was declared fit for duty, Padmé approached the Council with a request.

“I intend to return to Naboo for a time,” she said. “To recover fully. I would feel safer with Jedi protection.”

Mace Windu regarded her steadily. “And you have someone in mind?”

Padmé did not hesitate. “Anakin Skywalker.”

There was a pause.

Yoda tapped his cane once against the floor. “Approve this assignment, I do.”

Obi-Wan’s brow tightened, but he said nothing.

“So be it,” Windu said at last.

—

The journey to Naboo was quiet, almost peaceful.

Once they arrived, Anakin and Padmé wasted no time. They traveled directly to Varykino, the lakeside villa nestled among rolling green hills and shimmering waters. It was secluded, serene—far from the eyes of Coruscant and the burdens of duty.

On the second evening, beneath a soft Naboo sunset, they stood together in a small courtyard overlooking the lake.

A Naboo holy man waited for them, robes shifting gently in the breeze. C-3PO stood stiffly nearby, unsure of his place, while R2-D2 remained close, its photoreceptor quietly focused.

“Are you certain?” Padmé asked softly, her hands clasped with Anakin’s.

“I’ve never been more certain of anything,” he replied.

“This will change everything.”

“I don’t care,” Anakin said. “I care about you.”

She searched his face, then nodded.

“Then we do this.”

The ceremony was simple. No grand audience, no Senate delegates, no Jedi witnesses—only the quiet presence of the lake and the fading light of day.

“Do you, Anakin Skywalker,” the holy man intoned, “pledge yourself to Padmé Amidala, in unity and devotion?”

“I do.”

“And do you, Padmé Amidala, pledge yourself to Anakin Skywalker, in unity and devotion?”

“I do.”

“Then by the will of the Force and the traditions of Naboo, you are joined.”

Anakin slipped a small ring onto her finger. Padmé did the same for him.

They kissed as the sun dipped below the horizon.

Behind them, R2-D2 emitted a soft series of beeps as it recorded the moment in full.

—

In the Jedi Temple, the recording arrived in silence.

Obi-Wan watched it first. He stood alone, arms at his sides, as the image flickered before him—the courtyard, the vows, the unmistakable truth laid bare.

He closed his eyes briefly.

When Yoda entered, Obi-Wan did not turn.

“You’ve seen it,” he said.

“Seen, I have.”

“They’ve married.”

A long pause followed.

“Grave, this is,” Yoda said at last. “Broken, the code has been.”

Obi-Wan’s voice was tight. “He’s my student.”

“And a Jedi, he was.”

Obi-Wan said nothing more.

—

The call came to Naboo the following day.

Anakin answered it in a quiet room within the villa. Obi-Wan’s image appeared, stern and distant.

“Master,” Anakin said, smiling faintly. “This is a surprise.”

Obi-Wan did not return the expression.

“It’s over, Anakin.”

The smile faded. “What do you mean?”

“You are expelled from the Jedi Order. Effective immediately.”

The words struck like a blow.

“Expelled? Why?” Anakin demanded. “What are you talking about?”

Obi-Wan’s gaze did not waver. “You already know why.”

“Master, I—”

The transmission ended.

Anakin stared at the empty projector, his jaw tightening.

—

On Coruscant, the matter did not remain contained.

Master Yoda brought the evidence before the Senate. The reaction was swift and explosive. A Jedi Padawan, bound by vows of discipline and detachment, had secretly married a sitting senator.

The scandal spread across the HoloNet within hours.

Padmé learned of it not from Anakin, but from the news.

She watched the report in stunned silence.

“…sources confirm that Senator Amidala of Naboo has entered into an undisclosed marriage with former Jedi Padawan Anakin Skywalker…”

Former.

She turned the projection off.

When Anakin entered the room, she looked at him steadily.

“It’s public,” she said.

“I know.”

“They told the entire Senate.”

He nodded once, anger simmering beneath the surface.

Padmé took a breath. “Then there’s no path forward for me there anymore.”

“You don’t have to—”

“I do,” she said firmly. “I chose this. I knew what it meant.”

He stepped closer. “Padmé—”

“I won’t hide from it,” she said. “I’ll resign.”

—

In the days that followed, Padmé Amidala formally stepped down from her position in the Galactic Senate.

Meanwhile, Anakin searched for answers.

“They couldn’t have known,” he muttered, pacing. “No one was there.”

C-3PO tilted his head. “Well, sir, there was myself, Artoo, and the officiant, of course—”

“Artoo,” Anakin said suddenly.

The droid beeped.

Hours later, both droids lay partially disassembled across a workbench.

“Hold still,” Anakin muttered as he examined R2-D2’s internal systems.

A moment later, he froze.

“There you are.”

He extracted a small device, turning it between his fingers.

Padmé leaned closer. “What is that?”

“A transmitter,” Anakin said darkly. “A very good one.”

He crushed it in his mechanical hand and tossed the pieces aside.

“They were watching us.”

Padmé’s expression hardened. “Then they already had everything they needed.”

Anakin exhaled slowly. “They outplayed me.”

—

With the truth laid bare and their former lives behind them, the weight of uncertainty lingered—but neither of them let it take hold.

One evening, as the sun set over the lake, Padmé stood at the balcony, the water reflecting gold and crimson.

Anakin joined her, his new arm resting lightly at his side.

“What happens now?” he asked.

She was quiet for a moment.

“Now,” she said gently, “we stop worrying about what comes next.”

He looked at her. “Just like that?”

“Just for a little while,” she said. “We’ve lost enough already.”

Anakin considered that, then nodded.

“All right.”

She smiled faintly and took his hand.

They spent their days walking the lake country, far from politics and war. They rode across green fields, shared quiet meals, and spoke of simple things—memories, dreams, and moments that had nothing to do with duty or expectation.

For a time, the galaxy felt distant.

And in that quiet corner of Naboo, Anakin Skywalker and Padmé Amidala allowed themselves something rare and fragile—

Peace.

# Part 2

The quiet weeks in Naboo’s lake country passed gently, almost untouched by the turmoil beyond its rolling hills. Morning mist drifted over the water, evenings glowed gold across the surface of the lake, and for a time, Anakin Skywalker and Padmé Amidala lived as though the rest of the galaxy had forgotten them.

But the galaxy had not forgotten.

War had begun to spread—system by system, report by report. Even here, faint echoes reached them through passing news transmissions and distant conversations. Neither of them spoke much about it at first.

Eventually, the stillness began to feel less like peace and more like a pause.

One afternoon, they returned fully to Varykino Villa after a long ride through the countryside. The air inside felt different—less like a retreat, more like a place waiting for decisions to be made.

Padmé stood near one of the tall windows, looking out over the lake. “We can’t stay in between forever,” she said quietly.

Anakin leaned against a nearby table, arms crossed. “I know.”

She turned to face him. “So… what do you want to do now?”

There was no hesitation.

“I want to go back,” Anakin said. “To Coruscant.”

Padmé studied him carefully. “Why?”

His jaw tightened. “Because they think they can just—spy on us, manipulate everything, throw me out like I’m nothing… and then expose you to the entire Senate like it’s some kind of lesson.”

His mechanical hand flexed slightly.

“They don’t get to do that and walk away.”

“Anakin,” Padmé said softly, “what are you saying?”

“I’m saying I want revenge.”

The word hung in the air.

Padmé didn’t react immediately. She took a slow breath, then stepped closer.

“No,” she said firmly.

His eyes narrowed. “No?”

“No,” she repeated. “You’re not doing that.”

“They ruined everything!” he snapped. “They took my place in the Order, they took your position in the Senate—”

“They didn’t take anything I didn’t choose to risk,” Padmé cut in. “I knew what I was doing.”

“That doesn’t make it right!”

“No, it doesn’t,” she said evenly. “But revenge won’t fix it.”

Anakin turned away, pacing. “You don’t understand. I can’t just let this go.”

“Yes, I do understand,” she said, following him. “And that’s exactly why I’m telling you not to do this.”

He stopped, looking back at her. “So what—just forget it? Pretend none of it happened?”

“I’m saying think about what happens if you act on this,” Padmé replied. “You go back to the Jedi Temple, angry, looking for payback. What do you think they’ll see?”

He didn’t answer.

“They won’t see someone wronged,” she continued. “They’ll see someone unstable. An intruder. A threat.”

Her voice sharpened slightly.

“And they will put you in your place. Quickly.”

Anakin’s expression hardened, but doubt flickered beneath it.

“You can’t fight the entire Jedi Order,” she said. “Not like that. It’s not strength—it’s self-destruction.”

He looked away again. “I’m not afraid of them.”

“This isn’t about fear,” Padmé said. “It’s about reality.”

Silence stretched between them.

He exhaled sharply. “I just… I can’t stand what they did.”

Padmé’s tone softened, but only slightly. “Then don’t repeat the same mistake.”

He frowned. “What do you mean?”

She held his gaze.

“Tatooine.”

The word alone was enough.

Anakin went still.

“You told me what happened,” she said quietly. “You said you went there because you were angry. Because you wanted revenge.”

His voice dropped. “That was different.”

“Was it?” she asked gently. “Or did it feel just as justified at the time?”

He didn’t answer.

“You said yourself,” Padmé continued, “that you lost control. That you did something you can’t take back.”

The room felt heavier now.

“If you chase revenge again,” she said, “how do you know it ends any differently?”

Anakin’s shoulders lowered slightly. The tension in his stance eased, just a fraction.

“I don’t,” he admitted.

Padmé stepped closer, placing a hand on his arm—his real arm.

“Then don’t walk that path again.”

He looked at her, conflict still visible, but no longer overwhelming.

After a long moment, he nodded once.

“…All right.”

She gave a small, relieved breath.

“I’ll let it go,” he said. “Or at least… I’ll try not to think about them.”

“That’s enough,” Padmé said.

—

The next morning, they sat together at a long table in the villa, datapads and notes spread between them.

“If we’re not going back,” Padmé said, “then we need to figure out what we *are* doing.”

Anakin leaned forward. “We start with what we can do.”

He gestured to himself. “I can still use the Force. I’m one of the best pilots in the Republic. I know how to fight—with a lightsaber or without one. And I can fix or build almost anything mechanical.”

Padmé nodded. “That’s a strong start.”

“What about you?” he asked.

She considered it. “I understand politics—how systems function, how people maneuver within them. I know negotiation, mediation… and how to recognize when someone is playing a deeper game.”

She paused, then added, “And I can handle a blaster well enough.”

Anakin gave a faint smirk. “I’ve seen that.”

She returned a small smile, then grew serious again.

“So,” she said, “we combine all of that.”

He tilted his head. “Into what?”

Padmé leaned back slightly. “We don’t belong to the Senate anymore. You don’t belong to the Jedi. No one controls us.”

Anakin’s eyes sharpened with interest.

“Go on.”

“We operate independently,” she said. “We choose what we involve ourselves in. Not because someone hires us or orders us—but because we decide it matters.”

“Like bounty hunters?” he asked.

“Not exactly,” Padmé replied. “They take jobs from others. We don’t.”

Anakin nodded slowly. “We pick our own missions.”

“Yes.”

He leaned forward, energy returning to his voice. “I handle combat, pursuit, flying—anything that requires speed or force.”

“And I handle negotiations,” Padmé added. “Diplomacy, strategy… knowing when to talk and when not to.”

“And when talking fails,” Anakin said, “you back me up.”

She met his gaze. “Always.”

A quiet understanding passed between them.

Outside, the galaxy was fracturing under the strain of war. Systems fell into chaos, alliances shifted, and countless lives were caught in the middle.

“There’s no shortage of places where we could make a difference,” Padmé said.

Anakin nodded. “And no one to tell us we can’t.”

For the first time since leaving Coruscant, his path felt clear—not defined by the Jedi, not dictated by the Republic, but chosen.

He reached across the table, taking her hand.

“Partners,” he said.

Padmé smiled, steady and certain.

“In every sense.”

And so, as the Clone Wars spread across the stars, Anakin Skywalker and Padmé Amidala stepped into the conflict not as servants of any order or government—but as something new.

A team bound by trust, by choice, and by a shared purpose they would define for themselves.

# Part 3

Before leaving Coruscant for Naboo, while the wounds of Geonosis were still fresh, Anakin Skywalker had been given something both familiar and new.

In a quiet chamber of the Jedi Temple, he stood at a workbench, tools laid out with careful precision. The prosthetic that replaced his right arm gleamed under the light—responsive, powerful, but still foreign in subtle ways. Across from him lay the components of a lightsaber.

The weapon he had used on Geonosis—a temporary one with a green blade—rested nearby, already disassembled.

“This one will be yours,” Obi-Wan had said earlier, before leaving him alone. “Constructed by your own hand. As it should be.”

Now, Anakin worked in silence.

Piece by piece, he assembled the hilt—adjusting the grip to suit the strength and sensitivity of his new hand, recalibrating the balance so it felt right when held between flesh and metal. When he finally installed the crystal and sealed the casing, he paused for a moment.

Then he activated it.

A blue blade burst to life with a sharp, clean hum.

Anakin studied it, turning his wrist slightly, feeling the weight, the response.

“It’ll do,” he murmured.

—

After the wedding on Naboo, after Obi-Wan’s cold message and the sudden severing of his place in the Order, Anakin had expected something else to follow.

A retrieval. A summons. Someone sent to take the weapon from him.

Days passed.

Then weeks.

No one came.

One evening, standing alone on the villa’s terrace, he ignited the blade again, watching its glow reflect across the lake.

“They’re not coming for it,” he said aloud.

Padmé, seated nearby, looked up. “For your lightsaber?”

He nodded. “I thought they would. It’s not exactly something they’d want… out here.”

“But they haven’t.”

“No.”

He turned the hilt slightly in his mechanical grip.

“Either they’re allowing it,” he said, “or they don’t care enough to stop me.”

Padmé studied him. “And what do you think?”

Anakin deactivated the blade with a soft snap-hiss. “I think it means it’s mine now.”

—

From that point on, he trained with it—earnestly, relentlessly.

At first, it wasn’t easy.

The prosthetic arm responded perfectly in terms of strength, but it lacked the instinctive nuance of living muscle. His movements were sometimes too sharp, too rigid. The balance felt different when one side of his body no longer tired the same way.

More than once, he stopped mid-motion, frustrated.

“It’s off,” he muttered during one session, lowering the blade.

Padmé, watching from the edge of the courtyard, tilted her head. “What is?”

“Everything,” he said. “The timing, the weight… it’s not how it used to be.”

She stepped closer. “Then stop trying to make it how it used to be.”

He looked at her.

“You’re not the same as before,” she continued. “So don’t fight like you are.”

Anakin frowned slightly, considering that.

“…Adapt,” he said.

Padmé gave a small nod. “Exactly.”

So he did.

He adjusted his stance, refined his strikes, learned how to let the mechanical precision of his new arm work *with* him instead of against him. Over time, the movements smoothed out. The blade became an extension of him once again—different, but no less effective.

By the time he and Padmé began their self-appointed work across the galaxy, the lightsaber felt natural in his hand.

—

Their first missions were measured.

They chose worlds balanced on the edge of decision—places where the Republic still held influence, but where the pull of the Confederacy of Independent Systems was growing stronger.

Padmé would meet with local leaders, speaking in council chambers and private offices, navigating delicate tensions.

“You don’t need to choose chaos to gain autonomy,” she told one planetary governor. “There are still paths within the Republic that preserve your voice.”

“And if those paths fail?” the governor asked.

“Then you’ll know you exhausted every option before turning to war,” she replied calmly.

Anakin remained close, often unseen but always present—watching, listening, ready.

Most of the time, words were enough.

When they weren’t, he stepped in.

Battle droids proved predictable opponents. Their movements were rigid, their tactics repetitive. Anakin cut through them with practiced efficiency, the blue blade flashing as blaster bolts deflected harmlessly away.

Padmé, when necessary, fought alongside him—steady, precise, never reckless.

They found a rhythm quickly.

—

Malastare was different.

The planet was already divided when they arrived. The Grans stood firmly with the Republic, while the Dugs—resentful, defiant—leaned toward the Confederacy.

Padmé met with both sides.

“You’re still one world,” she said to a mixed delegation. “If this turns into open conflict, you won’t just lose territory—you’ll lose each other.”

A Dug representative sneered. “We’ve already lost enough under Republic rule.”

“And you think the Confederacy will give you more?” Padmé asked. “Or simply use your anger to secure its own position?”

The talks faltered.

Then the reinforcements arrived.

CIS forces descended onto Malastare in full strength—transports, tanks, and ranks of battle droids. Emboldened, the Dugs made their decision.

They declared civil war.

From that moment, diplomacy gave way to survival.

Anakin and Padmé chose their side without hesitation.

“We’re with the Grans,” Anakin said, igniting his lightsaber.

Padmé nodded. “Then we hold the line.”

At first, it was familiar.

Blaster fire lit the battlefield. Droids advanced in waves. Anakin moved through them with speed and precision, cutting down one after another. Padmé coordinated defenses, rallying Gran fighters and directing movements where needed.

“Left flank—reinforce now!” she shouted. “Don’t let them break through!”

“I’ve got it!” Anakin called back, already moving.

For a time, it worked.

Then everything shifted.

A new presence entered the battlefield—tall, mechanical, unmistakable.

General Grievous.

The cyborg general advanced through the chaos, his movements unnervingly fluid for something so heavily armored. His gaze locked onto Anakin almost immediately.

“A Jedi,” Grievous rasped. “How fortunate.”

Anakin stepped forward, blade raised. “You’re not taking this planet.”

Grievous’ mechanical cough rattled. “We shall see.”

The duel began without ceremony.

Grievous moved with frightening speed, striking with multiple limbs, each blow calculated and relentless. Anakin met him head-on, deflecting, countering, pushing forward with everything he had.

But the difference showed.

Grievous pressed harder, forcing Anakin back step by step. The young Padawan adapted quickly, but not quickly enough.

A heavy strike knocked him off balance.

Another sent him to one knee.

His lightsaber barely came up in time to block the next blow—and even then, the impact drove him back across the ground.

“Anakin!” Padmé’s voice cut through the noise.

Grievous advanced.

“Your training is incomplete,” the general said coldly.

Anakin tried to rise, but his strength faltered.

Before Grievous could deliver the final strike, blaster fire erupted between them.

Padmé.

She moved quickly, firing with precision—not to defeat, but to distract.

“Get up!” she shouted.

Anakin forced himself to his feet as she reached him, grabbing his arm.

“We’re leaving,” she said.

“I can still—”

“No,” she said firmly. “Now.”

Grievous stepped forward again, but the moment had passed. Smoke, movement, and chaos swallowed the battlefield just long enough.

By the time Grievous cleared the obstruction, their ship was already lifting off.

—

They returned to Naboo without delay.

In Theed’s medical facilities, Anakin was treated for his injuries—burns, fractures, and the strain of a fight pushed too far. Padmé remained close the entire time.

When he was stable, they returned once more to Varykino Villa.

Recovery this time was slower.

One evening, as Anakin sat on the edge of the bed, flexing his restored strength, frustration broke through.

“I should have beaten him,” he said.

Padmé looked up from where she sat nearby. “You were outmatched.”

“I’m not strong enough,” he insisted.

She shook her head. “You did your best.”

“That’s not enough if I lose.”

“And what would you prove by winning?” she asked.

He frowned. “That I’m strong enough.”

“For who?” Padmé pressed. “For me? For yourself? For someone who isn’t even here?”

Anakin didn’t answer.

“You don’t need to prove your strength to anyone,” she said. “And chasing strength just because you lost once—that’s not a path you want to follow.”

He looked down at his hands—one flesh, one mechanical.

“…I hate losing,” he admitted.

“I know,” she said gently. “But you’re still learning.”

He glanced at her.

“You’re a Padawan,” she continued. “You’re not supposed to win every fight.”

The words settled in.

After a moment, he nodded slowly. “Yeah… I guess that’s true.”

Padmé stood and walked over, resting a hand lightly against his shoulder.

“I’m just glad you’re alive,” she said.

Anakin let out a quiet breath.

“…Yeah,” he said. “Me too.”

He reached for her hand, holding it firmly.

For all the pain of the defeat—for both body and pride—one truth remained clear between them.

They were still together.

And for now, that was enough.

# Epilogue

Ten years passed.

The Clone Wars consumed much of that decade before finally burning themselves out.

Three years into the conflict, Count Dooku and General Grievous were hunted down by Obi-Wan Kenobi and the clone forces under his command. Their deaths removed the Confederacy's greatest military leaders, and many across the Republic believed the end of the war had finally arrived.

Instead, another crisis erupted.

For years, Supreme Chancellor Palpatine had governed under sweeping emergency powers granted during the conflict. With the war effectively won, the Jedi expected him to surrender those powers and restore the Republic's ordinary constitutional order.

He refused.

Concerned by his growing authority, four Jedi Masters—Mace Windu, Kit Fisto, Agen Kolar, and Saesee Tiin—went to confront him in his office.

According to official records released later, Mace Windu addressed him plainly.

"The war is over, Chancellor. The Republic must return to its laws."

Palpatine remained seated.

"No."

"The Senate entrusted those powers to you."

"They entrusted them because they were necessary."

"They are no longer necessary."

"They are."

When the Jedi informed him that he was under arrest, Palpatine rose.

Without another word, a crimson lightsaber sprang from his sleeve.

The revelation stunned the Republic.

The Supreme Chancellor was a Sith Lord.

The duel that followed became one of the most studied lightsaber engagements in modern history.

Kit Fisto fell first.

Agen Kolar followed.

Then Saesee Tiin.

Only Mace Windu remained.

The battle carried through the Chancellor's office until, at last, Windu forced Palpatine's weapon from his hand.

"You've hidden behind this office long enough," Windu said.

Palpatine answered only with hatred in his eyes.

Moments later, Mace Windu struck the final blow.

With Palpatine dead, investigations uncovered years of deception and manipulation that had reached into every level of the Republic government. The revelations shook public confidence, but they also brought clarity to many mysteries that had lingered since the beginning of the war.

The Senate elected Bail Organa as the new Supreme Chancellor.

He inherited a Republic exhausted by years of bloodshed.

His first addresses to the Senate focused not on victory, but on rebuilding.

"We have survived," he said before the assembled senators, "but survival alone is not enough. We must restore trust where fear has ruled. We must restore law where emergency became habit. We must restore peace where war became ordinary."

The work took years.

Republic institutions were rebuilt.

Emergency powers were repealed.

Negotiations with former Separatist worlds slowly brought the fighting to an end.

The galaxy did not heal overnight, but it began to heal.

On Coruscant, another chapter quietly closed.

Maul, captured on Mandalore by Ahsoka Tano during the final years of the war, remained imprisoned under Republic custody.

During several public hearings concerning the Sith's role in galactic events, he made a declaration that surprised nearly everyone.

"I reject the Sith," he said through the transparent detention field surrounding his cell.

A senator frowned.

"You expect us to believe that?"

"I care little what you believe."

"You once called yourself a Sith Lord."

"I call myself that no longer."

"Why?"

Maul looked away for several long moments.

"Because that path brought nothing but chains."

Whatever anyone thought of the sincerity of his words, one fact became increasingly difficult to dispute.

With Palpatine dead, Count Dooku dead, and Maul openly renouncing the Sith, there were no known Sith Lords left in the galaxy.

For the first time in generations, the Sith Order ceased to exist.

—

Throughout those turbulent years, Anakin Skywalker and Padmé Amidala continued following the course they had chosen for themselves.

They answered to no government.

They belonged to no military.

Sometimes they found themselves fighting beside Republic forces against droid armies threatening civilian populations.

Sometimes they negotiated ceasefires between neighboring systems before conflicts could erupt.

Sometimes they exposed criminal syndicates exploiting the chaos of war.

Their independence allowed them to go where neither the Republic nor the Confederacy could easily act.

Neither side always welcomed their presence.

Many civilians did.

—

The year the Clone Wars finally ended also brought another beginning.

Twin children were born at a hospital in Theed.

Luke.

Leia.

Holding his son for the first time, Anakin laughed softly.

"He has your eyes."

Padmé smiled tiredly from the hospital bed.

"So does his sister."

Anakin looked down at the tiny girl sleeping peacefully.

"I think she has your determination already."

"She's three minutes old."

"I know."

"I still think you're right."

For several years afterward, missions became far less important than family.

Their work slowed almost to a stop.

Luke and Leia learned to walk among the gardens of Varykino.

They chased each other across Naboo's fields.

R2-D2 patiently tolerated countless games involving imaginary starships.

Whenever Anakin and Padmé accepted assignments considered too dangerous for children, they left the twins with the Naberrie family.

Jobal always welcomed them warmly.

"So," she would say with a smile as Luke and Leia ran inside, "have your parents gone off saving the galaxy again?"

Leia would nod proudly.

"They'll be back."

"What if you scratch it?"

"I won't."

"What if you break something?"

"I'll fix it."

Han narrowed his eyes.

"You promise?"

"I give you my word."

Padmé added quietly,

"He keeps his promises."

Han studied both of them for several moments.

Finally he sighed.

"...All right."

He pointed a finger at Anakin.

"But the moment you're done with Jabba..."

"...I want my ship back."

"You have my word."

Han nodded once.

"Then we've got a deal."

—

The remainder of the trip became an impromptu flying lesson.

Han slid into the copilot's chair while Anakin took the pilot's seat.

"All right," Han began.

"The *Falcon* handles differently from most freighters."

"I can tell."

"The steering response is—"

Anakin gently banked the ship through a hyperspace calibration maneuver.

"Like this?"

Han blinked.

"...Yes."

"And the inertial compensation feels a little delayed."

"It is."

Anakin adjusted another control.

"So you compensate before the turn instead of during it."

Han frowned.

"...Exactly."

Several minutes later—

"The power distribution—"

"I've already rerouted it."

Han looked at the display.

"...You did."

Another few minutes—

"The dorsal targeting sensors sometimes—"

"I recalibrated them."

"When?"

"While you were explaining the engines."

Han slowly leaned back.

"You really are not a typical pilot."

Anakin smiled.

"Well..."

He rested one hand lightly on the controls.

"After years and years of flying..."

"...you kind of just get the hang of things."

Han sighed dramatically.

"I spent years learning this ship."

Anakin shrugged.

"It's a nice ship."

"...That's somehow even more insulting."

Lando laughed from behind them.

"I think he's mastered it."

"So do I," Han admitted.

He stood up.

"All right."

"It's yours until Naboo."

He and Lando retreated into the lounge, leaving Anakin at the controls.

At first they watched.

Then they stopped worrying altogether.

Anakin flew the *Falcon* as though he had owned it for years.

—

A few hours later, the freighter settled onto a landing platform in Theed.

The group walked through the capital until they reached the Naberrie home.

Outside, parked neatly near the entrance, sat Anakin's custom-built starfighter.

R2-D2 stood proudly beside it.

"There you are," Anakin said.

R2 whistled happily.

"You did well."

The astromech emitted an especially pleased series of chirps.

Padmé opened the front door.

"We're home."

—

R2 immediately rolled into the house.

Padmé smiled.

"Han, Lando..."

She gestured inside.

"Come in."

By now, the tension between them had largely disappeared.

During the journey they had traded stories, laughed together, and discovered that despite their very different backgrounds, they enjoyed each other's company.

Anakin knelt beside R2.

"Han. Lando."

He rested a hand on the astromech's dome.

"This is R2-D2."

R2 gave a cheerful whistle.

"My astromech."

"He is very friendly..."

R2 chirped proudly.

"...and very, very loyal."

Han crouched beside him.

"Nice to meet you."

R2 answered with an enthusiastic beep.

Lando smiled.

"I like him already."

—

Padmé led them farther inside.

"My sister, Sola."

Sola smiled warmly.

"My parents..."

Ruwee stepped forward first.

"Welcome."

Jobal followed.

"It's good to meet both of you."

Before anyone could continue, footsteps sounded on the staircase.

Two children hurried down.

Behind them came two teenagers.

Padmé smiled.

"These are my children, Luke and Leia."

The twins waved enthusiastically.

"And my nieces..."

She gestured toward the older girls.

"Ryoo and Pooja."

Han smiled.

"Han Solo."

"Lando Calrissian."

The introductions continued until everyone had met.

Ruwee seemed particularly interested in their stories.

"So," he said after hearing a few of Han's adventures, "why don't you stay here while Anakin and Padmé take Jabba to Coruscant?"

Han blinked.

"...Really?"

"Of course."

Anakin looked uncertain.

"They're smugglers."

Everyone looked toward him.

"Are you sure it's a good idea for them to stay in the house with the children...?"

Ruwee smiled calmly.

"Smuggler or not..."

He looked toward Han and Lando.

"...they seem fun."

"Don't worry, Anakin."

"I'm sure they won't cause any issues."

Anakin hesitated.

"...I have my doubts."

He looked toward Luke and Leia.

"But your house..."

"...your call."

He sighed.

"Just keep Luke and Leia safe."

"We will," Ruwee promised.

—

Soon afterward, Anakin, Padmé, and R2 boarded the *Falcon* once more.

Jabba remained securely locked inside the storage compartment.

The freighter lifted off toward Coruscant.

—

Rather than landing at one of the Republic's primary spaceports, Anakin chose a secluded landing area on the outskirts of the capital.

Han's concerns about the ship had not been unfounded.

Its extensive modifications would invite unwanted attention from customs inspectors.

"Better not tempt fate," Padmé agreed.

Together they hauled Jabba out of the cargo compartment.

The Hutt grumbled continuously in Huttese.

"I know," Anakin replied.

"You've already said that three times."

Eventually they entered the Republic's central law enforcement headquarters.

The officers behind the reception desk froze.

One slowly stood.

"...Is that..."

Another officer looked twice.

"It is."

"That's Jabba the Hutt."

Within moments the building erupted into activity.

Several officers hurried over to secure the prisoner.

One senior investigator stared at Anakin in disbelief.

"...How on earth did you capture Jabba the Hutt?"

He shook his head.

"Capturing a crime lord on his level and successfully bringing him into custody would be a feat that almost nobody has accomplished."

Anakin scratched the back of his neck.

"Well..."

He smiled awkwardly.

"...it's complicated."

The officer waited.

"...Don't worry about it."

The investigator looked at Padmé.

She smiled politely.

"It really is a long story."

—

Meanwhile, back on Naboo...

Han looked toward the four younger members of the household.

"So."

He folded his arms.

"Do any of you know how to play sabacc?"

Luke nodded enthusiastically.

"A little."

Leia nodded too.

"We're not very good."

Ryoo smiled.

"We've played a few times."

Pooja shrugged.

"I'm still learning."

Han grinned.

"Want to see what *real* competitive sabacc looks like?"

All four answered together.

"Yes!"

Within minutes a table had been cleared.

Han and Lando sat opposite each other.

The four younger spectators crowded around from every possible angle.

Luke practically leaned over Han's shoulder.

Leia watched Lando's every move.

Ryoo and Pooja quietly debated possible hands between themselves.

From across the room, the adults observed.

Jobal smiled.

"Hopefully these two don't get the kids addicted to sabacc."

Ruwee chuckled.

"Oh, I'm sure they'll be fine."

"It's just one match."

Sola folded her arms.

"Father..."

"...you are too optimistic about everything."

"I've been told that before."

"Usually by me."

"Usually."

—

Some time later, Anakin, Padmé, and R2 returned.

They entered the house quietly.

The game was still underway.

Han and Lando had reached the endgame.

Neither had noticed anyone else entering.

Anakin and Padmé joined Jobal, Ruwee, and Sola at the edge of the room.

A few tense moments later—

Han laid down his final hand.

"I won!"

Lando looked at the cards.

"I know."

Leia tilted her head.

"Do you get a prize?"

Lando smiled.

"We agreed there would be no stakes before the match."

Luke frowned.

"So..."

"...you don't get anything for winning?"

"And you don't lose anything for losing?"

Han nodded.

"No."

"Not when we agreed beforehand on this."

Ryoo smiled approvingly.

"At least you are honorable about it."

Lando laughed.

"We may be criminals..."

"...but we are not jerks."

"We keep our words."

Pooja considered that.

"That is an interesting distinction."

"It matters," Han said simply.

—

Not long afterward, everyone gathered outside.

Anakin handed the *Millennium Falcon*'s controls back to Han.

"As promised."

Han looked over the ship carefully.

"No scratches."

"I told you."

"No confiscation."

"I told you."

"You even topped off the fuel."

Anakin smiled.

"It seemed polite."

Han laughed.

"I appreciate that."

The farewells were warm.

The *Falcon* lifted off and disappeared into the evening sky.

Inside the Naberrie home, the extended family shared a long, cheerful meal together before the time finally came to return to Varykino.

—

Aboard Anakin's starfighter, Luke and Leia sat behind their parents while R2 occupied his familiar socket.

The ship glided peacefully across Naboo's skies.

After several quiet minutes—

"Dad?"

Anakin smiled.

"Yes?"

Leia looked thoughtfully out the canopy.

"That Han guy..."

She nodded to herself.

"...he's *really* handsome."

For a heartbeat, there was silence.

Then Anakin burst into laughter.

Padmé laughed just as hard.

Luke looked between them.

"What?"

Padmé wiped a tear from the corner of her eye.

"Nothing."

She smiled at Leia.

"Nothing at all."

Anakin was still chuckling as he returned his attention to flying.

—

Far away, aboard the *Millennium Falcon*...

Han settled comfortably into the pilot's seat.

"Well."

He smiled.

"We met some pretty interesting people together, don't you think, Lando?"

Lando leaned back in the copilot's chair.

"We sure did."

Han shook his head in amazement.

"I still can't believe they captured us..."

"...and then let us go free."

Lando nodded.

"It worked out for everyone."

"They wanted Jabba gone."

"We wanted to stay free..."

"...and get paid for delivering the spice."

He smiled.

"Both sides got what they wanted."

Han laughed.

"And those kids."

He shook his head again.

"They were so into sabacc."

Lando chuckled.

"Well..."

"Sabacc is fun to play."

"And it's fun to watch."

"That's why it's stayed popular across the galaxy for so long."

Han grew thoughtful.

After a long pause, he spoke again.

"...Do you think..."

He looked ahead through the cockpit canopy.

"...I could eventually marry one of them?"

Lando stared at him for a second.

Then burst into laughter.

"Han!"

"What?"

"They're children and teenagers!"

Han grinned.

"I said *eventually*."

Lando shook his head, still laughing.

"I have absolutely no response to that."

The *Millennium Falcon* jumped into hyperspace once more, carrying the two smugglers toward whatever adventure awaited them next.
